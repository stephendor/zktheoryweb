\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Motivation: UK Social Mobility and Life-Course Dynamics}

Intergenerational social mobility in the United Kingdom has stagnated or declined since the 1970s: recent cohorts show lower rates of upward movement relative to their parents' generation \citep{blanden2004changes, blanden2013intergenerational, goldthorpe2008trends}. Behind this stagnation lie persistent unemployment, income churning, and structural inactivity --- dynamics that create multiple equilibria in employment-income states. Cross-sectional snapshots --- comparing parents' and children's occupational class or income at a single point --- often miss these dynamics. What matters is not merely where individuals start and end, but the shape of the path between.

The British Household Panel Study (BHPS, 1991--2008) and its successor Understanding Society (USoc, 2009--2023) together provide two of the longest continuous household panels in the social sciences. Our primary analysis draws on the USoc-era panel: from over 118,000 respondents, we reconstruct 27,280 individual trajectories with at least 10 consecutive years of data within the USoc observation window (start years 2009--2013). Each trajectory is a time-ordered sequence through a nine-state space combining employment status --- employed (E), unemployed (U), or inactive (I) --- with income band --- low (L), mid (M), or high (H) --- derived from equivalised household income relative to 60\% of the contemporary median. As cross-era validation, we separately reconstruct 8,509 BHPS-era trajectories (1991--2008, mean length 14.5 years) by fixing the BHPS \texttt{\{wave\}pid} $\to$ USoc \texttt{pidp} linkage via the cross-wave identifier file, and equivalising BHPS raw household income (\texttt{fihhmn}) using the modified OECD scale (\texttt{eq\_moecd}). We also identify 8,459 spanning trajectories observed in both surveys, enabling 20+ year longitudinal tracking (\S4.7).

The resulting trajectories reveal a labour market in which employment dominates (58\% of person-years) but inactivity is pervasive (32\%), and low income affects over a third (36\%) of observed person-years. These are not transient states: the modal trajectory length in our sample is 13 years, sufficient to observe structural persistence, cycling, and --- for some --- escape from disadvantaged states.

UK labour market trajectories are also shaped by regional economic structures and local labour market conditions \citep{hmgov2022levelling}. The present analysis operates at the national level; regional stratification (NUTS2 or Travel-to-Work Area) is a natural extension of the Markov memory ladder (\S5.4).

The policy question is whether disadvantaged trajectories are absorbing or transient. If low-income churning creates persistent cycles from which exit is rare, marginal interventions targeting single transitions will not be enough. If trajectories are well described by low-order Markov dynamics, targeting the right transitions could suffice. Distinguishing between these scenarios requires methods that go beyond measuring distances between sequences, and --- as we show --- test statistics that compare persistence diagrams as geometric objects rather than collapsing them to scalars.

\subsection{Research Gap}

Sequence analysis methods --- optimal matching (OM) and its many variants --- have dominated life-course research since \citeauthor{abbott1995sequence}'s (\citeyear{abbott1995sequence}) influential importation of biological sequence techniques into sociology. OM and related methods treat trajectories as strings and compute pairwise distances under substitution and indel (insertion-deletion) cost matrices, enabling cluster analysis to identify "types" of life course. This approach has been productive, generating typologies of employment careers, family formation, and housing trajectories across many national contexts \citep{abbott2000sequence, lesnard2010setting, gauthier2010multichannel}.

However, sequence analysis has limitations for the questions we pose. OM distances depend on the substitution cost matrix, which is typically set a priori or estimated from transition rates --- introducing subjectivity at a foundational level \citep{robette2008comparing}. OM and its variants also treat trajectories as one-dimensional objects: they measure pairwise distances but have no concept of the global geometry or topology of the trajectory space. They cannot naturally detect persistent clusters (connected components), loops (cyclical traps), or higher-dimensional voids in the point cloud of embedded trajectories --- though sequence-based clusters may capture similar substantive phenomena by other means. Nor is there a standard framework for formal significance testing --- for asking whether the observed structure exceeds what would be expected under explicit generative null models.

Markov chain models, the other workhorse of life-course analysis, capture local transition probabilities but are not designed to detect global shape. A Markov model can tell us the probability of moving from unemployment to employment, but it cannot detect whether the cloud of all trajectories in embedding space has a topological signature of cyclical trapping, or whether the number and separation of trajectory clusters exceeds what memoryless transitions would produce.

Topological data analysis (TDA), via persistent homology (PH), addresses these limitations. PH identifies topological features --- connected components ($H_0$), loops ($H_1$), and higher-dimensional voids --- that persist across a range of spatial scales, providing a multi-scale signature of the shape of a point cloud that is robust to noise and dimensionality \citep{carlsson2009topology, edelsbrunner2010computational}. TDA has been applied to financial time series \citep{gidea2018topological}, brain networks \citep{saggar2018towards}, gene expression \citep{rizvi2017single}, and materials science \citep{hiraoka2016hierarchical}. PH hypothesis testing has been developed by \citet{robinson2017hypothesis}, and TDA--Markov hybrids have appeared in network analysis \citep{stolz2017persistent}. Recent work has brought TDA to adjacent social-science settings --- notably network persistence in organisational sociology \citep{sizemore2018importance} and simplicial complexes for modelling social contagion \citep{iacopini2019simplicial} --- but these operate on cross-sectional network snapshots rather than individual-level longitudinal sequences. To our knowledge, no study has applied persistent homology with structured null-model batteries to longitudinal socioeconomic trajectories.

Critical gaps remain: How does the topology of trajectory space relate to socioeconomic mobility regimes? Can we systematically test whether observed topological structure exceeds Markov baselines of varying complexity? And does the choice of test statistic --- scalar summary versus diagram-level comparison --- change the answer?

\subsection{Contribution and Roadmap}

We apply persistent homology to longitudinal life-course data and validate the resulting topology against a hierarchy of generative null models. The regime typology qualitatively resembles what sequence analysis finds in similar data \citep[e.g.,][]{bukodi2019social}; TDA adds multi-scale geometric characterisation and formal significance testing. A direct comparison with optimal matching yields ARI = 0.26 at matched \textit{k} = 7 (\S5.1).

The \textbf{Markov memory ladder} --- five null models of increasing complexity (label shuffle, cohort shuffle, order shuffle, Markov order-1, Markov order-2) --- decomposes the topological signal into contributions from state frequencies, temporal ordering, and memory length. We implement the ladder with two test statistics: total persistence (scalar) and Wasserstein diagram-level distances (geometric). The two give different answers for the Markov null. Total persistence says the topology is bounded by first-order dynamics; Wasserstein says the observed diagram is an outlier. This discrepancy --- detectable only because we compared both statistics on the same null battery --- has implications for TDA hypothesis testing more broadly.

We also analyse overlapping career-phase windows to quantify regime stickiness. Phase-order shuffle testing (\textit{n} = 500) finds no evidence that phase ordering creates additional topology ($H_0$ \textit{p} = 0.98, 95\% CI [0.968, 0.992]; $H_1$ \textit{p} = 0.542, 95\% CI [0.498, 0.586]).

We find that $H_0$ topological structure (cluster separation) is order-dependent under total-persistence testing (order-shuffle \textit{p} < 0.005) but not rejected under first- or second-order Markov nulls (Markov-1 \textit{p} = 1.000, Markov-2 \textit{p} = 1.000). However, upgrading to Wasserstein diagram-level distances reverses the Markov-1 conclusion: Markov-1 $H_0$ is rejected (\textit{p} = 0.002), indicating that the connected-component structure of the trajectory manifold cannot be reproduced by a first-order Markov chain when diagrams are compared as geometric objects. Markov-2 is \textit{not} rejected under Wasserstein (\textit{p} = 0.546), localising the non-Markovian structure to second-order memory. Label shuffle serves as a negative control under both statistics (\textit{p} = 0.315 total persistence, \textit{p} = 0.452 Wasserstein). Order-shuffle $H_0$ is borderline under Wasserstein (\textit{p} = 0.058), while $H_1$ remains non-significant under both statistics and all null types. These findings are stable across landmark counts from 2,500 to 8,000 (Appendix B) under total-persistence testing, replicate on BHPS-era trajectories under both total persistence and Wasserstein testing (\S4.7), and are robust to embedding sensitivity testing across six configurations (\S5.4). Stratified analysis reveals origin-dependent topology; all significant results survive Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction. Overlapping 10-year windows confirm extreme regime stickiness (5.6\% escape rate from disadvantaged regimes; 11.0\% under non-overlapping windows).


\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Sequence Analysis in Social Science}

The application of sequence analysis to life-course data originated with \citeauthor{abbott1995sequence}'s (\citeyear{abbott1995sequence}) proposal to adapt biological sequence techniques for sociological use. Optimal matching (OM) computes distances between state sequences using substitution and indel operations, analogous to edit distances in string matching. The resulting distance matrix is then used for cluster analysis (typically Ward's hierarchical clustering or partitioning around medoids) to identify trajectory "types".

OM has been extended in several directions: dynamic Hamming distances weight substitution costs by temporal proximity \citep{lesnard2010setting}, multichannel OM enables joint analysis of employment and family states \citep{gauthier2010multichannel}, and TraMineR provides a comprehensive software implementation \citep{studer2016what}. The resulting typologies span employment careers \citep{halpin1998class}, family formation \citep{elzinga2007destandardization}, and housing trajectories \citep{coulter2016rethinking}.

Sequence analysis has specific limitations for the questions we pose. The dependence on substitution cost matrices introduces subjectivity: different cost specifications can yield different typologies from the same data \citep{robette2008comparing}. The resulting distance measures are fundamentally pairwise --- they compare sequences but do not characterise the global geometry of the trajectory space. There is no natural notion of "closeness in trajectory space" that preserves topological invariants such as connected components, loops, or higher-dimensional voids. Significance testing is typically absent: cluster solutions are evaluated by silhouette scores or Calinski-Harabasz indices, but there is no formal test of whether the observed clustering exceeds what specific null models would produce.

\subsection{Topological Data Analysis Foundations}

Persistent homology (PH) provides a rigorous mathematical framework for detecting and quantifying topological features in data. Given a point cloud $X \subset \mathbb{R}^d$, PH constructs a filtration --- a nested sequence of simplicial complexes $K_0 \subseteq K_1 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq K_m$ --- by connecting points within distance $\epsilon$ and tracking which topological features appear (are "born") and disappear ("die") as $\epsilon$ increases \citep{edelsbrunner2002topological, zomorodian2005computing}.

The Vietoris--Rips (VR) complex, the most common choice for point cloud data, includes a $k$-simplex whenever all $\binom{k+1}{2}$ pairwise distances are below $\epsilon$. For $n$ points, the VR complex can have up to $2^n$ simplices, making direct computation infeasible for large datasets. Maxmin landmark selection (\citet{desilva2004topological}) addresses this by greedily selecting a subset of $L \ll n$ points that are maximally dispersed, constructing the VR complex on landmarks only. Ripser \citep{bauer2021ripser} provides state-of-the-art computational efficiency, exploiting the apparent pairs optimisation to compute VR persistence through $H_1$ (and optionally $H_2$) on thousands of landmarks in minutes.

The output of PH is a persistence diagram (PD): a multiset of birth-death pairs $(b_i, d_i)$ where feature $i$ is born at filtration value $b_i$ and dies at $d_i$. Features with large persistence $\ell_i = d_i - b_i$ are interpreted as genuine topological structure, while short-lived features near the diagonal are attributed to noise. In dimension 0 ($H_0$), features correspond to connected components: each merger of two components kills one $H_0$ feature. In dimension 1 ($H_1$), features correspond to loops or cycles: a loop forms at birth and is filled in at death. Summary statistics include total persistence $\sum_i \ell_i$, maximum persistence $\max_i \ell_i$, and persistence entropy $-\sum_i \hat{\ell}_i \log \hat{\ell}_i$ where $\hat{\ell}_i = \ell_i / \sum_j \ell_j$ \citep{atienza2019persistent}.

A fundamental property of PH is stability: small perturbations in the input point cloud produce small changes in the persistence diagram, measured by the bottleneck or Wasserstein distance \citep{cohen2007stability}. Topological summaries are therefore robust to noise, sampling variation, and minor measurement errors --- a property we rely on given the imputation and harmonisation challenges in survey data.

For comparing persistence diagrams across groups, the Wasserstein distance $W_p(D_1, D_2)$ measures the cost of the optimal transport between two diagrams, providing a principled metric for testing whether subpopulations have different topological structure \citep{turner2014frechet}. As we demonstrate in this paper, the choice between scalar summaries (total persistence) and diagram-level comparisons (Wasserstein distance) can change substantive conclusions --- a point that has methodological implications beyond our specific application.

\subsection{Mobility Regimes and Their Topological Signatures}

The economics of poverty traps posits that disadvantaged states can be self-reinforcing through multiple equilibria \citep{azariadis2005poverty}. When returns to human capital investment are nonlinear --- or when credit constraints, neighbourhood effects, or discrimination create threshold dynamics --- individuals may become trapped in low-productivity equilibria from which escape requires large, discrete shocks rather than marginal improvements \citep{barrett2013economics}.

Life-course researchers have identified analogous patterns in longitudinal data. \citet{vandecasteele2010poverty} distinguished between stable, mobile, and churning poverty trajectories using Belgian panel data. \citet{jenkins2011changing} documented extensive income churning in the UK, where many individuals cycle between poverty and near-poverty without achieving durable exits. \citet{savage2013new} used the Great British Class Survey to identify seven social classes, some characterised by cultural and social capital deficits that reinforce economic disadvantage.

The connection to topology runs through homological dimension. If mobility regimes exist as distinct clusters in trajectory space, they will appear as persistent $H_0$ components (connected components). The persistence values of $H_0$ features quantify the separation between regime clusters: large persistence indicates well-separated regimes that merge only at large filtration radii.

$H_1$ features (loops) have a distinct geometric meaning. Persistent $H_1$ loops correspond to cyclical oscillation patterns in the embedding space --- subsets of trajectories that trace closed paths through state space, returning to similar configurations after a sequence of transitions. This is geometrically distinct from the absorbing-state dynamics that characterise poverty traps in the economic literature \citep{azariadis2005poverty}. Poverty traps as absorbing states would manifest as dense $H_0$ clusters --- high-density regions in the point cloud from which few trajectories escape --- rather than as $H_1$ loops. The distinction matters: $H_1$ tests whether the trajectory manifold contains persistent cyclical structure, not whether disadvantaged states are absorbing.

In our data, $H_1$ is not rejected under any null model, including the first-order Markov null, regardless of which test statistic is used (\S4.3). This bounds the complexity of cyclical dynamics: the trajectory manifold lacks persistent cyclical structure beyond what first-order Markov dynamics produce --- ruling out non-Markovian topological trapping cycles while directing attention toward $H_0$ cluster absorptivity and escape rates (\S4.4.2) as the appropriate lens for poverty-trap dynamics.

The mapping between topological features and substantive social phenomena must respect the geometric meaning of each homological dimension; a topological analysis complements both sequence typologies and Markov transition models, but only when this mapping is drawn carefully.

\subsection{Statistical Null Models for TDA}

The statistical significance of topological features requires comparison to appropriate null distributions. \citet{robinson2017hypothesis} proposed permutation tests for PH, comparing observed total persistence or Betti numbers to distributions generated by permuting group labels. \citet{stolz2017persistent} applied PH to time-dependent functional networks, using surrogate data methods as nulls. These contributions establish the general framework; our contribution is to adapt and extend it with a structured hierarchy of null models tailored to sequential socioeconomic data.

For life-course trajectories, the relevant null models must be carefully chosen to test specific hypotheses. A label shuffle null (permuting state labels globally) tests whether the specific identity of states matters; an order shuffle (permuting each trajectory's state sequence) tests whether temporal ordering matters, preserving marginal state frequencies; and a Markov-$k$ null (simulating from an estimated $k$-th order Markov chain) tests whether topology exceeds what $k$-step memory produces.

Markov chains as generative models for social dynamics have a long history in sociology \citep{singer1976representation}. Our contribution is to arrange these null models into a systematic "memory ladder" --- a sequence of increasingly structured null hypotheses that progressively preserves more of the observed data's structure. When the observed topology first becomes consistent with a Markov-$k$ null, we can conclude that the topological complexity of the trajectory space is well captured by $k$-step memory. Critically, the answer depends on how "consistent" is measured. As we show, total persistence (a scalar) and Wasserstein distance (a diagram-level metric) give different answers for the Markov null --- the scalar test lacks sensitivity to structural differences in the configuration of persistence features that the Wasserstein test detects.


\section{Data and Methodology}

\subsection{Data: BHPS and Understanding Society}

Our data combine two longitudinal household surveys: the British Household Panel Study (BHPS, waves 1--18, 1991--2008) and Understanding Society (USoc, waves 1--14, 2009--2023). BHPS began as a nationally representative sample of approximately 5,500 households in Great Britain, with annual interviews tracking employment, income, and demographic characteristics. Understanding Society succeeded BHPS with a larger initial sample of approximately 40,000 households and incorporated the surviving BHPS sample as a sub-sample from wave 2 (2010--2011), enabling longitudinal tracking across both surveys.

Our primary analysis uses the USoc-era panel. From the combined dataset of over 118,000 respondents, we select individuals with at least 10 consecutive years of valid employment-income observations within the USoc observation window, yielding 27,280 trajectories. The mean trajectory length is 12.9 years (median 13, range 10--14), with start years spanning 2009--2013 and end years 2018--2022.

As cross-era validation, we separately construct BHPS-era trajectories (1991--2008) by linking BHPS person identifiers (\texttt{\{wave\}pid}) to USoc identifiers (\texttt{pidp}) via the cross-wave identifier file (\texttt{xwavedat.tab}). BHPS raw monthly net household income (\texttt{fihhmn}) is equivalised by dividing by the modified OECD equivalence scale (\texttt{eq\_moecd}) from the household response file, matching the equivalisation approach used for USoc's \texttt{fihhmnnet1\_dv}. This yields 8,509 BHPS-era trajectories (mean length 14.5 years). We also identify 8,459 spanning trajectories observed in both surveys. Cross-era results are reported in \S4.7.

Each person-year is assigned to one of nine states formed by crossing employment status with threshold-based income category. Employment status is derived from the \texttt{jbstat} variable: employed (E) includes full-time and part-time employment and self-employment; unemployed (U) includes ILO-definition unemployment; inactive (I) includes retirement, full-time education, long-term sickness, and caring responsibilities. Income bands are defined annually relative to the contemporary equivalised household income median: low (L, below 60\% of median), mid (M, 60\%--120\% of median), and high (H, above 120\% of median). The L/M/H classification uses fixed boundaries, not population-quantile cut-points; the resulting category sizes are unequal and vary across years. Sensitivity to alternative threshold choices (e.g., 50\%/150\% or population terciles) is not examined here.

Gaps of one or two years in otherwise continuous trajectories are forward-filled from the last observed state, affecting 4.9\% of person-years. We verify that imputation rates are balanced across regimes and do not drive topological results (Supplementary Material~SA).

For stratification analysis, we use three covariates: sex (Female \textit{n} = 14,402; Male \textit{n} = 11,243; 1,635 with missing sex excluded from gender stratification); parental NS-SEC (Professional/Managerial \textit{n} = 2,067; Intermediate \textit{n} = 6,777; Routine/Manual \textit{n} = 7,779; 10,657 missing); and birth cohort decade (1930s \textit{n} = 1,642 through 1990s \textit{n} = 1,364, with seven cohort groups).

Parental NS-SEC is missing for 39.1\% of the sample (10,657 of 27,280). This missingness is non-random: a chi-squared test of regime membership by NS-SEC availability yields $\chi^2$ = 147.74 (\textit{df} = 6, \textit{p} < 10$^{-29}$), and logistic regression confirms that regime membership, birth cohort, and trajectory characteristics all predict NS-SEC availability. All NS-SEC-stratified results (\S4.6) should be interpreted with this caveat.

\subsection{Trajectory Embedding}

To embed trajectories in a continuous metric space suitable for TDA, we compute n-gram frequency vectors. For each trajectory of length $T$, we calculate:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Unigrams} (9 dimensions): Relative frequency of each state $s \in \{EL, EM, EH,$ $UL, UM, UH, IL, IM, IH\}$ in the trajectory.
  \item \textbf{Bigrams} (81 dimensions): Relative frequency of each consecutive state pair $(s_t, s_{t+1})$, capturing transition structure.
\end{itemize}

The concatenated 90-dimensional term-frequency (TF) vector encodes both the composition and the sequential structure of each trajectory. We standardise using a fitted \texttt{StandardScaler} and project to 20 dimensions via Principal Component Analysis (PCA), retaining the top 20 components. The resulting point cloud of 27,280 points in $\mathbb{R}^{20}$ preserves both global cluster structure and local sequence variation.

As a sensitivity check, we compute UMAP embeddings with 16 dimensions and compare GMM cluster assignments to those from PCA-20D. The adjusted Rand index (ARI) between the two clusterings is 0.33 at matched $k = 7$, indicating that while three broad macro-types (secure employment, inactive/marginal, churning/mixed) are stable across embedding methods, finer regime boundaries are embedding-dependent. UMAP and PCA capture different aspects of local versus global geometry \citep{mcinnes2018umap}, which explains the divergence at fine scales. We report PCA-20D as the primary embedding and UMAP-16D as a supplementary sensitivity analysis (Figure S1).

To verify that substantive conclusions are robust to embedding choice, we re-run the order-shuffle null on the UMAP-16D embedding ($n = 100$). With GMM fixed at $k = 7$, UMAP yields ARI = 0.33 against PCA regime assignments. On the UMAP-16D point cloud with 5,000 maxmin landmarks, the order-shuffle null rejects $H_0$ at $p < 0.001$ (observed total persistence 3,203.3, null mean $2{,}372.9 \pm 323.3$) while $H_1$ is non-significant ($p = 1.00$), reproducing the PCA pattern. Full Markov-1 replication on UMAP is omitted due to computational cost; given the close agreement in regime structure and order-shuffle results, we treat PCA as the primary embedding and UMAP as a robustness check.

\subsection{Persistent Homology}

We compute Vietoris--Rips persistent homology on the PCA-20D point cloud using the following pipeline:

1. \textbf{Maxmin landmark selection}: From the 27,280 embedded trajectories, we select $L$ landmarks using the greedy furthest-point (maxmin) algorithm (\citet{desilva2004topological}). This selects an initial seed point and iteratively adds the point maximally distant from the current landmark set, producing a well-dispersed subsample that preserves the geometric structure of the full cloud. For total-persistence tests, $L = 5{,}000$ (approximately 18\% of the point cloud); for Wasserstein tests, $L = 2{,}000$ (approximately 7\%). We verify robustness to this choice by recomputing PH at $L = 2{,}500$ and $L = 8{,}000$ (Appendix B).

2. \textbf{VR complex construction and PH computation}: We construct the Vietoris--Rips filtration on the landmarks and compute persistence through $H_1$ using Ripser \citep{bauer2021ripser}, with the filtration threshold set to the 75th percentile of pairwise distances.

3. \textbf{Persistence diagrams and summaries}: The output comprises $H_0$ (connected components) and $H_1$ (loops) persistence diagrams. For each dimension, we compute total persistence $\sum_i (d_i - b_i)$, maximum persistence, feature count, and persistence entropy.

$H_0$ features correspond to the merging of trajectory clusters as the filtration radius increases: long-lived $H_0$ features indicate well-separated regimes. $H_1$ features correspond to loops in the trajectory space: persistent loops may indicate cyclical trapping where trajectories circulate among a subset of states without converging to a single attractor.

\subsection{Null Model Battery: The Markov Memory Ladder}

We introduce a battery of five null models arranged in order of increasing structural fidelity to the observed data. For each null model, we generate surrogate trajectory datasets and process them through the same pipeline: re-embed using the n-gram + PCA pipeline with PCA loadings fixed from the observed data (i.e., surrogate n-gram vectors are projected onto the observed PCA axes, not recomputed). Maxmin landmarks are re-selected on each surrogate to avoid coupling landmark geometry to the observed data. We then recompute PH and compare to the observed data.

\textbf{Null 1 --- Label shuffle} (\textit{n} = 200): Permute state labels globally across all person-years, destroying all individual-level structure.

\textbf{Null 2 --- Cohort shuffle} (\textit{n} = 200): Permute trajectories within birth-cohort bins, testing whether cross-cohort differences drive the topological signal.

\textbf{Null 3 --- Order shuffle} (\textit{n} = 100): For each individual, randomly permute the temporal order of their state sequence, preserving per-person state frequencies but destroying all transition and temporal structure.

\textbf{Null 4 --- Markov order-1} (\textit{n} = 100): Estimate a first-order Markov transition matrix from the observed data via maximum likelihood and simulate surrogate trajectories of the same lengths, conditional on each trajectory's observed starting state.

\textbf{Null 5 --- Markov order-2} (\textit{n} = 200): Estimate a second-order Markov transition matrix (conditioning on the previous two states) via maximum likelihood, with Laplace smoothing (adding pseudocounts of 1 to all 729 cells). Surrogates are simulated conditional on the observed first two states of each trajectory.

\subsubsection{Test statistics}

We implement two test statistics to compare observed and null persistence:

\textbf{Total persistence} (scalar): $\sum_i (d_i - b_i)$ for each homological dimension, separately for $H_0$ and $H_1$. The \textit{p}-value is the proportion of null realisations with total persistence as extreme as or more extreme than the observed value. This is the standard approach in TDA hypothesis testing \citep{robinson2017hypothesis}.

\textbf{Wasserstein diagram distance} (diagram-level): For each null realisation \textit{j}, we compute $W_1(D_{\text{obs}}, D_{\text{null}_j})$ --- the 1-Wasserstein distance between the observed persistence diagram and the null diagram, using the \texttt{gudhi.wasserstein} implementation backed by the POT (Python Optimal Transport) library. We summarise these as the \textit{mean} observed-null distance $\bar{W} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_j W_1(D_{\text{obs}}, D_{\text{null}_j})$; using the mean rather than individual distances averages over sampling noise in the null realisations and produces a stable scalar test statistic. We also compute a null-null baseline: $W_1(D_{\text{null}_i}, D_{\text{null}_j})$ for 500 sampled pairs of null diagrams, which characterises the typical spread within the null distribution and accounts for its variance. The \textit{p}-value is the proportion of null-null distances that equal or exceed $\bar{W}$. This approach tests whether the observed diagram is more distant from the null distribution than null diagrams are from each other --- i.e., whether the observed diagram is an outlier relative to the null cloud in Wasserstein space.

The Wasserstein test is more sensitive than total persistence because it compares the full configuration of birth-death pairs rather than collapsing them to a scalar. Two diagrams can have identical total persistence but very different geometric structure: for instance, many short-lived features versus few long-lived ones. The Wasserstein distance detects such shape differences; total persistence does not.

With \textit{n} = 100 for the order-shuffle and Markov-1 nulls, we have resolution to approximately \textit{p} = 0.01 for one-sided tests. For BHPS-era validation (\S4.7), null tests use \textit{n} = 500 permutations.

\subsection{Regime Discovery}

We identify mobility regimes by fitting Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) to the PCA-20D embedding, using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) for model selection across $k = 3, 4, \ldots, 15$ components. The BIC-optimal model has $k = 7$ components.

$H_0$ informs but does not replicate GMM: all $H_0$ features are born at filtration radius 0 (each landmark starts as a singleton); the 44 features with persistence > 10 merge gradually (7th/8th most persistent ratio = 1.03$\times$), reflecting single-linkage chaining in 20-dimensional Vietoris--Rips complexes on 5,000 landmarks. The merge tree yields one dominant connected component (~99.98\% of trajectories) plus 6 singleton outliers --- no balanced clusters matching GMM density modes. We use GMM for regime labels and $H_0$ for connectivity validation (\S4.4.1, Appendix A).

Regime profiles are constructed by mapping GMM-assigned labels back to the original state sequences and computing, for each regime: employment rate, unemployment rate, inactivity rate, income distribution (fraction in low/mid/high), and a stability index (fraction of trajectory years spent in the dominant state).

To verify that the $k = 7$ solution is not an artefact of a particular sample, we perform a bootstrap stability analysis: 200 resamples of the 27,280 trajectory embeddings with replacement, each fitted with GMM $k = 7$, yielding ARI distribution with mean 0.646 $\pm$ 0.086 (95\% CI: [0.461, 0.795]).

\subsection{Overlapping Windows and Regime Persistence}

To assess regime persistence over the life course, we construct overlapping career-phase windows using a sliding window of 10 years with a 5-year step. For each individual, this produces a sequence of overlapping windows, each embedded and assigned to a regime using the same PCA-GMM model fitted on full trajectories. This yields 54,560 windows from 27,280 individuals (mean 2.0 windows per person).

We construct a 7$\times$7 regime transition matrix from consecutive windows and compute escape probabilities: the fraction of individuals starting in disadvantaged regimes (Low-Income Churn or Inactive Low) who reach an advantaged regime (Secure EH or Employed Mid) within a given number of windows.

We also compute PH on the phase-level embedding cloud and test a phase-order shuffle null that preserves each individual's multiset of windows but randomises their temporal order, testing whether the life-course ordering of career phases adds topological structure beyond the composition of phases. We run \textit{n} = 500 permutations with 3,000 maxmin landmarks and report p-values with 95\% confidence intervals.

\subsection{Sequence Analysis Baseline}

To contextualise our TDA results, we compute a standard sequence analysis baseline using optimal matching (OM) with dynamic Hamming distances \citep{lesnard2010setting}. We compute pairwise distances between all 27,280 trajectories, apply Ward's hierarchical clustering, and select \textit{k} via the average silhouette width \citep{studer2016what}.

The OM-optimal solution selects \textit{k} = 2 clusters (silhouette = 0.223), producing a coarse division between predominantly inactive trajectories (40.2\%) and predominantly employed trajectories (59.8\%). At \textit{k} = 7, OM achieves silhouette = 0.215. The ARI between OM \textit{k} = 7 clusters and GMM \textit{k} = 7 regimes is 0.26, indicating overlapping but non-identical regime boundaries. Regime-level profile correlations range from 0.97 (high-employment, which both methods identify cleanly) down to 0.02 (mixed-churn, where OM and TDA draw boundaries differently).

The moderate ARI confirms that the broad structure of UK trajectory space --- secure employment versus inactivity versus churning --- is recoverable by either method, while finer distinctions depend on the analytic approach. The primary added value of TDA lies not in the regime typology itself but in the formal null testing infrastructure.

Label shuffle serves as a negative control: the observed diagram is indistinguishable from shuffled diagrams ($H_0$ \textit{p} = 0.452, $H_1$ \textit{p} = 0.538), confirming that the specific identity of states does not drive diagram geometry when all temporal structure is preserved. The Wasserstein results change the Markov conclusion. The observed $H_0$ persistence diagram is significantly different from Markov-1 null diagrams (\textit{p} = 0.002): the mean Wasserstein distance between observed and null diagrams (11.42 $\pm$ 2.15) is nearly double the mean distance between pairs of null diagrams (5.99 $\pm$ 1.79). The observed diagram is an outlier relative to the Markov null cloud in diagram space, even though it has less total persistence. The Markov process generates \textit{more} features overall but in a different geometric configuration --- different birth-death pairs, different persistence structure --- than the real data.

Order-shuffle $H_0$ is borderline under Wasserstein (\textit{p} = 0.058), weaker than the clear rejection under total persistence (\textit{p} < 0.005). This implies that temporal ordering creates aggregate persistence differences (total persistence is higher in ordered data) but the diagram-level structure is only marginally different from the order-shuffled baseline. The effect of temporal ordering is real but operates more through the sum of feature lifetimes than through specific birth-death configurations.

$H_1$ is non-significant under both null types, consistent with the total-persistence results. Markov-1 $H_1$ is borderline (\textit{p} = 0.086) --- closer to significance under Wasserstein than under total persistence (\textit{p} = 1.000) --- suggesting that diagram-level $H_1$ differences may emerge with larger permutation counts, though we do not pursue this here.

\textbf{Markov-2 resolves the Wasserstein discrepancy.} Upgrading from first- to second-order Markov dynamics eliminates the diagram-level rejection: Markov-2 $H_0$ is not rejected under Wasserstein (\textit{p} = 0.546), with the mean obs-null distance (6.62 $\pm$ 1.72) falling well within the null-null distribution (7.19 $\pm$ 2.09). Markov-2 $H_1$ is also non-significant (\textit{p} = 0.078), though closer to the boundary than Markov-1 $H_1$. This result localises the non-Markovian structure detected by the Wasserstein test: the diagram-level differences between observed and Markov-1 surrogates reflect second-order memory --- dependence on the state two steps back --- rather than higher-order or non-Markovian effects that no finite-memory model could capture. The Markov memory ladder thus yields a sharp conclusion: total persistence is insensitive to first-order violations (Markov-1 \textit{p} = 1.000), Wasserstein detects them (\textit{p} = 0.002), and second-order Markov dynamics suffice to reproduce the observed diagram geometry (\textit{p} = 0.546).

\subsubsection{The discrepancy between test statistics}

The discrepancy between total persistence and Wasserstein distance for the Markov-1 $H_0$ test is the structurally interesting finding of this analysis. Total persistence says: the observed data has \textit{less} aggregate persistence than the Markov null, so the null is not rejected. Wasserstein says: the observed data has a \textit{different shape} of persistence diagram than the Markov null, and this difference is larger than the variation among null diagrams, so the null \textit{is} rejected. Both are correct statements about different aspects of the comparison. The Markov process generates a more diffuse trajectory cloud (more total persistence, more features) but the specific pattern of which components form and when they merge differs from the observed data.

We interpret this as follows: the connected-component structure of the trajectory manifold --- which clusters form, at what filtration scale, and in what hierarchical relationship --- carries information about the data-generating process that a first-order Markov chain cannot reproduce. The Markov chain gets the total amount of persistence roughly right (in fact, overshoots it) but distributes it differently across birth-death pairs. The trajectory data has regime-specific substructure that the globally estimated Markov transition matrix smears out.

This finding has methodological implications beyond our specific application. Scalar persistence summaries are standard in TDA hypothesis testing, but they can produce false negatives when the null generates as much or more aggregate persistence while differing in diagram shape. Wasserstein diagram comparisons should be considered alongside total-persistence tests in any TDA null-testing framework.

\textbf{Intra-regime compactness confirms temporal order contributes to regime differentiation.} We compute mean within-regime pairwise distances in the PCA-20D embedding under 500 order-shuffled re-embeddings, with PCA loadings frozen from the observed data. All seven regimes show significantly tighter compactness in observed versus order-shuffled embeddings (z-scores ranging from -30.5 to -67.9, all \textit{p} < 0.001). Regime 0 (Mixed Churn, observed mean distance 14.68, null 19.12, \textit{z} = -55.0) and Regime 6 (Low-Income Churn, observed 9.52, null 14.58, \textit{z} = -67.9) show the largest compactness ratios, temporal ordering is most informative for the churning regimes, where transition sequences rather than state composition define the trajectory type.

\textbf{Bootstrap null stability.} To verify that null-model conclusions are not artefacts of a particular GMM partition, we conduct a bootstrap stability analysis: 10 bootstrap resamples of the trajectory embeddings, each re-fitted with GMM ($k = 7$), with order-shuffle and Markov-1 null tests (50 permutations each, 500 landmarks). Order-shuffle $H_0$ is significant in all 10/10 draws (\textit{p}$_{\text{mean}}$ = 0.016, range [0.00, 0.04]). Markov-1 $H_0$ is significant in all 10/10 draws (\textit{p}$_{\text{mean}}$ = 0.000). No conclusion flips are observed across any bootstrap draw.

\textbf{Regime 3 --- Employment-Inactive Mix} (\textit{n} = 3,333, 12.2\%): Mixed employment (58.8\%) and inactivity (41.2\%), zero unemployment. Income relatively balanced (low 20.0\%, mid 39.6\%, high 40.4\%). Stability 0.280. Likely captures career breaks, part-time work, and caring responsibilities.

\textbf{Regime 6 --- Low-Income Churn} (\textit{n} = 2,064, 7.6\%): Employment (59.1\%), unemployment (9.8\%), and inactivity (31.1\%), with the highest low-income rate (61.1\%) of any regime. \textbf{Regime 5 --- High-Income Inactive} (\textit{n} = 1,813, 6.6\%): Mixed employment (41.3\%) and inactivity (58.3\%), with high income (67.2\%). Stability 0.400. Likely early retirees with substantial assets or high-income households with one inactive partner.

\subsection{Cycle and Trap Analysis}

The 5,962 $H_1$ features indicate substantial looping in the trajectory space, but the cycling is not topologically distinctive under either test statistic. Under total persistence, order-shuffle $H_1$ is significant at $L = 5{,}000$ (\textit{p} = 0.020) but landmark-sensitive (non-significant at $L = 2{,}500$ and $L = 8{,}000$). Under Wasserstein distances, order-shuffle $H_1$ is clearly non-significant (\textit{p} = 0.854), and Markov-1 $H_1$ is non-significant (\textit{p} = 0.086). The $H_1$ null result is therefore robust across test statistics: the trajectory manifold lacks persistent cyclical structure beyond what first-order Markov dynamics produce.

$H_1$ loops correspond to cyclical oscillation patterns in the embedding space, not to poverty traps in the absorbing-state sense (\S2.3). The most persistent loops (maximum persistence 3.21) involve cycling between low-income employment, unemployment, and inactivity --- states concentrated in the Low-Income Churn and Mixed Churn regimes. These descriptive features may prove useful for identifying target populations even though they do not exceed Markov baselines.

\subsection{Stratified Comparisons}

Pairwise Wasserstein distances between group-specific persistence diagrams reveal significant topological differences by social origin, gender, and birth cohort. \section{Discussion}

\subsection{What TDA Adds Beyond Sequence Analysis}

PH provides a geometric characterisation of the trajectory point cloud unavailable from pairwise dissimilarity methods. The persistence diagrams encode the multi-scale connectivity ($H_0$) and cyclical structure ($H_1$) of the entire trajectory space: the most persistent $H_0$ component (15.81) is nearly four times the mean (4.08), indicating a few well-separated regimes with many weakly separated sub-clusters. The near-zero ARI between $H_0$ components and GMM assignments (\S4.4.1) demonstrates that $H_0$ PH and GMM operate on different geometric properties --- global connectivity versus local density --- and are complementary analyses.

The Markov memory ladder provides a framework for bounding the memory-complexity of observed topology. That the Markov conclusion depends on the test statistic (\S4.3) is the methodologically important finding: scalar persistence summaries can mask structural differences that Wasserstein comparisons detect.

OM-based clustering at matched \textit{k} = 7 yields ARI = 0.26: the Secure High-Employment regime is identified nearly identically (profile correlation = 0.97), while Mixed Churn diverges most (correlation = 0.02). The broad regime typology is recoverable by either method; TDA adds the geometric characterisation and formal testing.

\subsection{The Markov Memory Ladder and the Test-Statistic Discrepancy}

The Markov memory ladder is our primary methodological contribution. The central finding is that the Markov conclusion depends on how persistence diagrams are compared. Under total persistence, Markov-1 surrogates generate \textit{more} aggregate persistence than observed (\textit{p} = 1.000), suggesting the topology is "bounded" by first-order dynamics. Under Wasserstein distances, the observed $H_0$ diagram is significantly different from Markov-1 null diagrams (\textit{p} = 0.002), indicating that the specific configuration of connected components --- which clusters form, at what scale, and in what hierarchical relationship --- carries structural information that a first-order Markov chain cannot reproduce.

We interpret this discrepancy as follows. The globally estimated Markov transition matrix generates trajectories that, in aggregate, are more diffuse than the observed data --- they spread more widely in embedding space and produce more topological features. But the pattern of those features differs from what the real trajectories produce. The real data has regime-specific substructure: clusters that form at particular filtration scales and merge in particular ways. The Markov process, which uses a single global transition matrix without regime-specific parameters, smears out this substructure. The total persistence is higher (more features, longer total lifetime) but the configuration of the diagram --- the distribution of birth-death pairs --- is different.

This interpretation is consistent with the intra-regime compactness results (\S4.3): all seven regimes are significantly more compact in the observed embedding than under order-shuffled re-embeddings, with the churning regimes showing the largest compactness ratios. The trajectory data has structural regularity within regimes that a global Markov process does not capture.

These results come with caveats that bear on interpretation. The Wasserstein test uses $L = 2{,}000$ landmarks rather than the $L = 5{,}000$ used for total-persistence tests, because computing persistence diagrams for each null realisation is expensive; the landmark count difference could affect the comparison, though total-persistence results are stable across $L$ = 2,500 to 8,000 (Appendix~B). The Wasserstein battery covers label shuffle, order shuffle, Markov-1, and Markov-2 nulls but omits cohort shuffle due to metadata constraints --- its total-persistence result (\textit{p} = 0.405) already establishes it as a negative control alongside label shuffle. More fundamentally, the Markov-1 Wasserstein rejection does not specify \textit{what kind} of non-Markovian structure is present --- it could reflect individual-level heterogeneity, duration dependence, or higher-order memory. Richer null models (hidden Markov, latent-type, variable-order) would help decompose this.

The Markov memory ladder is transportable: it can be applied to any TDA analysis of sequential data. The lesson from our analysis is that the ladder should be climbed with both scalar and diagram-level test statistics, since they can give different answers.

\subsection{$H_1$ Cycles: A Qualified Negative Result}

$H_1$ loops correspond to cyclical oscillation patterns in the embedding space rather than to poverty traps in the absorbing-state sense. In the USoc data, $H_1$ is non-significant under Wasserstein distances for both order-shuffle (\textit{p} = 0.854) and Markov-1 (\textit{p} = 0.086). The landmark-sensitive order-shuffle $H_1$ significance under total persistence (\textit{p} = 0.020 at $L = 5{,}000$, non-significant at other $L$) does not replicate under Wasserstein testing, reinforcing a cautious interpretation.

However, the BHPS-era data produces a stronger signal: Markov-1 $H_1$ is decisively rejected under Wasserstein (\textit{p} = 0.000), with the mean obs-null distance (149.11) far above the null-null baseline (134.24). The BHPS trajectories --- longer on average (14.5 vs 12.9 years) and drawn from a different labour-market era --- show cyclical structure that exceeds Markov expectations at the diagram level. Whether this reflects genuine non-Markovian cycling patterns in the 1990s--2000s economy or is an artefact of the longer window length is a question that can be directly addressed: truncating BHPS trajectories to 12--13 years before running the Wasserstein $H_1$ test would discriminate between an era effect and a window-length artefact. The USoc $H_1$ borderline (\textit{p} = 0.086) is suggestive of the same effect, weakened by shorter trajectories.

This does not mean that people do not cycle between employment and poverty in the USoc era. They manifestly do --- the Low-Income Churn and Mixed Churn regimes demonstrate extensive state cycling. Rather, the USoc null result means that the topological shape of this cycling is predictable from one-step transition probabilities in that sample. The BHPS result suggests that with longer observation windows, the diagram-level structure of cycling may diverge from Markov baselines.

This qualified negative result directs analytical attention toward the stronger $H_0$ results and the escape-rate analysis (\S4.4.2), while flagging $H_1$ as worthy of further investigation with longer trajectories.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Spatial dimension.} The analysis is national and non-spatial. The Markov memory ladder is directly applicable to regional comparisons, which we identify as a priority extension.

\textbf{State space discretisation.} The nine-state space, while richer than most sequence analysis applications (typically 3--5 states), is a simplification. The 60\%/120\% thresholds are conventional but arbitrary; sensitivity to alternatives is not examined.

\textbf{Embedding sensitivity.} PCA-20D is the primary embedding. The UMAP-16D check yields ARI = 0.33 against PCA at \textit{k} = 7. We test a grid of six configurations varying n-gram order, weighting, and PCA dimensionality:

\textbf{$H_0$ chaining.} Single-linkage chaining limits balanced $H_0$-based clustering in high-dimensional VR complexes: the merge tree produces one dominant component (99.98\%) plus outlier singletons, yielding ARI $\approx$ 0 against GMM. This is a known property of VR $H_0$ in high dimensions \citep{bobrowski2018topology} and motivates future exploration of density-aware TDA methods (e.g., DTM filtrations, Mapper).

\textbf{Landmark sampling.} Maxmin landmark selection (5,000 from 27,280) preserves global geometry but may undersample rare trajectory types. Results are stable across $L$ = 2,500 to 8,000. The Wasserstein tests use $L = 2{,}000$ due to computational cost; validating at higher $L$ is future work.

\textbf{Age and lifecycle confounding.} The near-absorbing Inactive Low regime (97\% self-transition) partly reflects retirement. We address this via age-stratified analysis (\S4.4.2): 86.1\% of Inactive Low observations come from individuals aged $\geq$ 60. Working-age escape rates (17.8\%) are more than three times the raw rate (5.6\%).

\textbf{Markov null limitations.} Our Markov nulls test against globally estimated Markov dynamics. They do not account for individual-level heterogeneity or duration dependence. The Wasserstein rejection of Markov-1 (\textit{p} = 0.002) suggests that such heterogeneity may be precisely what the test is detecting --- the global Markov chain cannot reproduce the regime-specific substructure of the observed diagrams. Stratified Markov nulls (separate transition matrices per regime) could help disambiguate this. The per-regime stratified test under total persistence (\S5.2 in the previous draft) found non-rejection, but the effective stratification was limited (one regime contained 99.9\% of trajectories at the point-cloud level). Stratified Wasserstein testing is future work.

\textbf{Family dimension.} We analyse employment-income trajectories only; partnership and household composition are not included.

\textbf{Imputation.} Forward-fill imputation for gaps of up to two years affects 4.9\% of person-years.

\textbf{Cross-survey harmonisation.} BHPS and USoc use different sampling frames, questionnaire designs, and income measurement methodologies. Harmonisation artefacts may contribute to cross-era topological differences. The BHPS-era Wasserstein results (\S4.7) confirm the cross-era replication of the test-statistic discrepancy.

\textbf{Pipeline sensitivity.} We vary minimum trajectory length (8, 10, 12 years), weighting (TF vs TF-IDF), and filtration threshold (50th, 75th, 90th percentile). Under TF, results are highly stable: \textit{k} = 7 at 10/12 years, \textit{k} = 8 at 8 years; $H_0$ persistence varies < 6\%; threshold has no effect on $H_0$ and negligible effect on $H_1$. TF-IDF reshapes the embedding substantially (ARI = 0.466 against TF baseline, 93\% drop in total persistence), confirming that the choice of TF weighting is consequential.

\section{Conclusion}

The test statistic you choose determines whether UK career trajectories look Markovian.

Under total persistence --- the scalar summary standard in TDA hypothesis testing --- Markov-1 surrogates generate more aggregate persistence than the observed data (\textit{p} = 1.000), and the natural conclusion would be that first-order transition dynamics fully account for the topology of the trajectory manifold. Under Wasserstein diagram distances, the same null is rejected (\textit{p} = 0.002): the observed $H_0$ diagram is an outlier relative to the Markov null cloud. The Markov chain overshoots the total but distributes persistence differently across birth-death pairs --- it smears out regime-specific substructure that the real trajectories preserve. This discrepancy emerged from the dual-statistic design --- comparing both statistics on the same null battery --- and replicates cross-era on 8,509 BHPS trajectories (1991--2008) for both $H_0$ and $H_1$. Had we used only the standard scalar test, as much of the TDA hypothesis-testing literature does, we would have declared the topology Markov-bounded and moved on.

The substantive upshot is that the seven mobility regimes identified here --- from Secure High-Employment (27\%, stability 0.78) to Low-Income Churn (7.6\%, stability 0.29) --- are not merely density modes in the embedding space. Their hierarchical connectivity structure, encoded in the pattern of $H_0$ birth-death pairs, carries information about the data-generating process that cannot be recovered from a globally estimated transition matrix. The regime landscape has internal geometry. That geometry sits at the second-order Markov boundary: Markov-2 reproduces the observed diagram configuration (\textit{p} = 0.546), localising the non-Markovian signal to two-step memory rather than higher-order or indefinitely long dependence. For policy, this means that the regime structure is shaped by something richer than one-step transition probabilities --- recent trajectory history matters --- but it does not require modelling arbitrarily deep path dependence.

The $H_1$ result introduces a complication that future work will need to resolve. USoc-era loops are non-significant under all nulls and both statistics, suggesting that cyclical trapping is predictable from Markov dynamics in shorter observation windows. But the longer BHPS trajectories (mean 14.5 vs 12.9 years) decisively reject Markov-1 $H_1$ under Wasserstein (\textit{p} = 0.000). The trajectory manifold's cyclical structure may require longer windows to separate from Markov baselines, or the 1991--2008 labour market may have generated genuinely different cycling dynamics. Either way, the USoc $H_1$ negative result is weaker than it appears: it is bounded by trajectory length, not by the absence of cycling.

The 5.6\% escape rate from disadvantaged regimes --- 17.8\% for working-age individuals after removing the retirement confound --- quantifies what decades of panel-data research on poverty dynamics has described qualitatively: disadvantaged trajectories are sticky, the stickiness is regime-specific (Inactive Low is near-absorbing at 97\%; Low-Income Churn permits 20$\times$ higher escape odds), and the strongest predictor of escape is which disadvantaged regime you start in, not your class origin (NS-SEC is non-significant after conditioning on age, cohort, and initial regime). The topological lens adds the observation that phase ordering across the life course contributes no additional topological structure (\textit{p} = 0.98): the complexity of career dynamics lives inside each trajectory's sequential structure, not in the higher-order sequencing of career phases. The action, such as it is, happens locally.

Where does this leave TDA as a tool for life-course research? The regime typology itself is recoverable by optimal matching (ARI = 0.26 at matched \textit{k} = 7), and the broad distinction between secure, churning, and inactive trajectories is visible from either method. TDA's contribution is the null-testing infrastructure and the geometric characterisation --- and, as this paper demonstrates, the sensitivity of conclusions to how that geometry is measured. The Markov memory ladder is portable: it can be applied to any TDA analysis of sequential data, in any domain, with both scalar and diagram-level statistics. The lesson is to climb the ladder with both.

Immediate extensions include Mapper analysis of within-regime heterogeneity (Paper~2), zigzag persistence across annual cohort snapshots (Paper~3), and regional stratification at NUTS2 or Travel-to-Work Area level. Cross-national replication on SOEP and PSID will test whether the regime structure reflects UK-specific labour market institutions or a more general pattern in the topology of career dynamics across welfare states.
