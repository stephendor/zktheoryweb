# From Global Topology to Navigable Trajectory Space: Mapper Analysis of UK Employment Trajectories

**Authors:** Stephen Dorman
**Target Journal:** *Sociological Methods & Research*
**Date:** April 2026 (v4 — Reviewer Response Revision)

---

## Abstract

Mapper (Singh, Mémoli, & Carlsson 2007), applied to 27,280 employment-income trajectories from Understanding Society (2009–2023) in PCA-20D space, reproduces the seven Gaussian mixture model (GMM) regimes at node level (purity 0.999, NMI 0.434) while fragmenting each regime into sub-regions that vary on the PC1 income gradient. Low-Income Churn splits into 80 nodes spanning a 4-unit PC1 range (node-mean std = 1.23); 32 of these deviate by more than one standard deviation from the regime mean. Across all seven regimes, 358 of 1,060 nodes qualify as sub-regime outliers. A sensitivity analysis across 24 configurations — two clustering algorithms, four lens functions, three parameter values each — shows that the 49% noise rate and the absence of bridge nodes are DBSCAN properties, not data properties: agglomerative clustering covers 100% of trajectories and identifies 203–449 bridge nodes spanning regime boundaries. Within the PCA-20D embedding, within-regime heterogeneity persists across all 24 configurations; a UMAP-16D replication confirms qualitative robustness to embedding choice (§4.7). Colouring nodes by reconstructed trajectory outcomes reveals a within-R6 bifurcation: nodes near the Employed Mid boundary show employment rates of 0.7 and final income trending mid-range; nodes near Inactive Low show rates below 0.3 and persistently low income.

**Keywords:** Mapper algorithm, topological data analysis, trajectory analysis, within-regime heterogeneity, employment trajectories, KeplerMapper, sensitivity analysis, outcome geography

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Limitation of Global Summaries

In a companion paper (Dorman, 2026a), we applied Vietoris–Rips persistent homology to the same trajectory embedding analysed here. Temporal ordering creates topological structure (order-shuffle H₀ *p* < 0.005); Wasserstein diagram-level tests reject the first-order Markov null even when scalar total-persistence tests do not (*p* = 0.002 vs. *p* = 1.000); seven mobility regimes exhibit extreme stickiness (5.6% escape rate from disadvantaged regimes).

But global topology is, by construction, a summary. The persistence diagram records that trajectory clusters exist and how they merge as the filtration radius increases. It does not record what happens *inside* those clusters — whether a regime labelled "Low-Income Churn" is internally homogeneous or contains sub-populations with markedly different prospects. The seven-component GMM that defines the regime typology has the same limitation: it assigns each trajectory a single label, collapsing any within-regime variation into a regime-level mean.

The compression has practical consequences. If the Low-Income Churn regime contains both a sub-region where 25% of trajectories eventually escape to stable employment and another where virtually none do, the regime-level escape rate of 5.6% is a misleading average. Interventions targeting "Low-Income Churn" as a homogeneous population will misallocate resources, treating individuals near the boundary with stable employment identically to those deep in the near-absorbing core.

### 1.2 Mapper as Interior Explorer

The Mapper algorithm (Singh, Mémoli & Carlsson 2007) works at a different resolution. Where persistent homology computes a global topological invariant (the persistence diagram), Mapper constructs a *graph-based simplicial summary* that preserves local geometric structure. The algorithm projects the point cloud through a *lens function* to a lower-dimensional space, covers the lens image with overlapping intervals, clusters the pre-image of each interval, and records overlaps between clusters as edges. The result is a graph in which nodes are geometrically coherent groups of data points and edges connect groups that share members.

The graph can be *coloured* by any variable defined on the data points. Computing the mean of an outcome variable within each node transforms the abstract graph into a map on which outcomes are geographically readable. Regions where escape probability is high become visually and statistically distinguishable from regions where it is low, even when both carry the same GMM label. We call this an *outcome geography* of trajectory space: a spatial representation of where in the trajectory manifold outcomes diverge, not a claim about why they do so.

Outside the social sciences, Mapper has been used to identify cell-differentiation branches in single-cell RNA-seq (Rizvi et al. 2017) and racial-profiling subgroups invisible to standard clustering (Lum et al. 2013). Life-course sociology, where the question is not merely "how many types exist?" but "where within each type do outcomes diverge?", has not picked it up.

### 1.3 Contribution

Mapper, applied to the PCA-20D trajectory embedding from Dorman (2026a), reproduces the seven GMM regimes at node level while fragmenting the churning regimes into sub-regions with detectably different positions along the income gradient. A 24-configuration sensitivity analysis — varying the clustering algorithm, its parameters, and the lens function — establishes that within-regime heterogeneity is robust to all tested choices, but that noise rate (49% under DBSCAN, 0% under agglomerative) and bridge node count (zero vs. hundreds) are algorithm properties rather than data properties. Reconstructing trajectory-level outcomes from the raw state sequences and colouring Mapper nodes by employment rate, churning rate, and final income band produces an outcome geography that locates within-regime sub-populations with divergent labour market outcomes. These findings are robust to Mapper configuration within the PCA-20D embedding and replicate qualitatively under UMAP-16D (§4.7), though specific node counts are embedding-dependent. Prior applications of Mapper in social science have used cross-sectional data (Lum et al. 2013) or domain-specific similarity embeddings (Li et al. 2015); longitudinal life-course sequences, where the question is not which people are similar but which have similar *trajectories through time*, have not been analysed this way. Halpin (2010) documented that sequence-analysis clusters are internally heterogeneous (silhouette rarely above 0.3) but had no method to map where the heterogeneity concentrates; Mapper supplies that map. For sociology, this navigability matters: it converts an abstract statistical observation ("clusters are heterogeneous") into a concrete, spatially indexed answer about which sub-populations within a regime face divergent outcomes — enabling both more precise theoretical accounts of absorbing-state boundaries and better-targeted policy interventions.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 Mapper: Theory and Applications

The Mapper algorithm was introduced by Singh, Mémoli, and Carlsson (2007) as a method for producing simplified, graph-based summaries of high-dimensional point clouds. Mathematically, Mapper approximates the Reeb graph of a function defined on a topological space — a quotient space that collapses each connected component of each level set to a point. For finite point clouds, the approximation depends on a cover of the range of a lens function, with partial clustering replacing connected components.

The algorithm requires three choices: (1) a *lens function* (or *projection*) $f: X \to \mathbb{R}^k$ that maps the data to a low-dimensional space; (2) a *cover* of $f(X)$ consisting of overlapping intervals (parameterised by the number of intervals and the overlap fraction); and (3) a *clustering algorithm* applied to the pre-image of each cover element. Common lens functions include PCA projections, density estimators, and eccentricity measures. The overlap fraction controls the connectivity of the resulting graph: higher overlap produces more edges and a more connected graph; lower overlap produces a sparser graph with more isolated components.

KeplerMapper (van Veen & Saul 2019) provides the standard Python implementation, with built-in support for multiple projections, DBSCAN and agglomerative clustering, and interactive HTML visualisation.

### 2.2 Mapper and Persistent Homology: Complementary Views

Persistent homology and Mapper address different questions about the same point cloud. PH computes global topological invariants — the number and persistence of connected components, loops, and voids — that characterise the multi-scale shape of the entire space. These invariants are coordinate-free, mathematically stable, and amenable to formal hypothesis testing via null-model batteries (Robinson & Turner 2017; Dorman, 2026a).

Mapper, by contrast, produces a coordinate-dependent graph that preserves local geometric structure at the cost of global topological guarantees. The Mapper graph is not a topological invariant: it depends on the choice of lens function, cover parameters, and clustering algorithm. What it provides instead is *navigability* — the ability to inspect individual regions of the data space, colour them by outcome variables, and identify sub-populations that global summaries compress away.

PH answers: "Does the trajectory space have significant topological structure, and under which null models?" Mapper answers: "Given that structure exists, what does it look like locally, and how do outcomes vary across it?"

### 2.3 Within-Regime Heterogeneity in Life-Course Research

The recognition that typological labels can mask substantial within-type variation is not new to life-course research. Halpin (2010) noted that sequence-analysis clusters are often internally heterogeneous, with silhouette scores rarely exceeding 0.3. Studer (2013) proposed discrepancy analysis to test whether group differences in sequence data are statistically significant, acknowledging that cluster membership is an imperfect proxy for trajectory similarity.

The specific problem of within-regime heterogeneity in mobility outcomes has received less attention. Jenkins (2011) documented extensive income churning in the UK, noting that individuals with similar annual income trajectories can have very different long-run outcomes depending on the timing and sequencing of income shocks. Vandecasteele (2010) distinguished between "transient" and "recurrent" poverty trajectories that would typically be assigned to the same cluster. Mapper detects sub-regime structure without imposing additional parametric assumptions.

Recent methodological work has expanded tools for measuring complexity within sequence trajectories. Gabadinho et al. (2011) introduced complexity indices for life sequences including turbulence, distinguishing sequences that visit many states from those that alternate rapidly between states. Ritschard and Studer (2018) reviewed entropy-based measures of within-trajectory complexity, noting that high-entropy trajectories need not cluster together even when their average state distributions are similar. Liao et al. (2022) proposed sequence network analysis, constructing co-occurrence graphs among sequence patterns and identifying communities. These methods reduce within-trajectory complexity to scalars; Mapper complements them by preserving the spatial structure of the sequence-type distribution — identifying not just that some trajectories are more complex than others, but *where* in the trajectory manifold complexity concentrates and what other outcomes accompany it.

### 2.4 Comparison with Optimal Matching

Optimal matching (OM) and its variants (Lesnard 2010; Studer & Ritschard 2016) produce pairwise distance matrices that can be clustered into trajectory types. OM distances summarise total dissimilarity between two sequences without localising which aspects differ; cutting the dendrogram at a given height produces flat clusters with no internal structure. Mapper produces a graph with internal topology: branches, hubs, and peripheries that correspond to distinct geometric sub-regions of each cluster.

In our companion paper (Dorman, 2026a), the adjusted Rand index between OM (*k* = 7) and GMM (*k* = 7) clusters was 0.26, indicating overlapping but non-identical boundaries. The moderate concordance suggests that broad trajectory types (secure employment, inactivity, churning) are recoverable by either method, while finer distinctions depend on the analytic approach. Mapper operates downstream of both methods: it takes the embedding as input and reveals structure within whatever typology is imposed.

## 3. Data and Methods

### 3.1 Data Source and Trajectory Construction

We reuse the trajectory dataset from our companion paper (Dorman, 2026a). Briefly: from Understanding Society (USoc, waves 1–14, 2009–2023), we reconstruct 27,280 individual trajectories with at least 10 consecutive years of valid employment-income observations. Each person-year is assigned to one of nine states crossing employment status (employed, unemployed, inactive) with income band (low, mid, high), where low is defined as below 60% of the contemporary equivalised household income median, mid as 60–120%, and high as above 120%.

Mean trajectory length is 12.9 years (median 13, range 10–14). The range 10–14 is narrow because USoc operates on annual waves with the 14th fielded in 2022/23; shorter trajectories arise from cohort entry timing (later-entry cohorts have fewer waves available) and attrition, not from birth decade restrictions. The sample is 56% female; birth cohorts span the 1930s through the 1990s. Forward-fill imputation affected 4.9% of person-years.

Known panel survey limitations apply. Low-income workers face higher attrition in household panels (Watson & Wooden 2012), so the sample slightly overrepresents stable employment trajectories. USoc's annual refreshment samples (BHPS cross-linkage from wave 1, ethnic minority boost, immigrant sample from wave 3) partially mitigate attrition bias but do not eliminate it. The companion paper (Dorman, 2026a, §3.1) reports a full attrition analysis; results here are subject to the same caveats. Full data construction details, including BHPS cross-wave linkage and equivalisation procedures, are reported there.

### 3.2 Embedding

Trajectories are embedded in continuous space via n-gram frequency vectors. For each trajectory, we compute unigram (9 dimensions: relative frequency of each state) and bigram (81 dimensions: relative frequency of each consecutive state pair) frequencies, yielding a 90-dimensional term-frequency vector. After standardisation, we project to 20 dimensions via PCA, retaining the same frozen PCA loadings used in Dorman (2026a). The resulting 27,280 points in $\mathbb{R}^{20}$ serve as input to both persistent homology (companion paper) and Mapper (this paper).

### 3.3 Mapper Construction

We construct Mapper graphs using KeplerMapper 2.1 (van Veen & Saul 2019). The algorithm involves three configurable steps — lens function, cover, and clustering — each of which we vary systematically in our sensitivity analysis (§3.5).

**Lens function.** The lens function projects the PCA-20D embedding to a lower-dimensional space that defines the cover. Our primary lens projects onto the first two principal components (PCA-2D), preserving the dominant axes of variation — the employment-inactivity and income gradients. We additionally evaluate three alternative lenses: L2 norm (distance from the embedding centroid), sum projection, and kernel density estimation (KDE with bandwidth 1.0). These vary in dimensionality (1D vs. 2D) and in what aspect of the data they emphasise: PCA-2D tracks the two dominant gradients, L2 norm tracks trajectory extremity, sum captures aggregate n-gram magnitude, and KDE density emphasises local crowding.

**Cover.** The lens image is covered by a grid of overlapping hypercubes. Following parameter tuning in the companion analysis (§3.5), we set n_cubes = 30 and overlap fraction = 0.5 as the baseline. Higher n_cubes produce finer-grained graphs; higher overlap produces greater connectivity.

**Clustering.** We evaluate two clustering algorithms within each cover element:

1. *DBSCAN* (Ester et al. 1996) with eps $\in \{0.2, 0.3, 0.5\}$ and min_samples = 5. DBSCAN does not require pre-specifying the number of clusters and naturally handles noise points (which are excluded from Mapper nodes). Varying eps controls the neighbourhood radius: smaller eps produces tighter clusters with more noise; larger eps produces broader clusters with less noise. We use DBSCAN as a *conservative lower bound* on sub-regime structure: only geometrically dense sub-regions enter the graph, so detected structure is unlikely to reflect noise.

2. *Agglomerative clustering* with distance threshold $\in \{1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$ and no pre-specified cluster count. Unlike DBSCAN, agglomerative clustering assigns every point to a cluster — there are no noise points, and coverage is necessarily 100%. Varying the distance threshold controls the granularity: lower thresholds produce more, smaller clusters. We use agglomerative clustering as a *complete-coverage upper bound*: it captures boundary-spanning trajectories that DBSCAN treats as noise, but at the cost of producing large graphs that are harder to visualise. The two algorithms are complementary: DBSCAN for exploratory visual inspection of robust structure; agglomerative for coverage statistics and boundary analysis.

**Scaling.** Lens values are MinMax-scaled before cover construction, ensuring that the cover grid spans the data range in each lens dimension.

### 3.4 Node Colouring and Outcome Variables

For each Mapper node, we compute summary statistics (mean, standard deviation, minimum, maximum, count) for two continuous outcome variables:

1. **PC1 (income gradient proxy).** The first principal component of the PCA-20D embedding, which correlates with the employment-inactivity and income gradients. Higher PC1 values correspond to positions nearer the inactivity-low territory; lower values correspond to employed-high territory.

2. **L2 norm (trajectory extremity).** The Euclidean distance from the embedding centroid. Higher L2 norm indicates more atypical career paths.

For each node we also compute the **regime distribution**: the fraction of member trajectories belonging to each of the seven GMM regimes.

We additionally reconstruct six time-averaged outcome variables from the raw 9-state sequences. These are defined formally as follows. Let $T_i$ denote the trajectory length (number of observed waves) and $s_{i,t} \in \{EL, EM, EH, UL, UM, UH, IL, IM, IH\}$ the state at wave $t$ for individual $i$, where E = employed, U = unemployed, I = inactive, L/M/H = low/mid/high income band.

- **Employment rate:** $e_i = T_i^{-1} \sum_{t=1}^{T_i} \mathbb{1}[s_{i,t} \in \{EL, EM, EH\}]$
- **Unemployment rate:** $u_i = T_i^{-1} \sum_{t=1}^{T_i} \mathbb{1}[s_{i,t} \in \{UL, UM, UH\}]$
- **Inactivity rate:** $\iota_i = T_i^{-1} \sum_{t=1}^{T_i} \mathbb{1}[s_{i,t} \in \{IL, IM, IH\}]$
- **High-income rate:** $h_i = T_i^{-1} \sum_{t=1}^{T_i} \mathbb{1}[s_{i,t} \in \{EH, UH, IH\}]$
- **Low-income rate:** $\ell_i = T_i^{-1} \sum_{t=1}^{T_i} \mathbb{1}[s_{i,t} \in \{EL, UL, IL\}]$
- **Churning rate:** $c_i = (T_i - 1)^{-1} \sum_{t=2}^{T_i} \mathbb{1}[s_{i,t} \neq s_{i,t-1}]$

Note that $e_i + u_i + \iota_i = 1$ and $h_i + m_i + \ell_i = 1$ by construction. All six outcomes are computed over the entire observed trajectory.

**Information overlap.**  PC1 and the outcome variables are derived from the same n-gram state sequences, so some correlation is expected. We report the full PC1–outcome correlation matrix in §4.8 and interpret sub-regime structure accordingly. Outcome variables that correlate weakly with PC1 — specifically churning rate ($r = 0.12$) and final income band ($r = -0.22$) — provide information about within-regime heterogeneity that is partially orthogonal to the embedding geometry. Strongly correlated variables (employment rate: $r = -0.92$; inactivity rate: $r = 0.85$) essentially summarise the same gradient that PC1 already represents; colouring nodes by these variables is interpretively useful but not independent of the embedding.

### 3.5 Sensitivity Analysis Design

We conduct a systematic sensitivity analysis across 24 configurations formed by the full cross of:

- **Lens function:** PCA-2D, L2 norm, sum, KDE density (4 levels)
- **DBSCAN eps:** 0.2, 0.3, 0.5 (3 levels → 12 DBSCAN configurations)
- **Agglomerative threshold:** 1.0, 1.5, 2.0 (3 levels → 12 agglomerative configurations)

All configurations use the same cover parameters (n_cubes = 30, overlap = 0.5). For each configuration, we record: (a) graph topology (nodes, edges, components, coverage); (b) regime validation (NMI, mean node purity, bridge node count); and (c) sub-regime structure (number of nodes with |z| > 1.0 on PC1 and L2 norm relative to regime means).

### 3.6 Validation Against GMM Regimes

We validate the Mapper decomposition against the seven GMM regimes using three metrics:

**Normalised mutual information (NMI).** Each trajectory that appears in at least one Mapper node is assigned the label of its node (first-assignment for multiply-assigned points). NMI between this labelling and the GMM regime labels measures correspondence. NMI = 1 indicates perfect agreement; NMI = 0 indicates independence.

**Node purity.** For each node, the fraction of members belonging to the dominant regime. High mean purity indicates that Mapper nodes are internally regime-homogeneous.

**Bridge nodes.** Nodes where no single regime accounts for more than 70% of members. Bridge nodes span regime boundaries and may identify transitional populations.

### 3.7 Within-Regime Heterogeneity Detection

To detect within-regime sub-structure, we apply the following procedure for each of the seven GMM regimes:

1. Identify all Mapper nodes where the majority of members belong to the focal regime.
2. Compute the mean of the outcome variable (PC1 or L2 norm) for each such node.
3. Compute the regime-level mean and standard deviation of the outcome variable across all trajectories assigned to that regime in the full dataset.
4. For each majority-regime node, compute a z-score for the node-level mean relative to the trajectory-level regime mean and standard deviation, and flag nodes where |z| > 1.0.

The |z| > 1.0 threshold is a conventional screening criterion identifying nodes whose mean falls outside approximately one standard deviation of the regime mean. We do not claim statistical significance at this threshold; it serves as an exploratory flag for nodes that merit substantive inspection. To assess robustness, we report sub-regime counts at four thresholds (|z| > 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0) in Table 4a. Where formal inference is required — specifically, for the count of sub-regime nodes across 7 regimes × 2 variables (14 comparisons) — we apply Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction at $q < 0.05$ (§4.3). The permutation null tests in §4.3 provide a complementary, distribution-free assessment that does not depend on normality assumptions.

Flagged nodes are the sub-regions where outcomes detectably differ from the regime average.

### 4.3 Within-Regime Heterogeneity

Node purity is near-perfect (0.999), so binary indicators like escape probability produce no within-regime variation. Within-regime heterogeneity must be assessed on continuous variables. We use PC1 of the embedding (income gradient proxy) and L2 norm (trajectory extremity).

**PC1 as income gradient.** Sub-regime analysis (|z| > 1.0 from regime-mean PC1) identifies **358 sub-regime nodes** across six of seven regimes (Table 4).

**Permutation null test.** To assess whether 358 is larger than chance, we run two permutation tests (100 permutations each). *Regime-shuffle null*: shuffle all GMM labels, rebuild Mapper, recount sub-regime nodes. Observed 358 nodes vs. null mean = 86.3 (sd = 12.5), range 42–113; empirical $p < 0.01$ (0/100 permutations reached 358). *Within-node-shuffle null*: fix the Mapper graph, shuffle outcome values within each regime, recount. Observed 358 vs. null mean = 9.4 (sd = 5.2), range 0–26; $p < 0.01$. Both tests confirm that the observed sub-regime structure reflects genuine spatial concentration of outcome variation, not distributional properties of the outcome variable.

**Multi-threshold analysis.** Table 4a reports sub-regime node counts at four z-score thresholds. FDR-corrected binomial tests (Benjamini-Hochberg, 14 regime×variable comparisons) find 2 out of 14 statistically significant at $q < 0.05$ at the |z| > 1.0 threshold; both correspond to R6 (Low-Income Churn).

#### 4.5.1 Findings Robust to Algorithm Choice

**Within-regime heterogeneity persists across all 24 configurations (within PCA-20D).** Every DBSCAN and agglomerative configuration detects sub-regime structure on both PC1 and L2 norm. Section 4.7 confirms that these findings replicate qualitatively under UMAP-16D. The number of sub-regime nodes varies with resolution (more nodes → more sub-regime nodes), but the qualitative pattern — R6 and R5 most heterogeneous, R3 least — is invariant.

**Coverage is determined by the clustering algorithm, not the cover.** Within each algorithm class, coverage is effectively constant across lens functions and parameter values: ~40% at DBSCAN eps = 0.2, ~44% at eps = 0.3, ~51% at eps = 0.5, and 100% for all agglomerative configurations. The cover parameters (n_cubes, overlap) control graph resolution; the clustering algorithm controls what fraction of the sample enters the graph at all.

**Purity remains high (> 0.99) across all configurations.** Even agglomerative clustering, which produces far larger graphs (13,620–44,396 nodes), maintains purity above 0.990. The seven GMM regimes correspond to geometrically distinct regions regardless of how points are assigned to clusters within cover elements.

**NMI is stable in a narrow range (0.34–0.49).** All 24 configurations produce NMI between 0.339 and 0.488. The highest NMI values occur with 1D lenses (L2 norm: 0.485, sum: 0.488 at DBSCAN eps = 0.5), which produce fewer, larger nodes that aggregate more closely to the GMM labelling.

#### 4.5.2 Findings That Depend on Algorithm Choice

**The noise rate is a DBSCAN property, not a data property.** DBSCAN excludes 40–60% of trajectories as noise, concentrated in the churning regimes (Mixed Churn: 94% noise, Low-Income Churn: 63% noise). Agglomerative clustering eliminates this entirely: every trajectory is assigned to a node. The "churning trajectories are too geometrically dispersed to cluster" narrative holds only for DBSCAN at the tested eps values. Under hierarchical merging, the same trajectories form small but valid clusters.

**Bridge nodes are algorithm-dependent.** Under DBSCAN, zero or very few bridge nodes exist (0–2 across all 12 DBSCAN configurations). Under agglomerative clustering, 86–449 bridge nodes appear, scaling with the distance threshold and lens dimensionality. The PCA-2D lens produces the most bridge nodes (203–449), consistent with its 2D cover creating more boundary interactions. The regime boundaries are not as geometrically sharp as the DBSCAN results suggest — the sharpness reflects DBSCAN's exclusion of boundary-region points as noise rather than the absence of boundary-spanning trajectories.

**Bridge node scaling with distance threshold (Table 8a).** Table 8a reports bridge node counts as a function of agglomerative distance threshold for each lens function. The relationship is monotonically increasing across all four lenses, with no natural elbow — larger thresholds merge more boundary trajectories into multi-regime nodes. This monotonic scaling confirms that bridge node counts are threshold-dependent rather than reflecting a discrete boundary width.

### 4.6 Lens Function Effects

The four lens functions produce structurally similar Mapper graphs with interpretable differences (Figure 7). PCA-2D, the only 2D lens, produces the most nodes and edges (1,060 nodes, 1,774 edges at DBSCAN eps = 0.5) because its 2D cover grid has more cells than any 1D cover. The 1D lenses (L2 norm, sum, density) produce sparser graphs (494–586 nodes, 265–377 edges) but with comparable coverage.

L2 norm and sum lenses produce higher NMI (0.485, 0.488) than PCA-2D (0.434) or density (0.416), suggesting that these 1D projections align more closely with the GMM's classification boundaries. The density lens, despite capturing local crowding, does not improve regime correspondence — possibly because the GMM already captures density modes.

### 4.7 Embedding Robustness (UMAP-16D)

All results in §4.1–§4.6 use PCA-20D embeddings. To assess whether the sub-regime structure is specific to this embedding, we repeat the baseline Mapper analysis on UMAP-16D embeddings (McInnes et al. 2018) cached from the companion analysis. UMAP is a non-linear manifold learning method that preserves local neighbourhood structure at the cost of global distance properties; it often provides a more compact but less interpretable embedding than PCA.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Several findings from the PCA-20D analysis replicate under UMAP-16D:

1. **Sub-regime structure persists.** Under UMAP-16D, 576–828 sub-regime nodes are detected, compared with 358 under PCA-20D (the higher count reflects UMAP's 100% coverage vs. PCA-20D's 51%). The qualitative finding — distinct geometric sub-regions within regimes — is embedding-invariant.

2. **Regime purity remains high (0.954–0.967).** The seven GMM regimes occupy geometrically separable regions in UMAP-16D space, confirming that the typology is not PCA-specific.

3. **Bridge nodes appear under UMAP-16D.** The PCA-20D DBSCAN analysis produces zero bridge nodes; UMAP-16D produces 81–164. This is consistent with the agglomerative findings (§4.5): DBSCAN's zero bridge count partially reflects its noise exclusion in sparse boundary regions. UMAP's complete coverage exposes those boundary-spanning trajectories.

One finding differs: UMAP-16D NMI (0.400–0.422) is slightly lower than PCA-20D NMI (0.434). The GMM was fitted on PCA-20D, so some divergence is expected. The conclusion from §4.1–§4.6 that the dominant findings are robust to Mapper configuration holds; they are also substantially robust to the choice of embedding method, with the caveat that UMAP's non-linear compression may redistribute some inter-regime boundary trajectories.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 What Mapper Adds to Global Persistent Homology

The companion paper (Dorman, 2026a) established that UK trajectory space has significant topological structure: temporal ordering creates cluster separation (order-shuffle H₀ *p* < 0.005), and Wasserstein diagram-level distances reject the first-order Markov null (*p* = 0.002). The Mapper analysis resolves within-regime geometry that the global persistence diagram cannot represent.

The seven GMM regimes are geometrically distinct — purity exceeds 0.99 across all 24 tested configurations. But within these pure-regime regions, Mapper fragments each regime into nodes that vary on the PC1 income gradient. Low-Income Churn spans a 4-unit PC1 range across its 80 DBSCAN nodes; High-Income Inactive spans 4 units across 164 nodes. The persistence diagram summarises global connectivity and cannot see this; flat clustering assigns a single label and cannot see it either.

### 5.2 The Algorithm Matters: Noise and Bridge Nodes

Two results that appeared to characterise the data — the 49% noise rate and the absence of bridge nodes — turn out to characterise the clustering algorithm.

Under DBSCAN (eps = 0.5), 49.3% of trajectories are excluded as noise and zero bridge nodes exist, suggesting that regime boundaries are geometrically sharp and churning trajectories are too dispersed to cluster. Under agglomerative clustering (threshold = 1.5), 100% of trajectories are covered and 359 bridge nodes span regime boundaries. The same data, the same cover, the same lens — different algorithm, different conclusions about noise and boundary structure.

This does not invalidate DBSCAN-based results. DBSCAN's noise exclusion is itself informative: the trajectories it excludes are those without eps-neighbours in their cover element, confirming that they are locally sparse relative to their region of the embedding. But the noise label should not be mistaken for a data property when it is an algorithm property. Reporting results under both DBSCAN and agglomerative clustering — as we recommend — provides a more complete picture.

### 5.3 Implications for Within-Regime Heterogeneity

Regime-level statistics (mean escape rate, mean income, mean employment share) are incomplete descriptors of trajectory types, and the incompleteness is not uniform. The Low-Income Churn regime's 80 DBSCAN nodes span from PC1 ≈ −1.2 (near the Employed Mid boundary) to PC1 ≈ 2.8 (near the Inactive Low boundary) — a 4-unit range that a single regime label collapses to zero.

For policy, the Mapper graph works as a cartographic tool. Colouring nodes by PC1 shows where within a disadvantaged regime individuals sit on the income gradient. Nodes near the Employed Mid boundary plausibly contain individuals whose churning reflects temporary disruption with ongoing labour market attachment; nodes near the Inactive Low boundary contain individuals drifting toward the absorbing inactivity state. These sub-populations may require different interventions; the Mapper graph identifies *where* in trajectory space the distinction matters most, while causal identification of what interventions would work requires additional covariates and experimental or quasi-experimental variation. The phrase "outcome geography" we use throughout is intended to convey spatial structure in outcome distributions, not a claim about mechanisms.

### 5.4 Coverage and the Churning Regimes

The coverage gap under DBSCAN (40–51%) concentrates in the churning regimes. Mixed Churn covers only 6% of its members (232 of 3,787); Low-Income Churn covers 37% (755 of 2,064). Under agglomerative clustering, these regimes are fully covered — the trajectories are not too dispersed to cluster, only too dispersed for DBSCAN at the tested eps values.

The practical implication is that DBSCAN-based Mapper graphs underrepresent precisely the populations of greatest policy interest. Disadvantaged and churning trajectories are the ones most likely to be DBSCAN noise, and therefore the ones excluded from the outcome geography that Mapper is meant to provide. Agglomerative clustering recovers these populations, at the cost of producing much larger graphs (13,620–44,396 nodes) that are harder to visualise and interpret.

A pragmatic recommendation: use DBSCAN for initial visualisation and interpretive exploration (its sparser graphs are more readable), but supplement with agglomerative results for coverage statistics, bridge node analysis, and any claims about regime boundaries.

### 5.5 Comparison with Optimal Matching

OM produces flat clusters with no internal structure; the silhouette score of 0.215 at *k* = 7 (Dorman, 2026a) confirms internal heterogeneity. Mapper makes this heterogeneity explicit and navigable. Where OM says "these 2,064 trajectories all belong to the Low-Income Churn type", Mapper says "within this type, there are 80 geometrically distinct sub-regions (under DBSCAN, covering 37% of members) or over 5,000 (under agglomerative, covering 100%), with PC1 values spanning 4 units."

OM remains computationally efficient and well understood. Mapper is more complex and parameter-dependent, but the principal findings survive all 24 sensitivity configurations and the UMAP-16D embedding check. OM asks "how many types?"; Mapper asks "what is inside each type?"

**Computational cost comparison.** OM requires an $O(N^2)$ pairwise distance matrix (27,280× 27,280 = 744M pairs) followed by $O(N^2 \log N)$ Ward linkage, totalling roughly 15–30 minutes on a modern laptop. Mapper requires $O(N \cdot k)$ where $k$ is the number of cover elements (30× 30 = 900 bins here), each clustered with DBSCAN or agglomerative clustering; total runtime for our 24-configuration sweep is approximately 120 seconds. For exploratory analysis with iterative parameter tuning, Mapper is substantially more practical.

**OM dendrogram comparison (Figure 11).** Figure 11 presents the OM Ward dendrogram truncated to 50 representative leaves and coloured by cluster-mean employment rate at the $k = 7$ cut, using the same colour scheme as the Mapper employment rate graph (Figure 8). The dendrogram confirms that two broad super-clusters account for most separability: a high-employment group (left, employment rate 0.85–0.99) and a mixed-to-inactive group (right, 0.00–0.59). Within each super-cluster, sub-divisions are visible at lower Ward distances, but these sub-divisions do not correspond directly to the spatial sub-regions Mapper identifies within each regime. This is because the OM dendrogram lacks position information: two trajectories that are spatially adjacent in PCA-20D but belong to different sub-clusters can appear far apart in the dendrogram if their pairwise OM distance is large. Mapper's spatial layout — where adjacency reflects geometric proximity, not summary statistics — is what makes the within-regime bifurcation in R6 visible. For a sociology audience, the Mapper graph is interpretable as a map ("these individuals are near the boundary with stable employment"), whereas the dendrogram is interpretable as a hierarchy ("these sub-clusters merge at Ward distance 12"). The former is more natural for questions about where within a regime someone sits.

### 5.6 Limitations

Mapper is not a topological invariant: the graph depends on the lens function, cover, and clustering algorithm. Our sensitivity analysis, while broader than most published Mapper applications, does not exhaust the parameter space. In particular, we test only one cover resolution (n_cubes = 30, overlap = 0.5), having established its selection via prior grid search. The interaction between cover resolution and clustering algorithm remains unexplored.

The within-regime heterogeneity we detect is descriptive. The Mapper graph shows *where* outcomes differ but not *why*. Identifying mechanisms requires additional covariates (age, education, region) and potentially causal methods; the Mapper graph identifies the sub-populations for whom the distinction matters most, but does not explain it.

The analysis depends on the PCA-20D embedding from n-gram frequency vectors. Section 4.7 reports that the principal sub-regime findings replicate on UMAP-16D embeddings, but with some quantitative differences (lower NMI, higher sub-regime count from complete coverage). All claims about within-regime heterogeneity and algorithm dependence are therefore robust to Mapper configuration and qualitatively robust to embedding choice, but contingent on the n-gram frequency representation; alternative sequence representations (e.g., optimal matching distance matrices, recurrent neural network embeddings) remain untested.

The agglomerative clustering graphs (13,620–44,396 nodes) are too large for effective visual inspection. We visualise only the 1,500 largest nodes (Figure 6, right panel). Alternative approaches — interactive tools, hierarchical node aggregation, or topological simplification — may be needed to make large agglomerative Mapper graphs interpretable.

## 6. Conclusion

Within-regime heterogeneity is real. Across all 24 Mapper configurations — two clustering algorithms, four lens functions, three parameter values each — and under both PCA-20D and UMAP-16D embeddings, the seven GMM regimes fragment into sub-regions with distinct positions along the dominant embedding gradient. Low-Income Churn nodes span 4 units on PC1; the endpoints represent qualitatively different positions in trajectory space. The persistence diagram cannot see this (it tracks global connectivity). The GMM label cannot see it (it assigns one type per trajectory).

The clustering algorithm matters more than is typically acknowledged. Under DBSCAN, half the sample is noise and no bridge nodes exist; under agglomerative clustering, coverage is complete and hundreds of bridge nodes span regime boundaries. Mapper papers that report noise rates or bridge statistics under a single clustering algorithm may be reporting algorithm properties.

Colouring nodes by reconstructed trajectory outcomes — employment rate, churning rate, final income band — turns the graph into an outcome geography that a policymaker or a sociologist can read. Within Low-Income Churn, nodes near Employed Mid show employment rates of 0.7 and final income trending mid-range; nodes near Inactive Low show rates below 0.3. A regime-level analysis treats these sub-populations identically. Mapper does not.

The immediate next step is cross-national replication on SOEP, PSID, and CNEF, where the regime boundaries and within-regime bifurcations should differ if they reflect UK-specific labour market institutions. If they do not, the structure is generic — and the policy implication is that the sub-population near the R6/R4 boundary is identifiable from trajectory data alone, regardless of national context.
