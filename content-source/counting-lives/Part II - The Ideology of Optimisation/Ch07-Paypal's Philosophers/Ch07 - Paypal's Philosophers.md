# Ch7 – PayPal's Philosophers: Silicon Valley and the New Eugenicists

## Core Claim
The new eugenicists of Silicon Valley did not resurrect Galton's vocabulary — they laundered it. IQ replaced "natural ability"; market returns replaced "fitness"; "meritocracy" replaced "hereditary genius"; and the venture portfolio replaced the breeding programme. The statistical tools are identical — normal distributions, regression to the mean, the ranking of human populations — but they are now embedded in the logic of capital rather than the logic of the state. This chapter shows that Transition 3 (from cybernetic governance to algorithmic ideology) runs directly through a small network of Stanford dormitories, libertarian philosophy seminars, and early PayPal whiteboards.

## Argument in Sections

### 1. The Statistical Inheritance: From Galton to the g-Factor
Charles Spearman's "general intelligence" factor (g, 1904) was the direct statistical heir of Galton's anthropometric programme — a latent variable extracted by factor analysis from a battery of cognitive tests, reifying the assumption of a single underlying human quality that could be ranked. By the time Herrnstein and Murray published *The Bell Curve* in 1994, the psychometric tradition had a century of accumulated statistical machinery: norm-referenced scoring, heritability estimates, regression of life outcomes on IQ. *The Bell Curve*'s core move — presenting social inequality as the natural consequence of a normally distributed cognitive capacity — was Galton's argument restated in the idiom of modern psychometrics. This section establishes the technical lineage explicitly, cross-referencing [[Ch02 - The Eugenic Ledger]] and showing that the mathematics had not changed, only the institutional vehicle.

### 2. The Stanford Crucible: Where Libertarianism Meets Ranking
The Stanford Review, founded in 1987, was not merely a conservative student newspaper. It was a practised space for deploying statistical and economic arguments against redistribution, affirmative action, and diversity programmes — arguments that framed existing hierarchies as outcomes of measurable ability differences rather than structural advantage. Peter Thiel's intellectual formation here is decisive: René Girard's mimetic theory provided a philosophical framework for understanding society as a competition for scarce distinction, while libertarian economics provided the tools to argue that market outcomes reveal merit. The crucial move — and the one this chapter traces — is the fusion of the psychometric ranking tradition with venture capital logic: the good investor, like the good geneticist, identifies and concentrates quality. The portfolio is the new eugenics programme.

### 3. PayPal and the Meritocracy Machine
The founding and scaling of PayPal (1998–2002) was self-consciously conducted as an experiment in ability-sorting. Thiel's hiring philosophy, documented in *Zero to One* and in accounts by founding team members, treated the company as a proof of the claim that concentrating high-g individuals produces disproportionate returns. Diversity — of background, experience, perspective — was framed as statistical noise that degraded signal. The PayPal Mafia's subsequent network of investments, introductions, and recommendations functioned as a private credentialing system in which the initial sorting decisions compounded over decades. This section examines that network mathematically: it operates as a power-law amplifier, where early inclusion confers geometric advantage and early exclusion is effectively permanent. The model is not Galton's state eugenics programme but it produces the same result — a self-reinforcing hierarchy legitimated by the language of natural ability.

### 4. Neoreaction as Control Theory: Yarvin, Land, and the Cathedral
Curtis Yarvin's neoreactionary philosophy — developed under the pseudonym Mencius Moldbug from 2007 — is best understood as cybernetics applied to political theory. Democracy, in Yarvin's model, is a corrupted feedback system: the "Cathedral" (universities, media, civil service) generates error signals that push the system away from its true optimum (efficient, hierarchical, technocratic governance). The solution is to replace the corrupt controller with a clean one — ideally a sovereign corporation run like a startup, with shareholders rather than voters. The mathematical vocabulary is directly derived from Ch5's cybernetics: the state as a control system, politics as noise, efficiency as the only legitimate target function. Thiel's funding of Yarvin's Urbit project, and his public endorsement of Yarvin's ideas, makes this not merely an intellectual curiosity but a live political programme. Nick Land's accelerationism provides the more extreme version: if the current system is irredeemably corrupted, the correct move is to accelerate its collapse so that a higher-quality order can emerge — an argument that maps directly onto power-law thinking about creative destruction.

### 5. The New Anthropometric Laboratory: From IQ to Embryo Selection
The chapter closes with a contemporary scene that closes a 140-year circle. Galton's 1884 anthropometric laboratory charged visitors threepence to be measured; today, genomic prediction companies charge couples tens of thousands of pounds to select embryos for polygenic scores that include predicted cognitive ability. The statistical logic is identical — a latent trait is measured, individuals are ranked, and selection is applied — but the instrument has moved from callipers to GWAS arrays. Elon Musk's public statements about dysgenics and the Collins pronatalist network's explicit advocacy for IQ-selectivereproduction bring this full circle, and this section makes the connection explicit: not as rhetoric but as a traceable genealogy of statistical ideas from Galton's regression-to-the-mean through *The Bell Curve* to polygenic embryo scoring. The tools evolved; the project did not.
