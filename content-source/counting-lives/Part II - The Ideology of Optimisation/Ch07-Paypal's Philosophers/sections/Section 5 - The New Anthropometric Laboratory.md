# 5. The New Anthropometric Laboratory: From IQ to Embryo Selection

In 1884, Galton charged visitors threepence to be measured. In 2024, Genomic Prediction charged prospective parents approximately $400 per embryo to be scored. The instruments are different — callipers and dynamometers in one case, genome-wide association study arrays in the other — but the statistical logic is identical. A latent trait is posited. Individuals are measured against it. The measurements are used to rank. The ranking is used to select. In Galton's laboratory, selection meant breeding policy: encourage the reproduction of the well-measured, discourage the rest. In a San Francisco fertility clinic, selection means implantation: transfer the highest-scoring embryo, discard or freeze the rest. The coercive apparatus of the state has been replaced by the consumer apparatus of the market. The sorting function is preserved.

## The technology

Polygenic scores for complex traits — educational attainment, cognitive ability, disease risk — are derived from genome-wide association studies (GWAS) that correlate thousands of genetic variants across the genome with measured outcomes in large adult populations. For a trait like cognitive ability, a GWAS identifies thousands of single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) each associated with a tiny fraction of the variance in test scores. The polygenic score for an individual embryo sums these variant effects, weighted by their estimated effect sizes, to produce a single number: a prediction of where on the distribution that embryo, if implanted and raised to adulthood, would fall.

The single number is the point. Factor analysis reduced the complexity of cognitive variation to *g*. IQ testing reduced *g* to a score normed at 100. Polygenic scoring reduces the genome to a predicted IQ — or, more cautiously, a predicted educational attainment — expressed as a percentile rank. At each step in the 140-year lineage, the instrument becomes more sophisticated and the output becomes more abstract, but the operation remains the same: compress the irreducible complexity of a human life into a single rankable quantity. Gould's reification critique, formulated against Spearman and applied to *The Bell Curve*, applies with equal force here. The polygenic score is a statistical construct — a weighted sum of population-level associations — treated as a property of the individual embryo. The score does not measure a thing in the embryo. It estimates a probability derived from a population, and then attaches that probability to a specific cluster of cells as if it were a measurement of that cluster's worth.

Commercial embryo selection based on polygenic scores has been available in the United States since 2019, when Genomic Prediction began offering the service. By 2026, at least four companies — Genomic Prediction, Orchid, Herasight, and Nucleus — offered prospective parents the option to score their IVF embryos for a range of traits. The services are marketed in the language of health: reduced risk of diabetes, heart disease, schizophrenia. But the cognitive-trait scoring — the scoring for predicted educational attainment and, in some offerings, predicted intelligence — is the service that connects the industry to this chapter's argument. It is Galton's anthropometric programme, rebuilt with GWAS arrays instead of reaction-time apparatus, and funded by parents willing to pay rather than by a state willing to coerce.

## The scientific gap

The scientific literature on polygenic embryo selection is cautious where the commercial literature is confident. Karavani and colleagues, writing in *Cell* in 2019, modelled the realistic gains from selecting the highest-scoring embryo in a typical IVF cycle and found them modest: for cognitive traits, the expected gain was roughly equivalent to a few months of additional schooling. The reason is mathematical. A typical IVF cycle produces two to five viable embryos — a selection pool so small that even a strongly predictive polygenic score has limited leverage. The variance within a set of full siblings is constrained by shared parental genomes; the score's predictive power, derived from population-level associations, drops sharply when applied to a handful of genetically related embryos rather than to thousands of unrelated individuals. Treff and Zimmerman, in a 2022 *New England Journal of Medicine* analysis, catalogued the further limitations: polygenic scores derived from predominantly European-ancestry GWAS perform substantially worse in non-European-ancestry populations; the scores capture none of the environmental variation (schooling, nutrition, family resources, neighbourhood quality) that accounts for a large fraction of the variance in complex traits; and the confidence intervals around individual-level predictions are wide enough to make the ranking of two or three embryos statistically meaningless.

The gap between scientific caution and commercial promotion is analytically productive for this chapter because it performs the same function as the gap between *The Bell Curve*'s technical appendix and its verbal conclusions. The statistical machinery is present — the GWAS, the effect sizes, the polygenic score — and its presence confers the authority of quantification. But the conclusions that reach the consumer are far stronger than the data support. Parents are told that embryo selection can meaningfully improve their child's cognitive prospects. The published science says the gains are marginal, uncertain, and dwarfed by environmental factors. The gap between these two statements is the space in which the ideology operates: the appearance of scientific precision in the service of a selection programme whose actual efficacy is modest but whose symbolic function — the assertion that cognitive capacity is genetic, measurable, and selectable — is doing the real work.

## Musk, the Collinses, and the dysgenics narrative

The embryo-selection industry operates within a broader ideological framework that connects it directly to the PayPal network and the *Bell Curve* tradition. Elon Musk has made public and repeated statements about what he calls "dysgenics" — the claim that high-IQ populations are reproducing below replacement rate while low-IQ populations reproduce above it, producing a secular decline in average cognitive capacity. The argument is Galton's regression anxiety, restated: if intelligence is heritable and the intelligent are not reproducing, the population's mean intelligence will decline toward mediocrity. Galton's proposed solution was state-sponsored positive eugenics — encourage the fit to breed. Musk's proposed solution is pronatalism among the cognitive elite, supported by technology (IVF, embryo selection) that can enhance the next generation's genetic endowment.

Malcolm and Simone Collins, founders of the Pronatalist Foundation and part of the Thiel-adjacent Silicon Valley network, have made the programme fully explicit. They have publicly described selecting embryos for cognitive traits, advocated for increased fertility among high-IQ populations, and framed their project in the language of existential risk: if the cognitive elite does not reproduce, civilisation will decline. The vocabulary — dysgenics, cognitive elite, population quality — is Galton's, updated with genomic terminology. The institutional form is different: a nonprofit foundation and a commercial IVF service rather than a state breeding programme. The ranking logic is unchanged.

The circle that this chapter has been tracing closes here. Galton measured bodies in South Kensington and proposed that the state breed for quality. Spearman extracted *g* from schoolchildren's test scores and declared it a real heritable property. Burt fabricated twin studies to prove heritability. Jensen and Herrnstein and Murray argued that the distribution was natural and redistribution futile. Thiel and the PayPal network built institutions that selected for the right tail and excluded the rest. Yarvin and Land provided the political philosophy: the welfare state decelerates; markets sort; democracy corrupts the signal. And now, in a fertility clinic that charges by the embryo, the programme completes itself: not as state coercion but as consumer choice, not as a breeding registry but as a polygenic score, not as Galton's callipers but as a GWAS array — but with the same operation, performed on the same assumption, producing the same result. A latent trait is posited. Individuals are ranked. Selection is applied.

The statistical tools evolved. The project did not.
