# 1. The Statistical Inheritance: From Galton to the g-Factor

In 1884, Francis Galton charged visitors threepence to be measured at his Anthropometric Laboratory in South Kensington. He recorded their grip strength, reaction times, visual acuity, and auditory discrimination — seventeen variables in all, collected from over nine thousand individuals across the run of the International Health Exhibition. The threepence bought you a card with your measurements; it also enrolled you, unknowingly, in a statistical programme whose aim was to demonstrate that a single underlying quality — what Galton called "natural ability" — was distributed across the population as a normal curve, that its distribution was hereditary, and that the right tail of the curve contained the men who deserved to govern. The laboratory produced no clear evidence of this single underlying intelligence. The correlation between sensory acuity and eminence was weak; the data refused to yield the unitary factor Galton was looking for. But the laboratory established the methodological template that would outlast its failed measurements: gather many observations across multiple dimensions, look for systematic correlations among them, and extract from those correlations a single underlying factor that could be used to rank human beings on a linear scale.

Twenty years later, Charles Spearman completed the project Galton had begun.

## The invention of *g*

Spearman's 1904 paper, "'General Intelligence,' Objectively Determined and Measured," published in the *American Journal of Psychology*, is simultaneously a psychological hypothesis and a statistical invention @spearman1904g-factor. Working with data from British schoolchildren's performance across subjects — classics, French, English, mathematics, music — Spearman observed what Galton had expected but failed to demonstrate: children who performed well on one subject tended to perform well on others, producing a matrix of positive intercorrelations across tasks. From this pattern of correlation, Spearman developed a mathematical procedure that would later be formalised as factor analysis. The procedure decomposed the correlation matrix into a common factor shared across all tests — which he labelled *g*, for general intelligence — and residual factors specific to each task, labelled *s*. The common factor was not measured directly. It was a latent variable, extracted mathematically from the pattern of correlations, posited to be present in varying degrees in each individual, and presumed to explain why performance across diverse cognitive tasks hung together.

The statistical machinery was elegant. The intellectual move it performed was not. Spearman treated this mathematically extracted latent variable as a real property of individual persons — something that existed "in the head," that varied in quantity from one person to the next, and that could, in principle, be inherited. This was not a mathematical result. It was a philosophical choice dressed in the authority of the correlation matrix. Factor analysis identifies dimensions that summarise patterns of covariation; it does not determine whether those dimensions correspond to real physical or biological entities. Spearman called his factor "general intelligence," assumed its heritability, and treated it as a unitary cognitive capacity — interpreting the mathematics, rather than following where it led. But the mathematics made the interpretation seem inescapable, because the numbers looked like measurements of something real.

Stephen Jay Gould, writing eighty years later, gave this move a precise name — two names, in fact. In *The Mismeasure of Man*, Gould argued that the entire tradition of biological determinism rested on two recurring fallacies: reification — the treatment of an abstract statistical construct as a concrete entity — and ranking — the compression of multidimensional human variation onto a single ascending scale of worth @gould1981_mismeasure_man. Spearman's *g* was, for Gould, the paradigm case of both. The factor was reified: an artefact of the correlation matrix was declared to be a biological reality. It was then used to rank: individuals were ordered from low-*g* to high-*g* as if cognitive capacity were a single substance that could be measured like height or weight. Gould showed that the reification was technically unforced. The same correlation matrices that produced a dominant first factor under one rotation method could produce multiple group factors of roughly equal importance under another. The decision to extract *g* rather than several independent capacities belonged to the analyst, and different analysts made different decisions. The defenders of the psychometric tradition responded, correctly, that the hierarchical factor model — *g* at the apex, group factors below — produced the most consistent results across many datasets. But "most consistent" is a pragmatic criterion, and pragmatic criteria do not settle ontological questions. The debate persists in psychometrics. The important point for what follows is that *g*'s existence as a real heritable property of individuals was contested from 1904 onward — and that the contest was decided by the institutional weight of the tradition that found *g* useful, rather than by any experiment that settled the matter.

## The chain of descent

The line from Spearman's 1904 paper to Silicon Valley's meritocratic ideology runs through three institutional waypoints, each of which added new statistical machinery to the original claim while leaving the fundamental architecture intact.

The first was Cyril Burt. Spearman's student and successor at University College London, Burt spent the 1940s through the 1960s producing twin studies that appeared to demonstrate extraordinarily high heritability for intelligence — correlations of IQ between identical twins reared apart that were not merely high but suspiciously precise, with identical values recurring across papers published years apart. After Burt's death in 1971, Leon Kamin demonstrated that several of his reported correlations were statistically impossible: the numbers were too consistent, the sample sizes kept growing without changing the results, and some of Burt's co-authors appeared never to have existed. The fraud mattered less for psychometric theory — Galton and Spearman had established the hereditarian argument before Burt — than for educational policy in Britain, where Burt's numbers had provided the empirical anchoring for three decades of ability-sorting. The 11-plus examination, which sorted British children into grammar schools and secondary moderns on the basis of a single IQ-adjacent test at age eleven, relied in part on the Burt-derived claim that intelligence was substantially fixed by heredity and therefore measurable early. When the data collapsed, the policy survived.

The second waypoint was Arthur Jensen. In 1969, Jensen published "How Much Can We Boost IQ and Scholastic Achievement?" in the *Harvard Educational Review*, arguing that compensatory education programmes like Head Start had failed to close the IQ gap between Black and white Americans because the gap was substantially genetic in origin. Jensen's article synthesised existing psychometric literature rather than presenting new data, drawing heavily on Burt's twin-study results and framed throughout by Spearman's *g*-factor theory. Its political timing — published in the aftermath of the Great Society, when the apparent failure of anti-poverty programmes demanded explanation — ensured that it was read as a scientific verdict on redistribution: if cognitive capacity was heritable and normally distributed, then no amount of social spending would compress the distribution, and the gap between those at the right tail and those at the left was natural, permanent, and not the government's problem to solve. Jensen's 1969 argument is the direct intellectual ancestor of *The Bell Curve*. Herrnstein and Murray cite it extensively and reproduce its core statistical framework.

The third waypoint was the Pioneer Fund. Established in 1937 by the textile magnate Wickliffe Draper, the Pioneer Fund was created explicitly to finance research into "racial betterment" and the "problems of heredity and eugenics." Over the second half of the twentieth century, it funded Jensen, the psychologist J. Philippe Rushton, and a constellation of researchers who kept the hereditarian IQ programme alive in a scholarly landscape that had largely moved away from it. Seventeen of the sources cited in *The Bell Curve*'s bibliography were authors who had received Pioneer Fund grants. The Fund operated openly. Its effect was to ensure the continuous production of peer-reviewed research supporting the hereditarian position — research that could then be cited in a book marketed to a general audience as if it represented mainstream scientific consensus. The statistical arguments circulated; the funding source was noted only in footnotes.

## *The Bell Curve* as restatement

Richard Herrnstein and Charles Murray's *The Bell Curve: Intelligence and Class Structure in American Life*, published in 1994, was Galton's argument restated in the idiom of late-twentieth-century social science, with regression tables in place of pedigree charts and the NLSY dataset in place of biographical dictionaries.

The book rests on three interlocking statistical claims. First, that *g*, operationalised as an IQ score, is real, stable, substantially heritable, and predictive of life outcomes. Second, that in multivariate regression analyses using data from the National Longitudinal Survey of Youth, IQ is a stronger predictor of poverty, crime, welfare dependency, educational attainment, and employment than is socioeconomic background. Third, that the approximately fifteen-point gap in mean IQ scores between Black and white Americans is substantially genetic in origin. The statistical method is hierarchical multiple regression: Herrnstein and Murray enter IQ and a composite socioeconomic-status variable into models predicting various social outcomes, and report that the IQ coefficient is consistently larger.

The regression specification is the central site of methodological dispute. The SES composite — derived from parental income, education, and occupational prestige — is a severely attenuated measure of the structural disadvantages faced by poor and minority Americans. It excludes residential segregation, school quality, exposure to environmental toxins including lead (which has measurable effects on cognitive-test performance), access to healthcare, neighbourhood poverty concentration, and the accumulated effects of intergenerational wealth exclusion. By controlling for this thin proxy and attributing the residual variance to IQ, Herrnstein and Murray mismeasured the socioeconomic effects and relabelled the residual as intelligence.

The critique came quickly from three directions. Gould's revised *Mismeasure of Man* (1996) attacked the book's statistical foundation at the level of factor analysis: the decision to extract a single dominant *g* and treat it as a biological reality was the same reification fallacy that had dogged the psychometric tradition since Spearman. Claude Fischer and colleagues' *Inequality by Design* (1996) took a different approach, re-analysing Herrnstein and Murray's own NLSY data with a richer set of social controls — school quality, neighbourhood disadvantage, family structure, environmental exposures. When these variables were included, the predictive power of IQ dropped substantially and the predictive power of social circumstances rose correspondingly. Fischer's team concluded that what Herrnstein and Murray had actually demonstrated was that AFQT scores — the book's IQ proxy, though the Armed Forces Qualifying Test measures developed academic skills rather than innate cognitive capacity — correlate with social outcomes because both are produced by the same social conditions: the quality of the school, the safety of the neighbourhood, the resources of the family. The omitted-variable critique was the most technically precise: when a regression model excludes variables correlated with both the predictor and the outcome, the coefficient on the included predictor is inflated. The book's thin SES control guaranteed that the IQ coefficient would absorb effects that belonged to structure rather than cognition.

These critiques were published promptly, in mainstream academic venues, by credentialled researchers using the same datasets. They did not prevent the book from selling over 400,000 copies in hardback, or from providing — and this is the point that matters for what follows — the intellectual vocabulary through which a small network of Stanford-educated libertarians would frame human inequality as the natural product of a cognitive distribution that markets merely revealed.

## The mathematics did not change

The through-line from Galton's anthropometric laboratory to *The Bell Curve*'s regression tables can be stated precisely: the mathematics did not change, only the institutional vehicle. Galton proposed that a single underlying quality, normally distributed and heritable, explained the hierarchy of human worth; he built a laboratory to measure it and used biographical dictionaries to validate it. Spearman replaced the anthropometric measurements with schoolchildren's test scores and the biographical dictionaries with correlation matrices, but the inference was the same: extract a single latent factor, reify it as a real property of individuals, rank the population along it. Burt added twin studies to demonstrate heritability; the twin studies were fraudulent, but the framework they supported — fixed, heritable, normally distributed intelligence — survived the fraud intact. Jensen added the political argument: if intelligence is heritable, compensatory education is futile, and racial inequality is a fact of nature rather than a product of policy. Herrnstein and Murray added the regression tables: IQ predicts poverty better than SES does, therefore poverty reflects cognitive sorting, and the welfare state is addressing the wrong problem.

At each stage, the statistical tools become more sophisticated — from pedigree tables to correlation matrices to factor analysis to multivariate regression — but the underlying claim remains Galton's: that a single measurable quality, distributed normally across the population, explains the social hierarchy, and that the hierarchy is therefore natural, inherited, and beyond the reach of redistribution. The tools evolved. The project did not.

What changed, between Galton's laboratory and *The Bell Curve*'s reception, was the institutional setting in which the claim was put to work. Galton's programme was a project of state eugenics: the state should identify and encourage the breeding of superior stock. By 1994, the state was the wrong vehicle. The argument that inequality reflects cognitive sorting did not need a state breeding programme to operationalise it; it needed a labour market, a venture-capital ecosystem, and a hiring philosophy that treated the concentration of high-*g* individuals as the driver of economic returns. The statistical claim made by *The Bell Curve* — that IQ predicts life outcomes and that the distribution is substantially heritable — was exactly the claim that a certain kind of Silicon Valley founder needed to believe in order to treat his own hiring decisions as acts of meritocratic justice rather than exercises of accumulated structural advantage.

The next section follows that claim from the pages of *The Bell Curve* into a specific dormitory in Stanford, California, where it met libertarian economics, mimetic theory, and the editorial board of a student newspaper called the *Stanford Review*.

1. **IQ-test norming circularity.** The research note on the g-factor (§3.2) contains strong material on how IQ tests are *designed* to produce a normal distribution (mean 100, SD 15) by selecting and weighting items to match the expected curve. The bell curve of the book's title is engineered, not found. This is a major point for the book's argument about how statistical instruments produce the realities they claim to discover — connects directly to the Quetelet–Galton framework from Ch02. Could be a full subsection (~800 words) between "The invention of g" and "The chain of descent."

2. **AFQT / IQ conflation — expand.** The current draft compresses the AFQT point into the Fischer critique paragraph. The research note details that the AFQT tests arithmetic, word knowledge, paragraph comprehension, and numerical operations — academic skills, not innate capacity. This deserves its own paragraph in the Bell Curve subsection, because the conflation of "what school taught you" with "what your genes gave you" is the technical hinge of the entire meritocratic argument that Section 2 picks up.

3. **The 11-plus as material scene.** The Burt paragraph mentions the 11-plus but doesn't ground it in a specific child, school, or year. A brief scene — a particular year's exam, the sorting of children into grammar vs. secondary modern, the consequences for a specific cohort — would give the chain-of-descent section its material anchor and connect to the book's recurring concern with how statistical abstractions land on actual lives.

4. **Pioneer Fund — expand the funding chain.** The seventeen-sources-from-Pioneer-Fund-grantees claim is powerful but currently unsupported by a citekey. A paragraph naming two or three specific grantees, their specific publications, and their specific appearances in *The Bell Curve*'s bibliography would turn a statistical claim into a traceable genealogy.

5. **Howard Taylor's "deliberately manipulative" charge.** The Bell Curve research note mentions the statistician Howard Taylor describing the statistical presentation as deliberately manipulative — the regression coefficients were buried in a difficult appendix while the main text presented only verbal summaries. This is a strong detail about how statistical authority operates: the numbers are present enough to confer legitimacy but inaccessible enough that the reader must take the authors' verbal interpretation on trust. Worth a paragraph.

6. **Galton's *Hereditary Genius* sampling bias.** The literature note details that Galton's categories (judges, statesmen, commanders) were defined around offices women could not hold, and that "eminence" was circular — visible men counted as eminent because they held positions that made them visible. A sentence or two in the opening could sharpen the point that the template was biased from the start, not merely in hindsight.
