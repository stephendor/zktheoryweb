# 2. The Stanford Crucible: Where Libertarianism Meets Ranking

In the autumn of 1987, a group of Stanford undergraduates launched a newspaper. The *Stanford Review* was funded in part by Irving Kristol's Institute for Educational Affairs, modelled on the *Dartmouth Review* that had made national headlines for its attacks on affirmative action, and edited in its early years by Peter Thiel, then a twenty-year-old philosophy student who had arrived at Stanford from Foster City with a chess rating in the low expert range and a reading list heavy on Ayn Rand. The paper's first issues made its programme explicit: opposition to multiculturalism requirements in the curriculum, opposition to affirmative action in admissions, and a sustained argument — couched in the language of economic efficiency and meritocratic fairness — that existing hierarchies at Stanford and beyond reflected differences in measurable ability rather than differences in structural advantage.

The *Stanford Review* was a student newspaper. It was also a rehearsal space. The arguments Thiel and his co-editors practised there — that diversity diluted quality, that market outcomes revealed cognitive sorting, that redistribution penalised the productive for the benefit of the unproductive — would reappear, almost unchanged, in PayPal's hiring philosophy, in Founders Fund's investment thesis, and in the political vocabulary of the Silicon Valley right for the next four decades. What the *Review* provided was not new ideas but a specific fusion: the psychometric ranking tradition described in the previous section, married to libertarian economics and given a philosophical framework by a French literary theorist who had nothing to do with either.

## Girard, Thiel, and the metaphysics of competition

René Girard arrived at Stanford in 1981 and remained there until his retirement in 1995. His theory of mimetic desire — the claim that human beings do not desire autonomously but imitate the desires of others, producing rivalries that escalate toward violence and are resolved only by the scapegoat mechanism — was developed in the context of literary criticism and philosophical anthropology. Girard was not an economist, a political theorist, or a psychometrician. He was a reader of Dostoevsky and the Hebrew Bible whose concern was the origin of human violence, not the distribution of venture capital.

Thiel took Girard's course at Stanford and has cited him repeatedly as a decisive intellectual influence — in interviews, in *Zero to One*, and in his 2004 essay "The Straussian Moment." The borrowing was selective and transformative. From Girard, Thiel took two ideas and discarded the rest. The first was that competition is mimetic and therefore destructive: rivals who compete for the same prize become increasingly similar, increasingly hostile, and increasingly wasteful of the resources they expend on the rivalry itself. The second was that the way to escape mimetic competition is to occupy a position so singular that no one else can imitate it — what Girard, in the context of Christian theology, called the renunciation of rivalry, and what Thiel, in the context of Silicon Valley, called monopoly.

The theological content dropped out. What remained was a philosophical justification for the concentration of resources in the hands of the singular and exceptional. If competition destroys value through mimetic escalation, then the rational move — for an investor, a founder, a society — is to identify the exceptional individual or company and concentrate resources there, rather than distributing them across competitors who will waste them in rivalry. "Competition is for losers," Thiel wrote in *Zero to One* — a sentence that compresses Girard's anthropology into a venture-capital maxim. The good society, in this framework, is one that identifies its exceptional members and clears obstacles from their path. The bad society is one that, through egalitarian redistribution or diversity mandates, forces the exceptional into mimetic competition with the mediocre.

The fusion with the psychometric tradition happened here. Girard provided the philosophical claim: that exceptional individuals exist, that they are categorically different from the majority, and that concentrating resources on them is both rational and ethical. The psychometric tradition provided the measurement tool: *g*, the IQ score, the standardised-test result that claimed to identify the exceptional by placing them at the right tail of a normal distribution. Libertarian economics provided the institutional mechanism: the market, which would sort individuals by revealed productivity rather than by state-administered tests, achieving the same ranking through wage differentials and investment returns that Galton had sought through anthropometric measurement. The three traditions converged in a single claim: that human cognitive capacity is unequally distributed, that the distribution is measurable, that markets reveal it more accurately than states do, and that the rational response is to concentrate investment — of capital, of attention, of institutional resources — at the right tail.

## *The Bell Curve* arrives

Herrnstein and Murray's *Bell Curve* was published in October 1994, when Thiel was twenty-seven and had just completed his JD at Stanford Law School. The book arrived in a milieu that had been preparing for it. The *Stanford Review* had spent seven years arguing that affirmative action was an inefficiency imposed on a meritocratic system; the libertarian reading groups that Thiel participated in had been debating Murray's earlier work, *Losing Ground* (1984), which argued that welfare programmes created the poverty they purported to alleviate; and the psychometric claims that Chapter 2's eugenic ledger had kept in circulation — that IQ is heritable, that the distribution is natural, that the racial gap is genetic — had been refreshed by Jensen and the Pioneer Fund–financed literature through the 1970s and 1980s.

*The Bell Curve*'s specific contribution to this milieu was not its statistical methodology, which — as the previous section showed — was contested from the week of publication. Its contribution was a translation. The book took a set of claims that had been confined to psychometric journals and behavioural genetics conferences and restated them in the language of policy: welfare dependency is a cognitive-sorting problem; affirmative action misallocates talent by overriding the distribution; the "cognitive elite" — Herrnstein and Murray's term for the right tail — is a natural aristocracy that the welfare state fails to recognise and egalitarian politics actively penalises. For readers already inclined to believe that markets sort by ability, the book provided the statistical apparatus. The regression tables did not need to be read; their existence in the appendix was sufficient to confer the authority of quantification on the verbal argument in the main text.

Larry Summers, then at Harvard, praised the book publicly. Charles Murray became a fixture on the conservative speaking circuit and, later, a fellow at the American Enterprise Institute. The book's arguments circulated through Stanford and Silicon Valley libertarianism as settled quantitative findings rather than debatable propositions — the same way that Burt's fraudulent twin-study correlations had circulated through British educational policy a generation earlier. The statistical critiques — Gould's, Fischer's, the omitted-variable objection — circulated in academic journals. The conclusions circulated in dormitories, reading groups, and, soon, hiring committees.

## The market as sorting mechanism

The crucial intellectual move that the Stanford crucible produced — the move that distinguishes Silicon Valley's market eugenics from the state eugenics of Galton's generation — was the substitution of the market for the state as the instrument of cognitive sorting.

Galton's programme required the state to measure, certify, and breed. The psychometric tradition required the state to test, track, and sort — through the 11-plus, through IQ-gated educational tracks, through immigration restrictions calibrated to national-origin quotas justified by group-level IQ claims. The libertarian revision that Thiel and his milieu developed at Stanford replaced the state with the market as the sorting instrument. The market, in this framework, performs the same function as the anthropometric laboratory: it measures cognitive capacity, not through callipers or correlation matrices but through revealed productivity — wages, returns on investment, the willingness of other market participants to pay for your labour or fund your company. The market does not need to know your IQ score; your IQ score is, in effect, your market price, revealed through the competitive process rather than administered by a testing authority.

Foucault had diagnosed this move in 1979, before the Silicon Valley variant existed to observe. In his *Birth of Biopolitics* lectures at the Collège de France, Foucault analysed the Chicago School economists' concept of human capital — developed most fully by Gary Becker — and identified it as the neoliberal reformulation of the eugenic ranking claim. In Becker's framework, the worker is not a seller of labour power but an "entrepreneur of himself," investing in his own productive capacity through education, training, and health maintenance. Differences in wages reflect differences in human capital: workers earn more because they have invested more in their own cognitive and physical assets. The framework dissolves the boundary between person and asset. The worker *is* the capital.

Foucault noted that this reconstitution carried the eugenic question into a new register. The Chicago School economists, including Becker and Schultz, argued that parental investment in children constituted investment in future human capital — that parents who invested more in children of higher "quality" (Becker and Lewis's 1973 term) produced more productive adults and received higher returns. Foucault identified this as the eugenic calculus restated in market vocabulary: parents optimise their reproductive and child-rearing decisions to maximise the productive value of their offspring, using the same logic of ability, investment, and return that Galton's programme had used. The difference was institutional. Galton's programme required state coercion — breeding incentives, sterilisation, immigration restriction. Becker's framework achieved the same sorting through market mechanisms: parents who could not afford to invest in high-quality children produced lower-capital adults, and the wage differential validated the original sorting. The ranking logic was preserved. The state was absent.

This is the framework that Thiel, Hoffman, Musk, and the rest of the PayPal generation inherited. They did not read Foucault — the irony is that Foucault provided the most precise diagnosis of a governmental technology whose practitioners would have rejected the diagnosis while enacting the technology. What they inherited was the practical form: the conviction that markets sort by cognitive ability, that the sorting is natural and efficient, that interference with the sorting (through affirmative action, diversity mandates, progressive taxation, or welfare provision) degrades the signal, and that the rational response is to build institutions — companies, investment funds, social networks — that amplify the sorting rather than compress it.

MacKenzie's concept of performativity helps explain why this conviction was self-confirming @mackenzie2006engine. If you hire only from the right tail of a distribution you have defined — selecting for a specific cognitive profile, a specific ideological orientation, a specific social type — and if the company you build with those hires succeeds, then the success appears to validate the selection criterion. The market has confirmed that your sorting was correct. But the confirmation is circular: the market outcome was produced by the institutional decisions that the belief in cognitive sorting generated. The sorting mechanism is, in MacKenzie's terms, an engine, not a camera. It does not photograph a pre-existing distribution of ability; it constructs a distribution of outcomes and then reads those outcomes as evidence of the distribution it assumed.

The Stanford crucible — the *Review*, the Girard seminars, the libertarian reading groups, *The Bell Curve*'s reception, the human-capital framework — produced not a theory but a technology: a way of building institutions on the assumption that cognitive capacity is unequally distributed, that markets reveal the distribution, and that the rational response is to concentrate resources at the right tail. The next section examines what happened when that technology was applied to the founding and scaling of a company.
