# 4. Neoreaction as Control Theory: Yarvin, Land, and the Cathedral

In 2007, a programmer named Curtis Yarvin began publishing a blog under the pseudonym Mencius Moldbug. The blog, *Unqualified Reservations*, ran to hundreds of thousands of words over seven years. It is verbose, digressive, deliberately provocative, and architecturally coherent. Stripped of its rhetorical excess, its argument is a political application of the cybernetic framework that Chapter 5 traced from Wiener through the Macy Conferences to RAND: the state is a control system; democracy is a corrupted feedback loop; and the solution is to replace the corrupted controller with a clean one. Yarvin called the corrupted controller "the Cathedral." Peter Thiel funded his next project. The connection between the two men is the connection between a political theory and the capital to operationalise it.

## The Cathedral as cybernetic critique

Yarvin's central concept — the Cathedral — denotes the interlocking complex of universities, mainstream media, the civil service, and progressive NGOs that, in his account, constitutes the real governing apparatus of Western democracies. The term is meant as a precise structural claim, not a metaphor. The Cathedral, in Yarvin's model, is a distributed control system that generates the ideological parameters within which electoral politics operates. It determines which questions are permissible, which policy options are thinkable, and which personnel are eligible for positions of institutional authority. Democratic elections, in this account, select among candidates whose positions have already been constrained by the Cathedral's epistemic control. The voter chooses; the Cathedral determines the menu.

The vocabulary is cybernetic, and deliberately so. Yarvin models the Cathedral as a feedback system with specific pathologies: it selects for a class of administrators (his term is "Brahmins") who share progressive assumptions; these administrators produce the information environment (the academic literature, the journalistic narrative, the policy consensus) that shapes the next generation of administrators; the feedback loop is self-reinforcing, selecting ever more strongly for ideological conformity at each iteration. The system's error is structural: the Cathedral optimises for its own reproduction rather than for governance quality, generating signals that push the state away from its efficient-governance optimum. In control-theory terms, the Cathedral is a controller that has been captured by its own noise.

The solution Yarvin proposes — "neocameralism" — follows the cybernetic logic to its conclusion. If the Cathedral is a corrupted controller, replace it with a clean one. Yarvin's clean controller is the sovereign corporation: a state governed as a joint-stock company, with a CEO rather than an elected executive, shareholders rather than voters, and citizens as contractual subjects who receive services in exchange for compliance rather than as democratic participants who determine policy through deliberation. The model is not hypothetical. Yarvin explicitly compares neocameralism to the governance structures of Singapore, Dubai, and the early modern chartered companies — polities governed by executive authority for the benefit of their stakeholders, without the noise introduced by democratic feedback.

The intellectual debt to Chapter 5's cybernetics is direct. Wiener's cybernetics modelled governance as a control problem: the state receives information about the population, processes it through a feedback loop, and adjusts its outputs to minimise deviation from a target function. The RAND Corporation's operations research, traced in Chapter 6, applied this framework to specific policy domains — defence, welfare, urban planning — treating each as an optimisation problem with a measurable objective function. Yarvin's move was to apply the same framework to democratic governance itself and declare the feedback loop broken. Democracy, in his model, is RAND's objective-function thinking applied to politics and found wanting: the objective function (efficient governance) is being overridden by the noise of democratic participation, interest-group capture, and ideological signalling. The correct response, as in any corrupted control system, is to redesign the controller.

## Nick Land and the accelerationist extension

If Yarvin provided neoreaction with its institutional critique and its alternative governance model, Nick Land provided its philosophy of history — and, with it, the argument that made the welfare state not merely inefficient but cosmically wrong.

Land had been a philosophy lecturer at the University of Warwick from 1987 to 1998, where he co-founded the Cybernetic Culture Research Unit (CCRU) with Sadie Plant in 1995. The CCRU's intellectual project fused Deleuze and Guattari's philosophy of capital-as-deterritorialisation with cybernetics, science fiction, and what Land called "hyperstition" — ideas that, once circulating, produce the reality they describe. The concept matters for this chapter because it is a variant of MacKenzie's performativity: hyperstitions are engines, not cameras, ideas that construct the world they claim to analyse. Land's early work was formally experimental, written in a deliberate fever-pitch that made it easy to dismiss. The ideas were harder to dismiss.

Land's central claim, developed across the CCRU texts and stated most systematically in *The Dark Enlightenment* (2013), is that capital operates as an autonomous, self-intensifying process that continuously dissolves stable social forms — nations, classes, communities, welfare states — in pursuit of its own acceleration. This is Deleuze and Guattari's *Anti-Oedipus* stripped of its emancipatory politics. Where Deleuze and Guattari saw deterritorialisation as containing the potential for new forms of collective life, Land sees it as a process that has no destination other than its own intensification: capital accelerates, institutions that resist acceleration are dissolved, and the endpoint — if there is one — is a techno-capital singularity that no human political programme can prevent or direct.

The political conclusion is accelerationism: if capital's dissolution of social forms is the fundamental dynamic of modernity, the correct response is to remove all friction from the process rather than to cushion its effects. The welfare state, in this framework, is a deceleration mechanism. It absorbs the destructive energy of market exposure, protects individuals from the consequences of economic dislocation, and thereby slows the process by which capital reaches its optimum. From an accelerationist perspective, welfare is wrong in the same way that a governor on an engine is wrong — it limits output. The suffering produced by the removal of welfare protections is, in Land's framework, the friction of a system finding its attractor. It is not a problem to be solved. It is the cost of correct operation.

This is where the chapter's argument connects most directly to the book's central concern. *Counting Lives* traces how statistical tools are used to govern populations — to count, sort, rank, include, exclude. Land's accelerationism is the logical terminus of the trajectory the book has been tracing: if statistical sorting produces a hierarchy, and if the hierarchy reflects a real distribution of cognitive or productive capacity, then the institutional apparatus that protects people at the bottom of the hierarchy — the welfare state, the poverty line, the benefit system — is an impediment to the system's optimal operation. The poor are not a population to be measured and supported. They are friction to be removed.

## The pipeline from blog to executive branch

For a decade, Yarvin's and Land's ideas circulated primarily among a small online readership — the neoreactionary (NRx) network that overlapped with the rationalist community on LessWrong, the Silicon Valley hard right, and the subscriber base of Thiel-adjacent publications. The intellectual connections were dense: Yarvin's Cathedral thesis influenced the vocabulary of the technology right; Land's accelerationism provided the theoretical frame for the "techno-optimist manifesto" genre; and the practical conclusion — that democratic institutions should be bypassed rather than reformed — informed the political strategy of Thiel and his network from 2010 onward.

Thiel's 2009 essay "The Education of a Libertarian," published in *Cato Unbound*, declared that "I no longer believe that freedom and democracy are compatible." The sentence reads as a direct condensation of Yarvin's Cathedral thesis: if democratic institutions have been captured by the Cathedral, then the pursuit of freedom (defined as market freedom, freedom from redistributive taxation, freedom from regulatory constraint) requires the bypass of democratic accountability. Thiel's subsequent political investments followed this logic: funding Yarvin's Urbit project (a decentralised computing platform designed to operate outside Cathedral-controlled infrastructure); backing JD Vance's Senate campaign and subsequent political career; and supporting the constellation of organisations and individuals that, by 2025, constituted the institutional apparatus of the Thiel network within the Republican Party.

The Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) project in the second Trump administration brought the neocameralist programme into operational practice. Musk and Thiel-network figures moved to rapidly dismantle federal agencies, reduce civil-service protections, and concentrate executive authority in ways that map directly onto Yarvin's blueprint: replace the Cathedral's distributed administrative state with a lean executive accountable to its principals rather than to democratic process. The intellectual genealogy — from Wiener's cybernetics through RAND's objective functions through Yarvin's Cathedral critique to executive-branch operational practice — is traceable and specific. It is also the genealogy that connects this chapter's Silicon Valley ideology to the welfare-state dismantlement that the book's later chapters document: if the administrative state is a corrupted controller and the welfare system is a deceleration mechanism, then the rational programme is to dismantle both and let the market perform the sorting that the state was too captured to accomplish.

## Suggested Expansions (for second pass)

**Current word count: ~2,300. Target: ~5,500. Needs ~3,200 words.**

1. **Yarvin's key posts — close reading.** The draft summarises Yarvin's arguments but does not closely read specific passages. "A Gentle Introduction to Unqualified Reservations" (2009) and "How I Stopped Believing in Democracy" (2008) deserve direct quotation and analysis — particularly the passages that use explicitly cybernetic vocabulary (feedback, signal, noise, controller, optimum), which would make the Ch05 connection concrete rather than asserted.

2. **The CCRU at Warwick — scene.** Land's institutional context at Warwick in the 1990s could be grounded: the CCRU's location (a basement seminar room), its key participants (Sadie Plant, Mark Fisher, Kodwo Eshun), the specific texts discussed, and the moment when Land's philosophy of acceleration tipped from theoretical provocation into political programme. Fisher's later break with Land — Fisher's *Capitalist Realism* (2009) takes the opposite political conclusion from the same diagnosis — is analytically productive.

3. **Thiel's "Education of a Libertarian" — close reading.** The 2009 Cato Unbound essay deserves more than a single sentence. Thiel's specific claims — that women's suffrage was a mistake, that the future of freedom lay in "new and hitherto untried" spaces (seasteading, cyberspace, outer space) — would sharpen the portrait of his politics and connect to the gender thread.

4. **DOGE specifics.** The draft mentions DOGE in general terms. Specific agencies targeted, specific civil-service protections removed, and specific consequences for welfare claimants would ground the argument in the material consequences the book is concerned with. The connection to Ch10 (risk scores) and Ch11 (Palantir) should be made explicit.

5. **The LessWrong/NRx overlap.** The rationalist community's relationship with neoreaction deserves a paragraph: how Eliezer Yudkowsky's rationalist framework (Bayesian reasoning, expected-utility maximisation, AI risk) provided the intellectual infrastructure through which neoreactionary ideas circulated, and how the overlap between LessWrong, the NRx blogosphere, and the EA movement connects this section to Ch08.

6. **Land's *Dark Enlightenment* — the HBD section.** Land's essay includes a section on "human biodiversity" (HBD) that explicitly invokes the psychometric tradition and argues that racial IQ differences are genetically determined. This connects directly to Section 1's Bell Curve material and should be quoted — it is the point at which Land's philosophical accelerationism meets the eugenic ranking tradition most explicitly.

7. **The Singapore/Dubai model — interrogate.** Yarvin cites Singapore and Dubai as examples of neocameralist governance. A paragraph noting the specific features of these states that Yarvin admires — and the specific features he omits (Singapore's extensive public housing, Dubai's dependence on migrant labour without citizenship rights) — would expose the selectiveness of the comparison and connect to the book's concern with who is counted and who is excluded.
