# 3. PayPal and the Meritocracy Machine

In December 1998, Peter Thiel and Max Levchin incorporated Confinity in Palo Alto. The company that became PayPal was, from its first hires, a deliberate experiment in the proposition that had been rehearsed at the *Stanford Review* and confirmed, in the minds of its founders, by *The Bell Curve*: that concentrating individuals from the right tail of a cognitive distribution would produce disproportionate returns. The hiring was conducted accordingly.

## The same kind of nerd

Thiel's account in *Zero to One* describes the early PayPal team as "the same kind of nerd." The phrase is worth pausing over. Levchin, Thiel later recounted, believed that the earliest employees should be "as personally similar as possible" — the justification offered was operational efficiency (a small team moves faster when its members share assumptions), but the criterion applied was social and ideological rather than narrowly technical. The hires shared a specific cultural profile: they read Neal Stephenson's *Cryptonomicon*; they preferred, in Thiel's telling, "the capitalist *Star Wars* to the communist *Star Trek*"; they had dropped out of or deferred from graduate programmes in mathematics, physics, or computer science at elite universities; and they shared a libertarian orientation toward monetary policy that made an internet payments company feel like a political project as well as a commercial one.

The sorting criterion was not IQ — no test was administered. It was something more diffuse and more effective: a social-type filter that selected for a specific conjunction of cognitive style, political orientation, institutional pedigree, and cultural taste. Thiel later made the operative criterion explicit in a 2014 talk at Columbia University: "Could we be friends with these people?" was the actual hiring question. The question sounds informal, even whimsical. In practice, it was a homogeneity filter with demographic consequences. The people Thiel and Levchin could imagine being friends with — people who shared their educational backgrounds, their reading lists, their political assumptions, their social networks — were overwhelmingly male, overwhelmingly white or Asian, overwhelmingly products of a handful of universities, and overwhelmingly of a specific class origin. The filter did not name these categories. It did not need to. By selecting for a social type defined by cultural consumption, ideological alignment, and institutional origin, it achieved demographic homogeneity as a structural consequence of the sorting mechanism rather than as an explicit policy.

This is the point at which Hacking's concept of making up people becomes analytically precise @hacking1986making. The PayPal hiring filter did not identify a pre-existing natural kind — "the high-IQ engineer" — and select from it. It constructed a category — "the same kind of nerd" — through the act of selection, and then treated the category as if it had been discovered rather than invented. The category was defined by its boundaries: not merely smart but smart in a specific way; not merely technical but technical with specific cultural and political commitments; not merely productive but productive within a framework that treated ideological homogeneity as a performance-enhancing feature. Once constructed, the category acquired the appearance of a natural kind, because the people selected through it shared visible characteristics that could be retrospectively attributed to a common underlying quality — the same move Spearman had made when he extracted *g* from a correlation matrix and declared it to be real.

## Diversity as noise

The intellectual framework behind the PayPal hiring philosophy treated diversity — of background, experience, perspective, demographic identity — as statistical noise that degraded signal quality. The logic followed directly from the *g*-factor argument: if performance is primarily determined by a single latent cognitive variable, then variation in other dimensions (gender, race, disciplinary background, life experience) is irrelevant to performance and potentially harmful to cohesion. The optimal team, in this framework, is a team with maximum variance on *g* (everyone at the right tail) and minimum variance on everything else (everyone the same kind of nerd).

Thiel's language in *Zero to One* reinforces this: each PayPal employee was to be responsible for "just one thing," evaluated solely on a single performance metric. The managerial structure mirrored the psychometric logic. Just as Spearman's factor analysis reduced the complexity of cognitive variation to a single ranked dimension, PayPal's organisational design reduced the complexity of each employee's contribution to a single measurable output. The individual worker was, in Foucault's terms, reconstituted as an enterprise of one: a self-optimising unit legible to the organisation only through a single variable, competing not against other workers in a labour market but against their own potential as a capital asset. The framework dissolved the distinction between worker and investment. Each hire was a bet — the same kind of bet that Thiel's subsequent venture-capital career would make on companies — that concentrating resources at the right tail of a distribution would produce returns that followed the power law rather than the normal curve.

The empirical literature on team performance tells a different story. Research on collective intelligence — the capacity of groups to perform well across diverse cognitive tasks — has consistently found that the best predictor of group performance is not the average or maximum IQ of group members but the diversity of cognitive styles within the group, combined with conversational turn-taking and social perceptiveness. Homogeneous high-IQ groups perform well on tasks that resemble IQ tests; diverse groups outperform them on complex, ill-defined problems that require multiple frames of reference. The PayPal philosophy, in other words, was optimising for a specific kind of task — fast execution on a well-defined technical problem — by selecting for the *g*-factor argument's prediction, while ignoring the conditions under which that prediction fails. The company's success validated the philosophy in the eyes of its founders. The validation was indistinguishable from survivorship bias: the company succeeded, the hiring philosophy was unusual, therefore the hiring philosophy caused the success. The alternative explanation — that a well-funded company in a winner-take-all market with strong network effects would have succeeded with a range of hiring philosophies — was not considered, because it did not fit the theory.

## The Mafia as power-law amplifier

PayPal was acquired by eBay in October 2002 for $1.5 billion. The acquisition was financially significant; the network that emerged from it was transformative in a different sense. The "PayPal Mafia" — the group of approximately twenty founding and early employees who left eBay and went on to found or fund the next generation of Silicon Valley companies — became, in the decade after the acquisition, the most concentrated node of power in the technology industry.

The list is familiar: Thiel founded Founders Fund and Palantir; Elon Musk founded SpaceX and became CEO of Tesla; Reid Hoffman founded LinkedIn; Steve Chen, Chad Hurley, and Jawed Karim founded YouTube; Jeremy Stoppelman and Russel Simmons founded Yelp; David Sacks founded Yammer and later became COO of Zenefits; Keith Rabois became a partner at Khosla Ventures and later at Founders Fund. The 2007 *Fortune* photograph of the group — posed in silk suits and dark glasses, captioned "the PayPal Mafia" — became the iconic image of the network's self-presentation: a fraternity of overlapping investments, board seats, introductions, and hiring recommendations that operated as a private credentialling system parallel to and more powerful than the formal institutions (universities, professional associations, regulatory bodies) that credentialled the rest of the economy.

The mathematical structure of this network is a power-law amplifier. Each member of the group, having been selected through the PayPal filter, applied the same filter — or a filter calibrated to the same social type — to their own hiring and investment decisions. Thiel's Founders Fund preferentially funded founders referred through the PayPal network. Musk's companies drew from the same elite-institution, libertarian-oriented talent pool. Hoffman's LinkedIn was built partly to formalise and scale the network's self-referential circulation of opportunities. The mechanism was compounding: each successful company founded by a PayPal alumnus validated the network's sorting criterion, attracted new entrants who matched the social type, and generated capital that was reinvested through the same network. The result, over two decades, was a feedback loop in which an initial sorting decision — made by Thiel and Levchin in a Palo Alto office in 1999, using the "could we be friends" criterion — compounded into a self-reinforcing hierarchy of access, capital, and institutional power.

The compounding operated in both directions. Early inclusion in the network conferred geometric advantage: access to deal flow, introductions to investors, credibility with potential hires, invitations to the conferences and dinners where the next generation of investments was discussed. Early exclusion was effectively permanent. If you were not selected through the PayPal filter — if you were not the right kind of nerd, did not share the ideological orientation, did not come from the right institutional background — you were excluded not merely from one company but from the entire self-reinforcing network that company generated. The exclusion did not announce itself as exclusion. It announced itself as a market outcome: you were not funded because your company was not good enough; you were not hired because you did not meet the bar. The language of meritocracy ensured that the sorting mechanism was invisible to those it sorted against, because the criterion — "the same kind of nerd," "could we be friends" — was framed as a quality signal rather than a social filter.

This is the structure the chapter is arguing produces the same result as state eugenics through different institutional means. Galton's programme required the state to identify and concentrate superior stock through measurement, certification, and breeding incentives. The PayPal Mafia achieved the same concentration through a private credentialling system: initial selection by social type, compounding through network effects, exclusion of the non-selected through the withdrawal of access rather than the coercion of the state. The mechanism was voluntary. The result — a self-reinforcing hierarchy legitimated by the language of natural ability — was structurally identical.

## Suggested Expansions (for second pass)

**Current word count: ~2,400. Target: ~5,500. Needs ~3,100 words.**

1. **Specific founding hires.** The draft discusses the PayPal filter in abstract terms. Naming two or three specific founding team members — their educational backgrounds, how they were recruited, the specific criterion that selected them — would make the sorting mechanism concrete. Levchin's recruitment of engineers from the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign (his own alma mater) is a documented case of the homogeneity filter at work.

2. **The Fortune photograph.** The 2007 Fortune shoot could be expanded into a brief scene — who was there, how the group presented itself, the deliberate self-mythologisation that the image represents. The contrast between the silk-suit image and the "same kind of nerd" self-description is itself analytically productive.

3. **Power-law mathematics — worked example.** The section claims the PayPal Mafia operates as a power-law amplifier but does not show the mathematics. A brief worked example — tracing how Thiel's initial investment in Facebook (2004, $500,000 for 10.2%) compounded through the network into Founders Fund's subsequent investments — would ground the power-law claim in specific dollar figures and show the compounding mechanism concretely.

4. **The collective-intelligence literature — citekeys.** The paragraph on team diversity cites "research on collective intelligence" without specific sources. Woolley et al. (2010) on collective intelligence and Anita Williams Woolley's subsequent work should be cited. Page (2007), *The Difference*, on diversity and problem-solving is also directly relevant.

5. **Hacking's looping effect — extend.** The Hacking paragraph establishes that the PayPal filter constructed a category. A second paragraph could trace the looping effect: how the category "the same kind of nerd" changed the people it classified — how being selected into the PayPal Mafia reshaped alumni's self-understanding, career trajectories, and subsequent hiring decisions, producing the feedback loop Hacking describes.

6. **Concrete exclusion cases.** The section argues that exclusion from the network was effectively permanent. A specific case — a company or founder who was outside the PayPal network and experienced the capital-access consequences — would ground this claim materially. The contrast between PayPal-network-adjacent founders and non-network founders in the same market segment would be especially telling.

7. **The eBay acquisition and the "exit" as sorting event.** The $1.5 billion eBay acquisition could be expanded: how was the payout distributed? Did the power-law logic apply to equity stakes within PayPal itself? The internal equity distribution would show whether the sorting philosophy was applied to compensation as well as hiring.
