# Section 3: The System Dynamics of Poverty: Forrester and Urban Dynamics

In 1969, Jay Forrester published *Urban Dynamics*. He was a professor at MIT's Sloan School of Management, the inventor of system dynamics as a formal discipline, and a man with no prior expertise in urban poverty, housing policy, or the sociology of American cities. He brought to the problem a simulation language called DYNAMO, a set of differential equations representing the rates of change of population stocks and flows in a generic urban system, and the conviction — which the Macy Conferences had made intellectually available to his entire generation — that any complex self-regulating system could be adequately modelled by specifying its feedback loops.

The book's central argument was that public housing construction and welfare transfer payments were "positive feedback loops" that made urban poverty worse. By attracting more low-income migrants to a city than the economy could absorb, they perpetuated the very conditions they were intended to relieve. The "optimal" policy, as the model's simulations demonstrated, was to demolish the low-income housing stock and replace it with housing attractive to managerial and professional workers, thereby drawing in the businesses and tax revenue that would eventually benefit everyone. The poor, on this analysis, were not a population with material needs that the city was failing to meet. They were a variable in a feedback system that was currently miscalibrated, and the way to recalibrate it was to reduce the inputs that were drawing them in.

Daniel Patrick Moynihan, then serving as Richard Nixon's urban affairs advisor, called the book "a remarkable work." The Boston Redevelopment Authority engaged with its conclusions. The House Subcommittee on Urban Growth held hearings on its policy implications. *Urban Dynamics* did not determine the trajectory of American housing policy in the 1970s by itself — Nixon's simultaneous dismantling of the Model Cities programme, the FHA's ongoing redlining, and the political economy of white suburbanisation were all larger forces — but it provided a mathematically credentialled version of arguments that were already doing political work by other means. What made it useful to that politics was precisely what made it interesting as a case study for this book: the model's conclusions were not the findings of an empirical investigation. They were the assumptions of the model made visible in simulation form.

---

System dynamics works by representing a social or physical system as a network of stocks, flows, and feedback loops. Stocks are accumulated quantities — Forrester's urban model had stocks for three classes of residents, nine categories of housing, and eight categories of business and industry. Flows are the rates at which stocks change — births, deaths, construction, demolition, migration, upward mobility. Feedback loops are the relationships through which a change in one variable causes changes that eventually return to affect the original variable: a growing business attracts workers, who attract services, which attract more businesses (positive feedback, amplifying change); or rising house prices reduce demand, which reduces prices (negative feedback, damping change). The modeller's task is to specify which stocks, flows, and feedback relationships the system contains, and what the values of the model's parameters are.

Forrester's urban model used three population classes. At the top were the "managerial-professional" residents — high-income workers in professional and management roles, whose presence in a city was understood to attract investment and generate tax revenue. Below them were the "labour" class — medium-income skilled and semi-skilled workers. At the bottom were the "underemployed" — low-income workers in unskilled employment, unemployed people, and welfare recipients, grouped together in a single model variable. The feedback loop that generated the book's controversial conclusions worked as follows. Low-income housing provision attracted in-migration from the underemployed population outside the city. Increased underemployment crowded the labour market, reducing wage growth and slowing upward mobility from the underemployed to the labour class. Reduced upward mobility increased demand for welfare services. Increased welfare services attracted more in-migration. The loop closed without any negative-feedback damper — it was, by construction, a positive feedback loop that amplified the underemployed population indefinitely in response to any increase in housing or welfare provision.

The simulation produced its headline conclusions from this architecture. Increase the low-income housing stock, and the model showed more underemployed residents and a declining city economy. Increase welfare payments, and the model showed the underemployed remaining underemployed for longer. Demolish the slum housing and build for managerial-professional residents, and the model showed economic revival, increased tax revenue, and — eventually — more employment opportunities for the remaining poor. This was the "optimal" policy: what the feedback system's target state required.

---

What the model did not contain is as significant as what it did.

*Urban Dynamics* had no variable for racial discrimination in housing markets. By 1969, the evidence that racial discrimination was a structural determinant of American urban poverty was not contested: Charles Abrams had documented it in *The City Is the Frontier* (1965); the Kerner Commission's report, published just a year before *Urban Dynamics*, had stated directly that "white institutions created" the ghetto, "white institutions maintain it, and white society condones it." Redlining — the systematic denial of mortgage credit to residents of Black urban neighbourhoods — had been documented by federal housing researchers since the 1930s. None of this appeared in Forrester's model. The variable "underemployed" aggregated Black and white residents in low-income employment, without any feedback relationship representing the differential access to housing credit, employment, or upward mobility that separated them.

The model also contained no variable for labour market monopsony or employer wage suppression; no variable for differential school quality between neighbourhoods; no variable for the cost or availability of transport between low-income residential areas and employment centres. These were not obscure factors. By 1969 they were the primary mechanisms that sociologists, urban economists, and housing researchers had identified as the structural causes of urban poverty. Their absence from the model was a methodological choice, and it was a choice with a predetermined consequence: if the structural causes of poverty are not in the model, the model cannot find them. What remains — the decision of the underemployed to move to a city offering welfare services and low-income housing — becomes, by default, the sole driver of the poverty equilibrium.

This is what the book's research note calls the tautology problem. Forrester's model produced conservative policy conclusions because conservative assumptions were embedded in its feedback structure. The underemployed population's migration response to housing and welfare was specified as large and fast; the rate of upward mobility from underemployed to labour was specified as slow and weakly responsive to intervention; the model's "target state" — the definition of a revived city — was measured by the ratio of managerial-professional to underemployed residents. Each of these specifications was a claim about how poverty works, and each claim was contested at the time. The simulation made those claims look like findings. It converted contested political assumptions into the output of a technical procedure, and it is precisely this conversion — assumption-to-finding, via formal model — that Porter identifies as the characteristic political function of quantification: the transfer of political argument into an arena where it can no longer be contested as political [@porter1995trust].

---

The conference reception of *Urban Dynamics* in Boston in 1969 makes the political logic visible. Several of Forrester's MIT colleagues mounted detailed technical criticisms. The American Institute of Planners was sceptical. The sociological literature on race, housing discrimination, and urban poverty — all of which the model had excluded — was not silent. But Moynihan's endorsement gave the book a political purchase that the technical criticisms could not easily dislodge: *Urban Dynamics* was now a mathematically-dressed version of arguments that had been circulating in policy circles as mere opinion, and mathematical dress is, as Porter argues, a form of authority that survives the rebuttal of its technical foundations because its authority is institutional rather than epistemological. The model's conclusions looked like results. Results require further modelling to rebut, not just argument.

The fullest technical reckoning came four years later, in 1973, when Douglass B. Lee Jr. published "Requiem for Large-Scale Models" in the *Journal of the American Institute of Planners*. Lee identified seven pathologies shared by *Urban Dynamics* and similar large-scale simulation projects: the models were hypercomprehensive (claiming to represent the whole system), gross (aggregating away the detail that matters), hungry (requiring more data than actually exists), wrongheaded (theoretically unsound), complicated (unintelligible to non-specialists and therefore unmodifiable), mechanical (treating contingent social relationships as fixed parameters), and expensive (consuming more resources than their value justified). *Urban Dynamics* displayed all seven. Lee's paper effectively ended the first wave of large-scale urban simulation modelling as a live policy instrument, but it ended it by demonstrating technical inadequacy — not by identifying what the models had done politically. The political work had already been done.

---

For the purposes of this chapter, *Urban Dynamics* demonstrates the cybernetic displacement of the Part I question in its most fully elaborated form.

Rowntree, walking the streets of York in 1899, was asking: what do these households need, and is what they have sufficient to provide it? Orshansky, constructing her poverty threshold in 1963 from the Agriculture Department's economy food plan, was asking: what does a family of this configuration require in order to eat adequately, and what income is needed to afford it? Both questions are addressed to the material circumstances of specific households. Both require attending to what is actually there — the food costs, the family configuration, the rent, the heating bill — before any modelling can begin. The person is the subject; the measurement is derived from the person.

Forrester's urban model inverts this relation entirely. The "underemployed" are not persons whose material requirements the model is attempting to characterise. They are a level variable — an accumulated stock in a system of differential equations — whose rate of change is determined by migration flows and mobility rates that are themselves functions of other model variables. Their needs do not appear in the model because needs are not the kind of thing that appears in system dynamics models. What appears in system dynamics models are stocks, flows, feedback coefficients, and target states. The city's "health" is defined by the ratio of managerial-professional to underemployed residents, not by whether the underemployed residents have adequate housing, food, or income. The target state is set by the modeller. The underemployed are whatever they are in relation to that state.

This is the cybernetic displacement at its most operational. The question "what do these people need?" has been replaced by the question "what correction to the system will move it toward the target state?" And the answer to the second question — demolish the low-income housing, restrict the welfare services — is directly contrary to any answer that the first question could generate, if the first question were allowed to be asked. Which, in Forrester's model, it cannot be. The model has no syntax for it.

Scott's argument about high-modernist statecraft is directly applicable here [@scott1998seeing]. The model makes the city legible by rendering it as a set of quantifiable stocks and flows — and in doing so, destroys the particularity of actual urban life that is, for Scott, both the source of practical knowledge and the target of high-modernist simplification. What the model cannot see — racial discrimination, employer wage suppression, differential school quality — is not absent from the city. It is absent from the model. And having been rendered absent from the model, it becomes, for the purposes of policy, absent altogether. The map, at this scale of ambition, does not describe the territory. It replaces it.

The Boston city planners who engaged with *Urban Dynamics* were not stupid. They were operating in a political environment in which a model that provided mathematical cover for cutting public housing budgets was useful, and a model that required confronting racial discrimination in the labour and housing markets was not. Forrester gave them the former. What he took from the Macy Conferences — the conviction that the same feedback framework that modelled thermostats and anti-aircraft guns could model a city's poverty — was not a technical contribution to urban policy. It was an ideological transfer: the authority of the natural sciences, brokered through a simulation that had no racism in it because its author had chosen not to put any in.
