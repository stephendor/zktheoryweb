# Section 2: Wiener's Warning and its Suppression

In 1947, Norbert Wiener wrote a letter to the editor of the *Atlantic Monthly* and then, apparently deciding that a letter was insufficient, expanded it into an article. The article, published in January 1947 under the title "A Scientist Rebels," was a public refusal. Wiener announced that he would no longer accept contracts from the military or from any institution connected to military research, and he explained why. The mathematical tools he had developed for fire-control and anti-aircraft prediction were, in his view, capable of being turned against civilian workers as readily as against enemy aircraft. He had seen enough of what happened when scientists handed over their work without asking what it would be used for. He was not going to do it again.

The article did not make him famous in the way that *Cybernetics* would. MIT did not respond to it. The NDRC did not respond to it. The management scientists who were already building the institutional machinery that would carry cybernetic logic into social policy read it, or did not, and continued. The *Atlantic Monthly* piece is, for the purposes of this book, less important as a public intervention than as evidence of what Wiener understood in 1947 that his colleagues at MIT, RAND, and the emerging management sciences either did not understand or chose not to examine. He had built a system for modelling a human being as a predictable mechanism. He knew, before almost anyone else, what that system could be used for — and he spent the rest of his career trying, unsuccessfully, to limit those uses.

The book *The Human Use of Human Beings*, published in 1950, was the systematic version of the warning. It is a strange, urgent text — part popular science, part political philosophy, part self-criticism — written by a man who understood that his own wartime mathematics had provided the conceptual engine for something he feared. Its argument was specific. Wiener was not merely warning that technology could be misused; he was arguing that the application of cybernetic feedback logic to the management of human labour was *inherently* a form of domination, not a neutral technique that might or might not be handled responsibly.

The argument ran as follows. A cybernetic control system, applied to a factory, measures the outputs of workers against a target rate, identifies deviations as error signals, and adjusts the inputs — wages, penalties, conditions, information — to bring the workers' behaviour back toward the target. This is formally identical to the feedback loop that governed the anti-aircraft predictor. The worker, in the control system, occupies the position that the enemy pilot occupied in the predictor: an entity whose future behaviour is to be estimated from past performance, corrected for deviation, and optimised toward a target state. What the worker needs — adequate pay, rest, dignity, security — does not appear in the system's model, any more than the enemy pilot's fear appeared in Wiener's radar equations. The feedback loop has no variable for need. It has a target state and an error signal, and the person is the source of the signal that the correction mechanism is designed to suppress.

"We have modified our environment so radically," Wiener wrote, "that we must now modify ourselves in order to exist in this new environment. We can no longer live in the old one." What he meant by this, in the specific context of the book's argument about automation and labour, was something darker than the paraphrase suggests. The new environment he was describing was one in which the mathematical tools of cybernetic control had been handed to employers, administrators, and governments. In that environment, the worker who could not or would not behave as the feedback system's model predicted — whose needs, illnesses, family circumstances, or acts of resistance introduced deviation into the control loop — was, by the system's own logic, noise. Noise was what the filter was designed to eliminate. The worker who registered as noise was the worker the system was designed to correct, remove, or discipline.

Wiener put the comparison directly: the system of factory automation and feedback-controlled labour management that was then being developed — and that he had helped to make mathematically possible — was a new form of slavery. It was slavery not in the metaphorical sense in which every form of unfree labour gets called slavery, but in the specific functional sense: a system in which the worker's behaviour was controlled by a feedback mechanism whose target state was set by someone else, and whose deviation from that target was met with correction rather than accommodation. The fact that the mechanism was mathematical rather than physical did not change its logic. The anti-aircraft predictor had been designed to destroy an enemy whose interiority could not be accessed. The factory feedback system was designed to optimise a worker whose interiority — needs, judgement, resistance — had been defined as irrelevant by the same mathematical move that had defined the pilot's interiority as irrelevant.

His colleagues proceeded anyway.

---

To understand why Wiener was ignored, it helps to look at what he was arguing against — and specifically at the technical disagreement with Shannon that underpinned his political critique.

At the centre of *The Human Use of Human Beings* is an argument about what information is. Shannon had defined information as the reduction of uncertainty in a message source: a purely statistical measure, deliberately stripped of semantic content. The "meaning" of a message was, for Shannon's purposes, irrelevant to its information content. A sentence and its translation into a foreign language carried the same information if they had the same statistical uncertainty properties, regardless of what either meant to anyone who read it. This was Shannon's semantic bracket — the decision, encoded in the mathematics, that meaning was outside the theory's scope.

Wiener's position was technically adjacent but philosophically incompatible. Where Shannon identified information with entropy — the thermodynamic measure of disorder — Wiener argued that information was *negentropy*: organised pattern actively resisting the tendency of physical systems toward disorder and disintegration. In Wiener's framework, information was not a neutral statistical measure but a force in the world, something closer to meaning, value, and life than to a probability distribution over message ensembles. "Information is our primary weapon," he wrote, "against nature's tendency to degrade the organised and to destroy the meaningful." For Wiener, the information-processing capacities of human beings were not just computationally equivalent to those of machines — they were the specific form in which human beings resisted entropy, maintained themselves as organised systems, and generated the meanings by which they lived.

This distinction was not merely philosophical. It had direct implications for what the cybernetic framework could and could not do to human beings. If information was Shannon's statistical measure, then a welfare claimant's case file and an anti-aircraft radar return were, formally, the same kind of thing: patterns of differences, carrying information in the sense of reducing uncertainty about what the transmitter would produce next, with no semantic content that the model was required to attend to. The claimant's needs, circumstances, and claims on the system were, by construction, outside the model. If information was Wiener's negentropy — organised pattern that carries meaning and resists disintegration — then a welfare claimant's case file was something different: the record of a person's attempt to maintain themselves as an organised system against the entropy that poverty represented. Need was not outside the model. Need was the model's subject.

Wiener was trying to restore the body to information theory at exactly the moment his own framework was being deployed to evacuate it. *The Human Use of Human Beings* is, in this reading, a sustained attempt to make the semantic bracket visible — to show that Shannon's decision to define meaning as irrelevant was a decision, historically specific and politically consequential, not a neutral technical necessity. Warren Weaver, co-publishing with Shannon in 1949, had attempted a similar rescue. His tripartite framework — the technical problem of accurate signal transmission, the semantic problem of conveying intended meaning, the "influential" problem of affecting the receiver's conduct — was designed to show that Shannon's layer was only one of three that a complete theory of communication would need. But Weaver's framework was a prose commentary appended to a mathematical paper. Only Shannon's first layer generated quantitative tools. The semantic and influential problems remained prose, and prose does not write welfare policy.

---

The political context of Wiener's failure is as specific as the technical one.

In 1947, the same year as the *Atlantic Monthly* article, Wiener met with Walter Reuther, the president of the United Auto Workers union, and with other trade union leaders, to discuss the implications of factory automation. He wanted them to understand what was coming: that the feedback-controlled manufacturing systems being developed at MIT and elsewhere were not simply more efficient machines but a reorganisation of the production process that would make large portions of the working class redundant, and that this redundancy was not an accident or a side effect but a consequence of the system's design logic. The machines were the correction mechanism; the workers were the deviation.

The FBI was monitoring his associations. The FBI had opened a file on Wiener in the early 1940s, partly because of his political connections and partly because of his contact with left-wing scientists. The surveillance continued through the 1950s. Wiener was aware of it, and it constrained what he was willing to do publicly, though it did not constrain what he was willing to write. What *The Human Use of Human Beings* amounts to, read in this context, is a public record of an argument that could not be made effectively in public — because the institutional infrastructure required to act on it (the unions, the political left, the academic independence that might have sheltered it) was being dismantled in parallel by McCarthyism.

Shannon, by contrast, continued at Bell Laboratories. McCulloch, the dominant figure at the Macy Conferences and the primary vector of cybernetics into institutional practice, had extensive connections to the military and intelligence establishment. The conceptual framework that flourished after 1950 was not Wiener's semantically troubled, politically anxious version of cybernetics but Shannon's clean, technically rigorous, militarily applicable version. RAND took it up. The management sciences at Carnegie Mellon — Simon's bounded rationality, March's organisational decision theory — were built on Shannon's information-theoretic foundations, not Wiener's. When the British National Health Service adopted systems analysis in the 1960s, and when the US Department of Health, Education and Welfare began applying PPBS budgeting to social programmes under McNamara's former colleagues from the Pentagon, they were applying Shannon's bracket, not Wiener's attempt to break it.

*The Human Use of Human Beings* has never been out of print. It is cited in the literature of technology ethics, in the literature of automation and labour, and — increasingly — in the literature of algorithmic governance. It has been read, discussed, and not acted on. This is, precisely, Wiener's position in the intellectual history this book is tracing: a dissenter whose analysis was accurate and whose warnings were absorbed into the tradition they were directed against. The book became a reference point; the feedback loop became welfare policy.

---

Wiener's position in *Counting Lives* is the position of the second internal dissenter, alongside Mollie Orshansky.

Both Wiener and Orshansky built the frameworks that made the systems they warned against possible. Wiener's wartime mathematics provided the conceptual engine for the cybernetic control systems he subsequently argued would constitute a new form of domination. Orshansky's 1963 poverty threshold provided the administrative measure that was subsequently simplified, stripped of its empirical grounding, and fixed in place through CPI indexing — against her explicit objection — as a bureaucratic convenience that bore no systematic relationship to what households actually needed. Both produced their work within institutions — MIT and the Social Security Administration — that had interests in deploying the work differently from the way its author intended. Both continued to argue, in print and in correspondence, against the uses to which their work was being put. Both lost.

The parallel runs deeper than the shared fate of individual dissent. Both Wiener and Orshansky were arguing that the person could not be reduced to their position in a formal system — that need, embodiment, and material circumstance were not incidental complications in the relevant model but its necessary subject matter. Wiener argued this through negentropy: information, properly understood, was organised pattern that carried meaning, and human beings were organised systems whose needs and meanings could not be subtracted from their behaviour without falsifying the description. Orshansky argued it through the food budget: the poverty threshold was a claim about what a specific household configuration actually needed to eat, and any measure that lost sight of the grocery receipt had lost its grip on the reality it was supposed to measure. One argument was about physics; the other was about food. But the structure of the dissent was the same in both cases, and so was the outcome.

In each case, the framework survived its author's objection. Cybernetics without Wiener's troubled semantics became the intellectual foundation of the management sciences, the welfare state's systems analysis, and eventually the algorithmic governance of benefits. The poverty threshold without Orshansky's empirical grounding became a fixed administrative line, periodically updated by inflation but never re-anchored to the food costs from which it had been derived. The tradition that flourished was the one both of them had warned against. And the tradition they had warned against is the one this book is tracing.
