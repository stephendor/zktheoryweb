# Section 1: From the Bomber to the Boardroom: The Military Birth of Cybernetics

On 21 March 1946, in a conference room in New York City, a group of men who had spent the previous six years solving problems of killing reconvened to discuss problems of living. The occasion was the first of what would become ten conferences on "Feedback Mechanisms and Circular Causal Systems in Biological and Social Systems," organised by the Josiah Macy Jr. Foundation and chaired by Warren McCulloch, a neuropsychiatrist who had spent the war years building mathematical models of the brain. Among those present were Norbert Wiener, who had designed a system for predicting the future position of enemy aircraft; Julian Bigelow, who had helped him build it; John von Neumann, who had spent the war on the Manhattan Project; and Gregory Bateson and Margaret Mead, anthropologists who had been analysing Axis propaganda and Japanese culture for the Office of War Information. What united this room was not a shared discipline but a shared mathematical language, one that had been forged under the pressure of war and that its inventors were now asking to do a different kind of work.

That language was cybernetics. And the question the first Macy Conference was attempting to answer — whether the same formal framework that described the behaviour of an anti-aircraft gun tracking an evading enemy aircraft could also describe the behaviour of a brain, a family, an economy, or a government — was not merely a question about the generality of a mathematical technique. It was a question about the nature of human beings: whether persons, like guns and thermostats, were best understood as systems defined by their inputs, their outputs, and the feedback loops that moved between them. The answer the Macy Conferences arrived at, over ten meetings across seven years, was yes. And that answer, translated from the conference room into social science, economics, and eventually social policy, has governed how states have thought about, measured, and managed the poor ever since.

The story begins not in 1946 but in September 1940, in a problem that had nothing to do with poverty and everything to do with aircraft.

---

Norbert Wiener volunteered for war research in the autumn of 1940, approaching Vannevar Bush at MIT's National Defence Research Committee with a proposal that would define his career. The problem Bush gave him was deceptively simple in its formulation and technically ferocious in its solution: given a radar return from an enemy aircraft flying an evasive course, and given that the shell fired in response would take up to twenty seconds to reach the target, design a system that could predict where the aircraft would be when the shell arrived, and aim the gun accordingly.

The difficulty was that the radar return was noisy — distorted by atmospheric interference, instrument lag, and the physical limits of signal propagation — and that the aircraft was not flying a straight course. The enemy pilot was taking evasive action, following a trajectory that no formula could predict in advance because it was the product of human intention under stress. To aim a gun at where an aircraft *would be*, rather than where it was, required solving both problems simultaneously: extracting a true signal from a noisy radar return, and extrapolating that signal forward in time to produce a prediction of future position.

Wiener's solution, developed in collaboration with Bigelow and the Mexican physiologist Arturo Rosenblueth, was to treat both problems as questions of statistical estimation. He modelled the aircraft's trajectory as a *stationary stochastic process* — a random process whose statistical properties, specifically its mean, variance, and autocorrelation over time, remained stable even as its individual values fluctuated. From this model, he derived a linear filter: a mathematical function that, given the noisy observed signal up to the present moment, produced the minimum mean-squared-error estimate of the signal's future value. The filter was optimal in a precise technical sense. It was also, in a sense that Wiener did not fully articulate until later, a philosophical position.

To apply stochastic prediction to the enemy pilot, Wiener had to make an assumption that was, in its quiet way, an act of considerable conceptual violence. He had to assume that the pilot's future behaviour was a sample from a probability distribution — that it was, in the relevant mathematical sense, random. Not random in the sense of arbitrary or unpredictable, but random in the statistical sense: governed by a probability law whose parameters could be estimated from past observations and held stable into the future. The pilot was not a person deliberating under conditions of uncertainty, drawing on training, fear, tactical knowledge, and the specific grain of the engagement; the pilot was a stochastic process, characterised entirely by the statistical properties of their historical trajectory.

Wiener's classified report, delivered to the NDRC in 1942 and circulated in a yellow-covered binder that gave it the nickname among mathematicians the *Yellow Peril*, never used the language of ontology. It was an engineering document. But Peter Galison, in his foundational essay on the intellectual origins of cybernetics, identified precisely what was happening beneath the mathematics [@galison1994ontology, 228–240]. To build the anti-aircraft predictor, Wiener had done something that would ordinarily have required philosophical justification: he had treated the enemy pilot, for the purposes of the system, as a machine. Not metaphorically. The predictor's mathematical architecture required that the pilot-plane assembly be modelled as a *servomechanism* — a feedback-controlled device characterised entirely by its input-output transfer function — because that was the only kind of entity for which stochastic prediction of the relevant form was possible. "Wiener," Galison writes, "simply treated the pilot-plane assembly as a complex machine — a servomechanism — that once characterized could be simulated in order to predict what it would do. And then it could be blown out of the sky" [@galison1994ontology, 240].

The ontological move — person as servomechanism — was a technical necessity born of a specific constraint. Wiener had no access to the interior of the enemy plane. He could not know what the pilot was thinking, feeling, or deciding. All he had was the observed trajectory: the sequence of positions, altitudes, and bearing changes that the radar had recorded. The black-box method — treat the system as defined entirely by its observable input-output relationships, without reference to whatever is happening inside — was not chosen for its philosophical appeal. It was chosen because it was the only thing that the available data could support.

The technical necessity became, via the Macy Conferences, a general theory of mind. And the general theory of mind became, across the following decades, a framework for governing the poor.

---

Wiener's wartime insight had a precedent, and that precedent is important for understanding the conceptual genealogy the book is tracing. In 1943, before the Yellow Peril was declassified, Wiener published a short paper with Rosenblueth and Bigelow in the journal *Philosophy of Science*, titled "Behavior, Purpose and Teleology." The paper argued that purposive behaviour — behaviour directed toward a goal — could be defined operationally as *negative feedback toward a target state*, without any appeal to intention, consciousness, or interior mental states. A thermostat pursues the goal of maintaining room temperature; a guided torpedo pursues the goal of destroying a ship; a human being reaching for a cup of coffee pursues the goal of holding the cup. In each case, the goal-directed character of the behaviour is visible in the feedback loop: the system measures its deviation from the target state and adjusts its outputs to reduce that deviation. What was inside — whether it was a bimetallic strip, a gyroscope, or a nervous system — was, in principle, irrelevant to the description of the behaviour.

This was the theoretical seed of the Macy Conferences. If purposive behaviour was formally equivalent whether the purposive agent was a thermostat, an aircraft, or a human being, then the same mathematical framework could describe all three. The anti-aircraft predictor was the first systematic demonstration that the formal apparatus of feedback control, prediction, and error-correction could model a human being in real time, with sufficient accuracy to aim a gun at them.

McCulloch and Walter Pitts had reached the same conclusion from the opposite direction. Their 1943 paper, "A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity," modelled the neuron as a threshold logic unit — a device that fired or did not fire depending on whether its inputs exceeded a threshold — and argued that neural networks of such units could compute any Boolean function. The brain, McCulloch and Pitts proposed, was a logical machine. It processed information according to the same formal rules that governed digital computation. Whether or not this description was biologically accurate — and the question of its accuracy has been disputed ever since — it accomplished something conceptually decisive: it placed the human brain on the same formal continuum as Wiener's anti-aircraft predictor. The pilot whose evasive manoeuvres the predictor was tracking had, in the back of their skull, a logical computing machine whose outputs could, in principle, be predicted from its inputs.

The Macy Conferences were the institutional mechanism through which these wartime insights were generalised, legitimated, and exported to the social sciences. Frank Fremont-Smith, the Macy Foundation's secretary-general, had deliberately designed the conference format to disrupt disciplinary hierarchy: papers were circulated in advance, discussion was informal, and the explicit goal was to produce conceptual synthesis across fields that ordinarily did not speak to each other. The result was that Wiener's stochastic control theory, McCulloch's neural network formalism, and Shannon's mathematical theory of communication — all products of wartime military research — were subjected to repeated application across domains: to brain function, to family therapy (Bateson), to anthropology (Mead), to economics (Savage and Morgenstern), to ecological population dynamics (Hutchinson). Each application reinforced the others. If the same feedback framework could describe a thermostat, a brain, a family system, and an ecological community, the inference was irresistible: it was describing something fundamental about the structure of all self-regulating systems.

What made this inference possible was a decision made within the mathematics itself — one that Hayles argues was not innocent [@hayles1999posthuman, 19]. Shannon's 1948 paper, "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," defined information as the reduction of uncertainty in a message source, expressible as entropy — and explicitly set aside the question of what the messages meant. The decision was deliberate and acknowledged. Shannon wrote it directly into the paper: the semantic aspects of communication were "irrelevant" to the engineering problem he was solving. His information theory was a theory of signal transmission and noise suppression, not a theory of meaning.

The consequences of that exclusion were specific and durable. By defining information as pattern rather than presence — as a statistical property of a message source rather than a property of a message-in-relation-to-a-receiver — Shannon accomplished a kind of erasure. Information, in his framework, had no body. It existed as pure pattern, separable from any material substrate that happened to carry it. A telegram and a nerve impulse and a radar return were all, formally, the same kind of thing: patterns of differences, defined by their probability distributions, indifferent to the physical medium in which they were instantiated.

At the Macy Conferences, the debates about Shannon's exclusion of meaning were real and, at moments, sharp. Donald MacKay, a neurophysicist who attended as a guest, pressed the point consistently. MacKay distinguished between what he called *selective information* — Shannon's measure, the reduction of uncertainty about which message was sent — and *structural information*: the change in the receiver's internal representational state that a message produced. A message's meaning, MacKay argued, could not be separated from the observer who received it. The same statistical signal could produce entirely different structural effects in receivers with different prior states, different knowledge, different circumstances. MacKay's objection ran deeper than a dispute about semantics: he was arguing that you could not, in principle, have a general theory of information that abstracted away from the receiver, because the receiver's interiority was constitutive of what information was [@macy-conferences-note, §5].

McCulloch, who chaired the conferences and controlled their intellectual direction, did not pursue the objection. Neither did Wiener or von Neumann. MacKay's argument was heard, discussed at the eighth and ninth conferences in 1951 and 1952, and left unresolved. The reasons were partly disciplinary — MacKay was working in neurophysics, at an angle to the dominant cluster of mathematicians and engineers — and partly structural. A theory of information that required accounting for each receiver's internal state was not tractable at scale. You could not apply it to telecommunications engineering, to cryptography, to the design of anti-aircraft systems, to the modelling of economic behaviour, without building a separate model of every receiver. Shannon's theory was powerful precisely because it abstracted away from content: it applied to everything, because it was about nothing in particular. It was universal because it was empty.

The suppression of MacKay's objection at the Macy Conferences is the first of several suppressions the book will document. The pattern is consistent: in the intellectual history of quantification, the arguments that insist on the particularity of persons — on embodiment, on material circumstance, on the irreducibility of individual situation — are heard and set aside in favour of frameworks that can operate at scale, that can process many persons at once by stripping them of precisely the features that make them specific. The framework's power comes from what it excludes.

---

The clearest statement of what was being excluded appeared, paradoxically, in Wiener's own later work. In *Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine*, published by MIT Press in 1948, Wiener synthesised what the Macy Conferences had been developing into a single theoretical system. The book's ambivalence about the social applications of cybernetics was already audible. By 1950, in *The Human Use of Human Beings*, the ambivalence had become an explicit warning — a warning the book will deal with fully in the following section.

But the framework's architecture, as established at the Macy Conferences, had already foreclosed certain possibilities before the social applications were even attempted. To model a human being as a feedback system — as an entity characterised by its inputs, its outputs, and the error-correction mechanisms that moved between them — was to perform, in advance and by definitional fiat, precisely what Hayles calls the erasure of embodiment. The entity under analysis had a target state, and deviation from that target state was error, and error was the occasion for correction. What the entity might need, what claims it might have, what the target state's legitimacy was — these were outside the system. Not overlooked. Outside, by construction.

This is the chapter's central claim made formal. The question that Part I of this book has been asking — what do people need? — is not merely difficult to answer within the cybernetic framework. It is unanswerable in principle, because the framework has no variable for need. It has target states and deviations. It has error signals and correction functions. It has feedback loops and equilibrium conditions. Need, in the specific sense that Rowntree meant when he walked the streets of York in 1899, or that Orshansky meant when she calculated the minimum cost of adequate food for a family of four in 1963, is a property of a body in a material circumstance. It is inherently specific, inherently local, inherently resistant to the abstraction that formal modelling requires. The cybernetic framework produces, as its governing concept, not a body with needs but a system with a target state. The difference is everything.

Hayles identifies the precise moment at which this substitution was accomplished. At the Macy Conferences, the decision to define information as pattern rather than presence — Shannon's move — meant that "what had to be elided, forgotten, or put in brackets for information to lose its body" was the starting premise of the entire enterprise, not a conclusion arrived at later [@hayles1999posthuman, 2]. The embodied person, with their specific hunger, their specific cold, their specific labour, was outside the model from the beginning. They had to be, for the model to work.

This is what Galison means when he identifies the anti-aircraft predictor as cybernetics' origin scene. The enemy pilot was first a servomechanism, characterised by their observable input-output trajectory, predicted and targeted on that basis. The fact that they were a person — with fear, with tactical training, with a specific aircraft in a specific engagement — mattered only as input to the servo-model. The person was second. The servomechanism was first [@galison1994ontology, 245]. And when the Macy Conferences generalised this ontology from the enemy pilot to human beings as such — when McCulloch's neural network, Bateson's family therapy, and Shannon's information theory converged on the claim that the feedback framework described all self-regulating systems — the enemy pilot became the template for everyone. Your own gunner, Wiener noted, was treated by the predictor's mathematics in exactly the same way as the enemy pilot. Once the framework was in place, it made no distinction.

---

By 1953, when the tenth and final Macy Conference concluded at Princeton, cybernetics had been exported from the conference room into a set of disciplines that would carry it into social policy. Warren McCulloch's neural network formalism was the direct intellectual predecessor of computational neuroscience and, in time, of machine learning. Gregory Bateson's application of feedback loops to communication and family systems became the theoretical basis of systems therapy and, later, of the systems thinking that would inform public administration from the 1970s onwards. Herbert Simon, whose *Administrative Behavior* had appeared in 1947 and who was in direct contact with the Macy group, was building at Carnegie Mellon's Graduate School of Industrial Administration a theory of organisational decision-making — bounded rationality — that translated cybernetic feedback into the language of management and public administration.

What travelled with the concepts was the foundational assumption. To model a system as a feedback loop — to ask of any social arrangement: what is the target state, what is the error signal, what is the correction mechanism? — was already to have performed the Macy erasure. Material needs, moral claims, and political demands did not appear in feedback loop diagrams not because the designers were malicious or careless, but because the formal framework had no place for them. A feedback loop described how a system responded to deviation from a target state; it said nothing about how the target state was chosen, whose interests it served, or whether the persons whose behaviour constituted the system's inputs had any standing to contest its outputs.

James C. Scott, tracing the ambitions of what he calls high modernist statecraft across the twentieth century, identifies a characteristic combination of legibility and faith: the confidence that complex social systems can be made fully visible to the administrative gaze, and that having been made visible, they can be improved by technical intervention [@scott1998seeing]. Cybernetics was not simply one instance of this faith. It was, for the postwar generation, its intellectual foundation. The feedback loop gave high modernism a rigorous mathematical form. The target state gave it a criterion of success. The error signal gave it a diagnostic method. And the correction mechanism gave it a theory of action. What Scott describes as "the failure of high-modernist schemes" — the grandiose interventions in agriculture, urban planning, and social organisation that produced catastrophe rather than improvement — was, in many cases, the cybernetic framework applied by people who believed in it completely and understood it only partially.

The full weight of that partial understanding will become clear in what follows. But the founding gesture was accomplished at the Macy Conferences, in the decade after the war, by men who had built systems for killing and were now building systems for governing. The same mathematics. The same target states and error signals and correction mechanisms. Different targets.
