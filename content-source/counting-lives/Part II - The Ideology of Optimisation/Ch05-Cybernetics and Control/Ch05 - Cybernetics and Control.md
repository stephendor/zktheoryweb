# Ch5 – Cybernetics and Control: Wiener to Social Physics

## Core Claim
Cybernetics did not merely introduce new mathematical tools — it introduced a new metaphysical claim: that human beings, social institutions, and governments could be understood as information-processing systems subject to the same laws of feedback, error-correction, and optimisation as anti-aircraft guns and thermostats. This is the chapter where the book's Part I question ("what do people need?") is displaced by the Part II question ("how does the system correct its errors?"). The poor cease to be persons with material requirements and become signals in a control loop. Norbert Wiener himself saw this danger — and was ignored.

## Argument in Sections

### 1. From the Bomber to the Boardroom: The Military Birth of Cybernetics
Norbert Wiener's wartime work on anti-aircraft prediction (the Wiener filter, 1942) required him to model the enemy pilot as a predictable mechanism — a servo-system whose error rate could be calculated and anticipated. The conceptual violence of this move — reducing a human being to a predictable feedback process — was the seed of cybernetics. At the Macy Conferences (1946–1953), this framework was generalised: Warren McCulloch modelled the brain as a logical network, Gregory Bateson applied feedback loops to family therapy and anthropology, and Margaret Mead brought them to social systems. What united all of these applications was the same formal abstraction: the entity under analysis was a system with inputs, outputs, and error-correction mechanisms. Material needs, moral claims, and political demands were — by construction — outside the model.

### 2. Wiener's Warning and its Suppression
Norbert Wiener was, unusually, aware of what he had created. *The Human Use of Human Beings* (1950) argued explicitly that applying cybernetic control to workers — using information feedback to optimise human labour — was not a neutral technical project but a new form of domination. He severed his ties to military funding and warned that the same logic which guided anti-aircraft guns could be used to discipline and automate away the working class. His colleagues at MIT, RAND, and the emerging management sciences largely ignored him. *The Human Use of Human Beings* remained in print but out of influence; the tradition that flourished was the one he warned against. This section positions Wiener as this book's second internal dissenter, alongside Orshansky — a figure who understood the stakes and lost.

### 3. The System Dynamics of Poverty: Forrester and Urban Dynamics
Jay Forrester's *Urban Dynamics* (1969) is the clearest case study in what happens when cybernetic system modelling meets social policy. Forrester built a computer simulation of a generic American city and ran policy experiments inside it. His model concluded — controversially and influentially — that low-income housing construction and welfare programmes were "positive feedback loops" that attracted more poor people and prevented economic recovery; the "optimal" policy was to demolish slums and restrict low-income services. The model's political conclusions were not incidental: they were baked in through the choice of variables (the model had no racism, no labour market discrimination, no housing redlining), the choice of feedback relationships, and the choice of what counted as the system's target state. Daniel Patrick Moynihan — who had used statistical analysis to similar ends — praised the book; city planners in Boston used it to justify cutting public housing budgets. This section shows that model choice is ideology, and system dynamics is not exempt.

### 4. The Behavioural Loop: Skinner, Token Economies, and Welfare Reform
B.F. Skinner's behaviourism offered a complementary cybernetic vision: human beings were operant conditioning systems, and behaviour could be shaped by adjusting reinforcement schedules. *Walden Two* (1948) imagined an entire community designed on these principles. By the 1960s and 1970s, token economy programmes — where welfare recipients or psychiatric patients earned "points" for compliant behaviour and lost them for non-compliance — were running in dozens of US and UK institutions. The conceptual architecture is identical to what the book has been tracing: a target state (compliance, employment, "stability") is defined, deviations are measured and penalised, and the correction mechanism is presented as scientific rather than political. This prefigures UC's claimant commitment and sanction regime directly — and this section draws that line explicitly.

### 5. Social Physics Redux: Pentland and the Quantified Community
Alex Pentland's *Social Physics* (2014) closes the chapter with a contemporary echo that is also a historical rhyme. Pentland, using mobile phone data from MIT's Human Dynamics Laboratory, argues that social change can be understood as "idea flow" through networks, and that optimal communities can be designed by shaping those flows. The vocabulary is updated (big data, network graphs, "engagement"), but the structure is Macy Conference cybernetics with better sensors. Crucially, Pentland's framework reactivates Quetelet's original "social physics" (1835) — the dream of applying physical laws to human collectivities — and loops it forward through Wiener into the data-rich present. This section also previews the Silicon Valley chapters: Pentland's framework is one of the direct intellectual predecessors of "social credit"-style scoring and the "nudge" architectures of welfare conditionality.
