# 1. The War Room Comes to Washington

In the late 1950s, the offices of the RAND Corporation occupied a low-rise complex a few blocks from the Pacific Ocean in Santa Monica, California. The building had been designed to look unremarkable — deliberately so, since RAND's principal clients were the United States Air Force and the Atomic Energy Commission, and its principal product was strategic analysis of nuclear war. The analysts who worked there were mathematicians, economists, and physicists, many of them recruited directly from the wartime operations research units that had spent the 1940s calculating optimal bombing patterns, convoy routing algorithms, and submarine detection probabilities. They had learned, under conditions of genuine urgency, that killing and destruction could be made more efficient by treating them as optimisation problems. At RAND, they were now applying the same logic to questions of survival: how to structure a nuclear deterrent, how to allocate a defence budget, how to survive a first strike and retaliate effectively. The blackboards in the corridors were covered in the notation of systems analysis — objective functions, constraint equations, cost-effectiveness ratios. It was, in the cold formalism of the Cold War, a particular kind of intellectual beauty.

By the mid-1960s, those same methods had been turned on the American poor.

The transfer was not accidental, and it was not primarily ideological in the sense of being driven by any conscious desire to harm. It was, rather, an institutional consequence of a framework's own success. Charles Hitch, who ran RAND's Economics Division from 1948 and who was the central theorist of the cost-effectiveness tradition, had argued in *The Economics of Defense in the Nuclear Age* — co-authored with Roland McKean and published in 1960 — that military and, by extension, public-sector decision-making should be treated as an economic problem. The state faces a budget constraint, multiple competing objectives, and a range of possible means for achieving them. The task of the analyst is to specify the objective, enumerate the available options, calculate a cost-effectiveness ratio for each, and identify the optimal allocation. The question the framework asks is deceptively simple: given that you want to achieve X, and that you have resources Y, what is the least-cost means of getting there?

What made the framework powerful — and what made it dangerous when it left the weapons laboratory — was precisely what it treated as given. In *The Economics of Defense*, Hitch and McKean presented the objective function as a technical parameter: something to be specified before analysis begins, whose content the analysis itself would not question. They acknowledged that defining objectives was difficult, and devoted careful pages to the problem of matching means to ends in conditions of uncertainty. But the choice of objective — what the programme was actually for — was treated as a matter of prior political decision, not as something the analytical framework would interrogate or contest. The analyst received the objective and optimised toward it.

For defence procurement, this division of labour was more or less workable. The objectives of nuclear deterrence — deter a Soviet first strike, retain second-strike capability, survive escalation — were at least debatable in terms the framework could handle: more deterrence or less, this basing mode or that one, this yield-to-weight ratio or a different one. The objective function had content that could be operationalised, even if the underlying question of how much national security was enough admitted no clean answer. What the framework could not do, and did not try to do, was challenge the prior decision that deterrence, rather than disarmament, was the appropriate objective. That decision arrived from outside, as a political given, and the analysts worked within it.

This is the structure Hitch brought to Washington, and it is worth dwelling on its architecture before following it into the welfare state, because the architecture is what matters. A framework that accepts objectives as given and optimises within them is not neutral with respect to power. It is, rather, structurally deferential to whoever has the authority to specify what the objective is. In a defence context, that authority rested with civilian leadership and, ultimately, with elected government. In a welfare context — as we shall see — the authority to define the objective passed to economists, to programme evaluators, and to the analytical culture that RAND had itself created.

---

The migration of RAND's methods into social policy was driven by an institutional force as much as an intellectual one. Throughout the late 1950s, RAND's board was actively seeking to reduce the organisation's dependence on Air Force contracts, which had accounted for 95 per cent of its funding at its founding in 1948. By 1959 that figure had fallen to 68 per cent as RAND diversified into work for the AEC, ARPA, NASA, and the Ford Foundation. The Great Society programmes of the mid-1960s created a substantial new market. The Office of Economic Opportunity, the Department of Health, Education, and Welfare, the Department of Housing and Urban Development, and the Department of Labor all required analytical capacity they did not have in-house. RAND's board, overriding the resistance of founder Franklin Collbohm, pushed the organisation into non-military research from 1965 onward. It was good for the balance sheet. The methods followed the money.

The RAND that arrived in the social policy arena of the late 1960s brought with it a complete analytical culture: a preference for quantitative modelling over qualitative case study, a commitment to cost-effectiveness ratios as the primary criterion of programme evaluation, a belief that objective functions could be specified without fundamental contestation, and — less often stated — a deep confidence that the role of expert analysis was to resolve questions that democratic deliberation had been too slow or too irrational to settle. This confidence was not cynical. The RAND analysts who turned their attention to poverty and urban policy in the late 1960s mostly believed they were bringing rigour to a domain that had previously been governed by sentiment and political patronage. They were right that sentiment and political patronage had failed American cities. They were wrong that rigour, in their particular sense, was the remedy.

The sharpest test of RAND's analytical culture outside the weapons laboratory was its work for New York City, which began in 1969 when RAND and the Lindsay administration established the New York City–RAND Institute. The Institute applied operations research methods to New York's public services: police deployment, fire services, housing, sanitation, and health. Its fire services work used queuing theory and optimisation modelling to calculate the placement and response capacity of fire stations. Acting on RAND's recommendations, the Lindsay administration reduced fire service capacity in precisely the communities under the most severe housing stress — the South Bronx, Brownsville, Bushwick — where overcrowding, arson-for-insurance, and the decay of the physical stock had created conditions of extreme fire risk. The model could not see this because fire risk of the relevant kind — the slow accumulation of abandoned buildings, the cascading effects of neighbour departure, the systemic collapse of a community's capacity for informal fire prevention — was not among its variables. The model measured response times and cost per call. It did not measure the community's capacity to survive.

The fires that followed were not caused solely by the RAND model. The political economy of disinvestment in New York City in the 1970s was overdetermined: blockbusting, redlining, landlord abandonment, municipal fiscal crisis, and federal neglect were all doing their work independently. But the RAND model's recommendations — authoritative, quantitative, cloaked in the legitimacy of systems analysis — accelerated the process and made it harder to contest. When community leaders argued that closing fire stations in Brownsville would be catastrophic, they were arguing against cost-effectiveness ratios. The analytical framework had, in effect, pre-empted the political argument. It had not done so dishonestly. The ratios were calculated correctly. The problem was the objective function: the model was optimising for the wrong thing.

---

Hitch's cost-effectiveness framework, when it arrived in the welfare state, brought with it a prior commitment to a particular kind of question. Not: what do people need? But: given a defined objective and limited resources, what is the optimal allocation? The question sounds like a clarification of the first, a more rigorous way of asking it. In practice, it is a replacement. The first question takes the material condition of poor people as its starting point; the second takes a defined objective — specified by analysts, programme administrators, or elected governments with their own interests — and asks how to achieve it at minimum cost. Whether the objective has anything to do with the material condition of poor people is not a question the framework raises.

Hitch was explicit that the choice of objective was not his to make. *The Economics of Defense in the Nuclear Age* is careful, even fastidious, on this point: the analyst serves the decision-maker, who serves the political process, which serves the public. But the framework was not equally fastidious about what happened to the objective function in transit. When cost-effectiveness analysis migrated from the Pentagon — where its use was at least formally constrained by civilian oversight and the adversarial scrutiny of the military services — to HEW's welfare programmes, the objective that proved most tractable was not "ensure that poor families have enough to eat" or "reduce material deprivation below a defined threshold." It was "reduce welfare caseloads" and "increase labour market participation." These objectives were measurable. They could be expressed as outcome metrics. They could anchor a cost-effectiveness ratio. Mollie Orshansky's 124 household matrices, which measured what families actually needed to survive, could not do any of these things. The framework had not chosen between them. But it had made one of them legible and the other invisible, and in the bureaucratic culture of the Great Society's analytical agencies, legibility was power.

The blackboards in Santa Monica were being cleaned. New equations were going up. They looked the same as the old ones.
