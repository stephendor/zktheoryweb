# 2. McNamara's Children: PPBS and the Social Service State

On 25 August 1965, Lyndon Johnson announced to his Cabinet that the Planning, Programming, Budgeting System he had watched transform McNamara's Pentagon would henceforth govern all federal agencies. The Bureau of the Budget formalised the requirement in Bulletin 66-3, issued in October of that year: every department was to produce multi-year programme budgets, justify major policy choices in cost-effectiveness terms through programme memoranda, and submit Special Analytic Studies — systematic economic comparisons of alternative approaches to each defined objective. The directive landed on the desks of the Department of Health, Education, and Welfare, the Office of Economic Opportunity, the Department of Housing and Urban Development, and the Department of Labor simultaneously. By the winter of 1965, every federal agency responsible for the welfare of poor Americans was legally required to speak in the language RAND had designed for choosing between nuclear weapons systems.

The mechanism was not accidental. The "Whiz Kids" who had implemented PPBS at the Pentagon — systems analysts drawn largely from RAND and the academic economics departments it funded — had always believed that the framework was generalisable. Robert McNamara was the instrument; Charles Hitch, arriving at the Pentagon as Comptroller in 1961, supplied the intellectual architecture. The system Hitch built organised the defence budget into programme categories defined by military mission rather than service branch, required each programme to specify measurable objectives, and forced cost comparison across alternatives pursuing the same goal. McNamara used it to break the Air Force's and Navy's entrenched budget claims. When it produced visible results in procurement rationalisation, the political temptation to apply the same logic to the rest of government was, as Johnson's announcement demonstrated, irresistible. The "Whiz Kids" believed they were not merely reforming defence accounting; they were discovering a general technology of rational governance. The poverty of the Great Society's target population was, from within this framework, simply a logistics problem that had not yet been properly optimised.

PPBS imposed on welfare agencies a new bureaucratic role: the programme analyst, trained in economics and systems analysis, whose job was to translate departmental activities into measurable output categories and assess them for cost-effectiveness. Alice Rivlin, who had joined HEW's analytical office in 1966 fresh from Radcliffe and from earlier work on federal financing of higher education, became the most important figure in this translation effort. The problem she inherited was immediate and, as she would eventually acknowledge, unresolvable: the outputs of welfare programmes resisted reduction to measurable, cost-rateable units in ways that weapons systems did not. A nuclear deterrent could be measured by megatonnage, throw-weight, survivability, and unit cost. What was the equivalent output unit for a housing assistance programme? For a nutrition subsidy? For a caseworker's visit to a family in arrears on rent?

HEW's analysts proposed measures: "poverty years eliminated," "number of families raised above the poverty line," "programme costs per beneficiary exit from welfare." Each of these measurements was technically defensible and analytically evasive. They imported, as a precondition of calculation, a particular theory of what welfare was for — a theory that the measurement apparatus rendered invisible precisely by making it foundational. Rivlin's analysts at HEW converted Orshansky's poverty line — which had been constructed to measure what specific household types needed to survive — from a measure of material adequacy into a target variable. The question the line had been designed to answer — are these families poor? — was replaced by the question the PPBS framework required: how many families crossed the threshold last quarter, and at what cost? The adequacy of support for those who remained below it was not a measurable programme output. It disappeared from the analytical ledger.

The incentive structure this created was not a matter of bad faith. The programme analysts at HEW were, by most accounts, serious people who believed in the social programmes they were administering. But PPBS rewarded what could be measured, and what could be measured was movement off the rolls. A programme that placed 100 families in low-wage employment could produce a cost-per-exit figure, a before-and-after poverty threshold comparison, a programme memorandum that satisfied the Bureau of the Budget's requirements. A programme that raised the real value of benefits to cover heating costs in winter — that kept families housed and warm through a Chicago January — could not easily translate that outcome into a PPBS-compatible output. There was no production function for warmth. The framework had not prohibited material adequacy as an objective; it had simply made it analytically invisible, which in a bureaucratic culture oriented around output measurement amounted to the same thing.

---

Alice Rivlin's *Systematic Thinking for Social Action*, published by Brookings in 1971 from Gaither Lectures she had delivered at Berkeley the previous January, is the chapter's most important internal document. It is a defence of systematic analysis in social policy written by the person who had tried hardest to make it work in practice — and who had, in the process, accumulated the most detailed evidence of why it could not. Rivlin did not repudiate the PPBS tradition. She recommended more social experimentation, better longitudinal data, improved outcome measures. But in documenting what the framework had failed to capture, she produced the evidence for a more radical conclusion than she was institutionally positioned to draw.

Her taxonomy of unmeasured outcomes is the most analytically precise account available of what was excluded. The first category comprised outcomes for which no reliable data existed: the long-run effects of early childhood programmes on adult income required decades of follow-up that no programme evaluation had conducted; the health consequences of housing quality required linkage between administrative datasets that had not been built. The second category comprised outcomes that were measured but were known to be inadequate proxies: test scores as educational achievement; employment entry as a measure of manpower training success; caseload reduction as poverty reduction. Rivlin was unusually direct about this second category — she named the proxy problem and declined to pretend it was a minor technical inconvenience. The third category, the one that matters most for this chapter's argument, comprised outcomes that were in principle measurable but had never been specified as programme objectives because the analytical framework did not require it: the health status of welfare recipients, the housing quality of AFDC families, the nutritional adequacy of households below the poverty line. These outcomes had not been excluded by accident or by data limitation. They had been excluded because a framework inherited from defence procurement did not have a slot for them.

The "production function problem" was Rivlin's name for the deepest difficulty. We do not know, she argued, what inputs produce what social outputs. We do not know how much a year of Head Start increases educational attainment, how much a dollar of welfare payment reduces poverty, how a specific manpower training programme changes long-run employment. Without production functions, cost-effectiveness analysis can compute cost-per-unit only for the proximate output — the number of programme completions, the number of benefit payments made, the number of caseload exits — and not for the ultimate outcome the programme was supposed to achieve. The system was measuring what it could reach.

What Rivlin stopped short of concluding is as instructive as what she concluded. Her evidence demonstrated, systematically and in domain after domain, that the production functions required for welfare cost-effectiveness analysis did not exist, that the available outcome measures were inadequate proxies for what the programmes aimed to achieve, and that the objective functions had been defined in terms of administrative measurability rather than material sufficiency. The logical conclusion of these findings — that the cost-effectiveness framework was the wrong grammar for welfare policy, not merely a grammar that needed better data — is nowhere in the book. Rivlin recommended more and better social experimentation. She would spend the rest of her career implementing that recommendation: in 1975 she became the first director of the Congressional Budget Office, institutionalising cost-effectiveness analysis in the legislative branch of government, extending rather than questioning the framework's reach. Her critique had diagnosed what was missing. The institution in which she worked required her to treat the diagnosis as a call for better instruments rather than a different question.

---

PPBS was formally cancelled as a government-wide requirement by Nixon's Office of Management and Budget in 1971. The proximate causes were administrative: the system had proved far more demanding in practice than its designers anticipated, and many agencies had satisfied its formal requirements by producing programme memoranda that were analytically empty. But the system's cancellation changed nothing of substance. The corps of programme analysts PPBS had created remained in their posts. The expectation that social programmes would justify themselves in cost-effectiveness terms had embedded itself in the institutional culture of HEW, OEO, and their successors. The vocabulary — output targets, cost ratios, efficiency metrics, caseload management — had become the default language of social policy evaluation in Washington, and through the influence of the Brookings Institution and, from 1975, the Congressional Budget Office, it would become the default language in Westminster too.

The question Orshansky had spent her career trying to keep on the table — what do specific families, of specific sizes and compositions, actually need to survive? — had not been answered or refuted. It had been outadministered. PPBS had not argued against material sufficiency as a welfare objective; it had simply created a bureaucratic culture in which that objective, being unmeasurable by the system's own methods, could not be advanced. The framework's legacy was not a policy position but a grammar: the grammar in which welfare policy would henceforth be required to speak, and in which some questions could not be articulated at all.
