# 4. The New Jersey Experiment: The Poor as Parameters

In 1968, the Office of Economic Opportunity authorised approximately eight million dollars for an experiment. The Institute for Research on Poverty at the University of Wisconsin, under the direction of the economist Harold Watts, would recruit low-income families in urban New Jersey and Pennsylvania, randomly assign them to treatment and control groups, pay the treatment group a guaranteed minimum income at varying levels, and measure what happened to their work hours. The research question was stated explicitly in the design documentation: would a guaranteed income reduce work effort among low-income households? The experiment was the first large-scale randomised controlled trial in social science. Everything about its design — the sample selected, the outcome variable chosen, the parameters varied, the households excluded — followed from that single prior question, and that question followed from the analytical culture this chapter has been tracing since Santa Monica.

The intellectual origin was Milton Friedman's negative income tax, advanced in *Capitalism and Freedom* in 1962. Friedman had argued that a guaranteed minimum income, structured as a negative tax on earnings below a threshold, would be more efficient than the existing categorical welfare system: simpler, less bureaucratic, less prone to the perverse incentives he identified in means-tested benefits. The OEO's interest in testing this proposal was genuine, but the framing of the test was determined by a prior commitment: the relevant question, from within the RAND tradition's objective-function logic, was whether the guarantee would reduce labour supply. Watts's team varied the treatment parameters — the guarantee level *G*, set between 50% and 125% of the poverty line, and the marginal tax rate *t* on earned income, set between 30% and 70% — specifically to generate data on labour supply elasticity across different guarantee and tax-rate combinations. The experiment was designed to estimate a parameter in an economic model. Everything else was secondary.

The sample reflected the research question's assumptions. Watts's team recruited 1,216 people from two-parent families in urban areas of New Jersey and Pennsylvania — Black, white, and Latino households with a male head of household aged 18 to 58 and family income below 150% of the poverty line. The restriction to two-parent, male-headed households was not an oversight. The experiment was measuring the work response of the male breadwinner, and the treatment parameters had been designed to generate variation in that response. Female-headed households — the majority of the AFDC caseload, the population most directly affected by the existing welfare system, the households whose material circumstances Orshansky's matrices had been constructed to describe — Watts's team excluded from the New Jersey design altogether. Their work responses, their material circumstances, their housing situations, their children's school attendance: none of this was the experiment's primary concern. A Rural Income Maintenance Experiment was initiated in 1970 partly to address the gap, but the governing question remained the same.

The act of random assignment is worth dwelling on. When Harold Watts's team randomly assigned households to treatment and control conditions, they performed, in the lives of actual households in Trenton and Passaic, the same analytical operation that PPBS had performed administratively on HEW's programme budget: sorting people not by their material circumstances or their needs, but by their function in an estimation problem. The treatment group were the cells from which labour supply elasticity would be measured; the control group were the counterfactual from which deviations would be computed. Whether a household in the control group had enough to eat was not a variable in the design. Whether a household in the treatment group was better housed was not the outcome the experiment was trying to identify. The experimental frame had operationalised the objective function: persons had become parameters.

---

The results, when they arrived, were modest and ambiguous — a fact that mattered, because the political uses to which they were subsequently put were neither. Male heads of household in the treatment group reduced their work hours by approximately 5 to 7 per cent relative to the control group. This was less than the experiment's opponents had predicted and less than the economics literature had suggested a rational actor should do in response to an income guarantee. Female labour supply showed a somewhat larger reduction, but this was concentrated in households where the guaranteed income enabled women to reduce secondary employment while caring for young children — an outcome that, measured against a different objective function, might have appeared as a welfare gain: a family choosing, when it could afford to, to have a parent at home. The experimental frame had no slot for this reading. The outcome variable was hours worked. Hours worked had declined. The design determined what the findings meant.

What the experiment also found — but did not publish with the same analytical rigour, and what was largely absent from the policy debates the experiment was designed to inform — was substantial evidence on outcomes the objective function had treated as secondary. Treatment group participants reported higher levels of happiness and marital satisfaction. Nutrition improved. School attendance among children in treatment households increased. Recent archival re-analysis of the suppressed data has confirmed these effects. They were collected. They existed in the data. But the experiment had been designed around labour supply, and findings outside the primary outcome measure had no technical standing in a debate conducted in the experiment's own terms. Senator Russell Long, chairing the Senate Finance Committee's 1972 hearings on the Family Assistance Plan, used the New Jersey findings to argue that a guaranteed income would decimate work incentives. He focused on the hours-worked reduction. Watts and his colleagues could not effectively contest this reading with evidence on household nutrition or children's school attendance, because the experimental design had accorded those outcomes no equivalent analytical authority. Having chosen labour supply as the primary outcome variable, the experiment had no technically equivalent measure of material sufficiency with which to reframe the debate. Porter's argument about quantification as a political technology applies precisely here: the cost-effectiveness frame did not merely describe a policy choice, it protected it from contestation by setting the terms on which evidence could count.

Long's hearings contributed to the Senate's defeat of Nixon's Family Assistance Plan in 1972 — a plan that, whatever its limitations, would have established a federal income floor for the first time in American history. The New Jersey experiment had been designed to generate evidence for rational welfare reform. Its ambiguous labour supply findings were used, instead, to foreclose it.

---

Four more income maintenance experiments followed between 1970 and the late 1970s: the Rural Income Maintenance Experiment, the Gary Experiment, the Seattle–Denver Income Maintenance Experiment, and the Manitoba experiment in Canada. Together they constituted the largest social science research programme of the twentieth century, spending tens of millions of dollars, recruiting thousands of families, and generating shelf after shelf of working papers from the Institute for Research on Poverty and RAND's Urban Policy Analysis programme. Every one of them was organised around a variant of the same primary question: what is the labour supply response to a guaranteed income? Every one of them produced ambiguous results that were contested in the political arena by opponents who read the modest work reductions as evidence of catastrophe. Every one of them collected data on health, nutrition, housing security, and family stability that arrived at the policy debate as footnotes.

The experimental frame the New Jersey experiment established did not end with the 1970s. It became the standard methodology for evaluating welfare-to-work and conditionality programmes in both the United States and the United Kingdom. When the Department for Work and Pensions evaluates Universal Credit conditionality regimes, or when American researchers assess TANF programme effects, they use variants of the same design: treatment group, control group, primary outcome measure. The primary outcome measures are employment entry rate, hours worked, and benefit exit — the direct institutional descendants of Harold Watts's labour supply objective function, unchanged in their essential structure from the design he submitted to OEO in 1967. The question of whether participants have enough to eat is, in these evaluations, as absent as it was in Trenton in 1968.

What changed between 1968 and the present is not the experimental grammar but the objective function it serves. The New Jersey experiment asked whether a guaranteed income reduced work effort; the implicit policy goal was to establish whether a floor could be afforded. The conditionality evaluations that inherit its methodology ask whether benefit sanctions increase employment entry; the implicit policy goal is to establish that the floor can be lowered. The mathematics is the same. The direction of travel is reversed.
