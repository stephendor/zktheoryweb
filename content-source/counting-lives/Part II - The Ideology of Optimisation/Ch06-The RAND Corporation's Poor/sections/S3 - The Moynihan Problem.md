# 3. The Moynihan Problem: Turning Poverty into Pathology

Daniel Patrick Moynihan wrote *The Negro Family: The Case for National Action* in the first months of 1965, in his capacity as Assistant Secretary of Labor under President Johnson. He had access to Bureau of Labor Statistics longitudinal data on Black unemployment, family composition, and welfare caseloads, and he had a specific empirical puzzle: the data series that had tracked each other reliably through the early 1960s had, around 1962, come apart. Black male unemployment and AFDC welfare caseloads had moved in close parallel through the postwar decades — as unemployment rose, welfare take-up rose; as it fell, welfare take-up fell. Then the relationship broke. From approximately 1962, Black male unemployment began declining as the economic expansion reached Black workers, but the welfare caseload continued rising. Moynihan called this decoupling "the Moynihan scissors" and made it the pivot of his report. Something other than unemployment was now driving welfare take-up. The question was what.

His answer was family structure. The higher proportion of female-headed households in Black communities, the higher illegitimacy rate, the absent father — these, Moynihan argued, had become a self-perpetuating cycle, "capable of perpetuating itself without assistance from the white world." He borrowed the phrase "tangle of pathology" from the psychologist Kenneth Clark's *Dark Ghetto*, published the same year, though Clark subsequently disowned the use to which Moynihan put it. The report was intended as an internal policy brief — an argument for going beyond the Civil Rights Act to address what Moynihan framed as the structural roots of Black economic disadvantage. When it was leaked to the press in late 1965, it became one of the most controversial social science documents of the twentieth century. The controversy was not about the data. It was about the analytical move Moynihan had made with them.

---

That move is the chapter's clearest instance of what it means to choose a model. Moynihan's central statistical procedure was to control for income when examining Black family stability. Having demonstrated that unemployment and welfare had tracked each other until 1962, he argued that their subsequent divergence could not be explained by economic factors — income was rising, employment was improving, yet welfare take-up and family instability were increasing. The residual after controlling for income was attributed to an autonomous family-structure effect: something non-economic was now determining outcomes. The model identified this residual, named it, and made it the target of policy.

What the model did not include was as consequential as what it did. A researcher who had controlled for residential segregation, redlining, occupational discrimination, differential access to the GI Bill's housing and education benefits, educational segregation, and the continuing exclusion of Black workers from unionised industrial employment — all of which were ongoing in 1965, documented, and measurable — would have found the residual family-structure effect shrink dramatically or disappear. The sociologist Herbert Hyman and his colleague Charles Willie conducted precisely this kind of re-analysis, demonstrating that when appropriate controls were applied, Black and white family structures differed far less than Moynihan's report implied, and that the differences that remained were explained by income, not culture. Moynihan's model had no racism in it — not because racism had ceased to operate in 1965, but because he had not put it in. The model's silence on structural discrimination was not a finding; it was a specification choice that determined the finding in advance.

This is the operations-research logic applied to race. If you accept the Hitch-McKean framework — define your objective, identify the key parameters, optimise toward the objective — then the choice of which variables to include in the model determines what interventions will appear rational. Moynihan's model identified family structure as the key parameter driving welfare take-up after 1962. Given that identification, the rational policy response was to intervene on family structure: marriage promotion, work requirements, paternity establishment, conditioned benefit receipt. The alternative model — one that included residential segregation and labour market discrimination as parameters — would have identified structural economic exclusion as the key variable and produced an entirely different set of rational policy responses. The mathematics, in either case, was correct. The choice of variables was the politics.

---

The feedback loop claim was the statistical assertion that most directly translated an analytical frame into a policy programme. Moynihan argued that family instability had become self-reproducing: the children of female-headed households were more likely to form female-headed households themselves, generating the next generation's poverty independently of economic conditions. This claim is structurally identical to the Forrester urban dynamics feedback loop argument that Chapter 5 examined — the claim that a social system has entered a self-reinforcing cycle that cannot be broken by addressing its original causes, only by intervening directly in the cycle's mechanism. In Forrester's case, the mechanism was housing stock dynamics; in Moynihan's, it was family structure.

The policy implication of a feedback loop is specific: if the cycle is self-sustaining, conventional redistributive policy cannot break it, because redistribution addresses the original cause rather than the self-reproducing mechanism. What is required is behavioural intervention. This is why Moynihan's report, though intended as an argument for expanded Great Society programmes, was read — and subsequently used — as an argument for a different kind of intervention altogether: one that targeted the behaviour of poor Black families rather than the conditions that produced their poverty. The 1996 Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act, which established TANF, listed among its explicit goals the reduction of out-of-wedlock births and dependency on government assistance. These are Moynihan's feedback loop variables, operationalised thirty years later as federal welfare policy targets. The model had determined what would count as success.

Eleanor Holmes Norton, Pauli Murray, and the sociologist Joyce Ladner were among the Black scholars and activists who, in 1965 and 1966, identified precisely this mechanism in Moynihan's report: he had mistaken the consequences of racism for its causes. The higher proportion of female-headed households among Black families was not a cultural pathology but a rational adaptation to labour market conditions that systematically excluded Black men from stable employment. The "tangle of pathology" was the tangle that racism had created; Moynihan had analysed the tangle rather than the racism. Norton was direct: the report had deflected attention from discrimination and onto the victims of discrimination, and in so doing had given analytical cover to policy-makers who wished to address Black poverty without confronting the white institutions that produced it.

---

MacKenzie's framework — the model as engine rather than camera — is most precisely applicable here. A camera reproduces what is in front of it; an engine transforms what it encounters. Moynihan's model did not discover "welfare dependency" as a pre-existing social phenomenon that his analysis then described. It constructed welfare dependency as a measurable, targetable category with a feedback mechanism — and in constructing it, brought it into administrative existence. Once the category existed, it could be measured, tracked, and made the objective of an optimisation problem: how to reduce welfare dependency, given a budget constraint and a set of available interventions. The question Hacking poses — what kinds of people are made up by our classifications? — receives a specific answer here. Moynihan's model made up the welfare-dependent family: a household type defined not by material circumstances but by its deviation from a behavioural norm, whose poverty was caused by its own self-perpetuating dynamics rather than by the conditions in which it lived.

This making-up had lasting institutional consequences. The analytical apparatus required to measure and target "welfare dependency" — which had no existence prior to Moynihan's specification of it as a residual — became the governing framework for American welfare policy in the three decades following the report. The measurement infrastructure built around caseload rates, out-of-wedlock birth rates, and labour market participation rates reflected the model's variables, not the material conditions of poor households. A measure of nutritional adequacy, or housing security, or heating costs met through winter, was not among them. Orshansky's question — what do specific families actually need? — had been replaced, by Moynihan's model and by the PPBS apparatus that gave it administrative form, with a question the measurement infrastructure could handle: how many families have stopped exhibiting the feedback loop's symptoms?

The scissors closed. The model named what lay between the blades.
