# 5. Legacy: From Cost-Effectiveness to Conditionality

On 13 October 1987, Senator Daniel Patrick Moynihan introduced the Family Support Act on the floor of the United States Senate. The Act, signed into law by President Reagan in October 1988, created the JOBS programme — the first federal requirement that welfare recipients engage in education, training, or work activities as a condition of receiving Aid to Families with Dependent Children. Moynihan, who had spent the twenty-two years since his report on the Negro family cycling between academic positions, cabinet roles, and electoral office, presented the legislation as a compassionate response to the evidence his 1965 report had produced. The feedback loop he had identified — family instability self-perpetuating, generating the next generation's poverty independently of economic conditions — demanded, he argued, precisely this kind of intervention: welfare receipt itself as the point at which the cycle could be broken.

The historical thread is worth pausing over. The 1965 report had identified family structure as the key parameter in a model from which structural racism had been excluded. The 1988 legislation operationalised that parameter as a policy target: if dependency is the problem, welfare conditionality is the instrument for addressing it. Moynihan had built the model that made this instrument rational, and twenty-three years later he wrote the legislation that deployed it. The analytical choice — which variables to include, which to exclude — had become the statutory framework. The model had authored the law.

---

The Family Support Act was a transition; the Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act of 1996 was the destination. President Clinton signed PRWORA on 22 August 1996, abolishing the Aid to Families with Dependent Children programme that had constituted the American welfare entitlement since 1935 and replacing it with Temporary Assistance for Needy Families — a block grant to states, time-limited, conditional, and stripped of the guarantee that any individual household meeting the eligibility criteria had a legal right to support. AFDC had been, whatever its inadequacies, a legal entitlement; TANF was a discretionary fund. The ending of the entitlement removed the one mechanism by which material need could compel a response from the state.

PRWORA's explicit statutory purposes are the most legible record available of what the RAND tradition had accomplished. The Act was designed not only to reduce poverty but to reduce "dependency on government benefits," to reduce out-of-wedlock births, to encourage the formation and maintenance of two-parent families, and to end the dependence of needy parents on government benefits "by promoting job preparation, work, and marriage." These are Moynihan's 1965 feedback loop variables, enacted as federal law: the outcome measures are dependency rates and family formation patterns, and the policy instruments are requirements and incentives addressed to behaviour. The question of whether a family receiving TANF assistance could meet its basic material needs — could heat its home in January, could feed its children to Orshansky's minimum nutritional standard — is absent from the Act's statement of purposes. It is not present as a secondary consideration. It is not present at all.

The work participation requirements completed the objective function. PRWORA required that 25 per cent of state TANF caseloads be engaged in "countable work activities" in fiscal year 1997, rising to 50 per cent by fiscal year 2002. The definition of "countable work activities" excluded most forms of education and vocational training. States that missed their work participation targets faced financial penalties; states that reduced their caseloads — by any means, including sanctions, administrative barriers, and procedural diversion that pushed eligible families off the rolls — received credit against their participation rate regardless of whether the reduction reflected genuine employment. The RAND objective function was now the statutory standard: minimise caseload, maximise labour market participation, with the adequacy of the benefit level treated as irrelevant to the programme's purpose.

---

The British conditionality trajectory ran parallel to the American one, driven by the same analytical culture through a different institutional genealogy. Jobseeker's Allowance, introduced in the same year as PRWORA — 1996 — formalised in the United Kingdom the principle that benefit receipt was conditional on the claimant's demonstrated compliance with job-search requirements. The Jobseeker's Agreement, signed by each claimant, specified the steps they were required to take: how many applications per week, what kinds of work they were available for, what geographical range they would accept. Failure to comply triggered a sanction — a temporary reduction or removal of benefit. The vocabulary was unmistakably RAND: the claimant's behaviour was the input variable, and the welfare system's role was to set the incentive structure that would produce the desired output.

The Welfare Reform Act 2012 deepened the architecture. Universal Credit, designed by Iain Duncan Smith's Department for Work and Pensions and brought into force incrementally from 2013, extended conditionality to groups previously exempt: in-work claimants, carers, and people in part-time employment were all subjected to work-related requirements and the sanctions regime for the first time. The claimant commitment — a written document, signed by the claimant as a condition of benefit entitlement, specifying their agreed work-related obligations — became the central instrument of the system. Sections 26 and 27 of the 2012 Act established a four-tier sanctions framework, with higher-level sanctions capable of removing 100 per cent of the standard allowance for up to three years. The DWP's own impact assessments framed the system's purpose in language that would have been recognisable to Harold Watts in 1967: Universal Credit was designed to "make work pay" and to ensure that "work always pays more than benefits." This is the New Jersey experiment's primary research question — will a guaranteed income reduce work effort? — translated from experimental hypothesis into statutory design principle.

Neither the Welfare Reform Act 2012 nor PRWORA 1996 contains a provision specifying that the standard benefit level must be sufficient to cover the material needs of a claimant household. Neither creates a legal mechanism for adjusting benefits to reflect actual living costs in specific places at specific times. Neither requires the state to demonstrate, before imposing a sanction, that a sanctioned household can meet its basic living requirements from other sources. The RAND tradition's displacement of material sufficiency by behavioural optimisation is not merely an analytical preference in these statutes; it is their absence — the question they were never asked to answer.

---

The legacy of operations research in welfare governance is not any particular policy outcome. It is a grammar: the grammar in which welfare policy is required to be written, and within which certain questions cannot be articulated. Charles Hitch, drafting the cost-effectiveness framework at RAND in the early 1950s, was solving a defence procurement problem. The objective was given — deter Soviet nuclear attack — and the analyst's role was to optimise within it. The framework's assumption — that objectives could be specified before analysis began, without political contestation, by whoever held the authority to specify them — was a working assumption in a context where the stakes of getting the weapons mix wrong were existential. When this assumption migrated to welfare governance, it carried with it a concealed structural consequence: authority over the objective function passed to economists, to programme analysts, to legislators whose political incentives pointed toward caseload reduction rather than material provision. The objective that proved tractable — minimise dependency, maximise labour market participation — was not neutral. It had authors, and it had beneficiaries, and neither was the family below the poverty line in Trenton in 1968 or in Tower Hamlets in 2015.

Mollie Orshansky understood what was at stake. She had spent years insisting that the poverty line was a measure of need — specific, household-specific, materially grounded need — and that any poverty measure that lost that grounding had ceased to measure poverty and had begun to measure something else: an administrative category, a political target, a cost-effectiveness ratio. She was right about what would be lost. The RAND tradition did not argue against her. It simply created a bureaucratic and legislative framework in which her question — what does this family need to survive? — could not be entered into the objective function, and therefore could not count as a programme output, and therefore could not compel a governmental response.

The blackboards in Santa Monica are long gone. The equations they carried are not.
