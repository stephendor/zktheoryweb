# Ch6 – The RAND Corporation's Poor: Operations Research Meets Welfare

## Core Claim
When operations research migrated from the Pentagon to the welfare state, it did not merely introduce new tools — it introduced a new question. Instead of "what do people need?", the system began to ask "what is the optimal intervention given a target objective?". The poor were recast as parameters in a control problem, and welfare was silently redefined from material provision to the minimisation of cost and "dependency." This is the chapter where Orshansky's contestable matrices lose to McNamara's efficiency calculus.

## Argument in Sections

### 1. The War Room Comes to Washington
Operations research — invented to solve military logistics problems (bombing runs, submarine detection, convoy routing) — was adapted at RAND into a generalised technique of rational decision-making under constraint. Charles Hitch's cost-effectiveness framework asked: given limited resources and a defined objective, what is the optimal allocation? When this question was turned on poverty, it carried with it an assumption that would prove fateful: that the objective could be specified in advance, without political contestation. The choice of objective (minimise cost? minimise caseload? maximise "self-sufficiency"?) was treated as technical, not moral.

### 2. McNamara's Children: PPBS and the Social Service State
Robert McNamara's Planning, Programming, Budgeting System brought RAND's logic wholesale into the Pentagon; Lyndon Johnson then mandated it across all federal agencies in 1965. Alice Rivlin and her colleagues at HEW faced the same challenge: define your objectives, enumerate your outputs, calculate cost-per-unit. The result was a bureaucratic culture in which welfare interventions had to justify themselves in the language of economics — measurable outcomes, comparison groups, programme cost ratios. Orshansky's 124 household matrices were not amenable to this framework; they measured needs, not outputs. PPBS needed the poor to be legible as programme effects, not as people with specific material requirements.

### 3. The Moynihan Problem: Turning Poverty into Pathology
Daniel Patrick Moynihan's 1965 report *The Negro Family: The Case for National Action* deployed multivariate statistical analysis to argue that Black family instability was a self-perpetuating "tangle of pathology." The statistical move — controlling for income to isolate a residual "family structure" effect — quietly relocated the locus of the problem from structural racism and labour market discrimination to internal behavioural dynamics. This is an operations-research framing: if the objective is to reduce Black poverty, and the model identifies family structure as a key parameter, then intervention targets family structure rather than discrimination. The mathematics did not compel this conclusion, but it made it look compelled.

### 4. The New Jersey Experiment: The Poor as Parameters
The New Jersey Income Maintenance Experiment (1968–72), funded by OEO and designed at the Institute for Research on Poverty, was the first large-scale randomised controlled trial in social science. Its explicit purpose was to test Milton Friedman's negative income tax: would a guaranteed minimum income reduce recipients' work effort? The experimental frame — treatment group, control group, outcome variable — transforms welfare claimants into subjects in an optimisation study. The primary outcome measure was labour-supply elasticity: how much does work decline per dollar of unearned income? The question of whether people could afford food, or whether they were housed, was secondary to the objective function. The experiment's ambiguous results were nonetheless weaponised in subsequent welfare debates to justify restricting universal benefits.

### 5. Legacy: From Cost-Effectiveness to Conditionality
The RAND tradition's lasting legacy is not any particular policy but a grammatical shift: welfare policy is now almost always articulated in terms of incentives, work requirements, and "dependency" reduction rather than material sufficiency. TANF's work requirements, time limits, and sanctions, and UC's claimant commitment regime in the UK, use essentially RAND-era objective-function logic: the goal is to minimise the caseload and maximise labour market participation, with benefit levels set to preserve "work incentives" rather than to cover material need. Orshansky would have recognised what was lost: the question of what people actually need to survive has been algorithmically suppressed by the question of how to change their behaviour at minimum public cost.
