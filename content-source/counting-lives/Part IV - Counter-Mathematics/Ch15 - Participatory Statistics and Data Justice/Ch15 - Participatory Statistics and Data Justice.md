# Ch15 – Participatory Statistics and Data Justice: Who Counts Gets to Count

## Core Claim
The arithmetic of power is not only a set of mathematical tools — it is a set of decisions about what to measure, whose experience generates the data, and whose categories organise it. Data justice is the political movement that names those decisions as decisions. Participatory statistics is the practice of making them differently. This chapter maps the counter-tradition at the level of representation itself: not just how welfare resources are allocated (Ch14) but what the state and community choose to measure, who is present in the data, and whose life is rendered legible by the resulting numbers. The shift from income poverty to housing security, from GDP to time-poverty, from police arrest records to community safety definitions, from administrative categories to self-defined identities — each of these is a mathematical act with political consequences, and each is being fought over, right now, in communities from Detroit to Delhi to Dundee.

## Mathematical Tools in Play
- Tool(s): Community-defined indicator frameworks (replacing administrative proxies with self-reported measures of what matters to communities); time-use survey methodology (measuring unpaid labour, care, and leisure as economic activity); participatory needs assessment (bottom-up data collection replacing top-down administrative records); CARE principles for indigenous data (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics — a formal governance framework for indigenous data sovereignty); feminist data gap analysis (systematic audit of what categories, populations, and experiences are absent from existing datasets); housing security indices (multi-dimensional measures replacing the binary housed/homeless administrative category)
- Governance function: Returns the power to define measurement categories to the communities being measured — contesting the representational monopoly of the state and capital that has been the arithmetic of power's most fundamental claim. Different measurements do not merely describe a different world; they make a different world governable, fundable, and politically actionable.

## Key Scenes / Narrative Beats
- [ ] Opening: Detroit, 2009 — the Detroit Digital Justice Coalition conducts a community technology survey in neighbourhoods that federal broadband maps show as "served"; the maps, compiled from ISP self-reporting, show 90% coverage; the community survey shows 40% actual access; the data gap is not an accident of measurement — it is the direct consequence of using ISP-reported data rather than resident-reported data; the gap determines which communities receive broadband infrastructure investment
- [ ] Kerala's People's Plan: Thiruvananthapuram, 1996 — the Communist Party of India (Marxist) government launches the People's Plan Campaign; 40% of the state's development budget is devolved to local bodies (grama panchayats); each panchayat conducts a participatory resource mapping exercise using community-developed survey instruments; the data collected — on local assets, needs, and priorities — is generated by the community it describes; the resulting development plans are not modelled from above but assembled from below
- [ ] The Algorithmic Justice League: MIT Media Lab, 2018 — Joy Buolamwini publishes "Gender Shades," demonstrating that commercial facial recognition systems misclassify darker-skinned women at rates up to 34 percentage points higher than lighter-skinned men; the error rate is not random — it reflects the composition of the training data, which over-represents the faces of those who built the systems; the paper is a masterclass in using the arithmetic of power's own tools (accuracy metrics, error rate analysis, demographic breakdown) to expose its failures
- [ ] DECODE Barcelona: Barcelona, 2017–2019 — the city government and EU Horizon 2020 project build a data sovereignty infrastructure allowing citizens to share data with the city on their own terms, retaining control over which data is shared, for what purposes, and with whom; the mathematical architecture — a cryptographic consent layer over a participatory data commons — is an explicit technical counter to the surveillance capitalism model of Ch9
- [ ] Indigenous Data Sovereignty: The First Nations Information Governance Centre's OCAP principles (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) and the Maori Data Sovereignty Network's CARE principles establish formal governance frameworks for indigenous data — asserting that data about a community belongs to that community and must be governed by its own institutions; the principles are not aspirational but operational, with concrete implications for how census data, health records, and welfare statistics involving indigenous peoples are collected, stored, and used
- [ ] The Missing Datasets: Artist and researcher Mimi Ọnụọha's "The Library of Missing Datasets" (2016) — a physical library of empty folders representing data that is not collected: police brutality, undocumented immigrant populations, people excluded from the census, informal care hours, the wealth of the global ultra-rich; the missing data is not random — it maps precisely onto the populations and phenomena that existing power structures prefer to keep invisible; the absence is a mathematical act
- [ ] Contemporary Echo: Scotland's 2022 Census and the debates around gender recognition data — the Scottish Government's decision to include a question allowing self-identification of gender alongside the biological sex question; the subsequent controversy; the ONS's different approach in England and Wales; the episode as a case study in how the measurement of identity is a site of political contest, not a technical question with a correct answer

## Argument in Sections

### 1. The View from Nowhere and the Cost of Its Pretence
Every dataset has a location. Every survey has a designer. Every indicator has a political history. The claim of statistical objectivity — that the numbers simply describe what is there — has been the arithmetic of power's most effective legitimation strategy, and it runs from Galton's anthropometric measurements through RAND's cost-effectiveness ratios to the FICO score's trade-secret algorithm. Donna Haraway's "situated knowledges" argument — that genuine objectivity requires making the position of the observer visible, not pretending it does not exist — is the epistemological foundation of this chapter. Data justice begins not with better data but with the acknowledgement that all data collection is an act performed from somewhere, by someone, for purposes that are never neutral. This section makes that argument concretely by tracing three specific absences from the datasets that govern welfare policy in the UK: unpaid care hours (absent from national accounts, present in feminist time-use research); housing insecurity short of statutory homelessness (absent from official figures, present in Shelter Scotland's surveys); and the experience of welfare conditionality (absent from DWP impact assessments, present in claimant testimony collected by CPAG and the Trussell Trust). Each absence is a choice; each choice has consequences.

### 2. Detroit and the Data Gap: Community Survey Against the State Map
The Detroit Digital Justice Coalition's 2009 broadband access survey is a methodological lesson in what happens when the people being measured take control of the measurement. Federal and state broadband maps — compiled from ISP self-reporting under FCC rules — showed most of Detroit as adequately served. The DDJC's door-to-door community survey, conducted with trained neighbourhood researchers using a community-developed questionnaire, found actual access rates 50 percentage points lower in some districts. The difference was not a technical error in the federal maps — it was a systematic consequence of using data generated by the industry being measured. This section uses the Detroit case to establish the chapter's central methodological argument: when administrative data is substituted for community data, the administrative categories determine what can be known, and the communities whose lives don't fit the categories become statistically invisible. The consequences are not merely academic — federal infrastructure investment followed the federal maps, not the community survey. This chapter's Detroit case connects forward to the book's Scottish thread: the 2021 Scottish Household Condition Survey, which measures housing quality through professional inspection, systematically underreports the damp, mould, and disrepair that residents of social housing report through qualitative research and community surveys, for the same structural reason.

### 3. Kerala's People's Plan: Statistics as Democratic Practice
The Kerala People's Plan Campaign (1996–2001) is the most ambitious attempt in democratic history to make development statistics a participatory practice rather than a technocratic one. Under the leadership of the CPI(M) government and economist Thomas Isaac, 40% of the state's development budget was devolved to approximately 1,000 grama panchayats, each of which was required to conduct a local resource survey using community-developed instruments before preparing its development plan. The surveys were not administered by statisticians — they were conducted by trained community volunteers, using questionnaires developed in local workshops that began from the question "what matters to us here?" rather than from national accounting categories. The resulting data — on local water sources, agricultural assets, health conditions, women's time-use, and infrastructure gaps — was richer, more locally specific, and more politically actionable than any top-down survey could produce. This section examines the methodology in detail, including its weaknesses (inconsistency across panchayats, data quality variation, the tension between local specificity and aggregation) and its achievements, as a practical demonstration that participatory statistics is not a theoretical aspiration but an operational practice with a documented record.

### 4. Data Feminism: Measuring What Has Been Made Invisible
Catherine D'Ignazio and Lauren Klein's *Data Feminism* (2020) provides the most systematic framework for the argument this chapter makes: that data science is a political practice, that its current politics are overwhelmingly shaped by the same power structures this book has traced, and that feminist principles — not as a set of identity politics but as an epistemological stance toward power — offer a practical alternative. This section applies their framework to three specific data gaps in UK welfare statistics. First, the gendered distribution of Universal Credit payments: UC is paid as a single household payment, meaning that in households where financial control is held by one partner, the other — disproportionately women — may receive nothing; DWP's administrative data does not record this distribution. Second, the measurement of domestic violence as an economic event: the cost of domestic abuse to the UK economy is estimated at £66 billion annually by SafeLives, but this figure does not appear in any official economic account; the women who lose earnings, housing, and health through abuse are statistically invisible as an economic category. Third, the gender pension gap: women in the UK retire with average pension wealth 40% lower than men's, a figure that standard earnings statistics obscure because they measure hourly rates rather than lifetime accumulation. Each of these gaps is an Orshansky moment: making them visible through mathematical specificity is a political act, not a technical one.

### 5. Indigenous Data Sovereignty: The CARE Principles as Counter-Framework
The First Nations Information Governance Centre's OCAP principles — Ownership, Control, Access, Possession — and the subsequent CARE principles developed by the Global Indigenous Data Alliance — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics — are formal governance frameworks for data sovereignty that challenge the foundational assumption of modern data infrastructure: that data, once collected, belongs to its collector. Indigenous data sovereignty asserts that data about a community is an extension of that community's sovereignty over itself, and must be governed by the community's own institutions according to its own values. The practical implications are substantial: census data about First Nations populations must be governed by First Nations institutions; health data about Maori communities must be accessible to Maori health providers; welfare statistics about indigenous Australians must be designed in partnership with indigenous communities. This section examines how these principles have been operationalised — the FNIGC's First Nations Regional Health Survey, the Maori Data Sovereignty Network's influence on Statistics New Zealand — and connects them to the book's Scottish thread: the ongoing debate about how Scotland's census should handle Gaelic-speaking communities, Gypsy/Traveller populations, and the Scottish diaspora, as a local expression of the same contest over who defines the categories that make a people legible.

### 6. The Glasgow Disability Alliance and the Politics of Self-Definition
The Glasgow Disability Alliance's approach to research and data collection is the chapter's closing Scottish case, and its most intimate. The GDA does not conduct research on disabled people — it conducts research with and by disabled people, using a participatory methodology in which the research questions, data collection instruments, and analytical frameworks are developed collaboratively with the communities being studied. Its 2020 report on the impact of COVID-19 on disabled people in Glasgow — produced during the pandemic by disabled researchers using peer interviewing — captured dimensions of experience (the collapse of informal support networks, the particular terror of shielding for people already socially isolated by inaccessible infrastructure, the interaction of benefit sanctions with pandemic financial pressure) that no administrative dataset recorded. The GDA's methodology is not a concession to qualitative softness — it is a claim about what rigorous measurement requires when the phenomenon being measured is lived experience in all its complexity. The contrast with DWP's quantitative impact assessments — which count benefit claims and sanction rates but cannot see the person behind the number — is the chapter's closing argument: not that quantification is wrong but that it must begin from the right question, and the right question can only be found by asking the people whose lives the numbers are supposed to describe.
