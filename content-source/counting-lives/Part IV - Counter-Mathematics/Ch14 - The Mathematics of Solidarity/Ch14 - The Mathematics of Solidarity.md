# Ch14 – The Mathematics of Solidarity: What Counting for People Looks Like

## Core Claim
The arithmetic of power has never gone uncontested. Alongside every system this book has examined — the eugenic ledger, the RAND objective function, the credit score, the claimant commitment algorithm — there has existed a counter-tradition: mathematical practices built not to rank, sort, and exclude but to pool, distribute, and include. This chapter does not argue that those alternatives are easy, complete, or sufficient. It argues that they are real, technically grounded, and genealogically connected — that solidarity has its own mathematics, and that mathematics has a history worth recovering. The pivot from Part III to Part IV is not from despair to hope but from diagnosis to construction: the same analytical attention given to the automated poorhouse is now applied to the question of what it would mean to build differently.

## Mathematical Tools in Play
- Tool(s): Transparent decision trees with published criteria and publicly auditable thresholds; participatory budgeting allocation algorithms (weighted priority voting, cumulative preference aggregation); cooperative surplus distribution formulae (patronage dividends, member equity); care economy measurement (satellite accounts, time-use surveys, shadow pricing of unpaid labour); community wealth indicators (local multiplier effect, asset ownership distribution, anchor institution spend analysis); Ostrom's design principles for commons governance as a formal framework
- Governance function: Demonstrates that the governance functions performed by the arithmetic of power — eligibility determination, resource allocation, risk distribution, value measurement — can be performed by different mathematical systems that make their criteria visible, their processes participatory, and their surpluses collective. The alternative is not the absence of mathematics but the presence of a different mathematics.

## Key Scenes / Narrative Beats
- [ ] Opening: Isle of Eigg, 1997 — the community buyout of the island from its private landlord is completed; the trust's governance structure requires a supermajority of residents for major decisions; its land allocation algorithm is published, contestable, and collectively owned; twelve miles from the mainland, a different arithmetic of power has been quietly assembled
- [ ] The Preston Model: Preston City Council, 2012 — facing austerity, the council maps its "anchor institutions" (hospital, university, housing association, council itself) and calculates how much of their combined procurement spend currently leaves the local economy; the local multiplier methodology is simple, transparent, and devastating: 5% of spend stays local; the council begins redirecting contracts to local cooperatives and suppliers; by 2019, 18% stays local; no new algorithm was needed, only a willingness to ask a different question
- [ ] Participatory Budgeting: Porto Alegre, 1989 — the Workers' Party introduces participatory budgeting; citizens directly allocate a portion of the municipal capital budget through a structured deliberative process; the mathematical mechanism — weighted priority voting across neighbourhood assemblies, aggregated through a transparent formula — is as precise as any RAND model, but its inputs are generated by the people affected rather than by analysts modelling them from outside
- [ ] The Care Economy Calculation: Hilary Cottam's Radical Help projects and the Office for National Statistics' household satellite accounts (2016) — when unpaid care work is measured using time-use survey data and shadow-priced at the market rate for equivalent services, it adds approximately £1.24 trillion to the UK economy annually; larger than manufacturing; almost entirely performed by women; entirely absent from GDP; the mathematical choice to exclude it from national accounts is the same choice that excluded Orshansky's material specificity from welfare models — the choice of what counts determines who counts
- [ ] The Scottish Land Reform: The Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003 and its successor legislation — community right to buy, crofting community right to buy, the Scottish Land Commission's transparency requirements for large landowners; Scotland's land is among the most concentrated in the developed world (432 people own half of all private rural land); the land reform programme is a mathematics of redistribution — rewriting the ownership formula, publishing the calculation, making the redistribution contestable and legal
- [ ] Contemporary Echo: The UK mutual aid networks of 2020, organising food and medicine distribution during COVID lockdowns using nothing more sophisticated than spreadsheets and WhatsApp; the mathematical infrastructure of solidarity — who needs what, who has what, how do we match them — assembled in days by people with no data science training; the contrast with the five-week UC wait is not incidental

## Argument in Sections

### 1. The Counter-Tradition: Solidarity Mathematics Has Always Existed
The arithmetic of power did not develop in a vacuum. The mutual societies, friendly societies, and cooperative movements of the nineteenth century were building mathematical systems for risk pooling, surplus distribution, and collective resource allocation at the same moment that Galton was building his anthropometric laboratory. The Rochdale Pioneers' cooperative principles (1844) included a specific formula for surplus distribution — patronage dividends proportional to member purchases rather than to capital contributed — that was an explicit mathematical rejection of investor-return logic. The actuarial traditions of the trade union movement, pooling strike funds and sick pay across memberships, were building solidarity-based risk models in direct competition with the insurance industry's individualised premium structures. This section recovers that counter-tradition not as nostalgia but as proof of concept: the mathematics of solidarity is not utopian projection but documented practice, and it predates the systems examined in Part III by decades.

### 2. Transparency as a Mathematical Property
The chapters of Part III returned repeatedly to opacity as the mechanism by which the arithmetic of power evades accountability: the FICO score is a trade secret, the UC sanction algorithm is proprietary, the Gangs Matrix criteria are operationally sensitive. This section argues that transparency is not merely a political demand but a mathematical property — a feature of some algorithmic systems and not others — and that building it in from the design stage changes what the system can do. A transparent decision tree for welfare eligibility — one whose criteria are published, whose thresholds are publicly set, and whose outputs can be traced through its logic by the person affected — is not technically inferior to a black-box classifier; in most welfare contexts it is technically superior, because the interpretability requirement forces precision about what the criteria actually are. Scotland's Social Security system, examined in Ch10, made this design choice deliberately. This section generalises from that example: what would "minimum transparency standards" for public-facing algorithms look like as a formal specification, and who already meets them?

### 3. Participatory Budgeting: When the Input Is the People
Porto Alegre's participatory budgeting process, introduced by the Workers' Party administration in 1989, is the most studied example of a public resource-allocation algorithm whose inputs are generated by the people the allocation affects. The mechanism is precise: neighbourhood assemblies identify priorities, delegates carry those priorities to regional assemblies, a weighted formula aggregates them against objective need indicators (sewage coverage, school places, road condition), and the capital budget is allocated accordingly. The formula is public. The inputs are contestable. The outputs are traceable. By 2001 the process had spread to 140 Brazilian cities and was being adopted in Europe, including in Scottish local authorities. This section examines the mathematics of participatory budgeting in detail — the weighting formula, the need indicators, the aggregation method — and asks what is lost and gained compared with technocratic budget allocation. What is lost: speed, administrative simplicity, the ability to make decisions that the public would not endorse if asked. What is gained: legitimacy, accuracy (local knowledge is better than modelled knowledge for local resource allocation), and the democratic standing of the output.

### 4. The Care Economy: Counting What Capitalism Suppresses
When the Office for National Statistics published its household satellite account in 2016, valuing unpaid care work in the UK at £1.24 trillion annually, it was performing the same operation Orshansky had performed in 1963: making visible, through mathematical specificity, what abstraction had rendered invisible. GDP counts a care home's revenue but not a daughter's care for her mother. National accounts count a restaurant meal but not the cooking of a family dinner. Labour market statistics count paid employment but not the unpaid labour — overwhelmingly performed by women — that makes paid employment possible. This is not a measurement error; it is a measurement choice, and like all the measurement choices this book has examined, it encodes a political priority. This section traces the exclusion of care from national accounts from its origins in the System of National Accounts (1953) through to present-day feminist economics' attempts to build a care-inclusive measurement framework. The Scottish Government's Wellbeing Economy approach, which includes time-poverty and unpaid care indicators alongside GDP, is the most advanced UK application of this framework — and a direct expression of the book's argument that different mathematical cultures produce different political possibilities.

### 5. The Scottish Land: Commons Mathematics Applied to Territory
Scotland's concentrated land ownership — the most extreme in the developed world among wealthy democracies, with 432 individuals owning half of all private rural land — is the territorial expression of the enclosure mathematics Federici traces to the sixteenth century. The community land reform programme, beginning with the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003 and extended in 2016, is an attempt to rewrite the ownership formula using the instruments of democratic law. The Isle of Eigg Heritage Trust, which successfully bought its island from a private landlord in 1997 after a decade of community organising, is the model: a trust structure with a supermajority governance rule, a published land allocation policy, and a community benefit obligation written into the title. The mathematics is simple — a threshold voting rule and a published allocation formula — but its political significance is large: it asserts that land governance can be transparent, participatory, and oriented toward community benefit rather than rental extraction. The Scottish Land Commission's current work on land value capture — requiring large landowners to publish ownership structures and development intentions — extends the transparency principle from cooperative governance to private ownership. This section reads the Scottish land reform programme as a case study in solidarity mathematics: not perfect, not complete, but technically grounded and politically real.

### 6. COVID's Accidental Experiment: Mutual Aid as Proof of Concept
The mutual aid networks that assembled across the UK within days of the March 2020 lockdown were not planned, not funded, and not algorithmically sophisticated. They used spreadsheets to map need, WhatsApp to coordinate volunteers, and handwritten notes to record deliveries. In the London Borough of Lambeth, a network of 4,000 volunteers was distributing food and medicine to 1,500 vulnerable residents within a week of forming. In Glasgow, Govanhill Baths Community Trust — already a working example of commons governance — repurposed its community kitchen as a food hub serving 500 families daily. The mathematics of these systems was legible, contestable, and accurate in the way that matters most: it connected actual need with actual resource. The contrast with Universal Credit — whose five-week wait, digital-by-default interface, and opaque sanction algorithm were unchanged by the pandemic — was not lost on the people inside both systems simultaneously. This section does not argue that mutual aid can substitute for a properly funded welfare state. It argues that the mathematical principles underlying mutual aid — visible criteria, participatory input, solidarity-based distribution — are not naive idealism but demonstrated practice, and that the question for Part IV is what it would take to build those principles into permanent institutions.
