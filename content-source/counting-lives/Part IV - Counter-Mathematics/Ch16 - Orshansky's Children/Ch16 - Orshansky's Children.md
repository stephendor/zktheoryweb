# Ch16 – Orshansky's Children: Researchers Carrying the Torch

## Core Claim
Mollie Orshansky is not a historical footnote. She is a living tradition. Across five decades of welfare retrenchment, algorithmic displacement, and the progressive abstraction of poverty into data points, a dispersed community of researchers, statisticians, and activist-scholars has continued her project: insisting that poverty is material, specific, and measurable without losing the human being inside the measurement. This chapter profiles that tradition — not as hagiography but as intellectual genealogy. These are Orshansky's children not because they all cite her (many do not) but because they share her core methodological commitment: that the right question is always "what do people actually need and experience?", and that answering it honestly requires resisting every institutional pressure to substitute an administratively convenient abstraction for the real thing. The chapter is also, necessarily, a reckoning with what it costs to do this work inside institutions built on different principles.

## Mathematical Tools in Play
- Tool(s): Multidimensional poverty index (Alkire-Foster method — simultaneous deprivation across weighted dimensions); relative deprivation measure (Townsend Index — systematic resource deprivation relative to customary living standards); capabilities approach indicators (Sen/Nussbaum — measuring what people can do and be, not just what they have); participatory poverty assessment (ATD Fourth World — community-defined poverty dimensions); feminist national accounting (Elson — care-inclusive satellite accounts); lived experience methodology (combining qualitative testimony with quantitative measurement to validate and contest administrative data)
- Governance function: Each of these methodological traditions represents a different answer to the book's founding question — what should welfare mathematics measure? — and each has been developed in direct contestation with the administrative and technocratic traditions examined in Parts I–III. Their common feature is the insistence that the person being measured must be recognisable in the resulting number.

## Key Scenes / Narrative Beats
- [ ] Opening: Oxford, 2010 — Sabina Alkire and James Foster present the Multidimensional Poverty Index to the United Nations Development Programme; the index measures poverty across ten indicators in three dimensions (health, education, living standards); a household is poor if it is deprived in more than 30% of weighted indicators; 104 countries adopt it; the UNDP replaces its income-only poverty headline figure; a single methodological choice — that poverty is more than income — shifts the political visibility of a billion people
- [ ] Peter Townsend in the Archive: Bristol, 1979 — Townsend publishes *Poverty in the United Kingdom*, the largest social survey ever conducted in Britain; his relative deprivation measure defines poverty not as a fixed nutritional threshold (Rowntree's method) but as the inability to participate in the customary living standards of one's society; the definition is political because it makes poverty a relational concept — you cannot be poor alone, only poor relative to others; the implication is that poverty cannot be solved by raising a floor but only by narrowing the distance between floor and ceiling
- [ ] Ruth Levitas and the Utopian Method: Bristol, 2013 — Levitas publishes *Utopia as Method*, arguing that social science needs a third register beyond description and critique: the imagining of alternatives; her earlier work on social exclusion discourse shows how the language of "exclusion" quietly substitutes participation in paid labour for the richer concept of human flourishing; her argument is methodological as much as political — the measure shapes what can be demanded, and demanding the right things requires first imagining them
- [ ] ATD Fourth World in the Room: London, 2014 — ATD Fourth World's participatory poverty research project, "The Roles People in Poverty Play in Society," brings together people with lived experience of poverty as co-researchers alongside academics; they develop their own poverty indicators, including dimensions (institutional maltreatment, social maltreatment, disempowerment) that never appear in official measures; the resulting framework is submitted to the UN Special Rapporteur on Extreme Poverty as evidence that official poverty measures systematically misrepresent the experience of poverty
- [ ] The Women's Budget Group's Gendered Spending Review: London, 2020 — the WBG publishes its analysis of the 2020 Spending Review showing that 86% of the net fiscal burden of austerity measures since 2010 has fallen on women; the methodology combines HMRC tax data, DWP benefit statistics, and ONS time-use data in a way that the Treasury's own impact assessment does not; the number — 86% — is not contested; it is ignored; the section examines what it means to produce a correct number that is structurally excluded from the decision it should inform
- [ ] Nick Bailey in Glasgow: Glasgow, ongoing — Nick Bailey's poverty research at the University of Glasgow, using the Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation and community survey data, consistently shows concentrations of poverty in Glasgow's post-industrial communities that official UK-wide measures obscure; his work on "destitution" — a category below official poverty, involving the inability to meet basic physical needs — gives numerical form to experiences that UC's administrative data renders invisible; the local precision of his methodology is explicitly in Orshansky's tradition
- [ ] Contemporary Echo: The Scottish Government's Child Poverty (Scotland) Act 2017 — the only piece of UK legislation to set statutory child poverty targets with required annual reporting; its four headline measures include both relative and absolute poverty, material deprivation, and combined low income and material deprivation; the multi-measure approach is a direct legislative expression of Townsend's relative deprivation argument and Alkire's multidimensional methodology; it is also, quietly, a rebuke to Westminster's approach

## Argument in Sections

### 1. The Methodological Inheritance: What Orshansky Left Behind
Mollie Orshansky's poverty line was not, in the end, a poverty line — it was an argument. The argument was that poverty is specific, that specificity requires data about actual households and their actual needs, that the resulting measure will be complex and contestable, and that the complexity and contestability are features, not bugs. The administrative simplification of her 124 thresholds into a single indexed figure betrayed the argument; but the argument survived, carried forward by researchers who understood what had been lost and why it mattered. This section maps the methodological inheritance: Peter Townsend's relative deprivation measure, which extends Orshansky's material specificity into the social domain; Amartya Sen's capabilities approach, which generalises it beyond nutrition into the full range of human functionings; and Sabina Alkire's multidimensional poverty index, which operationalises Sen's framework into a measure that can be applied at national scale without losing the specificity that makes it honest. The genealogy is not a straight line — these thinkers disagreed with each other on important points — but the family resemblance is real: all of them are doing what Orshansky did, which is refusing to let administrative convenience override the obligation to see the person behind the number.

### 2. Townsend's Relativity: Poverty as Social Distance
Peter Townsend's 1979 survey of poverty in the United Kingdom remains the most ambitious piece of poverty research ever conducted in Britain: 2,052 households, 60 items on a deprivation index, a systematic attempt to measure the distance between how people actually live and what their society considers normal. Townsend's key methodological move — defining poverty as the inability to participate in customary living standards, rather than as falling below a nutritional threshold — was politically explosive precisely because it was mathematically rigorous. A relative measure rises automatically with living standards; poverty defined this way cannot be solved by growth alone, because if everyone gets richer at the same rate, the distance between the poor and the average does not change. The political implication — that reducing poverty requires redistribution, not just growth — was unwelcome to every government that used poverty statistics to evaluate its own performance. Townsend spent the last three decades of his life watching his methodology be sidelined in favour of income thresholds that could be moved by economic growth without redistribution. This section reads his career as the story of a correct measurement losing to a convenient one, and connects it to every chapter in Part III where the same substitution has been made.

### 3. Alkire and the Multidimensional Turn: Technical Rigour in the Orshansky Tradition
Sabina Alkire and James Foster's development of the Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI) for the UNDP (2010) is the most significant methodological innovation in poverty measurement since Orshansky's matrices, and it shares the same core commitment: that poverty is not one thing but many things simultaneously, and that measuring it honestly requires measuring those things separately before asking whether they co-occur. The AF method is technically precise: a household is identified as deprived on each indicator separately (using dimension-specific thresholds); it is then identified as multidimensionally poor if its weighted deprivation score exceeds a cross-dimensional threshold (typically 30–33%); the resulting index can be decomposed by dimension, by population group, and by geography. The decomposability is the methodological gift: unlike a single income threshold, the MPI tells you which dimensions of deprivation are driving poverty in which communities, enabling targeted rather than generic policy responses. This section explains the mathematics accessibly and then examines the MPI's political life — how it was adopted, how it was resisted, how it has been used to make visible forms of poverty (educational deprivation, cooking fuel poverty, child nutrition) that income measures cannot see — as a case study in what technically rigorous solidarity mathematics can achieve inside international institutions.

### 4. ATD Fourth World: When the Measured Do the Measuring
ATD Fourth World's participatory poverty research programme is the methodological limit case of this chapter's argument: a research framework in which people with lived experience of poverty are not research subjects but co-researchers, contributing to the design of indicators, the collection of data, and the interpretation of results. The organisation's 2014 UK project, which developed a poverty framework including dimensions invisible to official measures — institutional maltreatment by welfare and social services, social maltreatment through stigma and exclusion, the disempowerment of having decisions made about you by people who have never experienced your situation — produced findings that challenged not just what poverty statistics measure but how they are produced. The methodological claim is epistemological as much as political: people with lived experience of poverty know things about poverty that researchers without that experience cannot access, and those things are relevant to measurement as well as to narrative. This section connects ATD's methodology to Haraway's situated knowledges (Ch15), to Orshansky's own biography (Ch1), and to the Glasgow Disability Alliance's research model (Ch15) — building a picture of a methodological tradition in which position is a resource rather than a bias.

### 5. The Women's Budget Group and the 86% Number
The Women's Budget Group's gendered analysis of UK fiscal policy is the most sustained application of feminist economics to real-time policy scrutiny operating in Britain. Their 2020 Spending Review analysis — showing that 86% of the net fiscal burden of austerity measures since 2010 had fallen on women — was produced by combining data sources that the Treasury's official impact assessment kept separate: HMRC tax records, DWP benefit statistics, and ONS time-use surveys measuring unpaid care. The number is not disputed; it is structurally excluded from the decision-making process it should inform, because Treasury impact assessments are not required to be disaggregated by gender. This section reads the WBG's work as a case study in the politics of a correct number: producing rigorous, technically unimpeachable evidence of gendered harm that is systematically ignored by the institutions it addresses. The WBG's response — sustained public advocacy, coalition building, and the development of an alternative feminist economic framework — is also the chapter's model for what ethical measurement work looks like when the institution does not want to hear it. The section connects to Janet Philip's work on the Beveridge Report (Ch3): women doing the statistical labour that makes welfare policy legible, while the policy's design reflects other priorities.

### 6. Scotland's Children: The Child Poverty Act and its Methodological Ambitions
The Child Poverty (Scotland) Act 2017 — passed with cross-party support in Holyrood, the only legislation of its kind in the UK — is the most direct legislative expression of the Orshansky-Townsend-Alkire methodological tradition in British law. Its four statutory targets measure child poverty using relative income, absolute income, combined low income and material deprivation, and persistent poverty (low income in three of the previous four years). The multi-measure approach is a deliberate legislative rejection of the Westminster government's 2016 decision to abolish statutory child poverty targets based on Townsend-style relative measures and replace them with non-income indicators of "life chances." Scotland's Act says, in statute, that poverty is both relative and material, both current and persistent, and that measuring it honestly requires more than one number. The annual progress reports required by the Act — published by the Scottish Government with full methodology documentation — create a public, contestable, annually updated record of child poverty in Scotland that has no equivalent in England and Wales. Nick Bailey's Glasgow research, Peter Matejic's JRF Scotland analysis, and the Poverty Alliance's advocacy work feed directly into that reporting framework. This section reads the Scottish Child Poverty Act as Orshansky's methodological inheritance enacted in law — imperfect, contested, and incomplete, but real.
