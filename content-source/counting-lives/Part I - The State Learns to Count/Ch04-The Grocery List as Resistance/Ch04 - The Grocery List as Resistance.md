# Ch4 – The Grocery List as Resistance: What Was Lost and Why It Matters

## Core Claim
The postwar moment produced two mathematics of poverty simultaneously. 
The first was actuarial and administrative — Beveridge's flat-rate 
model, the national accounts, the contribution record — built for 
scale and consistency at the cost of material specificity. The second 
was granular and insurgent — Orshansky's 124 thresholds, Townsend's 
deprivation index, the National Welfare Rights Organization's budget 
demands, the Child Poverty Action Group's household surveys — built 
from the bottom up, from actual kitchens and actual grocery receipts, 
insisting that poverty was specific and that specificity was the 
condition of honesty. The first mathematics won. This chapter is 
about why the second mathematics lost, what it cost, and why its 
loss matters for everything that follows in this book. Part II will 
show what happened when the ideology of optimisation colonised the 
administrative infrastructure the postwar welfare state had built. 
But before that colonisation could happen, the alternative — the 
grocery list, the deprivation index, the welfare rights budget — 
had to be defeated. This chapter traces that defeat as a political 
event, not a technical one, and recovers what was lost in it.

## Mathematical Tools in Play
- Tool(s): Material specificity as epistemological method 
  (measuring what households actually need rather than what 
  administrative categories assume they need); household 
  budget survey design (the NWRO's "adequate budget" demands, 
  Townsend's 60-item deprivation index, the BLS City Worker 
  Family Budget as competing methodological traditions); 
  relative deprivation indexing (Townsend's measure of 
  inability to participate in customary living standards 
  as a rival to Orshansky's absolute threshold); benefit 
  cliff calculation (the specific mathematical structure 
  of means-tested benefit withdrawal rates that trap 
  claimants in poverty — the arithmetic consequence of 
  abandoning material specificity for administrative 
  convenience); two-child limit arithmetic (the specific 
  policy mechanism that imposes a hard discontinuity 
  in the benefit formula at a third child — a mathematical 
  choice whose human consequences are precisely calculable 
  and deliberately not calculated by those who made it)
- Governance function: The grocery list tradition insists 
  that the governance function of a poverty measure is 
  not to make poverty administrable but to make it 
  visible on the terms of those who experience it — 
  which requires the measure to be specific enough 
  to be contested, transparent enough to be checked 
  against a receipt, and grounded enough in material 
  reality to be recognised by the people it describes. 
  The defeat of this tradition is the condition of 
  possibility for every administrative abstraction 
  examined in Parts II and III.

## Key Scenes / Narrative Beats
- [x] Opening: Washington DC, 1969 — Johnnie Tillmon and 
  the National Welfare Rights Organization present their 
  "adequate budget" demands to the Department of Health, 
  Education and Welfare; the budget is itemised by 
  household size and composition, priced from actual 
  grocery receipts and utility bills, and demonstrates 
  that AFDC benefit levels are systematically below 
  what households actually need to survive; the HEW 
  officials have a simpler number; Tillmon's specificity 
  is not adopted; it is, however, correct
- [x] Townsend in York: 1968 — Townsend and Brian Abel-Smith 
  publish *The Poor and the Poorest*, using Family 
  Expenditure Survey data to show that poverty in 
  Britain had not been abolished by the welfare state 
  but had been rendered invisible by the measure used 
  to assess it; the paper establishes CPAG and changes 
  British poverty research; its method is Orshansky's 
  in British dress — material specificity against 
  administrative abstraction
- [x] Margaret Reid's Unreceived Inheritance: the University 
  of Chicago home economics tradition — Reid's consumption 
  economics, her household production theory, her insistence 
  that unpaid domestic labour was economically productive 
  work — is the methodological foundation that Orshansky 
  drew on and that academic economics systematically refused 
  to incorporate; this scene traces what was lost when 
  home economics was gendered out of the economics 
  curriculum
- [x] The CEA's Victory: 1969 — the Council of Economic 
  Advisers' simpler threshold wins over Orshansky's 
  matrices in the official adoption; the indexed single 
  line replaces the 124 thresholds; poverty becomes a 
  statistical position rather than a material condition; 
  the administrative model inherits the name "Orshansky 
  poverty line" and discards the method
- [x] CPAG Scotland is Founded: Edinburgh, 1970s — the 
  Scottish branch of the Child Poverty Action Group 
  begins documenting the specific material costs of 
  poverty in Scottish conditions — higher fuel bills, 
  more expensive remote food prices, the specific 
  costs of damp housing, the particular poverty of 
  fishing communities and Highland crofts; this 
  work is the grocery list tradition applied to 
  Scottish material reality
- [x] The Benefit Cliff: a worked arithmetic example — 
  a lone parent on UC in 2026 earning slightly above 
  the work allowance, losing 55p of benefit for every 
  additional pound earned, paying 20% income tax and 
  12% National Insurance, net effective marginal rate 
  of 73%; the cliff is the mathematical consequence 
  of designing a benefit taper without reference to 
  actual household budget data; Orshansky would have 
  started with the cliff and worked back to the rate; 
  the DWP started with the rate and has never looked 
  at the cliff
- [x] Contemporary Echo: the Trussell Trust's food bank 
  referral data as the grocery list tradition's 
  contemporary expression — itemised records of 
  what households cannot afford, mapped against 
  benefit levels and sanction rates; the data that 
  the DWP's administrative systems do not collect 
  because they are not designed to see it

## Argument in Sections

### 1. Two Mathematics, One Moment
The late 1960s were the high-water mark of both 
mathematical traditions this chapter traces. The 
administrative tradition had its most sophisticated 
expression in the expanding postwar welfare states 
of Britain, Sweden, and the United States — national 
insurance, earnings-related pensions, AFDC — whose 
actuarial models were being refined by a generation 
of welfare economists trained in the Beveridge 
tradition. Simultaneously, the insurgent tradition 
was at its most politically powerful: the NWRO 
was presenting itemised household budgets to 
federal officials; Townsend and Abel-Smith were 
rewriting British poverty research from the ground 
up; CPAG was building the organisational infrastructure 
for a sustained campaign against poverty measured 
on its own terms. Both traditions were mathematically 
rigorous. Both were politically engaged. The question 
of which would govern welfare policy was settled not 
by argument but by power — specifically, by the power 
of administrative convenience over material accuracy, 
and of communicability over specificity. This section 
establishes that contest as the chapter's central 
argument, framing what follows as a political history 
rather than a technical one.

### 2. Johnnie Tillmon's Budget: Welfare as a Women's Issue
Johnnie Tillmon's 1972 essay "Welfare is a Women's 
Issue" is the most direct statement available of what 
the grocery list tradition demands and what the 
administrative tradition suppresses. Tillmon, who 
became the NWRO's executive director after years as 
a welfare recipient and community organiser in 
Nickerson Gardens, Los Angeles, built her argument 
from numbers: AFDC benefit levels in California, 
the actual cost of a week's groceries for a family 
of four, the hourly rate at which welfare recipients 
were being asked to work off their benefits, the 
gap between the two. The numbers are specific, 
sourced from receipts and utility bills and 
grocery store price surveys — the same methodology 
Orshansky had used at the SSA, now deployed as 
political advocacy by a Black woman welfare recipient 
whose knowledge of poverty was direct rather than 
statistical. The NWRO's broader "adequate budget" 
campaigns were built on the same methodology — 
itemised household costs, priced by local researchers, 
submitted to welfare agencies as evidence of what 
benefit levels would have to be to meet actual need. 
This section reads Tillmon and the NWRO as the 
grocery list tradition's most politically radical 
expression: not researchers studying poverty from 
outside but people inside it demanding that the 
mathematics of governance begin from their receipts 
rather than from actuarial projections.

### 3. Townsend's Britain: Relative Deprivation Against Flat Rates
Peter Townsend and Brian Abel-Smith's 1965 pamphlet 
*The Poor and the Poorest* performed for British 
poverty research what Orshansky's 1963 paper had 
performed for American: it demonstrated, using the 
government's own data, that poverty had not been 
abolished by the welfare state but had been hidden 
by the measure used to assess it. The Family 
Expenditure Survey data showed that a substantial 
proportion of British households were living below 
the National Assistance scale rates — meaning they 
were below the welfare state's own implicit 
subsistence threshold. Townsend went further in 
his 1979 survey: developing a 60-item deprivation 
index that measured poverty not as a position 
below a fixed threshold but as the inability to 
participate in the customary living standards of 
British society. The index was built from actual 
household surveys asking what people had and did 
not have — holidays, a warm coat, a Sunday meal — 
and its methodology was the British expression of 
the same epistemological commitment Orshansky made 
with her grocery receipts: that poverty is specific, 
that specificity requires data about actual households, 
and that the resulting measure will be complex, 
contestable, and more honest than the alternative. 
This section reads Townsend as Orshansky's British 
counterpart and the CPAG's founding research as the 
institutional expression of the grocery list tradition 
in British policy — and traces how both were 
progressively marginalised as means-testing returned 
and the administrative model reasserted its 
governance priority.

### 4. Margaret Reid's Unreceived Inheritance
The methodological tradition Orshansky drew on — 
home economics, consumption research, household 
production theory — had its most sophisticated 
American expression in Margaret Reid's work at 
the University of Chicago. Reid's 1934 *Economics 
of Household Production* argued that unpaid 
domestic labour was economically productive work 
that should be measured and valued in national 
accounts; her consumption research methodology 
insisted that household budget data was the 
primary empirical source for understanding 
economic welfare, not the labour market statistics 
that mainstream economics preferred. Reid's 
framework was methodologically superior to any 
of the administrative models examined in Ch3 
— it could capture both paid and unpaid labour, 
both market and non-market production, both 
individual and household-level welfare — and 
it was systematically excluded from the economics 
curriculum as home economics was reclassified 
as a feminised vocational subject rather than 
a scientific discipline. This section traces 
that exclusion as a political event with 
mathematical consequences: the tools that 
would have enabled a welfare economics grounded 
in actual household life were gendered out of 
the discipline before they could shape the 
postwar settlement, and the Beveridge model's 
actuarial abstraction filled the space they 
left. The chapter's gender thread argument 
reaches its Part I conclusion here: the 
suppression of home economics is not a 
curiosity of mid-century academic politics 
but a foundational political choice that 
determined what welfare mathematics could 
and could not see.

### 5. Scotland's Specific Costs: The Grocery List in the Highlands
The grocery list tradition's core claim — that poverty 
is specific, and that specificity requires data about 
actual households in actual places — has a particular 
resonance in the Scottish context that the flat-rate 
actuarial model was least equipped to capture. The 
specific material costs of poverty in Scotland 
diverge from UK-wide averages in ways that are 
economically significant and administratively 
invisible: heating costs are higher and heating 
seasons longer; food prices in remote and rural 
areas are substantially above urban equivalents; 
damp housing is more prevalent and more costly 
to address; transport poverty is more acute in 
communities without public transport infrastructure. 
CPAG Scotland's founding work in the 1970s began 
documenting these specific costs — building, in 
effect, a Scottish grocery list that demonstrated 
the inadequacy of UK-wide benefit rates for Scottish 
material conditions. The Red Clydeside labour 
movement's earlier counter-statistical tradition — 
the Glasgow rent strikes, the ILP's cost-of-living 
surveys, the Scottish miners' wage campaign data — 
was the precursor: working-class communities in 
Scotland had been producing their own material 
specificity against administrative abstraction 
since the beginning of the twentieth century. 
This section reads that tradition as the Scottish 
expression of the grocery list resistance, 
planting the thread that Ch13 (the respectability 
calculus) and Ch16 (Orshansky's children) will collect.

### 6. The Defeat and Its Cost: What Administrative Abstraction Cannot See
When the CEA's simpler threshold defeated Orshansky's 
matrices in 1969, when Beveridge's flat-rate model 
displaced the material specificity of the household 
budget surveys, when means-testing returned in the 
1970s dressed in the language of targeting 
efficiency — in each case, what was lost was not 
just a methodological preference but a capacity: 
the capacity of welfare mathematics to see what 
it cannot measure with aggregate tools. The 
administrative model cannot see the benefit 
cliff — the specific arithmetic of withdrawal 
rates, tax, and National Insurance that produces 
effective marginal rates above 70% for claimants 
moving into low-paid work — because it was not 
designed to look at it from the claimant's 
perspective. It cannot see the two-child limit's 
material consequence — the specific reduction 
in per-child spending among the families it 
affects, calculable from grocery receipts and 
school uniform costs — because it measures 
policy inputs rather than household outcomes. 
It cannot see the food bank queue — the 
people whose benefit has been sanctioned or 
delayed and who are below the poverty line 
by a margin that the line itself does not 
record — because the measure's floor is the 
threshold, and below the threshold there is, 
administratively, nothing. This section names 
these specific blindnesses as the cost of 
the defeat traced in the preceding sections, 
and frames them as the condition that Part 
II's computational turn will inherit and 
accelerate rather than correct.
