# 3. Townsend's Britain: Relative Deprivation Against Flat Rates

On the evening of 23 December 1965, Brian Abel-Smith appeared on ITV's nine o'clock news to publicise a pamphlet he had written with Peter Townsend. The following morning he repeated the exercise on BBC Radio. The pamphlet, *The Poor and the Poorest*, was seventy-eight pages long, published by G. Bell and Sons, and built from the Ministry of Labour's own Family Expenditure Survey microdata. Its finding was precise and, in the political climate of Harold Wilson's Britain, politically detonating: poverty in the United Kingdom had not been abolished by the Beveridge welfare state. It had risen. Between the 1953–54 household expenditure survey and the 1960 survey, the proportion of households living in poverty had increased from roughly ten percent to roughly eighteen percent — from some four million people to approximately 7.5 million. The pamphlet went to the Prime Minister in the form of a letter on 23 December 1965, signed by thirty-seven academics and practitioners, demanding urgent action on family poverty. By the time Wilson had the letter, CPAG had been meeting since March at Toynbee Hall. Abel-Smith's Christmas Eve television appearance was not spontaneous; it was a campaign.

The methodology behind that campaign was the British expression of the same epistemological commitment Orshansky was making at the Social Security Administration simultaneously, without the two projects being coordinated. Abel-Smith and Townsend worked from a definition of poverty as living below 140 percent of the current National Assistance scale rate appropriate to the household — the benefit the state itself acknowledged as a minimum — plus actual housing costs. The 140 percent multiplier was not arbitrary. It reflected the reality that bare-minimum National Assistance was not a functional income: it left households unable to meet irregular expenses, unable to replace worn-out items, unable to sustain the modest margin between survival and crisis that any honest standard of adequacy had to include. By anchoring the threshold to National Assistance plus a margin, Abel-Smith and Townsend were doing what Orshansky had done by multiplying food costs by three: using the state's own administrative categories as the foundation, and then insisting that a standard of adequacy built from those categories on their own terms was still above what the state was actually paying to the poor [@abelsmith1965poor]. The indictment was internal to the system's own premises.

The pamphlet's specific analytical finding — that poverty was concentrated among pensioners and large families, that it had grown during a period of postwar affluence, that the welfare state's own data showed it — was the finding the administrative model had not been designed to produce. The Ministry of Labour collected the Family Expenditure Survey to study cost-of-living patterns and aggregate consumption; it had not been commissioned to identify the households below an adequacy threshold, and its own publications had not done so. Abel-Smith and Townsend repurposed government data as a poverty audit: the same weekly expenditure diaries that told the Treasury about food price inflation told Abel-Smith and Townsend that millions of British households could not meet the government's own minimum. The grocery-list tradition's epistemological claim — that poverty is specific and material, and that specificity requires looking at what actual households actually spend — was here being made not from community organising but from a reanalysis of official data that the state had collected for different purposes and was therefore reading only partially.

The data told the story. The University of Edinburgh's social scientists — Tom Burns from Sociology, G. M. Carstairs from Psychiatry, Richard Scott from General Practice — were among the thirty-seven who signed the December letter, placing Scotland inside the founding coalition of British poverty advocacy at the moment of its formation. Scotland's poverty was higher than the UK average in every dataset available, though the specific figures were not yet produced on Scottish-specific terms; the FES data that Abel-Smith and Townsend used to make the national case was showing a pattern of higher deprivation in Scotland that the aggregate UK figure smoothed over. The founding of CPAG was also, from the beginning, a statement that the flat-rate national measure was not an adequate instrument for the material specificity of Scotland's poverty.

Townsend's own most ambitious attempt to make material specificity the foundation of poverty measurement came with the *Poverty in the United Kingdom* survey, which he conducted in 1968 and 1969 across fifty-one parliamentary constituencies — the same period in which the NWRO was presenting adequate budgets to the HEW, the same moment when both traditions were simultaneously at their height. The survey covered more than two thousand households and produced, among much else, a sixty-item deprivation index built from questions about what people had and did not have: a cooked breakfast most days of the week, a Sunday meal, a holiday away from home in the past year, a warm coat, two pairs of all-weather shoes, children's parties on birthdays, a refrigerator, adequate furniture, access to housing without damp [@townsend1979poverty]. The index was not designed to catalogue luxury or measure aspiration. It was designed to measure what Townsend called relative deprivation: the inability to participate in the patterns of living, customs, and activities that British society in 1968 considered ordinary — not lavish, not aspirational, but ordinary. The holiday away from home, the birthday party, the Sunday meal were proxies for social participation, for the kind of life that a working household in postwar Britain could expect to live. Their absence was not misfortune. It was poverty.

The sixty-item index was a technical achievement in the same sense that Orshansky's 124 thresholds were a technical achievement: it preserved complexity rather than discarding it. Where Orshansky had insisted on 124 distinct threshold configurations because households in different configurations had different needs, Townsend insisted on sixty deprivation indicators because poverty has sixty dimensions that a single income figure cannot see. The warm coat and the birthday party and the week's holiday are measuring different things — material necessity, social participation, recovery from daily hardship — and measuring all three together is more honest than measuring any one of them alone. Townsend identified an empirical deprivation threshold: a point in the income distribution below which deprivation scores increased sharply, suggesting a level at which participation in ordinary social life broke down [@townsend1979poverty, pp. 249–61]. This was his proposed objective basis for a relative poverty line — a line grounded in survey data about how people actually lived, not in administrative categories about what the state had decided to pay.

The indictment that *Poverty in the United Kingdom* delivered was different in register from *The Poor and the Poorest* but identical in structure. Abel-Smith and Townsend in 1965 had used the state's own expenditure data to show that poverty persisted below the state's own assistance levels. Townsend in 1979 used his own survey to show that poverty defined as participation deficit was larger and more geographically concentrated than any administrative threshold could see. In both cases the argument was the same: the official measure is not measuring poverty, it is administering a threshold, and the two are different activities. The administrative threshold defines a population eligible for benefits. The poverty measure identifies households unable to live in the way their society considers ordinary. When the threshold is set lower than the poverty measure requires, the administrative apparatus will systematically describe as non-poor households that a materially grounded measure would identify as poor. This is not a measurement error. It is a political choice expressed in actuarial form.

Townsend and Abel-Smith were not the only people making this argument in Britain in the 1960s and 1970s. Frank Field, who succeeded to the directorship of CPAG in 1969, turned the organisation into a sustained campaign machine for relative poverty measurement and benefit adequacy — running press campaigns, commissioning research, and confronting the Wilson, Heath, and Callaghan governments with the same internal indictment Abel-Smith and Townsend had deployed in 1965. Tony Lynes, one of CPAG's founding figures, spent the years after the pamphlet building the welfare rights infrastructure that would help claimants navigate the gap between what the benefit system said it provided and what it actually delivered. The grocery-list tradition in Britain had institutional expression well beyond the academy: in CPAG's policy campaigns, in Townsend's sustained engagement with official poverty statistics, and in the welfare rights networks that CPAG's publications supported.

That institutional presence came under direct attack in the 1980s. The Thatcher government's rejection of relative poverty measurement was not merely rhetorical: in 1985 Norman Fowler's Social Security Review explicitly refused to adopt any relative poverty measure in its assessment of benefit adequacy, and the Department of Social Security introduced its *Households Below Average Income* series in 1988, using income distribution thresholds derived from median income, while refusing to allow the word "poverty" to appear in its pages for the better part of a decade. The argument the Thatcher government made — that if everyone's income rises, relative poverty by definition never falls, making a relative measure an instrument of permanent dissatisfaction rather than a gauge of real need — had some intellectual force. It was also a direct attack on the epistemological claim that Townsend had been making since 1965: that poverty must be measured against what a society considers ordinary, not against what the government considers administratively convenient. The Thatcher government's preferred answer was a return to absolute subsistence thresholds that, by design, fell as incomes rose. The administrative model that Section 1 identified as triumphant in the United States in 1969 won the same argument in Britain by a different route, roughly a decade later.

Townsend's relative deprivation framework survived the attack institutionally because it had penetrated European poverty measurement more deeply than it had penetrated the British welfare state's own administrative categories. The European Community's poverty line, set at 50 percent of mean equivalised expenditure in 1975 and later revised to 60 percent of median equivalised income — the measure that now underlies EU and UK after-devolution Scottish poverty statistics — is Townsend's relative deprivation argument expressed as a single threshold. European statisticians stripped away the sixty items and retained the social-participation logic in the form of a distributional position. What Townsend had argued was that poverty was the inability to live as ordinary members of society lived; the 60 percent threshold captures that argument in a form the administrative model can process. It is, in that sense, a simplified Townsend, in the same way that the official US poverty line is a simplified Orshansky: the methodological commitment to material specificity survived its institutional adoption in the form of a single threshold that preserves the frame while discarding the instrument that generated it.

The parallel with Orshansky's trajectory follows from the same structural condition. Both researchers were working on the same problem — how to measure poverty in a way that was honest about what it cost to live — from different institutional positions and in different welfare systems, but arriving at the same difficulty. The administrative apparatus needed simplicity; the honest measure required complexity. Both saw their complex measures adopted in simplified form, praised for the conclusion while the methodology was discarded. Orshansky's 124 thresholds became a single indexed line; Townsend's sixty-item deprivation index became a distributional threshold. In both cases, what was lost was the same: the capacity of the measure to be contested by the people it described, because its elements were specific enough to be checked against a receipt, a fuel bill, a birthday cake. A single threshold, however correctly positioned in the income distribution, tells a household below it nothing about which specific inadequacies are driving its poverty or what a benefit rate adequate to its actual circumstances would need to cover. The grocery list, converted into an income position, has had its most politically important property removed: it can no longer be priced.
