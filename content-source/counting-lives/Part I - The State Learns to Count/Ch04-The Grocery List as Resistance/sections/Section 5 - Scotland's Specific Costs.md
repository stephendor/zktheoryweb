# 5. Scotland's Specific Costs: The Grocery List in the Highlands

On 17 November 1915, the city of Glasgow attempted to prosecute forty-nine tenants for participating in a rent strike. The attempt failed. A mass demonstration outside the courthouse — several thousand people, with trade union delegations threatening factory walk-outs that would stop munitions production — was accompanied by something the courthouse could not dismiss by dispersing a crowd: a dossier of individual cases. For each of the forty-nine, the Glasgow Women's Housing Association had documented the specific rent ledger, the specific tenancy history, the specific date of the rent increase that the tenant had refused to pay. The case-by-case evidentiary record made individual prosecution legally awkward and politically untenable. Three weeks later, Parliament passed the Increase of Rent and Mortgage Interest (War Restrictions) Act, freezing rent at 1914 levels unless landlords could demonstrate specific material justification for an increase. The legislation was drafted to reflect the argument the strikers had been making. If landlords wanted to charge more, they had to point to something specific — a cost incurred, an improvement made. The administrative presumption that a market rate was a legitimate rate was reversed. The grocery receipt became the statutory standard.

The women who built that evidentiary record — Mary Barbour of Govan, Helen Crawfurd of Gorbals, Mary Laird and Jessie Stephen — were not researchers. They were tenants, and they were building a tradition. The Glasgow Women's Housing Association, formed jointly by the Independent Labour Party Housing Committee and the Women's Labour League in 1914, had been working for eighteen months before the November courthouse confrontation, assembling case-by-case evidence of rent increases, housing disrepair, and eviction attempts — including the evictions of soldiers' wives whose husbands were at the Front, an argument that gave the counter-arithmetic a moral dimension the War Office could not ignore. John Wheatley, the ILP councillor whose 1913 pamphlet *Eight Pound Cottages for Glasgow Citizens* had demonstrated from municipal financial data that the city's tram profits could fund working-class housing at eight shillings a week, supplied the broader political framework: a specific cost model, built from verifiable inputs, demonstrating that the gap between what working-class families paid for slum tenements and what decent housing would cost was a political choice rather than an economic inevitability. When Wheatley became Minister of Health in 1924 and passed the Housing Act that generated 508,000 council homes through forty-year subsidies set to produce rents affordable to low-paid workers, he was doing exactly what the Rent Restrictions Act had done: translating a specific cost calculation into a statutory obligation. The grocery-list tradition in Scotland had a legislative record before the welfare state existed.

The Scotland that the postwar welfare state inherited from that tradition was materially specific in ways that Beveridge's flat-rate model was not designed to see. Scotland's housing stock in 1945 was predominantly tenement housing — stone-built, solid-walled, expensive to heat — alongside peripheral council estates built rapidly to low thermal performance standards and mining and fishing communities whose economic and physical geography set them structurally apart from the populations on whose average the Ministry of National Insurance had set National Assistance scale rates in 1948. These differences were not incidental: they compounded at the household level into a cost of living that diverged materially from the UK average at which benefit rates were set. The University of Edinburgh's social scientists who signed CPAG's founding letter in December 1965 — Tom Burns from Sociology, G. M. Carstairs from Psychiatry, Richard Scott from General Practice — were, among other things, putting Scotland's signature on a campaign whose founding data had shown that poverty in Britain was concentrated precisely in the places where the flat-rate model's assumptions about average costs were most wrong.

CPAG's first major survey evidence specific to Scottish children, the 1973 National Child Development Study published as *Born to Fail?*, demonstrated the extent of that divergence in numbers. Across Britain, one child in sixteen was severely disadvantaged by a combined measure of family structure, housing, and income. In Scotland, the figure was one child in ten. Greater Glasgow showed a disproportionate concentration of children in those conditions. The Strathclyde Regional Council, drawing on the study and on CPAG's subsequent advocacy, drew up a multiple deprivation strategy in 1976 that included systematic income-maximisation campaigns: its researchers estimated £75 million in unclaimed benefits in the Strathclyde Region alone — a figure that represented not a policy failure but a design feature, the welfare state's characteristic tendency to set eligibility criteria that the households most in need were least equipped to navigate. The grocery-list tradition's Scottish expression, in the 1970s, was the work of converting unclaimed entitlement into claimed income: not research into what benefit rates should be but advocacy using the rates that already existed, because the gap between what the system promised and what it delivered was already large enough to be worth documenting.

What CPAG Scotland's researchers were also beginning to document in this period, in their welfare rights guides and poverty surveys, was the three-way cost differential that makes Scotland's case structurally different from England's rather than simply more deprived. The first differential is heating. Scotland is two to three degrees Celsius colder than England as an annual average, and considerably colder in the Highlands and Islands; the heating season in Highland and island communities is six to eight weeks longer than in southern England. Approximately fifteen percent of Scottish dwellings — some 369,000 properties — are beyond the mains gas grid and rely on heating oil, electricity, or solid fuel at substantially higher unit costs, with rural dwellings disconnected from the grid at a rate of sixty-three percent. In 2023, the median fuel poverty gap for Scottish fuel-poor households — the additional expenditure required to reach adequate warmth — was £1,250, against £417 for fuel-poor households in England. Thirty-four percent of Scottish households were in fuel poverty; in England, thirteen percent. That headline differential is the single most concise available statement of what a welfare benefit calibrated to UK-wide average heating costs costs households in Caithness and Tiree and the Western Isles: it is a benefit set for a country England lives in, not the country those households inhabit.

The second differential is food. Scotland's remote rural and island communities face food prices that are structurally higher than UK urban equivalents because they are served primarily by small local stores that cannot match the pricing of large supermarket chains and that face higher supply chain costs. Research by Scotland's Rural College (SRUC) has documented a statistically significant remoteness premium: food basket prices in local community stores are approximately forty-four percent higher on the mainland and twenty-seven percent higher on the islands than their supermarket equivalents, with the Scottish Government's poverty evidence review translating these retail-level differentials into a population-level estimate of weekly food costs running up to thirteen percent higher for island communities. A 2023 survey in Uist and Barra found that a standard basket of basic fruit and vegetables cost twenty-eight percent more locally than the equivalent Tesco Online order, with less than half the items readily accessible at all. The SRUC research identifies what it calls the out-shopping effect: rural households who can access transport travel to urban supermarkets to shop more cheaply, suppressing the measured premium in aggregate data but converting it into a transport cost that falls on the same budget. For households without cars — disproportionately elderly, disabled, and poorest — local prices are not a supplementary option but the only option, and those prices are substantially higher than the national average that welfare rates assume.

The third differential is housing quality, specifically dampness. Scotland's stone-built tenement housing and its postwar peripheral estates have historically had higher rates of structural damp than English housing of comparable vintage: dampness that is more prevalent, more resistant to standard remediation in solid-walled buildings, and more costly to address through heating alone in a country where heating is already more expensive. The compounding effect is precise: a damp property requires more energy to achieve a given indoor temperature; the household is paying higher fuel costs per degree of warmth; and the physical consequences — mould, respiratory illness, school absence — generate downstream costs that appear nowhere in the benefit calculation that was supposed to prevent them.

Scotland's Fuel Poverty (Targets, Definition and Strategy) Act 2019 represents the clearest contemporary instance in British policy of the grocery-list epistemology being encoded in statute. The Act defines fuel poverty not as a statistical position in the national income distribution but as the condition of a specific household that cannot maintain adequate warmth after meeting its other essential costs and remain above Scotland's Minimum Income Standard. That definition requires measuring what a specific household's heating costs actually are, at the fuel prices available to that specific household, in the climate that household inhabits. The Scottish Government's 2025 cost-of-remoteness report extended the same logic to food and transport, incorporating price differentials for remote rural and island communities into the Minimum Income Standard uplift applied when calculating fuel poverty thresholds. Scotland is building, in effect, a location-specific cost matrix — different thresholds for different material conditions — that is structurally analogous to Orshansky's household-specific threshold matrices, applying the same insight to geography that she applied to household composition. A household in Stornoway needs a different income to achieve the same standard of living as a household in Sheffield, for reasons that can be measured, sourced from survey data, and openly contested. The Scottish measure says so in statute. Universal Credit does not.

That contrast — between what Scotland's devolved welfare institutions are building and what UK-wide benefit rates assume — is the contemporary form of the chapter's central argument. The Red Clydeside women who documented forty-nine individual rent ledgers in November 1915 were making the same epistemological claim as the SRUC researchers who documented the Uist basket in 2023: poverty is specific, the specificity is measurable, and a governance mathematics that averages it away has made a choice about what it will and will not see. Beveridge made it in 1942, and the National Assistance Act 1948 enacted it: a single national scale, administratively legible, calibrated to no specific geography. Scotland has been contesting it, intermittently and with varying legislative success, for more than a century. Chapter 13 and Chapter 16 will return to the current state of that contest; what this chapter establishes is its origin — in the tenement rent books of Govan, in the ILP's cost-of-living surveys, in *Born to Fail?*'s one-in-ten, and in the £1,250 heating gap that a benefit set without reference to where you live will never close.
