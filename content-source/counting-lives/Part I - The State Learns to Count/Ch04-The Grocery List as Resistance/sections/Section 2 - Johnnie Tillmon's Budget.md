# 2. Johnnie Tillmon's Budget: Welfare as a Women's Issue

In 1969, a delegation from the National Welfare Rights Organization arrived at the Department of Health, Education and Welfare in Washington with a budget. It was not a federal budget, not an actuarial projection, not a table of aggregate expenditure. It was a household budget: itemised costs, priced from grocery receipts and utility bills and clothing store tags, broken down by family size and composition, demonstrating in columns that AFDC benefit levels were, in every configuration the NWRO had surveyed, below what a household of that size needed to survive. The delegates were welfare mothers, most of them Black, many of them from chapters that had begun as women sitting around kitchen tables in housing projects, comparing their benefit notices with their grocery receipts and arriving at the same arithmetic. The HEW officials on the other side of the table had a simpler number. The meeting ended without the NWRO's adequate budget being adopted as the basis for benefit rates. Tillmon's specificity was not institutionalised. It was, however, correct.

The woman who led the NWRO to that table had built her understanding of welfare mathematics from the ground up. Johnnie Tillmon was born in Scott, Arkansas, in 1926, the daughter of sharecroppers, and moved to the Nickerson Gardens housing project in Watts, Los Angeles, in the 1950s. In 1963, illness interrupted her work in a laundry and she was placed on welfare — a transition that, as she later described it, made visible to her in an immediate and administrative way what had always been structurally true: that the welfare system was designed to keep women poor on terms that preserved their availability as a labour reserve and an unpaid care workforce. In 1963 she organised Aid to Needy Children Mothers Anonymous at Nickerson Gardens, one of the first welfare mothers' organisations in the country, which within three years had affiliated with the newly founded National Welfare Rights Organization and elected Tillmon to its leadership. By the late 1960s she was the NWRO's executive director, and the adequate budget campaigns she had developed from kitchen-table arithmetic had become the organisation's primary tool for confronting federal welfare policy with the evidence the federal welfare system was not designed to collect.

The fullest single statement of what that confrontation demanded is Tillmon's 1972 essay "Welfare is a Women's Issue," published in the inaugural issue of *Ms.* magazine [@tillmon1972welfare]. The essay opens with a self-inventory: Tillmon identifies herself as "a woman," "a Black woman," "a poor woman," "a fat woman," "a middle-aged woman," and "on welfare," and then immediately quantifies the consequence — "if you're all those things, you count for nothing, except as a statistic." The move is deliberate. Welfare recipients appear in the administrative apparatus as cases, as costs, as aggregate poverty rates; they appear in Tillmon's arithmetic as women with specific expenses, specific working hours, and a specific government-defined income that does not meet them. The opening sets up what follows: a piece of welfare mathematics delivered not in the SSA's house style but in the first person, by someone inside the system rather than administering it from above.

The essay's central methodological claim is that welfare mothers already work. Tillmon calculates the labour involved in raising children on welfare — the round-the-clock childcare, the domestic production, the time spent navigating the welfare bureaucracy itself — and points out that it is unrecognised and uncompensated. "Nobody respects that. Welfare mothers are stereotyped as lazy, childbearing machines," she writes, while in practice "you can't really work and be on welfare at the same time" in any meaningful sense, because the earned income disregard rules clawed back benefits at a rate that made paid employment financially punishing [@tillmon1972welfare]. The arithmetic underlying the essay is precise even when its figures are not spelled out line by line: AFDC benefit levels in the early 1970s varied by state but were, in every state, below the Department of Labor's own "modest but adequate" budget standard for a family of four — the same standard the NWRO used to anchor its adequate budget demands. Tillmon did not need to print the two columns side by side to make the gap legible to anyone who had tried to feed four children on the lower number.

The NWRO's broader campaign formulated that gap into a specific demand. The Guaranteed Adequate Income — the GAI, the movement's central programmatic ask in its negotiations with the Nixon administration over the Family Assistance Plan — was not an abstract entitlement but a budget-anchored proposal. The figures most commonly cited in NWRO campaign materials in 1968–69 were $5,500 per year for a family of four, raised to $6,500 in 1970, derived explicitly from the Department of Labor's adequacy estimates and from the household budget surveys NWRO chapters had conducted across the country. Local chapters did not simply adopt the federal figures; they priced their own markets, their own utility rates, their own specific costs, and compared them with the DoL standard to produce city-by-city evidence of the gap between what the government said was adequate and what the government was actually paying. Methodologically, this was Orshansky's approach transposed into movement organising: start from what households actually need to buy, price it from the receipts, and derive the benefit level from the arithmetic rather than from the fiscal calendar [@piven1971regulating]. It produced a number that the Nixon administration's Family Assistance Plan — proposing a guarantee of $1,600 per year for a family of four — could not survive contact with, and the NWRO refused to endorse FAP precisely on those grounds.

That the NWRO's methodology was correct and its politics were defeated simultaneously is the argument that requires a different kind of explanation than methodological failure can provide. Bell hooks, in *Where We Stand*, identifies the mechanism [@hooks2000wherewestand]. The welfare rights movement's poverty arithmetic was produced by Black women whose class position, race, and gender placed them at the intersection of three forms of invisibility in American public life: class silence (hooks' central argument is that class is more systematically suppressed in American discourse than either race or gender, because naming it threatens capitalist ideology directly), racial exclusion, and the gendered devaluation of reproductive knowledge. What Tillmon and the NWRO's welfare mothers had was direct, material expertise in the cost of survival — precisely the knowledge that a welfare system grounded in household specificity would have had to take seriously. What they were, in the institutional categories that governed whose arithmetic was consulted, was welfare recipients: a category that the administrative apparatus had constructed to exclude its subjects from its deliberations. The adequate budget arrived at the HEW meeting bearing the wrong credentials.

hooks' analysis of how mainstream second-wave feminism reproduced class hierarchy is directly relevant here. The feminist politics that was gaining institutional purchase in the early 1970s — equal pay, workplace rights, access to professional careers — centred the aspirations of women who were already positioned to benefit from them. Tillmon's "Welfare is a Women's Issue" intervened to name what that politics could not see: that for the majority of women on welfare, the question of liberation could not be separated from the question of income, and that an income adequate for liberation had a specific dollar figure that could be checked against a grocery receipt. The essay reads, in retrospect, as a precise diagnosis of the class division within the feminist movement that hooks would theorise thirty years later — a division whose consequence was that the grocery-list tradition's most politically radical expression was also its most marginalised.

Dorothy Height and the National Council of Negro Women were pursuing a version of the same epistemological commitment through different organisational channels. Where Tillmon's strategy was confrontational — presenting the HEW with its own inadequacy in itemised form — Height's was developmental: using needs assessments drawn from women's testimony to design programmes that were adequate to the actual material conditions they addressed. The NCNW's Workshops in Mississippi, which became fully operational in 1966 and 1967, brought Black women leaders from across Mississippi's income spectrum together to document specific household needs — food, housing, income, childcare — and to use that testimony as the basis for programme design. The pig bank programme, which Height described as beginning from the direct statement by Mississippi women that their children had to eat, was a household economic instrument calibrated to what the borrowing families could actually sustain: an interest rate set not by actuarial table but by consultation with the households who would repay it. The NCNW's housing project in Gulfport — three-, four-, and five-bedroom brick homes, because the families the project served had children and needed rooms for them — was a household needs assessment translated into construction specifications and then into a HUD partnership. Height was building grocery lists into programme design at the moment Tillmon was presenting them to welfare officials.

Both efforts produced methodologically superior outcomes to what the administrative model could generate from aggregate data. Both were received by the federal apparatus as special cases, as community programmes, as social work — categories that placed them outside the genealogy of poverty *research* and therefore outside the policy infrastructure that would have allowed their methodology to become the basis for benefit rate calculation. The NCNW's household documentation exists today primarily in the organisational archives at the Mary McLeod Bethune Council House rather than in the Social Security Administration's *Bulletin*, because the SSA was not designed to publish findings from Black women's workshop testimony. The knowledge was produced. The canonisation did not happen.

The welfare mathematics that Tillmon and Height were building from the ground up between 1966 and 1975 was not defeated by being outargued. The NWRO's adequate budget figures were derived from the Department of Labor's own adequacy standards; they were not technically contestable on their own terms. What happened to them was what Piven and Cloward's expansion-contraction analysis predicts: the political conditions that had briefly made a $6,500 guarantee conceivable — the urban disorder of 1966–68, the pressure that political unrest placed on the welfare state to expand — shifted under them. Nixon took office, FAP was defeated in Congress by a coalition the NWRO had helped to build, and the NWRO's own formal organisation, with its foundation funding and its legislative strategy, lost the disruptive leverage that had been its primary source of power [@piven1971regulating]. By 1975 the organisation had collapsed, its membership had dispersed, and the adequate budget methodology it had developed over a decade of kitchen-table arithmetic had no institutional home. Tillmon died in 1995, a year before the Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act dismantled AFDC entirely and replaced it with TANF — a programme whose work requirements and time limits were precisely the mechanisms she had identified in 1972 as the instruments of a super-sexist marriage between the state and the women it claimed to support. She was still right. The arithmetic had not changed. Only the political conditions under which it could be heard had been removed.
