# 1. Two Mathematics, One Moment

In the autumn of 1968, two things were simultaneously true about poverty measurement in the United States and Britain that would not remain simultaneously true for very long. The first was that the administrative welfare state had reached its postwar maturity: AFDC rolls were expanding, the Social Security Administration's actuarial models were being refined by a generation of welfare economists trained on Beveridge and the Keynesian settlement, and the War on Poverty had produced a single official poverty line — one number, one threshold, applied to one hundred and ninety million Americans regardless of where they lived or how their households were composed. The welfare state had learned to count its poor in the way it needed to: efficiently, consistently, at scale, in a form that could be defended in a congressional hearing and printed in a newspaper. The second true thing was that, at this same moment, the most sustained challenge to that administrative mathematics was operating at the height of its institutional reach and political credibility. Johnnie Tillmon and the National Welfare Rights Organization were presenting itemised household budgets to federal officials, priced from grocery receipts and utility bills, demonstrating that AFDC benefit levels were systematically below what families needed to survive. Peter Townsend and Brian Abel-Smith had rewritten British poverty research from the ground up in 1965, using the government's own Family Expenditure Survey data to prove that a substantial minority of British households were living below the welfare state's own implicit subsistence floor. Tony Lynes, Townsend, and Abel-Smith had founded the Child Poverty Action Group on that finding and were building the organisational infrastructure for a sustained campaign against poverty measured on its own terms. Orshansky's 124 thresholds — six household sizes, four or more household types, multiple family composition categories — were published, contested by the CEA, and still formally alive as an alternative to Walter Heller's headline number.

Both traditions were mathematically rigorous. The NWRO's adequate budget methodology was as technically disciplined as anything the SSA produced: local researchers priced actual food items in actual stores, calculated utility costs by household size, and derived specific dollar figures for specific family types in specific cities. Townsend's deprivation index, built from a survey of more than two thousand households across fifty-one parliamentary constituencies, comprised sixty indicators covering diet, clothing, housing conditions, family activities, and social participation — designed to measure not just income but the material and social dimensions of ordinary life that income statistics alone could not capture. Orshansky had built her thresholds from the USDA's 1955 household food consumption surveys, multiplying economy food plan costs by a factor derived from actual household expenditure data. Each tradition required more data, more local knowledge, and more methodological sophistication to maintain than the administrative model it was contesting. Each was more accurate than that model. Neither was adopted.

What distinguished the two traditions was not technical quality but epistemological commitment. The administrative model's fundamental decision — made by Walter Heller's Council of Economic Advisers in 1963, when Heller proposed a single $3,000 annual threshold applied to all family sizes, and consolidated in the Bureau of the Budget's CPI-indexing decision of 1969 — was that a welfare mathematics must above all be administrable. A single threshold could be tracked over time, communicated to the press, and used to anchor a political argument without requiring the administrative apparatus to know anything specific about the households it described. The grocery-list tradition's fundamental commitment was the opposite: that a welfare mathematics must above all be honest, and that honesty requires specificity — specificity about household composition, about local costs, about what families actually needed to buy. The administrative model's threshold was simple precisely because it had stripped away the household-level detail that would have made it accurate. The grocery-list tradition's measures were accurate precisely because they preserved the complexity that stripped-down administration could not absorb.

Theodore Porter, writing a quarter century after the moment this chapter examines, identified this tension as one of the central dynamics of quantification in public life. Numbers gain institutional authority, Porter argues, not because they are the most accurate description of the phenomena they purport to measure but because they are the most communicable — because they discipline personal judgment, substitute impersonal rules for expert discretion, and allow decisions to appear objective while remaining politically defensible [@porter1995trust, p. 8]. A single poverty threshold embodied this logic exactly: it was trusted not because it was true but because it was impersonal, because it appeared to have been produced by a procedure rather than by a decision, because no individual official's judgment was visible in the resulting number. Orshansky's 124 thresholds refused this logic. They were the product of judgment — specific judgments about household composition, about the relationship between food costs and total household expenditure, about which categories of household required distinct measurement. Their accuracy derived from their specificity, and their specificity was what made them administratively unworkable. In Porter's terms, the poverty line's communicability was the condition of its authority, and communicability required the erasure of the material grounding that would have made it contestable.

James Scott would place the same dynamic within a longer history of how modern states acquire the capacity to act on populations [@scott1998seeing]. What the 1969 Bureau of the Budget decision accomplished, in Scott's terms, was to make household poverty legible — to reorganise a complex social phenomenon into a standardised, simplified category that could be centrally observed and administered. Heller's $3,000 threshold already did this by applying identically to a single elderly person living alone, a family of four, and a lone parent with three young children under ten. It could distinguish only two states — above the line and below it — with no further properties that administration was required to attend to. What Orshansky's matrices preserved, and what the single line discarded, was what Scott calls *mētis*: the practical, situated knowledge of how specific households in specific configurations actually sustained themselves, knowledge that could not be reduced to a single number without being destroyed. The state needed a legible poverty figure in the same way it needed legible maps and legible surnames: not because the simplified representation was more accurate than the complex reality it replaced, but because the simplified representation could be governed and the complex reality could not. What was lost was not methodological detail but the capacity of welfare mathematics to see what the administrative apparatus was not designed to look for.

But the defeat of the grocery-list tradition in the late 1960s was not primarily an epistemological event. It was a political one, and the political explanation for its timing matters as much as the intellectual account of what was methodologically at stake. Frances Fox Piven and Richard Cloward's analysis of welfare expansion and contraction across American history [@piven1971regulating] identifies a pattern that the chronology of the grocery-list tradition's defeats precisely fits. Welfare systems expand, Piven and Cloward argue, when urban disorder makes the political costs of inadequate provision exceed the fiscal and ideological costs of expansion; they contract when the disorder has subsided and the labour market again demands a compliant and economically precarious workforce. The period of the grocery-list tradition's maximum institutional presence — 1965 to 1969 — was the period of maximum urban unrest, maximum political pressure for welfare expansion, and maximum political leverage for organisations like the NWRO. When Nixon took office in January 1969, the urban disorder of 1966–68 had not disappeared, but its political momentum had shifted; the political coalition that had briefly made an adequate income demand thinkable was fracturing; and the administrative machinery that would consolidate the single threshold was newly empowered. The Bureau of the Budget's CPI-indexing decision of 1969 — which locked the official poverty line to the consumer price index and stripped away Orshansky's household-composition specificity — was the administrative consolidation of a political settlement, not a technical correction of a methodological error.

This matters because it changes what kind of story this chapter tells. The defeat of the grocery-list tradition could be narrated as an intellectual concession — a case where a more complex methodology acknowledged the legitimate claims of parsimony and practical administration. The chapter resists that narrative entirely. The grocery-list tradition was not defeated by being wrong. Orshansky's matrices were more accurate than the CEA's single threshold: she said so in writing, repeatedly, from 1965 until her retirement, and the subsequent history of poverty measurement — the Supplemental Poverty Measure of 2011, the various state-level alternatives, the persistent scholarly consensus that the official line understates poverty among households with children — has vindicated her. Townsend's relative deprivation framework became the foundation of European Union and Scottish poverty measurement precisely because no more adequate measure was produced to replace it; it was not superseded, it was sidelined. The BLS City Worker Family Budget — the administrative tradition's own most sophisticated attempt to price what a working family actually needed — was discontinued by the Reagan administration in 1981, not because its methodology had been discredited but because it would have demonstrated the inadequacy of the benefit rates being cut.

What this chapter traces is the grocery-list tradition's political defeat: the specific mechanisms by which material specificity was made administratively inconvenient, by which a simpler mathematics was made to appear more authoritative than a more accurate one, and by which the Bureau of the Budget reorganised the institutional infrastructure of the postwar welfare state around a measure that preserved the appearance of empirical grounding while surrendering the substance of it. The sections that follow tell the specific stories of that defeat in four registers: the NWRO's adequate income campaigns and Johnnie Tillmon's welfare mathematics (Section 2); Townsend and Abel-Smith's British parallel and the CPAG's founding research (Section 3); Margaret Reid's home economics tradition and its gendered exclusion from the welfare economics mainstream (Section 4); and the Scottish grocery-list tradition and its specific challenge to flat-rate UK-wide benefit levels (Section 5). Section 6 names what was lost in each case: the specific blindnesses the administrative model acquired when it discarded the material grounding that would have corrected them, and the costs those blindnesses continue to impose on households that the measure was not designed to see.

Before Part II can examine what happened when the ideology of optimisation colonised the administrative infrastructure the postwar welfare state had built, it is necessary to understand what that infrastructure looked like after the grocery-list alternative had been defeated. The cybernetic systems modellers of the 1950s and the RAND analysts of the 1960s inherited a welfare mathematics that had already made a specific set of political choices: it had traded accuracy for communicability, specificity for administrability, and the grocery receipt for the index number. Part II will show those choices being taken further, faster, and with far more ideological confidence. But the choices themselves were made here, in the late 1960s, at the moment when both mathematics were available and one of them was selected. This chapter is about that selection and about what it cost — not in the abstract, but in the specific arithmetic of benefit cliffs, two-child limits, and food bank queues that the defeated mathematics would have been precise enough to calculate.
