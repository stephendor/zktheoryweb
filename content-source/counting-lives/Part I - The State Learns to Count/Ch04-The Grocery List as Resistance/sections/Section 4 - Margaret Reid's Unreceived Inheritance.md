# 4. Margaret Reid's Unreceived Inheritance

In 1965, the same year that Brian Abel-Smith and Peter Townsend published *The Poor and the Poorest* and the same year that Mollie Orshansky's poverty thresholds entered their first official use, Gary Becker published "A Theory of the Allocation of Time" in the *Economic Journal*. The paper argued that households were not merely consumers of market goods but producers of commodities using time and purchased inputs together — that a household's real constraint was not its budget but the combination of its income and the hours available to convert that income into what Becker called "basic commodities." The argument was celebrated as a theoretical breakthrough. It became one of the most cited papers in economics, and the ideas it developed were central to the Nobel Prize in Economics that Becker received in 1992.

Margaret G. Reid had made essentially the same argument in 1934.

Reid's *Economics of Household Production*, published by John Wiley and Sons when she was completing a decade of research at the University of Chicago, argues from its opening pages that the household is "our most important economic institution" and that the enormous quantities of labour performed within it — preparing food, caring for children, managing the material infrastructure of domestic life — are economically productive work excluded from standard economic analysis by a statistical convention rather than a conceptual necessity [@reid1934economics]. Her central methodological device, which she called the delegability test, was precise: if an activity could be carried out by a paid worker — if it was substitutable with market labour — then it was productive regardless of whether a wage was attached to it. Cooking, laundering, and childcare passed this test. The labour of a mother preparing meals for her children was, under Reid's analysis, economically productive work. Its exclusion from national accounts was not analytically defensible; it was an artefact of how the production boundary had been drawn.

The difference between Reid's 1934 reception and Becker's 1965 reception is not a difference in analytical content. Multiple surveys of the household economics literature have identified Reid as a direct antecedent to Becker, with the same conceptual move — households as producers, domestic time as an economic input, opportunity cost as the framework for analysing unpaid work — made more than three decades earlier [@reid1934economics]. The difference is a difference in institutional position. Becker made his argument from within the economics department at the University of Chicago, in the idiom of formal microeconomic theory, publishing in the discipline's flagship journals. Reid made the same argument from within the University of Chicago's home economics tradition, in the idiom of consumption research and household budget analysis, publishing in a field that the economics profession had spent the preceding decades classifying as vocational and feminised. The Nobel committee's 1992 citation did not mention Reid.

The home economics tradition Reid worked in had not always been peripheral. Ellen Richards, a chemist trained at MIT and the first woman the institution admitted, founded the discipline through a series of Lake Placid Conferences beginning in 1899, and established the American Home Economics Association in 1908. Richards's explicit aim was the professionalisation of household knowledge along scientific lines: sanitary chemistry, applied nutrition, consumption economics, and household budgeting treated as objects of rigorous empirical inquiry. At the University of Chicago, Hazel Kyrk's 1923 *A Theory of Consumption* developed a strand of this tradition into a serious engagement with the economics of consumption behaviour, arguing that consumption was not a simple reflex of income but a complex social and material practice that required its own analytical framework. Reid's 1934 book extended Kyrk's consumption economics into a systematic treatment of unpaid household labour, completing what was, by the early 1930s, a coherent and technically accomplished research programme in household-centred economic analysis.

What this Chicago programme could see that the actuarial and labour-market models of the Beveridge tradition could not was precisely what the grocery-list tradition requires: the material content of household life, in its specific configuration. Reid's framework could measure both paid and unpaid labour, both market and non-market production, both cash income and the flow of services produced within the household from that income. A welfare economics built on Reid's framework would have had to ask, for any given benefit level, whether a household of specific composition could actually produce from that income the goods and services required for subsistence. It would have had to price the domestic labour involved in converting a food budget into meals, the childcare involved in converting a housing allowance into a liveable home. It would have produced, in effect, a welfare mathematics that began from the kitchen rather than from the contribution record — which is to say, it would have produced something that looked very much like Orshansky's matrices, built from food plans and household composition data, or like the NWRO's adequate budgets, priced from grocery receipts and utility bills. The lineage from Richards through Kyrk through Reid to Orshansky is direct, even if it was never taught as such in any economics curriculum.

The institutional mechanisms that severed that lineage from the welfare state's governing mathematics operated in three phases. The Smith-Hughes National Vocational Education Act of 1917 provided federal funding for vocational education in the United States and classified home economics alongside agriculture and trade training as a vocational subject. Federal money flowed to home economics departments that taught cookery and domestic management as practical skills; it did not flow to the theoretical and research programmes Kyrk and Reid were developing. Over subsequent decades, the vocational strand — better funded, more legible to state legislatures, more institutionally entrenched — came to dominate the field's public identity, making it steadily harder for the research strand to claim standing as serious economics. By the 1950s and 1960s, home economics in most American and British universities had been reclassified under names that performed the retreat from any claim to disciplinary economics: family studies, human ecology, consumer science. These terminological shifts were not neutral. They reflected and reinforced a cumulative institutional verdict that the work being done in these departments was not economics — which is to say that household production theory, consumption-based welfare measurement, and the analysis of unpaid labour were not economics.

The second mechanism was the postwar formalisation of economics itself. The professionalisation of the discipline after 1945, centred on mathematical methods, econometrics, and macroeconomic modelling, constructed increasingly sharp boundaries around what counted as proper economics. Home economics, with its empirical focus on household behaviour, time-use data, and domestic labour, sat poorly within a disciplinary self-image organised around formal theory and quantitative labour-market analysis. The gender composition of the two fields compounded this: economics was becoming an almost exclusively male discipline in its graduate programmes and faculty, while home economics remained predominantly female in both students and staff. The association between femininity and the domestic sphere became, in academic practice, a mechanism of devaluation: the discipline had decided, structurally, that the domestic sphere was not its domain and that women were not its practitioners, and assessments of rigour followed from that verdict rather than producing it.

The third mechanism was the consequence of the first two for the people who designed the welfare state. The economists who constructed Beveridge's National Insurance system, who staffed the Council of Economic Advisers, who ran the social security actuarial bureaux, had been educated in a curriculum from which household production theory had been removed. They had not been taught to analyse unpaid domestic labour as an economic input; they had not been taught to measure welfare from the standpoint of what a household of specific composition needed to produce subsistence from a given income. When Orshansky built her matrices from USDA food plans and actual household expenditure data, drawing directly on the Chicago home economics tradition through her training at Hunter College and her work at the Bureau of Home Economics, her SSA colleagues had no disciplinary framework in which to locate what she was doing. For the CEA officials who preferred the $3,000 single threshold, administrative convenience and epistemological limitation were the same thing: they were choosing the only kind of economics their training had equipped them to recognise as economics. Reid's research programme had been removed from the curriculum that produced them, in three stages across three decades, and the exclusion was doing exactly what an exclusion does: it was making certain forms of knowledge invisible before they could become inconvenient.

Reid herself continued her research for decades after the 1934 book, producing influential empirical work on consumption behaviour, housing economics, and permanent income that was cited extensively by Friedman and Modigliani, among others, without the household production framework of her earlier work being incorporated into their theoretical apparatus. She died in 1991 at the age of ninety-four, a professor emerita at Chicago with a reputation in consumption economics and almost none outside it. Her papers are held at the Hanna Holborn Gray Special Collections at the University of Chicago library: forty-three boxes covering 1904 to 1990, the archive of a career spent developing an economics of household life that the discipline she worked in had decided to classify out of economics [@reid1934economics].

The argument this chapter is tracing reaches, in Section 4, its deepest level. Sections 2 and 3 documented specific political defeats: the NWRO's adequate budget was not adopted, Orshansky's matrices were simplified, Townsend's deprivation index was not institutionalised by the British welfare state. Those defeats happened in the late 1960s and 1970s, in specific political confrontations whose timeline Piven and Cloward's analysis can explain. But Reid's exclusion happened earlier and at a more foundational level. The political defeat of the grocery-list tradition was prepared by an intellectual defeat that preceded it: the reclassification of the research programme that would have supplied its methodological foundations as a feminised, vocational, non-economic activity. By the time Orshansky was at the SSA building the tools that Reid's tradition made possible, that tradition had already been stripped from the curriculum of the economists who would decide whether to adopt what she produced. The administrative model that won had inherited the discipline itself — which is why it was more powerful, and why the discipline had already cleared the field before the political contest of the 1960s began.

Bell hooks, in *Where We Stand*, identifies as one of capitalism's most effective operations the systematic silencing of class analysis in American public life — the refusal to name class as a structure rather than a personal condition [@hooks2000wherewestand]. The exclusion of home economics from the economics curriculum is a more specific instance of an analogous operation: the refusal to recognise the work of maintaining life — the labour of feeding, clothing, sheltering, and caring for specific human bodies — as economic work that belongs inside the discipline that governs welfare policy. Both silences served the same function: they kept the arithmetic of survival outside the domain of the people setting the benefit rates. What Reid built was precisely the toolkit that would have made that arithmetic unavoidable. Its exclusion was not an accident of academic politics. It was, in effect, a choice about what the welfare mathematics would be allowed to see.
