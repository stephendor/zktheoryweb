# 2. From Moral Failure to System Parameter: What the Actuarial Turn Changed

The Poor Law asked one question of the person standing before it: do you deserve relief? The answer was personal. It depended on the magistrate's assessment of character, the relieving officer's impression of the household, the parish's judgment about whether the applicant's poverty was the consequence of misfortune or moral failure. The 1834 New Poor Law formalised this assessment into the workhouse test — you proved your destitution by accepting conditions deliberately made worse than those of the lowest independent labourer — but the logic remained individual and moral. Each case was a case. Each applicant was examined. And the examination was designed, explicitly, to humiliate: T.H. Marshall noted that under the New Poor Law, paupers "suffered a species of attainder" and "ceased to be citizens in any true sense of the word" @marshall1950_citizenship_social_class. The price of relief was the forfeiture of civil status.

Beveridge's welfare state replaced this question with a different one: has this person made sufficient contributions to qualify for benefit? The shift was from moral assessment to actuarial record. Instead of a magistrate evaluating character, a clerk checked a contribution card. Instead of a household investigation, there was a National Insurance stamp. The contribution record was impersonal, consistent, and it conferred a right rather than a concession — the point on which Marshall's entire account of social citizenship turns @marshall1950_citizenship_social_class. The contributor was entitled to benefit in the same way that an insured person is entitled to a payout: by the terms of the contract, not by the judgment of the insurer. Marshall's three-stage sequence — civil rights in the eighteenth century, political rights in the nineteenth, social rights in the twentieth — culminates here, in the postwar welfare state's promise that a "guaranteed minimum" of economic security is part of what it means to be a citizen @marshall1950_citizenship_social_class.

This was a genuine achievement. The actuarial turn removed the personal humiliation of the parish relief application. It replaced the subjective discretion of the relieving officer with a criterion that was, within its own terms, consistent: the same contribution record produced the same benefit in Salford and Surbiton, in Dundee and Dover. It did not ask whether you drank, whether your house was clean, whether your wife was sufficiently industrious. It asked whether you had paid your stamps. For millions of people — particularly men in stable industrial employment, who were the system's intended subjects — this was liberation from the moral surveillance that had defined state welfare for a century.

But the actuarial model performed its own kind of abstraction, and the abstraction had consequences that were not accidental.

The claimant ceased to be a person with particular circumstances and became a parameter in a formula. The Beveridge Report's categorical architecture — insurance class, age band, sex, marital status, dependency status, employment status — mapped every individual in the covered population into a finite set of administrative categories, and each category carried a fixed schedule of contributions and benefits @beveridgereport1942. A Class I male employee aged 21 or over paid 4 shillings and 3 pence per week; his employer paid 3 shillings and 3 pence; in the event of unemployment or disability he received 40 shillings per week if married, 24 if single @beveridgereport1942. The system was not interested in whether his rent was high or low, whether his children had particular needs, whether his wife's health required expenditure the standard rate did not cover. It was interested in which cell of the table he occupied. The table was the system.

Ian Hacking's argument about classification applies with particular force to this moment. Hacking's claim is that the human sciences do not merely describe pre-existing kinds of person — they bring new kinds into being @hacking1986making. The category creates the people it classifies, and the classified people reshape the category: this is his "looping effect," the feedback loop between administrative classification and human identity that makes social statistics fundamentally different from counting quarks @hacking1986making. The postwar welfare state's contribution categories — "contributor," "claimant," "dependent," "insured person" — were not neutral descriptions of people who already existed in those forms. They were administrative constructions, built into the National Insurance system's paperwork and its actuarial tables, that called into institutional existence the identities they described. Before the 1946 National Insurance Act, there were workers, the unemployed, the sick, the old. After it, there were contributors and claimants — categories that carried specific legal entitlements, specific administrative procedures, and specific relationships to the state that the older vocabulary had not contained.

The looping effect operated through governance rather than through identity in the psychiatric sense that Hacking's original examples foreground. No one formed a social movement around being a "Class I contributor." But the categories shaped institutional treatment in ways that were consequential and persistent. To be a "contributor" was to have a record, to accumulate entitlement, to stand in a rights-bearing relationship to the state. To be a "dependent" — the married woman whose welfare was derived from her husband's record — was to lack a record of one's own, to have no independent accumulation, to stand in a relationship to the state that was mediated entirely through another person's employment. The category did not describe a pre-existing difference between these two positions. It produced the difference, because it was the administrative act of recording one person's contributions and not another's that created the institutional reality of entitlement and dependence. Fraser's "masculine" and "feminine" subsystems were not parallel tracks that happened to exist; they were manufactured by the same classificatory machinery @fraser1994_after_family_wage.

James Scott's concept of legibility provides a complementary framework for understanding what the actuarial turn accomplished and what it cost @scott1998seeing. Scott argues that modern states require the reorganisation of complex social phenomena into standardised, simplified categories that can be centrally observed and administered — a process he calls "making legible" @scott1998seeing. The Beveridge system is a legibility project of the first order. It took a population whose poverty, illness, unemployment, and old age were experienced in irreducibly particular ways — this woman's rent, that family's medical costs, this crofter's seasonal income, that widow's lack of any income at all — and reorganised it into a uniform grid of insurance classes, contribution records, and benefit schedules. The grid made the population administrable. The National Insurance system could be run through the post office precisely because it did not need to know anything about the particular circumstances of the people it served. A stamp was a stamp. A contribution was a contribution. The system's administrative elegance — and it was elegant, in the way that a well-designed table is elegant — depended on the suppression of everything that made one contributor's life different from another's.

Scott distinguishes between the state's simplified, schematic knowledge and the practical, situated knowledge he calls *mētis* — the knowledge of the fisherman who reads the water, the farmer who knows her soil, the nurse who can tell from the colour of a patient's skin what no chart will show @scott1998seeing. The distinction is useful for the welfare state, but it requires adjustment. Beveridge's model did not suppress *mētis* in the dramatic fashion of Scott's paradigm cases — Soviet collectivisation, Tanzanian villagisation. It suppressed it incrementally, through the ordinary institutional preference for categories that could be administered over knowledge that could not. The relieving officer under the Poor Law, for all the moral ugliness of the system, possessed a kind of situated knowledge: he knew the households in his parish, knew which families were in genuine distress and which were managing, knew the local price of bread and coal. The National Insurance clerk who checked a contribution card knew none of this, and did not need to. The information had been rendered irrelevant by the system's design. The cost of consistency was the loss of particularity — and because particularity is where actual need lives, the cost was borne disproportionately by people whose needs did not fit the standard categories.

This is the characteristic pattern of the actuarial turn. The mathematical move — from individual moral assessment to population-scale parametric classification — is simultaneously a move toward justice (consistency, predictability, rights-based entitlement) and a move toward abstraction (the suppression of material specificity, the replacement of the person with the parameter). The two are not in balance; the justice is real and the abstraction is real, and there is no formula that resolves the tension between them. What the actuarial model accomplished was a change in the *kind* of injustice the welfare state produced. The Poor Law's injustice was personal and arbitrary — it depended on who you were, who judged you, and whether the parish liked the look of you. The postwar welfare state's injustice was structural and systematic — it depended on whether your life fitted the model's categories, and if it did not, the system was not equipped to notice. The woman whose labour was domestic, the crofter whose income was seasonal, the migrant whose contribution record was interrupted — these people did not fail a moral test. They fell outside a parametric grid. The grid did not care.

The shift from moral to parametric governance also changed what counted as evidence. Under the Poor Law, evidence was qualitative: the relieving officer's report, the magistrate's interview, the parish visitor's observation. Under National Insurance, evidence was documentary: the contribution card, the employment record, the benefit claim form. The move from testimony to paperwork was a move from situated judgment to bureaucratic verification — and it created the informational infrastructure that every subsequent welfare system would inherit. The contribution record that tracked a worker's stamps from 1948 onward was, in administrative terms, a longitudinal data file: a time-series of individual economic activity, recorded and stored by the state, available for retrieval when a claim was made. The household surveys that the Central Statistical Office began conducting — the Family Expenditure Survey from 1957, the General Household Survey from 1971 — extended this informational reach from the individual contributor to the household as a unit of observation. By the early 1970s, the British welfare state possessed a statistical infrastructure — contribution records, household surveys, national accounts, benefit administration databases — that would have been unrecognisable to the parish officials of the 1930s. It was this infrastructure, assembled to serve the actuarial welfare state's administrative needs, that the algorithmic welfare systems of the twenty-first century would inherit. Universal Credit's real-time information system, which draws earnings data directly from HMRC's PAYE records, is the Beveridge contribution card's digital descendant — the same principle (track individual economic activity, derive entitlement from the record) executed at computational speed and at a granularity Beveridge could not have imagined.

None of which means the actuarial turn should not have happened, or that the Poor Law was preferable. The actuarial model's genuine achievements — consistency, impersonality, rights-based entitlement — came packaged with a specific set of blindnesses, and that those blindnesses were not the accidental byproducts of a basically sound design but the structural consequences of the design's foundational choices. The choice to treat all contributors equally meant the system could not see that contributors were not equal. The choice to derive entitlement from contribution records meant the system could not reach those whose labour produced no records. The choice to define benefits at flat rates meant the system could not respond to the variable, particular, material needs of actual households. And because the system could not see these things, it could not correct for them — until subsequent governments, inheriting the infrastructure but not the solidarity logic, began reintroducing the moral assessment that Beveridge had designed the system to eliminate.
