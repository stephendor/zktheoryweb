# 7. Cracks in the Foundation: The Return of Means-Testing

The Beveridge model rested on an assumption that its designer stated plainly and its critics later demonstrated was wrong. National Assistance — the means-tested safety net that would replace the Poor Law — was designed to be residual in the strict sense: a temporary provision for the small minority of "abnormal" cases whose needs could not be met by contributory insurance or by the family incomes that full employment would generate. Beveridge had, as the IFS later noted, "a visceral dislike of means tests" @ifs2023meanstested. He designed the National Insurance system precisely to make them unnecessary. The flat-rate contribution, the flat-rate benefit, the subsistence floor — these were the instruments through which the moral surveillance of the Poor Law would be rendered permanently obsolete. National Assistance would exist, but it would serve vanishingly few people, and its existence would not define the welfare state's character.

By the mid-1960s, around 1.7 million people were receiving National Assistance — mostly pensioners, widows, disabled people, and low-paid workers whose incomes the contributory system had not managed to raise to the subsistence floor @townsend1979poverty. Research conducted by the Child Poverty Action Group and others suggested that several hundred thousand more people were eligible but not claiming, held back by the stigma and complexity that National Assistance had inherited from the Poor Law it replaced. The number was not catastrophically large, but it was large enough to expose the assumption. National Insurance coverage was not broad enough. Flat-rate benefits were not adequate enough. The residual system was doing structural, not residual, work.

Harold Wilson's Labour government chose to respond to this failure by improving the safety net rather than by questioning the contributory model's adequacy. The Ministry of Social Security Act 1966 replaced National Assistance with Supplementary Benefit, administered by a newly created Supplementary Benefits Commission. The change was substantive: statutory entitlement replaced discretionary relief, new scale rates raised benefit levels roughly twenty-seven per cent above October 1964 National Assistance rates against an 8.8 per cent rise in the Retail Price Index over the same period, and a government advertising campaign for the first time actively recruited claimants rather than waiting for them to present themselves @suppbenefits1966parliamentary. Richard Titmuss — Professor of Social Administration at the LSE, the theorist whose institutional/residual distinction had provided the analytical vocabulary for defending universalism against means-testing — was appointed vice-chair of the Supplementary Benefits Commission @titmuss1958essayswelfarestate. The author of the critique was given the task of civilising what he had critiqued. The appointment was not ironic. It was an accurate measure of the distance between having the right analytical framework and having the political means to prevent the system it described from expanding.

Within two years, around four million people were receiving Supplementary Benefit @suppbenefits1966parliamentary. The advertising campaign had worked. What the numbers revealed was not that the reforms had failed but that the underlying need had always been this large — that National Assistance's low take-up had been an artefact of stigma and inaccessibility, not evidence of the contributory model's adequacy. The more the means-tested system was made accessible and rights-based, the more visible became the gap between what the contributory model promised and what it delivered. Improving the safety net did not resolve the tension; it measured it.

The 1972 Family Income Supplement introduced a new dimension to this failure. Where Supplementary Benefit provided for those out of work, the Family Income Supplement paid a wage supplement to low-paid workers in full-time employment who had children: fifty per cent of the shortfall between gross earnings and a prescribed threshold, plus a flat amount per child, awarded for fifty-two weeks regardless of subsequent income changes @fis1971. The instrument's logic was a departure from Beveridge's architecture at its foundations. Beveridge's model assumed that employment — full-time, regular, insured employment — would generate sufficient income to sustain the household above the subsistence floor, with the welfare state stepping in only when employment was interrupted. The Family Income Supplement acknowledged that this assumption had failed: that full-time workers could earn poverty wages, that the labour market itself was producing the need that the welfare state was meant to prevent. Paying a benefit to people who were working was not residual welfare. It was an admission that the wages the economy produced were inadequate to the lives the welfare state had promised to sustain.

The vocabulary shifted before the architecture did. In Beveridge's framework, the question was whether a person had contributed sufficiently to be entitled to benefit. In the New Right's reframing of the 1970s and 1980s, the question became whether a person was making sufficient effort to work, and whether the benefit system was structured to reward that effort rather than undermine it. The vocabulary of "deserving" and "undeserving" — which Beveridge had hoped to displace through the actuarial model's impersonal criteria — returned as the vocabulary of "incentives" and "targeting." The same exclusionary logic was now expressed in the language of efficiency rather than morality. But the governance function was identical: to distinguish between those whose relationship to the labour market entitled them to support and those whose relationship did not, and to make the second category's support conditional, stigmatised, and structurally inadequate.

The Social Security Act 1986, implemented in April 1988, institutionalised this reframing at the level of the system's architecture. Supplementary Benefit was replaced by Income Support, which carried forward the income-tested minimum but added a stricter definition of "remunerative work": those working more than twenty-four hours per week were excluded from Income Support and directed toward a new in-work benefit, Family Credit — the Family Income Supplement's successor — if they had children, or toward no income-related support at all if they did not @socialsecurityact1986. Family Credit paid a maximum credit tapered at seventy per cent against net income above a threshold, producing marginal effective tax-benefit withdrawal rates that, combined with income tax and National Insurance, could exceed ninety per cent in certain income bands. The poverty trap was not a malfunction of the system. It was its topology: a structure in which the interaction of means-tests, tapers, and thresholds made it arithmetically difficult to increase net income by earning more. Housing Benefit was formalised alongside Income Support and Family Credit as the third pillar of an income-related architecture whose basic shape, the Institute for Fiscal Studies later concluded, determined the structure of the UK's means-tested welfare system until the introduction of Universal Credit @ifs2023meanstested.

The 1986 Act also moved against the contributory model's most ambitious component. The State Earnings-Related Pension Scheme, introduced by the 1975 Social Security Pensions Act as a limited British answer to ATP's earnings-related logic, was weakened: the accrual formula was reduced, the reference period extended, and the Thatcher government offered employees a two per cent National Insurance rebate to contract out into personal pensions @socialsecurityact1986. The individualisation of pension risk — the transfer of the earnings-related component from a socialised public scheme to private funds exposed to market returns — was presented as a matter of choice and sustainability. It was also a structural choice to reduce the public system's generosity for middle earners, making means-tested support more likely at both ends of the working life. The 1986 Act was not primarily an assault on universalism. It was a reorganisation of risk: more of it borne privately by individuals, more of the residue means-tested at the bottom.

What had Beveridge built? The question, by 1988, had a different answer than it had in 1948. The national insurance contribution record — the instrument through which social citizenship was supposed to express itself — had not proved sufficient to insulate the population from means-testing. The wage stop had been abolished in 1975, but its successor logic had returned as the benefit cap and the taper. The cohabitation rule persisted in attenuated form through the household aggregation principles of Income Support. The conditions Beveridge attached to universalism had not been dismantled. They had been extended, refined, and computerised. The 1986 Act's Income Support, Family Credit, and Housing Benefit were rule-based calculations — nationally prescribed scale rates, standardised disregards, detailed regulations on income and capital — that lent themselves to computerisation precisely because they had converted the question "does this person deserve relief?" into the question "does this person's household income fall below this threshold given these parameters?" The moral assessment had not been abolished. It had been encoded.

This is the mechanism the rest of this book will trace. The actuarial model was not replaced by the computational model. It was colonised by it. The administrative infrastructure that Beveridge's welfare state had assembled — contribution records, household surveys, benefit regulations, income-assessment categories — was inherited entire by the systems that dismantled its solidarity logic. Universal Credit's real-time income assessment draws on the same PAYE records that National Insurance had been generating since 1948. Its household payment structure derives from the same aggregation principles that Section 7(3) of the 1948 Act established. Its conditionality regime extends the same "are you available for work?" criterion that the wage stop had enforced through the arithmetic of previous earnings. The machinery is continuous. The politics that operate it are not.

Beveridge designed his system to make the moral surveillance of the Poor Law permanently redundant. The Poor Law's question — do you deserve relief? — was to be displaced by the actuarial question: have you contributed? By 1988, both questions had been superseded by a third: are you behaving in the way the system requires? The contribution record had been supplemented by the claimant commitment. The subsistence floor had been replaced by the applicable amount, subject to conditions, subject to sanctions, subject to a compliance infrastructure that neither Beveridge nor the Board of Supervision had imagined. The welfare state had not failed to abolish the Poor Law. It had built a more efficient version of it — one that ran not on the moral judgment of a relieving officer but on the parametric logic of a national computer system, and that was, for that reason, harder to argue with, harder to escape, and — because it generated a determination rather than a judgment — impossible to appeal to a human conscience.
