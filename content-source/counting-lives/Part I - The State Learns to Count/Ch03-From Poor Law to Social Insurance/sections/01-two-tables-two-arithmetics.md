# 1. Two Tables, Two Arithmetics: Beveridge and Myrdal

In the winter of 1942, two documents were shaping the postwar welfare state, and neither acknowledged the other. In London, William Beveridge's *Social Insurance and Allied Services* was published on 1 December to queues outside His Majesty's Stationery Office — 635,000 copies sold of a government white paper, a figure that still astonishes. In Stockholm, Alva Myrdal's *Nation and Family: The Swedish Experiment in Democratic Family and Population Policy*, published the previous year, was circulating among the policy intellectuals building what would become the Folkhem — the People's Home. Both texts proposed to replace the Poor Law's moral inquisition with actuarial calculation at population scale. Both treated the welfare state as a mathematical instrument: a system of contribution records, benefit schedules, eligibility thresholds, and projected claim rates that could be calculated, administered, and defended without reference to the character of the individual claimant. And both embedded, in the apparently technical details of their formulas, a political vision of who the standard person at the centre of the welfare state would be.

They chose differently.

Beveridge's standard person was a male industrial worker. The Report's flat-rate model — a single weekly insurance stamp, equal for all contributors regardless of income, purchasing a standard package of benefits — expressed a democratic ideal: social citizenship as equal membership in a risk pool @beveridgereport1942. The contribution was the same for a factory hand in Salford and a clerk in Surbiton because the principle was equality of status, not equality of income. The benefit was flat-rate — 40 shillings per week for a man and his wife, 24 shillings for a single adult — because Beveridge designed it as a subsistence floor rather than a replacement for previous earnings @beveridgereport1942. Beveridge derived these figures partly from Rowntree's nutritional surveys in York, uplifted by an assumed 25 per cent increase in the postwar cost of living, and partly from the Government Actuary's projections of what the contribution rates could sustain over a twenty-year horizon @rowntree1901povertyastudyoftownlife @beveridgereport1942. The Social Security Budget that forms Part IV of the Report is the system's actuarial backbone: £697 million projected for 1945, £858 million for 1965, each figure disaggregated by type of benefit and source of finance, each resting on assumed sickness rates, unemployment levels, and demographic trajectories @beveridgereport1942.

This was quantification performing what Theodore Porter calls its characteristic governance function: the substitution of impersonal rules for personal judgment, making distributional decisions appear technical rather than political @porter1995trust. Beveridge's contribution rates and benefit schedules had the authority of calculation — they looked like the output of an actuarial model rather than a set of political choices about who deserved what. But they were political choices. The flat-rate model's egalitarianism was real, and it was genuine progress over the Poor Law's parish-level moral assessment. It was also incomplete in ways that were structural, not accidental.

The person insured was a worker. The worker was assumed to be a man. His wife was assumed to be at home, and her welfare was derived from his: she appeared in the formula not as a contributor with her own record but as a dependent whose benefit was a fraction of his @fraser1994_after_family_wage. The 40-shilling couple rate was not 24 shillings for her and 16 for him; it was 40 shillings for a household unit defined by his employment. Nancy Fraser's analysis of the gendered two-track welfare state names this structure precisely: a "masculine" subsystem of social insurance, tied to labour-market participation and conferring rights, alongside a "feminine" subsystem of public assistance, addressed to those who fall outside formal employment and conferring not rights but dependence @fraser1994_after_family_wage. Beveridge's universalism was built on the first track. For those whose lives fitted the model — employed, male, paying stamps — it worked as advertised. For those whose lives did not, the conditions that the next section of this chapter will examine were waiting.

Myrdal started from a different question. Where Beveridge asked how to insure a breadwinner against the Five Giants — Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, Idleness — Myrdal asked how to engineer a society in which women's paid employment and motherhood could coexist. The question was not rhetorical. Sweden's total fertility rate had dropped from over four children per woman at the turn of the century to under 1.7 by the early 1930s, and the Myrdals' *Crisis in the Population Question* — published jointly with Gunnar Myrdal in 1934 — had reframed falling fertility not as a sign of moral decline but as a rational response to structural conditions: inadequate housing, insecure income, the impossibility of combining paid work with childcare in the absence of public provision @myrdal1941nation. The diagnosis was demographic, but the prescription was architectural. If the state wanted children, it had to make parenthood compatible with the economic participation of both parents. Childcare, housing reform, maternity benefits, and parental leave were not welfare add-ons in Myrdal's framework — they were actuarial necessities for a system whose population projections required women in the labour force.

*Nation and Family* (1941) translated this programme for an international audience. Its intellectual core was a move that Beveridge never made: bringing domestic labour, childrearing, and the organisation of the household into the domain of public policy as matters of economic calculation rather than private arrangement @myrdal1941nation. Myrdal called this "social motherhood" — the state sharing the costs and responsibilities of care not out of charitable sentiment but because demographic sustainability demanded it. The policy instruments that followed — universal child allowances (*barnbidrag*), publicly funded childcare, school meals, maternal and child health clinics, housing with rational kitchens designed to reduce domestic drudgery — were linked components of a single system, each justified in the same actuarial terms that Beveridge used for National Insurance: projected costs, anticipated returns, population-level sustainability. The mathematics was the same kind. The person at the centre of it was not.

Myrdal's standard person was a dual-earner household. The earnings-related benefit model that would eventually become the ATP pension system flowed directly from this assumption: if both parents were economic actors, then both needed individual contribution records, and benefits tied to lifetime earnings rather than flat-rate citizenship would provide the middle-class security necessary to sustain universal participation. Where Beveridge's flat-rate floor asked the minimum question — what is the least a person needs to survive? — the Swedish earnings-related model asked the continuity question — what does a person need to maintain their living standard across the life course? The difference in the question produced a difference in the formula. And the difference in the formula produced a difference in who was visible inside the system.

The Swedish model did not solve the problem that the British model encoded. Myrdal's dual-earner assumption was its own kind of fiction — it presupposed a white, heterosexual nuclear family; it treated population as an object to be planned, sharing more with its eugenic contemporaries than later celebratory accounts have been comfortable acknowledging; and the practical reality of Swedish women's employment would remain stubbornly part-time, interrupted, and lower-paid than men's for decades after the policy framework was in place. ATP's formally gender-neutral formula — individual earnings records, no dependent-wife category — would produce gendered outcomes through the same mechanism that gender-neutral language often does: by treating as equal people whose starting conditions are not. The mathematics of the dual-earner welfare state still had a standard person at its centre. She was simply a different fiction from Beveridge's dependent wife.

But the contrast matters, and it matters mathematically. Two welfare states, assembling their postwar settlements in the same decade, using the same actuarial tools — contribution records, replacement ratios, eligibility thresholds, population projections — made different choices about the person their formula described. Beveridge's formula described a male breadwinner and derived his wife's welfare from his record. Myrdal's formula described two earners and derived their children's welfare from public provision. The choices were not forced by the mathematics. They were political decisions embedded in technical parameters, and the technical form made them harder to see — which is, as the opening section of this chapter has already suggested through Porter, precisely what quantification does @porter1995trust. The postwar welfare state's administrative machinery — the contribution records, the household surveys, the eligibility categories — was assembled on those political foundations. The algorithmic systems that later inherited the machinery inherited the politics along with the infrastructure.

There is one more thing the contrast reveals, and it concerns who gets to be an architect.

Janet Philip — Scottish mathematician, trained at St Andrews from 1893 to 1897, School Secretary at the LSE for nearly two decades — made the Beveridge Report publishable. Her editorial and intellectual contribution — Oakley estimates 200,000 words of drafting and revision — transformed a committee document into the most widely read policy text of the twentieth century. Beveridge conceded as much: "it would be idle to separate what came from her mind and what from his" @oakley2021forgotten. She married him two weeks after the Report's publication. Her name appeared nowhere in the published document, nowhere in the headlines, nowhere in the standard histories — until Ann Oakley recovered the evidence from the Beveridge supplementary papers, donated by Philip's son in 1974, more than three decades after the fact @oakley2021forgotten. Chapter 1 has already traced the parallel between Philip's erasure and Orshansky's: both women performed work essential to the postwar welfare state's most consequential instruments, and both were removed from the headline account by mechanisms that were structural rather than conspiratorial. Orshansky lost her work through simplification — her 124 thresholds reduced to a single line. Philip lost hers through absorption — her prose became his report.

Alva Myrdal's name, by contrast, is on the cover of *Nation and Family*. She was a recognised author, a public intellectual, a political figure who would go on to serve as a Swedish cabinet minister, a UNESCO director, and a Nobel Peace Prize laureate. The difference in their visibility is not a biographical accident. Beveridge's system encoded a world in which women's work — including the intellectual work of making the Report legible — was absorbed into male authorship and male contribution records. Myrdal's system, for all its limitations, encoded a world in which women were at least expected to appear as economic actors in their own right, with their own records and their own names. Philip was invisible inside the system she helped build. Myrdal was an architect of hers. The arithmetic did not determine this outcome. But it made one far more likely than the other.

The rest of this chapter traces the consequences of these different choices: the actuarial turn that both models shared, the specific conditions through which Beveridge's universalism hid its exclusions, the deeper structure of the Swedish alternative, and the statistical infrastructure that both models required — the machinery of governance that would outlast the political settlements it was built to serve.
