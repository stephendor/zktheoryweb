# 4. The Folkhem's Mathematics: From Myrdal's Vision to ATP's Formula

Sweden's welfare architects were asking a different question. In London, Beveridge was asking how to insure a breadwinner against Want. In Stockholm, Alva and Gunnar Myrdal were asking why Sweden's total fertility rate had fallen from above four children per woman to below 1.7 in three decades — and answering that the cause was structural rather than moral. Their 1934 *Kris i befolkningsfrågan* (*Crisis in the Population Question*) diagnosed the collapse not as evidence of individual fecklessness or national decline but as a rational response to economic insecurity, inadequate housing, and the incompatibility of women's paid employment with motherhood under existing social conditions @myrdal1934kris. If the state wanted Swedes to have children, it had to change those conditions. Fertility was a policy variable.

This framing had a political implication that Beveridge's did not. Beveridge was solving a welfare problem: how to prevent destitution among workers who fell out of employment. The Myrdals were solving a demographic problem: how to design a society in which modern women's lives — lives that included paid work and public roles — were compatible with reproduction. The difference is not merely a matter of scope. It concerns the person assumed to be at the centre of the formula. Beveridge assumed a male industrial worker with a wife at home. The Myrdals assumed a household of two economic actors whose labour, housing, and childcare were all objects of policy design. The welfare mathematics that followed from each assumption was not the same mathematics.

*Nation and Family* (1941), Alva Myrdal's English-language elaboration of the project, brought the domestic sphere into the domain of public administration with a directness that distinguished the Swedish approach from the British @myrdal1941nationfamily. Swedish apartments were too small, their kitchens designed for domestic drudgery rather than economy. Women who worked could not simultaneously mother, not because of biological incapacity but because the institutional infrastructure for combining these did not exist. Universal child allowances, publicly funded day-care, school meals, maternal and child health services, housing reform: these were not concessions to women's preferences but actuarial necessities for a system whose demographic projections required women's paid employment. The private sphere was already enmeshed with economic and political structures — with the price of housing, the availability of childcare, the wage differential between men and women. The question was whether the state acknowledged that enmeshment or pretended it did not exist.

The contrast with Beveridge's team logic is sharp enough to be stated plainly. Beveridge recognised women's domestic labour by incorporating them into their husbands' insurance class — a recognition that erased their independent economic identity. Myrdal recognised women's domestic labour by proposing to reorganise the institutions that made it incompatible with paid work — a recognition that required women to be independent economic actors rather than dependants. Where Beveridge's formula encoded the household as a gendered pair with one earner, Myrdal's encoded it as two earners whose care obligations were state responsibilities rather than private arrangements. Both were working with actuarial reasoning, population-scale projections, and public finance. The political choices embedded in their designs were not technical necessities.

Ernst Wigforss, the Social Democratic finance minister who held the portfolio almost continuously from 1932 to 1949, was the figure who translated the Myrdals' demographic vision into fiscal architecture. Wigforss's understanding of welfare as an investment — that expenditure on housing, child health, and education reduced future costs and increased productive capacity — provided the political economy within which the Folkhem programme accumulated institutional substance. The Folkhem (People's Home) was not a single piece of legislation but a decades-long accretion: housing construction, school meals from 1946, universal child allowances (*barnbidrag*) from 1947, gradual expansion of public childcare from the 1960s onward. The demographic projections drove the policy agenda; the policy agenda required the fiscal commitments that Wigforss's budget politics made possible.

The centrepiece of the Swedish model's actuarial architecture is the ATP pension system, introduced in 1960 after a political battle that ran through most of the previous decade and nearly failed at the last moment. *ATP-frågan* — the pensions question — dominated Swedish politics through the late 1950s because it was not primarily a technical dispute about contribution rates but a question about the meaning of universalism and the organisation of capital. Before ATP, white-collar and professional workers had occupational pensions negotiated with employers; blue-collar workers organised through the LO unions largely lacked such provision and faced a sharp drop in living standards at retirement. The Social Democrats and LO argued that a statutory earnings-related scheme was the only way to prevent a two-tier society in old age and to extend middle-class security to manual workers. Employers and bourgeois parties argued for a voluntary occupational solution. In 1957 the government held a consultative referendum. No option won a majority. The Social Democratic minority government pressed ahead regardless @atpfragan. In March 1959 the ATP bill passed the Riksdag's Second Chamber by a single vote, when liberal MP Ture Königson abstained rather than oppose it @pensionsstriden. One vote, one MP's abstention: the dual-earner welfare state's defining institution reached the statute book by the narrowest possible margin.

ATP's mathematical structure encodes a different actuarial logic from Beveridge's flat-rate benefit at every level. The core formula: a full pension requires thirty years of covered labour-market participation; the pension base is the average of the fifteen highest earnings years; a full ATP pension replaces sixty per cent of that base, on top of the existing flat-rate *folkpension* @palme2005atpfeatures. Formally, if *E*₁₅ is the average of the fifteen best pensionable years and 30 is the full accrual period, then ATP = 0.6 × *E*₁₅ × (years worked / 30). The system was financed by employer payroll contributions — initially around three per cent of payroll in 1960, rising to ten per cent by 1970 — channelled partly through public pension funds (*AP-fonderna*) that became significant actors in Swedish capital markets. Individual earnings were tracked from the scheme's inception, creating a longitudinal record of labour-market participation for the entire covered population @palme2005atpfeatures.

The design's critical features for the book's argument are two. First, ATP tracked individual earnings, not household composition. There was no dependent-wife category, no derived benefit, no contribution made "on behalf of a team." A woman's pension reflected her own employment history, not her husband's. The Beveridge architecture's defining asymmetry — one person's stamps bought two people's security, but only on the terms the formula prescribed — was absent from the Swedish design. Second, ATP was income smoothing across the life course rather than a subsistence floor. Where Beveridge asked "what is the minimum a contributor should fall below?", the ATP formula asked "what fraction of a worker's accustomed earnings should be maintained into retirement?" The target replacement rate of sixty per cent was a middle-class aspiration, not a poverty threshold. ATP was explicitly designed to bind the professional and white-collar middle classes into a universal public system by making that system's benefits comparable to what occupational schemes had previously offered. The political logic was not Beveridge's solidarity of shared risk but the Social Democrats' strategy of making the universal welfare state the best deal available to every income group.

Titmuss's distinction between institutional and residual welfare @titmuss1958essayswelfarestate illuminates why both models required — and consistently failed to escape — a standard person. The institutional welfare state, in Titmuss's schema, offers universal entitlements as a right of citizenship rather than a response to failure; it prevents the residual safety net from having to do much work because universal coverage is broad and generous. Beveridge's National Insurance was institutional in exactly this sense. ATP was institutional in a structurally different way: it incorporated the middle classes not through citizenship alone but through benefit levels that matched what occupational schemes had previously offered privately. A Social Democratic civil servant or LO factory worker enrolled in the same system because the formula was good enough for both. The political effect was the same — a universal public system rather than a tiered market — but the mechanism of inclusion was interest rather than right. Both strategies deployed contribution records, replacement rates, and population-scale actuarial projections to achieve universalism. What neither produced was a system whose parameters could account for lives that did not fit the formula's standard person.

Those lives were overwhelmingly women's lives. The ATP pension formula was formally gender-neutral; it said nothing about male or female workers, only about individual earnings histories and years of covered employment. But it was not substantively neutral, because the labour market whose records it tracked was not neutral. Swedish women's employment in the 1960s and 1970s was concentrated in part-time work, in lower-paid occupations, and in sectors with more interrupted career patterns — the result of a domestic division of labour that the Folkhem's childcare expansion was reducing but not yet dissolving. A woman who had worked part-time for fifteen years to manage childcare accumulated pension points for her actual hours and wages, not for the full-time equivalent that the formula's best-fifteen-years logic privileged. The fifteen-best-years rule introduced a further complication: it severed the link between lifetime earnings and the pension base, favouring workers whose earnings peaked sharply in mid-career — disproportionately men in ascending professional trajectories — over those with flatter or more interrupted histories. Swedish women ended up with lower ATP pensions than Swedish men not because the formula discriminated between them but because it treated an unequal labour market as if its inequalities were irrelevant @palme2005atpfeatures.

The Swedish model is therefore not the book's uncomplicated counter-example to Beveridge's conditioned universalism. It is evidence that the actuarial turn's gender politics could be configured differently — that the dependent-wife category was a British design choice rather than a mathematical necessity — while also showing that differently configured is not the same as escaped. The ATP formula had a standard person at its centre: a worker with a continuous, full-time, adequately paid career lasting thirty years. That worker was a fiction, as Beveridge's male breadwinner was a fiction, but it was a different fiction — one whose terms disadvantaged those who fell short of continuous full-time employment rather than those who fell outside formal employment altogether. Sweden's welfare mathematics did not abolish the gap between the formula's model person and actual lives. It relocated the gap, making it smaller for some women and barely changed for others.

The Myrdal/Beveridge contrast does not resolve into one model right, the other wrong. Both provided income security at a scale the Poor Law had never approached. Both assembled the contribution records, survey instruments, and actuarial projections that welfare states would administer through for the rest of the twentieth century. What the contrast shows is that the arithmetic of welfare is always political before it is technical: the choice of who counts as the system's standard person, which kinds of work are legible to its records, and what fraction of income the formula is designed to protect are not fixed by mathematics but by the political settlements within which mathematics is put to work. Beveridge and Myrdal each encoded a vision of the good society in the parameters of their formulas. The parameters could have been different. In Stockholm and London, they were.
