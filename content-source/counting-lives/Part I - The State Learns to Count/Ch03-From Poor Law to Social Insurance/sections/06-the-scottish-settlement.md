# 6. The Scottish Settlement: Differential Application and Its Limits

Scotland did not have one relationship to Beveridge's welfare model. It had two. In the industrial belt — Glasgow, Motherwell, Dundee, the mining villages of Fife and Lanarkshire, the shipyards of the Clyde — the male breadwinner working full-time in a single heavy industry, contributing National Insurance stamps from a single employer, came closer to the system's standard person than almost anywhere else in Britain. These were precisely the communities for which the flat-rate contribution model was designed: men in regular waged employment, generating clean contribution records, supporting households on a single income. In the crofting counties — Sutherland, Ross and Cromarty, Inverness, the Western Isles — the standard person did not exist. The labour that sustained these communities was seasonal, mixed, partly non-monetised, and divided across multiple income sources in ways the actuarial model could not track. The same national formula landed differently in these two Scotlands, and the difference was not random.

Before Beveridge arrived, Scotland had already been running a distinct poor relief system for a century. The Poor Law (Scotland) Act 1845 created the Board of Supervision for the Relief of the Poor in Scotland — headquartered in Edinburgh, composed of the Lord Provosts of Edinburgh and Glasgow, the Solicitor General, the Sheriffs of several counties, and three Crown-appointed members — to oversee a reformed system of parochial boards @poorlawscotland1845. The Scottish system differed from the English New Poor Law in two respects that matter for the story this chapter is telling. First, it did not insist on the workhouse test. Outdoor relief in cash or kind was not only permitted but common, particularly in rural parishes where a poorhouse was inaccessible and the parish knew its applicants by name. Second, and more consequentially, the able-bodied unemployed in Scotland had no legal right to poor relief until the 1920s. Relief was confined to those disabled by age or bodily infirmity, to widows and deserted wives with children, and to orphans. The man who simply could not find work had no claim on the Scottish parish.

This was a system built on intimate local knowledge and resistant to the kind of centralised, standardised administration that the Beveridge model would eventually impose. The Board of Supervision's minute books — held in the National Records of Scotland as the HH23 series — record a century of parochial boards negotiating with Edinburgh over poorhouse designs, disputing liability for paupers whose settlement was contested between parishes, investigating complaints from applicants who had been refused or inadequately relieved @boardofsupervision1845. In one case from 1855 to 1865, the Board spent nearly ten years considering and repeatedly revising Arbroath Parochial Board's plans for a poorhouse, eventually approving a combination with neighbouring St Vigeans after sustained pressure from ratepayers. In 1871, the Board investigated the death of a pauper, Alexander Macdonald, whose Inspector of Poor had contributed to his death; the Board issued a severe reprimand but stopped short of dismissal. These were not edge cases. They document the administrative texture of a relief system that governed through local judgment, personal knowledge, and case-by-case negotiation — a system whose grammar was entirely different from the actuarial model that would replace it.

The National Assistance Act 1948 abolished the Scottish Poor Law as it abolished its English and Welsh counterpart. From that date, National Insurance and National Assistance replaced parochial relief as the main mechanisms of support. But a century of Scottish practice did not simply dissolve. The categories through which local authorities had administered relief — Ordinary Poor, Able-Bodied Poor, outdoor versus indoor, settlement liability — were administrative constructs whose underlying social realities persisted. County councils and burgh councils, who had administered public assistance since the 1929 Local Government (Scotland) Act transferred poor-law functions away from parish councils, became the local nodes through which the new national system operated. The translation was real but uneven. Many of the administrative practices that the Board of Supervision had supervised for a century — the assumptions about who deserved support and on what terms, the informal assessments of household circumstance, the local knowledge of who could and could not be pressed — persisted in the early postwar welfare state's application in Scotland, carried forward by officials who had learned their trade under a different system.

The industrial Lowlands absorbed the Beveridge model most readily, and that absorption was itself a source of future vulnerability. The National Insurance system was designed for economies like Lanarkshire's and Clydeside's: stable, male-dominated industries with large employers, regular employment contracts, and clear contribution records. When those industries collapsed — as they began to do from the 1960s onward, and catastrophically in the 1980s — the communities whose welfare had been most thoroughly organised around the male-breadwinner contribution record lost not just their incomes but their structural eligibility for the contributory benefits the system had promised them. The welfare state's most committed subscribers became its most dependent claimants in a single economic generation. Deindustrialisation made visible what the model's fit with industrial Scotland had always concealed: the system's universalism was conditional on the industries continuing to employ the men whose contribution records it had counted on. When the industries went, the records became evidence of a world that no longer existed.

In the crofting counties, the mismatch was not contingent but structural. The Crofters Holdings (Scotland) Act 1886, enacted in the aftermath of the Highland Clearances and described by contemporaries like John Lorne Campbell as a "Magna Carta of the Highlands and Islands" for small tenants, had created a distinct legal tenure in seven crofting counties — Shetland, Orkney, Caithness, Sutherland, Ross and Cromarty, Inverness, and Argyll @croftersact1886. Crofts were typically small holdings of five hectares or fewer, associated with shared common grazings and requiring additional income sources to sustain a household. Historically, crofting households combined subsistence cultivation and livestock with seasonal labour on estates or Lowland farms, fishing, kelp, and later wage work in local industries or public services. By the 2007 crofting survey, only around thirty per cent of crofting households' income came directly from crofting activities @croftingcommission2022. The rest came from off-croft employment, benefits, pensions, and other sources. In 2022, crofting activities generated an average of approximately £4,538 per crofter per year — an income that required supplementation by almost every household that depended on it.

This was not a failure of the crofting system. It was its nature. The National Insurance model Beveridge designed assumed something quite different: a Class I employee in regular full-time employment, whose employer affixed a weekly stamp, accumulating a contribution record that translated into rights-based entitlement. Crofting households straddled NI categories in ways that produced exactly the gaps the model was designed to prevent. A crofter who also worked seasonally in local construction was an employee for part of the year and self-employed on the croft for the rest. Self-employed people paid Class 2 flat-rate NI contributions and were entitled to a basic retirement pension but not to unemployment benefit — the contingency that seasonal and intermittent work made most relevant. National Insurance unemployment benefit rules were built around the concept of a "full normal extent" of employment: benefit was not available for days when a worker "does not in the normal course expect to be working" @hansard1960NIseasonalworkers. For crofters and Highland workers whose normal pattern was precisely not full-year, full-time employment, this rule was a systematic exclusion mechanism dressed in actuarial language. The formula did not discriminate between a Glasgow shipyard worker temporarily unemployed and a Harris crofter at the end of the fishing season. It simply noted that one had a full contribution record and a normal working pattern, and the other did not.

Beyond the contribution record problem lay the question of statistical visibility. The postwar poverty surveys whose findings shaped welfare debates — Abel-Smith and Townsend's *The Poor and the Poorest* (1965), Townsend's *Poverty in the United Kingdom* (1979) — drew on the Family Expenditure Survey, whose sample of seven to ten thousand households nationally left crofting communities represented by a handful of cases in any given year @abelsmithtownsend1965. The specific forms of deprivation characteristic of the Highland economy — high transport costs, reliance on non-cash production, housing stock in poor condition, extreme distance from service providers — were too thinly sampled to shape national poverty lines and benefit adequacy thresholds. The actuarial model had been calibrated on urban and industrial populations. Its parameters reflected the cost-of-living and income patterns of those populations. When applied to crofting communities, the model assumed a kind of household and a kind of poverty that was not theirs.

A 1968 House of Lords debate on "Problems of the Scottish Highlands" captures the distance between the national framework and the Highland reality. Speakers described seven crofting counties covering nine million acres, suffering chronic underemployment and the steady out-migration of young people, and criticised the way national policies — the Selective Employment Tax, transport cuts, the absence of industrial investment — compounded the structural weakness of an economy built on smallholdings and extensive grazing @hansard1968highlands. The welfare state's presence in these communities was not primarily through contributory benefits earned by contribution records. It was through means-tested National Assistance and later Supplementary Benefit — the residual system Beveridge had designed as a minimal last resort, and which became the main support mechanism for the people his universal system could not reach.

Chapter 13 will return to these communities in detail. What matters here is the mechanism. The Scottish Poor Law was not merely a historical precursor to the UK welfare settlement; it was a distinct administrative tradition with its own categories, its own geography of inclusion and exclusion, and its own records of who counted as deserving and on what terms. When Beveridge's model arrived in 1948, it did not replace that tradition cleanly. It was layered on top of it, incorporating some of its practices and ignoring others, extending universal coverage to the industrial communities that fitted its parameters while leaving the crofting communities to fall back on means-tested provision. The mismatch was not accidental. It was the structural consequence of applying a formula designed for one kind of economy to a country with two — and of assuming, as all actuarial models must, that the standard person in the formula would be sufficiently universal to serve them both.
