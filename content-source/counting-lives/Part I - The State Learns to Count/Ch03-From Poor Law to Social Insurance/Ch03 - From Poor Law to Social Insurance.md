# Ch3 – From Poor Law to Social Insurance: Beveridge, Myrdal, and the Arithmetic of Two Welfare States

## Core Claim
The postwar welfare state did not abolish the arithmetic of power — it
reorganised it. In London, Beveridge's flat-rate contribution formula
replaced the Poor Law's moral assessment with actuarial calculation,
turning the claimant from a supplicant before a magistrate into a
parameter in a formula. In Stockholm, Alva Myrdal's *Nation and Family*
proposed a different formula for a different person: earnings-related
benefits designed around a dual-earner household where women's paid
employment was an actuarial assumption, not an exception. Both models
shared the foundational mathematical move — parameterisation at
population scale — and both were genuine progress over what came
before. But they encoded different political visions of who the
"standard person" in the formula was: Beveridge's was a male
industrial worker with a dependent wife; Myrdal's was a
household of two economic actors whose childcare, housing, and
parental leave were public responsibilities. The contrast reveals
that the gender politics embedded in each welfare arithmetic were
not technical necessities but political choices — choices whose
consequences persist in the administrative machinery that
algorithmic governance would later inherit. The national accounts,
household surveys, contribution records, and eligibility thresholds
assembled in this period are the infrastructure on which automated
welfare systems are built. Beveridge and Myrdal did not build the
automated poorhouse. They built its foundations — with different
blueprints, different assumptions about women, and different
blindnesses.

## Mathematical Tools in Play
- Tool(s): Flat-rate contribution formula (equal weekly contributions 
  regardless of income, producing equal flat-rate benefits — a 
  mathematical expression of citizenship rather than actuarial 
  risk); subsistence-level threshold calculation (Beveridge's 
  minimum income standard, derived partly from Rowntree's nutritional 
  work, defining the floor below which no contributor should fall); 
  replacement rate (benefit as a proportion of previous earnings — 
  the core design variable distinguishing the British flat-rate 
  model from the Nordic earnings-related one); actuarial risk pooling 
  (national insurance as a collective risk pool whose contribution 
  rates are set by projected claim rates across the covered 
  population); national accounts methodology (the statistical 
  infrastructure — GDP, household income surveys, employment 
  statistics — that makes the welfare state's performance 
  measurable and governable)
- Governance function: Transforms the individual moral assessment 
  of the Poor Law into population-scale actuarial calculation, 
  producing a welfare state that is simultaneously more just 
  (consistent, non-stigmatising, rights-based) and more 
  abstracting (the claimant becomes a parameter in a formula 
  rather than a person in a room). The administrative machinery 
  assembled for this transformation — contribution records, 
  eligibility thresholds, household surveys — becomes the 
  infrastructure that later algorithmic systems inherit and 
  accelerate.

## Argument in Sections

### 1. Two Tables, Two Arithmetics: Beveridge and Myrdal
The chapter opens by juxtaposing two welfare arithmetics
assembled in the same years on different assumptions about
who the "standard person" in the formula is. In London,
Beveridge's flat-rate contribution formula — a single weekly
stamp, a subsistence floor derived from Rowntree's nutritional
surveys, the Five Giants translated into actuarial parameters —
takes shape as a blueprint for a universal welfare state. Its
mathematical precision gave it political authority: the Report
was presented as a technical solution to a social problem,
which is precisely the depoliticisation move Porter identifies
as the characteristic function of quantification. But the
formula encoded a household: a male industrial worker, a
dependent wife, a cohabitation rule that policed who counted
as a family. In Stockholm, Alva Myrdal's *Nation and Family*
(1941) proposed a different formula for a different person.
Where Beveridge asked how to insure a breadwinner against
Want, Myrdal asked how to engineer a society where women's
paid employment and motherhood were simultaneously possible.
Her welfare mathematics assumed women worked — childcare,
housing reform, and parental leave were not exceptional
provisions but actuarial necessities for a system whose
demographic projections required dual-earner households. The
contrast is not Britain-bad, Sweden-good. It is that two
welfare states, building with the same actuarial tools —
contribution records, replacement ratios, eligibility
thresholds — made different choices about the person at the
centre of their formula, and those choices had consequences
that persist into the algorithmic present. Janet Philip enters
this frame not as the chapter's protagonist but as evidence
of how the Beveridge model worked in practice: the Scottish
mathematician whose editorial and intellectual labour made
the Report readable and politically effective, her 200,000-
word contribution absorbed into Beveridge's authorship by
exactly the male-breadwinner logic the formula itself encoded.
Philip's invisible labour is an instance of the pattern that
the Women's Budget Group's gendered spending reviews (Ch16)
and Orshansky's suppressed matrices (Ch1) will repeat —
women producing the statistical work that gives welfare
policy its authority while remaining outside the authorship
that confers credit and influence. Myrdal, by contrast, was
a named architect of her welfare settlement — and the
difference in their visibility is itself an argument about
what each system's arithmetic made possible for women.

### 2. From Moral Failure to System Parameter: What the Actuarial Turn Changed
The Poor Law's governing question was: does this person deserve 
relief? Beveridge's governing question was: has this person 
made sufficient contributions to qualify for benefit? The 
shift from moral assessment to actuarial record is genuinely 
significant — it removed the personal stigma of the 
parish relief application, replaced the magistrate's 
subjective judgment with a consistent administrative 
criterion, and grounded welfare entitlement in a concept 
of social citizenship rather than charitable condescension. 
But it also performed a different kind of abstraction: 
the claimant ceased to be a person with particular 
circumstances and became a parameter in a formula — 
a contribution record, a household composition, a 
replacement ratio. Hacking's "making up people" argument 
applies precisely here: the categories "contributor" and 
"claimant" are not descriptions of pre-existing kinds of 
person but administrative constructions that call into 
existence the institutional identities they describe. 
This section traces the actuarial turn's genuine achievements 
and its characteristic blindnesses, establishing the 
analytical frame that the chapter's subsequent sections 
will use to read the postwar welfare settlement across 
its national variations.

### 3. The Conditions: Where Universalism Hid its Exclusions
Beveridge's universalism was conditional, and the conditions 
were not administrative accidents but deliberate design 
choices that encoded a specific model of social life as 
the norm from which deviation would be penalised. The 
contribution record requirement excluded people who had 
not been in formal paid employment — disproportionately 
women, agricultural workers, and domestic servants, 
whose labour did not generate the National Insurance 
stamps that activated entitlement. The wage stop limited 
benefits to a claimant's previous wage, ensuring that 
the lowest-paid workers received the lowest benefits — 
a formula that reproduced labour market inequality 
inside the welfare state. The cohabitation rule deemed 
a woman living with a man to have access to his income 
regardless of whether she did, encoding both a moral 
model of heterosexual domesticity and a practical 
exclusion mechanism for women in violent or controlling 
relationships. These three conditions are not peripheral 
to Beveridge's design — they are load-bearing elements 
of a universalist system that was universal only for 
those whose lives matched the model's assumptions. 
They are also, as Ch13 will show, the direct ancestors 
of Universal Credit's household payment structure, 
its standard allowance formula, and its claimant 
commitment conditions.

### 4. The Folkhem's Mathematics: From Myrdal's Vision to ATP's Formula
Section 1 established the Myrdal/Beveridge contrast as the
chapter's organising frame. This section goes deeper into the
Swedish side: the intellectual origins in the Myrdals' *Crisis
in the Population Question* (1934), which diagnosed Sweden's
falling fertility as a structural problem requiring
comprehensive social reform rather than moral correction;
Ernst Wigforss's role as the finance minister who translated
this vision into fiscal architecture; and the Folkhem
programme that institutionalised it. The centrepiece is the
ATP pension system (1960), whose mathematical structure —
60% replacement of the best 15 earnings years over a
30-year accrual period, individual records with no
dependent-wife category, employer-financed payroll
contributions channelled through national pension funds —
embodies the dual-earner model's actuarial logic. ATP
was a different mathematical object from Beveridge's
flat-rate benefit: income smoothing over the life course
rather than a subsistence floor, binding the middle classes
into a universal public system rather than guaranteeing a
minimum. But the section also traces the model's own
blindnesses. By tying pensions strictly to individual
earnings, ATP favoured continuous full-time employment —
and because women's work remained disproportionately
part-time, interrupted, and lower-paid, many women ended
up with lower ATP pensions despite the formally gender-
neutral design. The Swedish model demonstrates that the
actuarial turn's gender politics could be configured
differently — but not that they could be escaped entirely.
The mathematics of the dual-earner welfare state still
had a standard person at its centre; she was simply a
different fiction from Beveridge's dependent wife.

### 5. The Statistical Infrastructure: Building the Machinery of Governance
The postwar welfare state required, and produced, a 
statistical infrastructure that had not previously existed 
at national scale: the Central Statistical Office (1941), 
the Family Expenditure Survey (1957), the General Household 
Survey (1971), the national accounts methodology that made 
GDP a governance instrument. Each of these was created 
to make the welfare state's performance measurable — 
to answer the questions that universal provision raised: 
are contributions sufficient to fund projected claims? 
Are benefit levels adequate to the subsistence thresholds 
they are meant to meet? Is poverty being reduced? The 
answers required data of a kind that had not been 
systematically collected before, and collecting it 
required new administrative categories — household 
composition, employment status, income band, 
contribution record — that would become the building 
blocks of every subsequent welfare model. Scott's 
legibility argument applies precisely here: the 
statistical infrastructure that makes redistribution 
possible also makes the population governable in 
new ways, and the categories it creates persist 
long after the political purposes that created them 
have changed.

### 6. The Scottish Settlement: Differential Application and Its Limits
The postwar welfare settlement was a UK-wide institution 
built on British statistics, but its application to Scotland 
was never uniform and the gaps were significant. Scotland's 
industrial economy — concentrated in heavy manufacturing, 
mining, and shipbuilding — meant that the male-breadwinner 
model Beveridge assumed was more accurate in Glasgow and 
Motherwell than in many English regions, but also that 
the welfare state's exposure to deindustrialisation 
would be proportionally greater when the industries 
collapsed. The Scottish crofting and Highland communities, 
whose poverty was structural and land-based rather than 
industrial and wage-based, fitted the contribution record 
model badly — their labour was often seasonal, informal, 
and non-monetised, generating few National Insurance 
stamps and therefore limited entitlement. The Scottish 
Poor Law's tradition of community-based, locally 
administered relief had been partially absorbed into 
the UK National Insurance framework, but the 
Board of Supervision's records show that the 
translation was incomplete and its costs were 
borne disproportionately by the communities 
that fitted the model least well. This section 
plants the Scottish thread that Ch13's analysis 
of the Highland Clearances and the Scottish Poor 
Law will collect: the postwar welfare settlement 
was a UK-wide mathematical model applied to a 
Scottish reality it did not fully describe, 
and the mismatch was not random.

### 7. Cracks in the Foundation: The Return of Means-Testing
The postwar consensus held, imperfectly, until the 
early 1970s — at which point the actuarial model's 
internal tensions, combined with stagflation and 
the ideological offensive of the New Right, produced 
a progressive reintroduction of means-testing that 
Beveridge had explicitly designed the National Insurance 
system to make unnecessary. The 1966 introduction of 
the Supplementary Benefit means-test, the 1972 Family 
Income Supplement, and the 1986 Social Security Act's 
replacement of Supplementary Benefit with Income Support 
were each presented as technical improvements to the 
welfare system's targeting efficiency. Each was a 
political choice to reintroduce the moral assessment 
that the actuarial model had displaced — now in the 
language of "targeting" and "incentives" rather than 
"deserving" and "undeserving," but performing the 
same governance function. This section reads the 
return of means-testing as the first iteration of 
the transition to algorithmic welfare that Parts 
II and III will trace: the actuarial model was 
not replaced by the computational one — it was 
colonised by it, its administrative infrastructure 
inherited and its solidarity logic discarded.
