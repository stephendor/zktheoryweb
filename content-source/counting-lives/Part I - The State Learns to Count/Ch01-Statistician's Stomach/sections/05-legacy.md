# 5. Legacy: Thresholds as Governance, Then and Now

The poverty line Orshansky built in 1963 is still the official US poverty measure in 2026. The Census Bureau updates it each January by the percentage change in the Consumer Price Index, as the Bureau of the Budget directed in 1969, and the resulting thresholds are published in the *Federal Register* as the poverty guidelines that determine eligibility for Medicaid, SNAP, the National School Lunch Program, Head Start, the Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program, and dozens of other federal programmes. In 2025, the threshold for a family of four was $31,800 — a number derived from Orshansky's 1963 calculation, itself derived from the USDA's 1961 Economy Food Plan cost multiplied by three, itself derived from the 1955 Household Food Consumption Survey's finding that families of three or more spent one-third of their after-tax income on food. Sixty-two years of price inflation separate the 2025 threshold from the 1963 food budget that produced it. No intervening survey of actual household consumption has been allowed to revise the relationship between food costs and total income on which the threshold rests. The multiplier is still three. Food's share of the average American household's budget fell below one-fifth decades ago.

The number is a fossil. It records, in its frozen arithmetic, a relationship between food expenditure and household income that existed in 1955 and had already begun to shift by the time Orshansky used it in 1963. It has been carried forward for six decades not because anyone believes it accurately describes the material circumstances of poor American families — no serious poverty researcher has believed that for at least forty years — but because changing it would change the poverty rate, and changing the poverty rate would change the number of people eligible for federal programmes, and changing the number of people eligible for federal programmes would cost money. The line endures because the institutional logic that simplified it in 1969 is the same logic that prevents it from being revised in 2026: administrative convenience, fiscal constraint, and the political utility of a number that everyone knows is wrong but no one has the institutional incentive to fix.

This is not a failure of knowledge. It is a success of governance. The official poverty line does exactly what the institutions that maintain it need it to do: it provides a stable, predictable, legally defensible threshold that can be applied uniformly across programmes without requiring any agency to investigate what households actually need. It is Porter's technology of distance operating at full capacity — a number that creates the appearance of objectivity precisely because it has been stripped of the material content that would make it contestable @porter1995trust. Orshansky's original thresholds invited argument because their inputs were published and their assumptions were named. The CPI-indexed line discourages argument because there is nothing to argue about: the inflation adjustment is mechanical, the base year is fixed, and the relationship between the threshold and actual household need is not a question the measure is designed to answer.


## The Supplemental Poverty Measure: acknowledgement without action

In 2011, the Census Bureau released the Supplemental Poverty Measure, developed in collaboration with the Bureau of Labor Statistics and based on recommendations from a 1995 National Academy of Sciences panel @census2011supplementalpovertymeasure. The SPM was an institutional acknowledgement that the official poverty measure no longer measured poverty — or rather, that it measured poverty as it had existed in 1963, not as it existed in the twenty-first century. The SPM attempted to restore several of the properties Orshansky had built and the Bureau of the Budget had removed.

Where the official measure derives its thresholds from a sixty-year-old food budget, the SPM uses a rolling five-year average of household expenditures on food, clothing, shelter, and utilities — restoring the connection between thresholds and actual consumption patterns that CPI indexing had severed @census2011supplementalpovertymeasure. Where the official measure applies the same threshold nationwide, the SPM adjusts for geographic variation in housing costs — a family in San Francisco faces different shelter costs than a family in rural Mississippi, and the SPM says so. Where the official measure counts only pre-tax cash income, the SPM adds in-kind benefits (SNAP, housing vouchers) on the resources side and subtracts taxes, work expenses, and medical out-of-pocket costs — producing a more accurate picture of what a household actually has available to meet its needs.

In its first year, the SPM poverty rate was 16.1 per cent, compared to the official rate of 15.1 per cent @census2011supplementalpovertymeasure. The difference was driven mainly by geographic cost variation and medical expenses pushing more households below the SPM threshold than the official line would have counted. For elderly Americans, the SPM rate was substantially higher than the official rate, because the SPM subtracted out-of-pocket medical costs that the official measure ignored — costs that Orshansky herself had flagged as a deficiency of any income-only threshold.

The SPM was a partial recovery of Orshansky's original ambition: a poverty measure grounded in what households actually spend, differentiated by the material circumstances that shape household need, and transparent enough in its construction that its assumptions can be examined and contested. It was also, in its institutional status, a confirmation of everything the previous section argued about the politics of measurement. The Census Bureau publishes the SPM annually. Federal programmes do not use it. No statute requires any agency to consult it. It remains, fifteen years after its introduction, an advisory measure — a research tool that sits alongside the official poverty line without replacing it, acknowledged as more accurate but denied the institutional authority that accuracy alone cannot confer.

The SPM's advisory status is the chapter's final exhibit in the case against the idea that poverty measurement is a technical problem awaiting a technical solution. The technical solution exists. The Census Bureau built it. The government publishes it. And the official poverty measure remains unchanged, because the obstacle to better measurement is not methodological but political: a better measure would produce a higher poverty rate, a higher poverty rate would increase programme eligibility, and increased programme eligibility would increase federal spending. The arithmetic is straightforward. The decision not to use it is deliberate.


## Across the Atlantic: Universal Credit's standard allowances

The pattern the previous section identified — build, adopt, simplify, administer — is not confined to the American poverty line. In the United Kingdom, Universal Credit's standard allowances are the structural descendants of the same simplification, operating in a different institutional context but producing the same result: thresholds set by fiscal negotiation and political target, presented as objective eligibility criteria, with no systematic relationship to what households actually need.

UC replaced six legacy benefits (Income Support, income-based Jobseeker's Allowance, income-related Employment and Support Allowance, Housing Benefit, Child Tax Credit, and Working Tax Credit) with a single means-tested payment, rolled out nationally between 2013 and 2024. At the centre of UC's architecture are the standard allowances — flat monthly amounts, differentiated only by age (under 25 or 25 and over) and partnership status (single or couple), that form the baseline of a claimant's entitlement before housing, child, and disability elements are added. The standard allowance for a single claimant aged 25 or over in 2025–26 is £393.45 per month. The Department for Work and Pensions does not publish a methodology showing how this figure was derived from any assessment of what a single adult needs to live on. It was set at introduction in 2013, has been uprated by CPI in some years and frozen in others, and bears no documented relationship to the cost of food, shelter, fuel, or any other specific household expenditure in any specific place in the United Kingdom.

The parallel with the post-1969 US poverty line is structural, not coincidental. Orshansky built a threshold from the bottom up: start with a concrete need (food), establish its cost, derive an income threshold from the empirical relationship between that cost and total household expenditure. The Bureau of the Budget severed that derivation by substituting CPI indexing for food-cost recalculation. UC's standard allowances never had the derivation in the first place. They are thresholds without a methodology — numbers set by fiscal envelope rather than household assessment, presented to claimants as the amount the state has determined they need, without any published account of how that determination was made.

This is the endpoint of the trajectory Orshansky's measure inaugurated. Not because her work caused UC's standard allowances — the institutional histories are distinct, the national contexts are different, the political decisions were made by different people for different reasons. But because the conceptual move that made UC's standard allowances possible — the idea that a welfare threshold can be a governable number rather than a measurable condition, a fiscal target rather than an empirical finding — was the same move the Bureau of the Budget made in 1969 when it indexed Orshansky's line to CPI and stopped asking what households actually needed. The methodological defeat Orshansky suffered is the condition of possibility for every welfare threshold that presents administrative convenience as objective need. Chapter 10 will analyse UC's algorithmic architecture in full. The point here is the lineage: from Orshansky's 124 thresholds, each derived from a specific food budget for a specific household type, to UC's standard allowances, each set by a fiscal negotiation that no published methodology documents.


## What Orshansky left

Orshansky died in 2007, at ninety-one, having spent four decades arguing that the measure she built had been adopted in a form that defeated its purpose. Her late-career papers — from the 1969 *Monthly Labor Review* piece through the 1970 Moynihan memo, the 1976 compilation of *The Measure of Poverty*, the 1978 *Public Welfare* debate, the 1979 paper with Fendler, and the 1988 commentary — constitute the most sustained methodological critique any poverty researcher has directed at their own instrument @orshansky1988commentarypoverty. She did not argue that the poverty line should be abandoned. She argued that it should be what she had built it to be: transparent, contestable, materially grounded, and granular enough to distinguish between the different forms of poverty that different households experienced.

Her critique was not sentimental. It was precise. In the 1979 paper with Fendler, she and her co-author recalculated the thresholds using 1965 food consumption survey data instead of the 1955 data, producing a multiplier closer to four than three — which would have raised every threshold by roughly a third and reclassified millions of additional Americans as poor @orshansky1988commentarypoverty. The proposal was published in the *Proceedings of the American Statistical Association*. It was not adopted. The 1988 commentary, written for the twenty-fifth-anniversary reprinting of "Counting the Poor," stated the argument in its most concentrated form: the measure had been frozen, the freeze was political, and the people it failed to count were real.

What she left was an epistemological standard: the demonstration that poverty measurement could be rigorous and specific at the same time, that transparency and complexity were not opposed, that a measure could serve both the state's need for a number and the household's need to be seen. The 124 thresholds were a proof of concept: it was possible to count the poor without reducing them to a single category, possible to build a measure whose assumptions were visible enough to be argued about, possible to anchor a welfare threshold in what households actually needed rather than what governments found convenient to spend.

The history of what happened to her measure — simplified, frozen, indexed to a price series that severed it from the consumption data that gave it meaning — is the first chapter's argument in miniature and the book's argument in outline. The institutions that adopted Orshansky's thresholds chose convenience over honesty, described the choice as a technical improvement, and left the cost of that choice to accumulate in the lives of every family the simplified line failed to count.

The chapters that follow trace the same pattern across other instruments, other institutions, other countries, other decades. The poverty line is the first case, not the only case. But it is the paradigmatic one, because Orshansky did everything right — built the measure from the bottom up, published the inputs, named the assumptions, invited the argument — and the institutional apparatus of the welfare state still found a way to turn her precision into a convenient fiction. If the most transparent, most contestable, most materially grounded poverty measure in American history could be captured and simplified, then every measure this book examines operates under the same institutional gravity. Orshansky showed what honest measurement looks like. The rest of the book asks why it so rarely survives.
