# 3. The Matrices: Transparency as a Mathematical Property

What Orshansky published in July 1963 was not a poverty line. It was a poverty matrix — a two-dimensional array of 124 household-specific thresholds, each derived from the same method but producing a different number for a different kind of family @orshansky1963childrenpoor. The distinction matters, and it matters precisely because the institutions that adopted her work spent the next six years collapsing it. A line divides a population in two: poor and not poor. A matrix differentiates within the population it measures, preserving the information that poverty looks different in a household of two elderly persons living on a farm in Iowa than it does in a household of eight with six children in a Philadelphia row house. The line asks: how many? The matrix asks: who, specifically, and under what material conditions? Orshansky built the matrix. The government wanted the line.

This section explains what she built, step by step, because the method itself is the argument. The 124 thresholds were not a statistical exercise that happened to produce a large number of outputs. They were a representational commitment — a refusal to treat "the poor" as a single category when the material conditions of poverty varied by household size, age composition, the presence of children, the sex of the household head, and whether the family lived on a farm. Each of those dimensions recorded a real difference in what a household needed to survive. Each would be removed in the decades after the measure's adoption. Understanding what was lost requires understanding what was there.


## The method: from food to income

The method had three components, and each was grounded in empirical observation rather than political negotiation.

The first component was a food budget. The USDA published four tiered food plans — Economy, Low-Cost, Moderate-Cost, and Liberal — each derived from observed American household food-purchasing patterns, modified by the minimum amount necessary to meet the National Research Council's recommended daily nutritional allowances @usda1961economyfoodplan. The plans were not theoretical menus. They were costed baskets: specific quantities of specific foods, priced in actual retail markets, adjusted for household size and composition. A two-person elderly household required different foods in different quantities than a family of five with three school-age children, and the USDA's nutritionists had calculated the cost of feeding each configuration at each of the four levels.

Orshansky chose the Economy Food Plan — the cheapest of the four. The choice was deliberate and it was political, in the same way that Rowntree's choice of a nutritional minimum in York in 1901 had been political: she wanted a floor so austere that no critic could dismiss the people below it as merely improvident @orshansky1963childrenpoor. The Economy Food Plan was designed for "temporary or emergency use when funds are low" @usda1961economyfoodplan. It assumed a household with the knowledge, the equipment, the time, and the skill to prepare every meal from scratch, minimising waste and substituting cheaper protein sources for more expensive ones. It assumed, in other words, a household that managed its food budget with a precision most households did not and could not achieve. Anyone below this floor was not merely poor by some arguable standard. They were poor by the most conservative nutritional criterion the federal government published.

The second component was a ratio. The 1955 Household Food Consumption Survey — a nationally representative study of how American families actually spent their money — had found that families of three or more persons spent approximately one-third of their after-tax income on food @usda1955householdfoodconsumptionsurvey. This was not a normative claim about how much families should spend on food. It was an empirical observation about how much they did spend. The ratio varied by household size (smaller households spent a higher proportion on food) and by income level (poorer households spent more of their income on food than wealthier ones), but for the average family of three or more, one-third was the finding.

The third component was an inversion. If food costs equal one-third of a household's income, then the minimum income a household needs is three times the cost of its minimum food budget. Orshansky multiplied the Economy Food Plan cost for each household type by three, and the product was the poverty threshold for that household type @orshansky1963childrenpoor. The multiplier was not a guess. It was the arithmetic consequence of an observed expenditure pattern: the 1955 survey said families spent a third of their income on food, so a household that could not afford three times the cost of a minimum food basket could not, by the survey's own evidence, afford to eat at that minimum level and still pay for everything else — rent, fuel, clothing, transportation, medical care — that the remaining two-thirds of a household budget covered.

The method's economy was not aesthetic. It was epistemic. Each component could be examined independently. A critic who thought the Economy Food Plan was too generous — or too austere — could say so, and could say precisely which food items, at which prices, in which quantities, they would change. A critic who thought the one-third ratio was wrong could point to the survey data and argue that the ratio had shifted, or that it varied too much by household size to be applied as a single multiplier, or that two-person households should use a different ratio than five-person households. A critic who thought the multiplication itself was the problem — that food costs were not a reliable proxy for total household need — could make that argument and propose an alternative. At every stage, the inputs were published, the arithmetic was reproducible, and the assumptions were named. The measure invited disagreement. It did not suppress it.

This is what Theodore Porter means by mechanical objectivity's opposite @porter1995trust. Porter argues that quantification in governance typically functions as a "technology of distance" — a way of making decisions appear impersonal and therefore unchallengeable, by substituting rule-following for judgment @porter1995trust. The poverty matrix inverted this logic. Its objectivity derived not from impersonality but from transparency: anyone could see how the number was made, which meant anyone could argue about whether it was made correctly. Porter's own framework identifies Orshansky as a counter-case — quantification that resists depoliticisation because its specificity preserves contestability. The 124 thresholds were harder to administer than a single round number, but they were also harder to manipulate, because manipulation required changing a published input that other people could check. The CEA's $3,000 line, by contrast, was a round number with no published derivation — it could not be challenged because there was nothing to challenge. It was a political assertion dressed as a statistic. Orshansky's thresholds were statistics that could be politically contested, which is the more honest and more dangerous condition.


## The matrix: what the dimensions recorded

The 124 thresholds varied across five dimensions, and each dimension recorded a material fact about how households actually lived.

**Household size** was the most obvious. A single person living alone needed less food, less fuel, and less space than a family of seven. The Economy Food Plan's cost increased with household size, but not linearly — there were economies of scale in food purchasing, cooking, and housing that meant the per-person cost decreased as the household grew. Orshansky's thresholds reflected this: the poverty threshold for a seven-person family was not seven times the threshold for a single person. It was lower per person but higher in total, because the matrix tracked what households actually needed rather than what a per-capita calculation would predict.

**Age of household head** distinguished between elderly and non-elderly householders. Elderly persons living alone or in couples had different food requirements — fewer calories, different nutritional needs — and the Economy Food Plan's cost was lower for elderly household types @usda1961economyfoodplan. The threshold for a single person aged sixty-five or over was lower than the threshold for a single person under sixty-five, not because elderly poverty was less severe but because the food-budget floor from which the threshold was derived was lower. This distinction would survive the 1969 simplification, but its meaning would change once CPI indexing severed it from the food-plan data that justified it.

**Presence and number of children** was the dimension Orshansky cared about most, and the one that had motivated her to build the measure in the first place @orshansky1963childrenpoor. Her 1963 article was titled "Children of the Poor" because the existing measures — the CEA's $3,000 line above all — systematically understated the number of children in poverty. A single threshold applied to all households treated a family of two adults as equivalent to a family of two adults and four children. The matrix did not. It produced a different threshold for each configuration of adults and children, because a household with children needed more food, and the food cost more, and the multiplier therefore produced a higher income threshold. The matrix made children visible in the poverty count. The single line hid them.

**Sex of household head** was a dimension that recorded a specific economic reality of the early 1960s: female-headed households with children faced systematically different economic circumstances than male-headed households of the same size, including lower average wages and more constrained labour-force participation. Orshansky included this dimension because the data showed it mattered — the food-share ratio and the relationship between food cost and total household need varied by this characteristic @fisher1997developmentorshanskythresholds. The Bureau of the Budget removed the sex-of-head distinction in 1969 as part of the standardisation that accompanied CPI indexing, on the grounds that it was administratively unnecessary @bob1969povertythresholds. The material reality the dimension had recorded did not disappear when the dimension was removed from the matrix. Female-headed households with children remained poorer than their male-headed counterparts. The matrix no longer said so.

**Farm versus non-farm residence** was the final dimension. Farm families had lower cash food costs because they produced some of their own food, and the Economy Food Plan's cost was adjusted downward accordingly @usda1961economyfoodplan. The farm threshold was approximately 70 per cent of the non-farm threshold for the same household type — a substantial difference that reflected an observable material fact about how rural households ate. The Bureau of the Budget removed this distinction too in 1969, replacing it with a uniform national threshold @bob1969povertythresholds. The Bureau of the Budget framed the decision as a simplification. It was also an erasure: rural poverty and urban poverty were different in their material composition, and the matrix had said so. The simplified line treated them as the same.

The five dimensions produced 124 cells, and each cell contained a dollar figure derived from the same method: Economy Food Plan cost for that household type, multiplied by three. The matrix was large enough to be unwieldy for a press release and precise enough to be useful for a programme administrator who needed to know whether a specific family in a specific place qualified for assistance. Hacking's concept of "making up people" applies directly: the matrix did not merely measure a pre-existing population called "the poor" @hacking1986making. It created — in the administrative sense that mattered for programme eligibility, budget allocation, and political rhetoric — 124 different kinds of poor household, each visible to the state in a different way. An elderly woman living alone on a farm was a different kind of poor than a young mother with three children in a city apartment, and the matrix said so in a number the state could use. When the matrix was collapsed into a line, those 124 kinds became one kind — a binary category, below the threshold or above it, with no information about household composition, geographic circumstance, or the specific material conditions of the poverty it measured. The state could still count the poor. It could no longer tell them apart.


## Four mathematical properties

The matrix had four properties that deserve to be named, because each was a mathematical feature that also functioned as a political commitment, and each was progressively undermined in the decades after the line's adoption.

The first was **transparency**. The inputs were published. The USDA's Economy Food Plan was a public document with itemised food quantities and retail prices. The 1955 Household Food Consumption Survey was a public dataset. The multiplication by three was a one-step operation that anyone with the survey report could verify. There was no proprietary model, no unexplained coefficient, no black box. The threshold for a family of four with two children in a non-farm household could be recalculated from scratch by anyone who had access to a library and a pencil. This transparency was not incidental to the method. It was the method's central epistemic claim: a poverty threshold should be a number that anyone can check, because poverty is a condition that concerns everyone.

The second was **contestability**. Because the inputs were published and the arithmetic was simple, anyone who disagreed with a threshold could identify the precise point of disagreement. A nutritionist who believed the Economy Food Plan was nutritionally inadequate could specify which items she would add. An economist who believed the one-third food-share ratio was outdated could cite the later survey data showing the ratio had fallen. A demographer who believed the farm/non-farm adjustment was too large could present evidence from rural household budgets. The measure channelled disagreement into productive argument rather than suppressing it beneath an unchallengeable number. This is what Porter's framework identifies as both mathematical and political: a contestable measure distributes the power to challenge it rather than concentrating the power to assert it @porter1995trust.

The third was **material grounding**. The threshold was derived from something a household actually needed to do — eat — at a level the federal government's own nutritionists had defined as the minimum. It was not derived from a fiscal target (how much can the government afford to spend?), a political negotiation (what number will the president announce?), or a statistical artefact (where does this household fall in the national income distribution?). The anchor was nutritional: a household that could not afford three times the cost of feeding itself at the Economy Food Plan level could not, by the evidence of the 1955 survey, afford to feed itself at that level and pay for anything else. The material grounding was what made the threshold more than a number. It was an empirical claim about what a specific household needed to survive, and the claim could be wrong — but it could be wrong in a way that was checkable, not in a way that was arbitrary.

The fourth was **granularity**. The matrix distinguished between a single elderly person and a family of eight with six children. It distinguished between farm and non-farm households. It distinguished between households headed by men and households headed by women. Each distinction corresponded to a real variation in what households needed, and each produced a different threshold. The granularity was a moral commitment: poverty is not one thing. It is different for different people in different circumstances, and a measure that treats it as one thing — a single line below which everyone is equally poor and above which everyone is equally not poor — is a measure that has already decided not to see the differences that matter most to the people living them.

These four properties — transparency, contestability, material grounding, and granularity — were what Orshansky built. They were also what made her measure inconvenient for the institutions that needed to use it. A transparent measure can be challenged. A contestable measure invites argument. A materially grounded measure must be updated when the material conditions change. A granular measure is harder to administer than a simple one. Every property that made the 124 thresholds honest was a property that made them difficult, and the institutional history of the poverty line after 1963 is the history of those properties being removed, one by one, for reasons that were always described as technical improvements and were always, in their effect, political choices.
