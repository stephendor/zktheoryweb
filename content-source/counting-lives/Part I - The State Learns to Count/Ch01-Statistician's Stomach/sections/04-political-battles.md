# 4. Political Battles and the Cost of Complexity

Orshansky published "Children of the Poor" in July 1963. By August 1969, the Bureau of the Budget had issued a directive making a standardised, CPI-indexed version of her thresholds the official federal poverty measure — the first time the United States government had an official definition of who was poor @bob1969povertythresholds. Between those two dates, the measure passed through six years of bureaucratic reception in which every property that made it honest was tested against every institutional pressure that made it inconvenient. The story of those six years is a case study in what happens when a measure built to preserve complexity meets a state apparatus that needs to administer it.

The conflict began before Orshansky published. Walter Heller, chairman of the Council of Economic Advisers under Kennedy and then Johnson, had already settled on a poverty number. His CEA staff had adopted a threshold of $3,000 in annual family income — a single round figure, undifferentiated by household size, composition, or geography, chosen because it was simple enough to anchor a political argument @heller1963povertyline. The number appeared, without derivation or methodological justification, in the CEA's *Economic Report of the President* in January 1964 @fisher1997developmentorshanskythresholds. It was a political fact, not an empirical finding. Its purpose was to produce a headline: roughly one-fifth of American families lived below $3,000 a year. That headline could be announced in a press conference, printed in a newspaper, and repeated in a congressional appropriation debate. It could not tell you whether a child was poor.

Orshansky understood this as a methodological failure, not merely a political convenience, and she said so. Her specific objection was that a flat $3,000 line systematically understated the number of children in poverty relative to elderly persons, because a household of two adults and four children required substantially more income to meet basic needs than a household of two elderly persons, but the CEA's line treated both as equivalent @fisher1997developmentorshanskythresholds. The line counted heads. The matrix counted mouths. For Orshansky, the distinction was not academic — it was the difference between a measure that could identify which children needed help and a measure that could only tell a president how large a problem he had.

The rivalry between these two approaches — the CEA's round number and Orshansky's differentiated matrix — structured the political history of poverty measurement from 1963 to 1969. It was not a debate between two technical methodologies. It was a conflict between two theories of what a poverty measure is for. Heller needed a number that could mobilise political will. Orshansky needed a number that could describe material reality. The War on Poverty, launched by Johnson in his January 1964 State of the Union address, needed both: a number simple enough to justify the programme and rigorous enough to withstand scrutiny from the programme's critics. For a brief period, Orshansky's measure appeared to satisfy both demands simultaneously. It did not.


## Adoption: the OEO and the appearance of rigour

The Office of Economic Opportunity — the agency Sargent Shriver established to administer the War on Poverty — adopted Orshansky's economy-level thresholds in May 1965 as its working definition of poverty @fisher1997developmentorshanskythresholds. The adoption was quasi-official: the OEO used the thresholds for its own programme analysis and reporting, but no government-wide directive required other agencies to follow suit. The Bureau of the Census began using the thresholds for its annual poverty statistics shortly afterward, giving them a second institutional home.

The OEO's adoption mattered not because it gave Orshansky's measure legal force — it did not — but because it gave the government an answer to a question it had been unable to answer since the War on Poverty began: how do you know if you are winning? Johnson had promised to reduce poverty. The CEA's $3,000 line could track whether the number of families below a round threshold was going up or down. Orshansky's thresholds could do more: they could show whether poverty among children was declining faster or slower than poverty among the elderly, whether female-headed households were falling further behind male-headed ones, whether rural poverty was changing at the same rate as urban poverty. The matrix gave the War on Poverty a scorecard with enough detail to evaluate the war's actual effects on the households it claimed to be fighting for.

This is what made the measure useful. It is also what made the measure vulnerable. A scorecard with 124 cells produces results that are harder to spin than a scorecard with one number. If the single poverty rate declines by two percentage points, a president can claim progress. If the poverty rate for female-headed households with children rises while the overall rate falls, the same president faces a harder press conference. Granularity produces accountability, and accountability produces institutional discomfort with granularity.


## The Bureau of the Budget intervenes

The institutional pressures that would transform Orshansky's matrix into a line accumulated gradually between 1965 and 1969. Multiple federal agencies were now using some version of the Orshansky thresholds, but not always the same version — different agencies adjusted the thresholds differently, producing inconsistent poverty counts that embarrassed the administration and confused the public @fisher1997developmentorshanskythresholds. The need for a single, standardised definition became an administrative imperative, and administrative imperatives, as James Scott's framework predicts, resolve in the direction of simplification @scott1998seeing.

Scott argues that modern states require legibility — the reorganisation of complex social phenomena into standardised categories that can be centrally observed and administered @scott1998seeing. The Orshansky thresholds were complex, materially grounded, and differentiated: they were, in Scott's terms, illegible. They told the truth about 124 kinds of household, but they could not be reduced to a single number that all federal agencies could apply uniformly. The Bureau of the Budget's intervention in 1969 was a legibility project: it took Orshansky's granular knowledge of household need and converted it into the kind of standardised, administrable figure the federal apparatus required.

Scott's own cases — Prussian scientific forestry, Soviet collectivisation, the geometric planning of Brasília — involve dramatic impositions of state order through coercion or grand ideology. The poverty line's simplification was none of these. No one coerced Orshansky. No one invoked a grand theory of social engineering. The Bureau of the Budget issued a memorandum. The mechanism was institutional routine, not authoritarian fiat — but the effect was the same: local specificity was sacrificed to central legibility, and the knowledge that was lost in the process was precisely the knowledge that mattered most to the people the measure described.

On August 29, 1969, the Bureau of the Budget issued Circular A-46, directing all federal agencies to use a single standardised set of poverty thresholds, updated annually by the Consumer Price Index @bob1969povertythresholds. The directive made three changes to Orshansky's original matrix, and each change removed one of the properties Section 3 identified.

The first change was CPI indexing. Instead of updating the thresholds by recalculating the Economy Food Plan cost and reapplying the multiplier — which would have required new food-price data and a new food-share survey — the Bureau of the Budget directed that thresholds be adjusted annually by the percentage change in the Consumer Price Index @bob1969povertythresholds. This was simpler to administer. It was also a methodological severance. CPI indexing preserved the threshold's nominal relationship to prices but broke its substantive relationship to household consumption. The ×3 multiplier had been derived from the 1955 finding that food constituted one-third of household income. By 1969, food's share of household spending had already fallen below one-third and was continuing to decline @usda1955householdfoodconsumptionsurvey. If the multiplier had been recalculated using contemporaneous survey data, it would have been higher than three, and the resulting thresholds would have been higher — classifying more households as poor. CPI indexing ensured that this recalculation would never happen. The threshold was frozen at its 1963 relationship to household consumption, updated only for price inflation, diverging further from material reality with every year the food-share ratio continued to fall.

This is the change that destroyed material grounding. After 1969, the poverty threshold was no longer derived from what households actually spent on food. It was derived from what households had spent on food in 1955, multiplied by sixty years of price changes. The anchor to nutritional reality — the specific food baskets, the specific prices, the specific caloric requirements that Orshansky had built into every cell of the matrix — was replaced by an index that tracked the general price level without reference to any specific household need.

The second change was the elimination of the farm/non-farm distinction. The Bureau of the Budget replaced the differentiated farm and non-farm thresholds with a single set of national thresholds @bob1969povertythresholds. Farm households had lower food costs because they produced some of their own food; the original matrix had reflected this with thresholds approximately 70 per cent of the corresponding non-farm figures. The removal of this distinction did not mean that the difference between rural and urban food costs had disappeared. It meant that the poverty measure no longer recorded it. A farm family that grew a portion of its own food and a city family that purchased all of its food from retail markets were now measured against the same threshold. The matrix had said these households faced different material circumstances. The standardised line said they did not.

The third change was the elimination of the sex-of-household-head distinction. Orshansky had included this dimension because female-headed households faced systematically different economic conditions — lower wages, more constrained labour-force participation, different relationships between food expenditure and total household need @fisher1997developmentorshanskythresholds. The Bureau of the Budget removed it on the grounds that a uniform threshold was simpler to apply @bob1969povertythresholds. The economic differences between male-headed and female-headed households did not change. The poverty measure stopped noticing them.

Three changes, three losses. Material grounding severed by CPI indexing. Granularity reduced by the removal of farm/non-farm and sex-of-head dimensions. And transparency, the property that had made the measure's inputs visible and its assumptions checkable, was undermined not by a single decision but by the cumulative effect of all three: once the threshold was indexed to CPI rather than derived from a published food plan and a published survey, there was nothing left for a critic to check except the annual inflation adjustment. The measure's contestability — the property that distributed the power to challenge it — diminished in proportion to the number of published inputs that were removed from the calculation. A threshold that had once been reproducible from a library shelf and a pencil now required only a CPI table and a calculator. The arithmetic was simpler. The epistemology was emptier.


## Orshansky objects

Orshansky argued against the CPI indexing decision before it was made, and she continued to argue against it for the rest of her career @orshansky1988commentarypoverty. Her objections were not sentimental. They were precise, methodological, and documented across a series of papers and memoranda that constitute the single most sustained critique of the official poverty measure by anyone, and certainly the most authoritative — because it came from the person who built it.

In July 1970, she wrote a memorandum to Daniel Patrick Moynihan — then counsellor to President Nixon — laying out the political history of her measure from her own perspective @orshansky1988commentarypoverty. The memo documented how the thresholds had been adopted in a form she had not designed and would not have endorsed, indexed by a method she had argued against, and stripped of dimensions she considered essential. It is the closest thing in the archival record to a first-person account of institutional capture: the person who built the measure, documenting from inside the SSA how that measure was taken from her.

Her critique had three interlocking arguments, consistent across her published papers from 1969 to 1988.

The first was that the multiplier was frozen at the wrong baseline. The 1955 food-share ratio was already shifting by the time she used it in 1963; by 1969, when the Bureau of the Budget made the thresholds official, the one-third figure was observably obsolete. In a 1979 paper with Carol Fendler, Orshansky proposed recalculating the thresholds using 1965 survey data, which would have produced a multiplier closer to four and substantially higher poverty thresholds @orshansky1988commentarypoverty. The proposal was not adopted.

The second was that CPI indexing decoupled the threshold from the thing it was supposed to measure. A poverty threshold derived from food costs and updated by food costs would track changes in what households actually needed to eat. A poverty threshold derived from food costs and updated by the general price level would diverge from household reality whenever food prices moved at a different rate from the overall CPI — which they did, consistently. The method had been designed to anchor the threshold in a concrete material need. CPI indexing cut the anchor line.

The third was that the official poverty line had become a political ceiling rather than an empirical floor. In a 1978 multi-author debate in *Public Welfare* and again in her 1988 commentary for the twenty-fifth-anniversary reprinting of "Counting the Poor," Orshansky was explicit: the measure had been institutionalised at a level convenient for government rather than accurate for households @orshansky1988commentarypoverty. The line was used to minimise poverty counts rather than to find them. Governments had an interest in a low threshold because a low threshold produced a low poverty rate, and a low poverty rate could be presented as evidence that antipoverty programmes were working — or that they were unnecessary. Orshansky's original thresholds, derived from the Economy Food Plan's deliberately austere nutritional minimum, had been designed to produce a floor so low that no one below it could plausibly be described as anything but poor. The institutionalised, CPI-indexed version of her line was lower still in real terms, because the frozen multiplier failed to account for the declining share of food in household budgets, but it could no longer be contested by pointing to the food budget from which it was derived — because it was no longer derived from one.

The person who built the measure became its most searching critic. She did not argue that her original thresholds were perfect. She argued that they had been adopted in a form that removed the very features — material grounding, transparency, contestability — that had made them worth building. The measure she had designed to make poverty visible had been redesigned to make poverty administrable, and she knew the difference was not technical but political — a choice whose consequences accumulated in the lives of every family the simplified line failed to count.


## The pattern

The six-year history from publication to standardisation — from 124 thresholds to a CPI-indexed line — is the first iteration of the pattern this book traces across fifty years of welfare measurement. A mathematical tool is built to make a social condition visible. The tool's precision makes it useful for advocacy and inconvenient for administration. The institution that adopts the tool simplifies it: removes dimensions, freezes baselines, substitutes mechanical updating for empirical recalibration. Each simplification is described as a technical improvement. Each simplification is, in its effect, a political choice that reduces the measure's capacity to describe the condition it was built to measure. And the cumulative effect of the simplifications is a measure that serves the institution's need for legibility rather than the household's need for recognition.

This is not a story about bad actors. Heller was not trying to hide children. The Bureau of the Budget was not trying to freeze the poverty line. The individuals who made these decisions were solving real administrative problems — inconsistent definitions across agencies, cumbersome reporting requirements, the need for a stable baseline against which to measure progress. The decisions were rational within the institutional logic that produced them. But the institutional logic that makes simplification rational is the same logic that makes the simplified measure less honest, and the people who bear the cost of that dishonesty are the people the measure was supposed to count.

Orshansky understood this. She spent the last three decades of her career saying so, in papers and memoranda that her own institution largely ignored. The poverty line she built in 1963 is still the official US poverty measure in 2026, updated annually by CPI but otherwise unchanged in structure — a monument to administrative convenience and a rebuke to the methodological precision that produced it.
