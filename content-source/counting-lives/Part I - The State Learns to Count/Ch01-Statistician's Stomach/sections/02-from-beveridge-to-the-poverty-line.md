# 2. From Beveridge to the Poverty Line: The Postwar Demand for a Number

The postwar welfare state committed governments to reducing poverty without telling them what poverty was. This was not an oversight. The architecture William Beveridge designed in his 1942 report, *Social Insurance and Allied Services*, was built around a different question entirely: not "who is poor?" but "who is insured?" @beveridgereport1942. Beveridge's model was contributory and flat-rate: every employed person paid in; every insured person received benefits at a standard level in the event of sickness, unemployment, or old age. The system could tell the Treasury how many people were claiming, what the cost was, and whether the contribution rates were actuarially sound. What it could not do was tell anyone whether a family of four in York or a retired woman in Stepney had enough to eat. The model measured risk, not need. It administered coverage, not adequacy. And because it could not measure need, it could not tell the government it had committed to abolishing whether its project was succeeding or failing.

This paradox — a welfare state that needed to count the poor but had no agreed instrument for doing so — is the structural gap that Orshansky inherited and filled. Understanding how the gap opened requires looking at both sides of the Atlantic in the two decades after the war: the British welfare settlement that Beveridge's report inaugurated, the American welfare landscape that operated without any official poverty measure until 1969, and the various attempts — Rowntree's household surveys, Fisher's SSA experiments, the Bureau of Labor Statistics family budgets, Heller's CEA round number — that tried and failed to produce a number the state would adopt. In each case, the failure had the same structure: the state needed a number it could administer, but every number that was honest about poverty was too complicated to administer, and every number that was simple enough to administer was too dishonest about poverty to be defended.


## The Beveridge settlement and the question it could not answer

Beveridge's report was published on 1 December 1942, and 635,000 copies were sold — an unexpected figure for a government white paper, and a fact that requires explanation @beveridgereport1942. The explanation has a name, though the name did not appear on the cover: Janet Philip.

Philip was a mathematician, trained at St Andrews from 1893 to 1897, who served as School Secretary at the London School of Economics from 1920 to 1939 @oakley2021forgotten. She married Beveridge in 1942, two weeks after the report's publication. Her contribution to the report was not actuarial — the contribution rates, benefit levels, and actuarial costings came from the Government Actuary's Department, whose memorandum forms Appendix A @beveridgereport1942. What Philip did was rewrite the report's prose. She took a bureaucratic document produced by a government committee and turned it into the most politically effective policy text of the twentieth century. Ann Oakley's archival research in the Beveridge Papers at the LSE found that the 200,000-word report would have been far less readable and far less persuasive without Janet's intervention @oakley2021forgotten. Beveridge himself acknowledged this, conceding that it would be "idle to separate what came from her mind and what from his."

Her name appeared nowhere in the published document. It appeared in no headline, no subsequent account of the report's creation, no standard history of the postwar welfare state — until Oakley recovered the evidence from the Beveridge supplementary papers, donated by Philip's son in 1974, six decades after the fact @oakley2021forgotten.

The gender thread of this chapter runs through two parallel erasures, and the mechanisms differ. Orshansky's 124 thresholds were simplified: the institution could not use her complexity, so it discarded it, and the simplified version bore her name while abandoning her method. Philip's prose was absorbed: she made the Beveridge Report legible and persuasive, and because the work of making something readable is structurally invisible — it leaves no seam, no signature, no point at which a reader can say "this sentence is hers" — the contribution disappeared into the text it improved. Orshansky lost her work through reduction. Philip lost hers through integration. Both women performed work that was essential to the postwar welfare state's most consequential instruments. Both were removed from the headline account by mechanisms that were not accidental but structural: the same institutional logic that simplifies a 124-threshold matrix into a single line also absorbs a woman's editorial labour into a man's authorship, because in both cases the operation serves institutional administration by rendering invisible the human beings who made it work.

The report itself, whatever the credit for its prose, established the architecture of British postwar welfare: flat-rate contributions from employers and employees, flat-rate benefits paid as of right, a comprehensive system covering sickness, unemployment, maternity, retirement, and funeral costs @beveridgereport1942. The model was actuarial in structure: the Government Actuary's Department calculated the contribution rates needed to fund projected benefit payouts over a twenty-year horizon. Beveridge assumed that benefits would be set at a level sufficient for subsistence — enough to live on, not enough to live well — but the report contained no empirical investigation of what subsistence actually cost for specific household types. The benefit rates were round numbers — 24 shillings per week for a single adult, 40 shillings for a married couple — set by a combination of political judgment and actuarial constraint, not by any systematic study of what families actually needed to spend on food, rent, fuel, and clothing @harris1997beveridge.

This is the gap. Beveridge's model insured against risk. It did not measure need. The flat-rate structure — the same benefit regardless of household size, geographic location, or specific circumstances — was a deliberate design choice, not an accidental omission. Flat rates were administratively simple: they required no household assessment, no means testing, no inquiry into the domestic circumstances of claimants. They could be calculated by the actuary, administered by the post office, and defended in Parliament with a single number. But they could not answer the question that the welfare state's own logic demanded: were people still poor after the benefits had been paid?

James Scott's concept of state legibility is useful here, with a caveat @scott1998seeing. Scott argues that modern states reorganise complex social phenomena into standardised, simplified categories that can be centrally observed and administered — legibility projects that make populations visible to the state at the cost of discarding local, situated knowledge @scott1998seeing. Beveridge's flat-rate model is a legibility project in this sense: it made the insured population visible as a uniform category of contributors and claimants, abstracting away the household-level variation that would have made the system harder to administer but more responsive to actual need. The caveat is that Beveridge was not the authoritarian high modernist of Scott's paradigm cases — he was a liberal reformer building a system he believed would eliminate want. The simplification was not imposed by coercion but by institutional logic: the state needed a system it could run through a post office, and the post office could not run 124 different benefit rates.

The result was a welfare state that could report its own coverage but not its own adequacy. By the early 1960s, both Britain and the United States had welfare systems that spent substantial public money on income support, social insurance, and public assistance — and neither had an official measure of whether any of it was enough.


## The American landscape: partial measures, no standard

In the United States, the absence was even more conspicuous, because the American welfare state lacked even Beveridge's nominal commitment to subsistence. The Social Security Act of 1935 created old-age insurance and unemployment insurance, but it did not define poverty, did not set benefit levels by reference to any empirical standard of need, and did not create any mechanism for measuring whether its programmes were reaching the people who needed them.

What existed instead was a series of partial, unofficial, and mutually incompatible measures. Seebohm Rowntree's three York surveys — conducted in 1901, 1936, and 1951 — had established the methodological precedent that Orshansky would inherit: a poverty line derived from a nutritional minimum, priced in actual markets, scaled by household size @rowntree1901povertyastudyoftownlife. Rowntree's 1901 study found 27.8 per cent of York's population below his poverty line — 9.9 per cent in "primary poverty" (income insufficient to meet minimum physical needs even with optimal spending) and 17.8 per cent in "secondary poverty" (income technically sufficient but resulting in deprivation through the conditions of its expenditure) @rowntree1901povertyastudyoftownlife. The distinction is methodologically significant because it is the same distinction Orshansky's method would later operationalise: primary poverty is measurable through the gap between a nutritional floor and actual income; secondary poverty requires a theory of how income is spent, which is harder to quantify and easier to dismiss as personal choice.

Rowntree's reason for setting his poverty line at the nutritional minimum rather than at any higher standard was explicitly political and anticipates Orshansky's identical choice of the Economy Food Plan over the Low-Cost Food Plan: he wanted a floor "that could not be challenged for its generosity" @rowntree1901povertyastudyoftownlife, so that poverty measured below it could not be attributed to extravagance. The austerity of the standard was not a limitation but a rhetorical strategy — the more austere the floor, the harder it was for critics to argue that the poor had only themselves to blame. Orshansky would make the same calculation sixty years later, and for the same reason.

But Rowntree's surveys were academic, not administrative. They were conducted by a private researcher in a single English city. They established a method but not a standard that any government adopted as official. In America, the Bureau of Labor Statistics published city worker family budgets that estimated the cost of a "modest but adequate" standard of living, but these were descriptive accounts of middle-class spending patterns, not poverty thresholds. Gordon Fisher, whose 1992 and 1997 accounts of the poverty line's administrative history remain the definitive source, documented a scattered landscape of ad hoc measures within the SSA itself — experimental poverty lines used in internal reports, none with official standing, none based on a systematic methodology, none adopted by any programme as an eligibility criterion @fisher1997developmentorshanskythresholds.

Into this gap walked Walter Heller and the Council of Economic Advisers. In 1963, Heller proposed a single $3,000 annual income threshold as the poverty line — a round number chosen for its political utility, not its empirical foundation @heller1963povertyline. The CEA's purpose was specific: demonstrate that the rate of poverty reduction had slowed from approximately 1.0 per cent per year between 1945 and 1955 to 0.4 per cent per year between 1956 and 1960, thereby justifying the War on Poverty that Lyndon Johnson was preparing to announce @heller1963povertyline. The $3,000 line did this job. It could be announced in a press release, cited in a speech, printed in a headline. It told the country how many Americans were poor. It could not tell anyone whether a single elderly person's poverty was the same as a family of eight's, because it made no distinction between them.

Orshansky's specific objection was precisely this: a single threshold applied to all family sizes systematically understated the number of children in poverty relative to elderly persons, because larger families require more income to meet basic needs @fisher1997developmentorshanskythresholds. The CEA's line was not wrong in the way that a mathematical error is wrong. It was wrong in the way that an average is wrong when applied to a distribution that varies by household type: it was correct for no one in particular while being incorrect for almost everyone in specific.


## The SSA corridor and the decision to build

By 1963, Orshansky had been at the Social Security Administration for over a decade, working on children's welfare statistics. She was not a senior official. She was not running a programme. She was a statistician in a bureau that produced reports, and the reports she was producing kept running into the same wall: she could not find a measure of poverty that told her anything about actual children in actual households @orshansky1963childrenpoor.

The measures available to her were either too aggregate (the CEA's $3,000 line, which could not distinguish a child's poverty from an elderly person's), too narrow (the BLS city worker budgets, which described middle-class spending rather than poverty), or too unofficial (the SSA's internal experimental lines, which no programme used). None of them started where Orshansky's training had taught her to start: with the household itself, with what it needed to eat, with what that food cost, with the gap between the cost and the income. None of them were built from the bottom up.

She decided to build one.

The decision was not dramatic. There was no memo announcing a new research programme, no budget allocation, no institutional mandate. Orshansky built her poverty measure in the interstices of her regular work at the SSA, using data that was publicly available — the USDA's Economy Food Plan, the 1955 Household Food Consumption Survey — and a method that followed directly from her training in home economics: start with a concrete material need (food), establish its cost for a specific household type, and derive an income threshold from the empirical relationship between food expenditure and total income @orshansky1963childrenpoor.

The result — published in July 1963 as "Children of the Poor" in the *Social Security Bulletin*, a journal read by civil servants rather than the general public — was the first systematic, empirically grounded, household-specific poverty measure in American history @orshansky1963childrenpoor. It was also the measure that would become the official US poverty line, the most consequential welfare statistic of the twentieth century, and the number that — frozen at its 1963 methodology, indexed to CPI rather than to the food costs it was derived from — would still be defining who counted as poor in 2026.

Peter Townsend, working in Britain during the same period, was reaching for the same thing from a different direction @townsend1979poverty. Where Orshansky asked what a household needed to eat and derived a material threshold, Townsend asked what activities, customs, and forms of social participation a person needed to maintain in order to be a functioning member of society, and measured deprivation as the gap between those expectations and actual circumstances. His 1968–69 survey of over 2,000 UK households produced a "deprivation index" of 60 indicators — whether you could afford to have friends over for a meal, whether your children had a birthday party, whether you went on holiday @townsend1979poverty. The approaches were complementary: Orshansky measured whether a household could feed itself; Townsend measured whether a person could participate in ordinary social life. Orshansky's method was more precise but narrower. Townsend's was broader but harder to operationalise. And both — this is the pattern the book traces — were built to preserve complexity and were simplified by the institutions that adopted them.

The postwar demand for a number had been met. The question now was what the institutions would do with it.
