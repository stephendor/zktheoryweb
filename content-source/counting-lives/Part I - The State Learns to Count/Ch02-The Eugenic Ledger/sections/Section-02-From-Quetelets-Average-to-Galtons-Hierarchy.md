# 2. From Quetelet's Average to Galton's Hierarchy

Before Galton, the normal curve belonged to Adolphe Quetelet. In 1835, the Belgian astronomer and statistician published *Sur l'homme et le développement de ses facultés, ou essai de physique sociale* — a book whose ambition was to do for social life what Newton had done for celestial mechanics. Quetelet's central proposition was that if you measured a human trait across a large enough population — height, chest circumference, the propensity to commit crimes in a given year — the resulting distribution would follow the same bell-shaped curve that astronomers used to model observational errors. The mean of that distribution was what Quetelet called *l'homme moyen*: the average man, the statistical centre of gravity of a population, the figure around whom individual deviations scattered with lawful regularity [@quetelet1835homme-moyen].

The concept was, at its core, descriptive. Quetelet was interested in the stability of social phenomena — in the observation that murder rates, marriage rates, and even the number of letters that reached the dead-letter office in Paris held remarkably steady from year to year. The average man was the explanatory device for this stability: if individual behaviour varied unpredictably, the aggregate did not, because the underlying causes persisted. The normal curve captured this regularity. Deviations from the mean were, in Quetelet's framework, precisely what astronomers understood them to be: errors, fluctuations, noise around a signal. The mean was the signal. The tails were not pathological. They were incidental.

Quetelet sometimes drifted toward idealisation — as Desrosières puts it, his "average man" was "presented as a kind of prudent centrist, who avoided every conceivable form of excess — for perfection lies in moderation" [@desrosieres1998politicslargenumbers]. But the drift was inconsistent and secondary. The dominant register of his work was social physics: the discovery of regularities in aggregate data, the demonstration that statistical law operated in the human domain as it did in the physical one. The average man was a descriptive construct. Quetelet did not build a politics on the tails of his distribution. He did not ask what should be done about the people who fell far from the mean. He was interested in the curve as a feature of populations, not as a judgment on individuals.

## The Transformation

Francis Galton inherited the normal curve from Quetelet and performed a transformation that is at once mathematically subtle and politically enormous. He made the average a failure.

The word Galton used was "mediocrity" — from the Latin *mediocritas*, middle state. In ordinary English, mediocrity already carried connotations of inadequacy, of falling short. Galton exploited this ambiguity with precision. In *Hereditary Genius*, published in 1869, the normal distribution is no longer a description of how a population scatters around its centre. It is a grading system. The right tail contains the eminent — Galton's word for those whose ability, by his reckoning, placed them in the upper reaches of the distribution. The left tail contains the degenerate, the feeble-minded, the constitutionally incapable. The middle is mediocrity: not the most common condition of a healthy population, as Quetelet would have had it, but the place where ordinary people settle when nothing lifts them above the mean. Galton was explicit about the symmetry of the grading: "eminently gifted men are raised as much above mediocrity as idiots are depressed below it; a fact that is calculated to considerably enlarge our ideas of the enormous differences of intellectual gifts between man and man" [@galton1869hereditary-genius, 36].

The mathematical object is the same curve. The interpretive framework is entirely different. Quetelet's average man was a centre of gravity; Galton's mediocre man was a measure of what the population wasted by failing to breed selectively. Quetelet's tails were noise; Galton's tails were the only part of the distribution that mattered — the eminent right tail to be encouraged, the degenerate left tail to be contained, and the vast middle to be lamented as the price of uncontrolled reproduction.

This is not a misuse of Quetelet's mathematics. It is a repurposing — a demonstration that the same formal object can carry radically different political freight depending on who is interpreting it and what question they need it to answer. The normal curve does not tell you whether the tails are pathological. The curve is a shape. The pathology is a decision.

## The Architecture of *Hereditary Genius*

The argument of *Hereditary Genius* rests on a specific method and a specific dataset. Galton drew his sample of "eminent" men from biographical dictionaries, legal records, lists of fellows of the Royal Society, senior clergy, military commanders, statesmen, and literary figures — men whose eminence was defined by their visibility to the institutions of Victorian public life [@galton1869hereditary-genius]. He then counted how many of these men had eminent relatives: fathers, sons, brothers, nephews, grandfathers. The result, he claimed, was a concentration of eminence within families that could not be explained by chance. The clustering was too strong. Something was being inherited.

The statistical form of the argument was compelling on its own terms. Galton constructed pedigree tables showing the rate at which eminence appeared in successive degrees of kinship, and he modelled the expected rate under assumptions of random distribution. The observed rates exceeded the expected rates. The inference was that eminence — which Galton equated with "natural ability" — was transmitted hereditarily, following the same laws that governed the inheritance of physical traits.

The problems with this argument were visible at the time and have only become more obvious since. Gould's critique in *The Mismeasure of Man* identifies the two foundational errors with characteristic precision: reification and ranking [@gould1981_mismeasure_man]. Reification: Galton treated "eminence" and "natural ability" as though they were measurable quantities — real properties of individuals rather than social designations conferred by institutions. A man was eminent because he was a judge, a Fellow of the Royal Society, a bishop. But access to these positions was restricted by birth, class, education, and patronage. To count the clustering of eminence within families was, in large part, to count the clustering of privilege — the inheritance of social capital, not the inheritance of biological capacity. Galton's statistical argument could not distinguish between these two forms of inheritance, and he made no serious attempt to do so.

Ranking: the entire framework depended on arranging human beings along a single scale from degenerate to eminent, with the normal distribution providing the mathematical form that made this ranking appear natural rather than constructed. The scale itself was never justified. Why should "eminence" — defined as occupancy of elite institutional positions — be the measure of human worth? Why should the normal distribution be understood as a hierarchy rather than as a description? These are political questions, not mathematical ones, and *Hereditary Genius* answers them by refusing to ask them. The ranking is assumed. The mathematics provides the form. The reader is left to experience the hierarchy as something the data has revealed rather than something the author has imposed.

## The Sampling Problem

There is a further difficulty that deserves emphasis because it recurs throughout the history this book traces: the sample was constructed by the conclusion it was designed to support. Galton did not begin with a random sample of the British population and discover that ability clustered in certain families. He began with a list of men who occupied elite positions — positions that were, by the social structure of Victorian Britain, largely hereditary in fact if not in law — and then argued that the clustering of their positions within families demonstrated hereditary ability. The circularity is not subtle. A man became a judge because his father was a barrister, because his grandfather had the wealth to educate his sons, because the family owned property that generated the income that financed the education that produced the professional credentials that Galton counted as evidence of natural ability. To call this hereditary genius is to confuse the social reproduction of a class with the biological transmission of a trait.

MacKenzie's account of the Galtonian tradition makes clear that this confusion was not accidental [@mackenzie1981_statistics_britain]. Galton's class position — he was independently wealthy, a grandson of Erasmus Darwin, educated at Trinity College, Cambridge — was the condition of possibility for his research. He studied the heritability of elite status from within the elite, using the institutions of the elite as his data source, and arrived at the conclusion that the elite were biologically superior. The research programme and the class interest were inseparable. MacKenzie is careful to acknowledge that the arithmetic in *Hereditary Genius* is competent and the data collection for its time was thorough. His point is that the questions Galton asked, the categories he used, and the interpretive framework he imposed on his results were shaped by the social position from which he asked them. The statistics and the ideology were co-produced.

## The Invisible Women

There is an absence in *Hereditary Genius* that the text does not acknowledge and that most commentaries pass over quickly. Galton's entire dataset is male. The categories of eminence — judges, statesmen, commanders, men of science, literary men, divines — are categories from which Victorian women were formally or practically excluded. There are no eminent women in *Hereditary Genius* because the institutions that defined eminence did not admit women. The absence is structural, not incidental.

Women appear in Galton's pedigree tables only as connective tissue — mothers, wives, daughters through whom the lineage of eminent men passes. They are necessary to the arithmetic of hereditary transmission but invisible as subjects of measurement in their own right. The question *Hereditary Genius* asks — does natural ability run in families? — could only be asked of a population defined by institutional visibility, and institutional visibility was a male prerogative. The ranked distribution of British genius is, by construction, a ranked distribution of British men. The normal curve that Galton drew from his data described a male population and generalised to a human one, and the generalisation was accomplished by silence rather than by argument [@galton1897_average_contribution].

Galton's 1897 paper "The Average Contribution of each several Ancestor to the total Heritage of the Offspring," presented to the Royal Society and published in *Nature*, treats maternal and paternal inheritance symmetrically in its algebra — each parent contributes one quarter of the offspring's hereditary endowment, each grandparent one sixteenth, and so on [@galton1897_average_contribution]. The mathematics is gender-neutral. But the social framework within which the mathematics operates is not. Women are algebraically necessary and empirically absent: present in the equations as transmitters of hereditary value, invisible in the data as bearers of their own ability. This pattern — the mathematical inclusion of women as variables and their practical exclusion as subjects — will recur throughout the book. It is present in the actuarial tables that Beveridge's welfare state was built on (Chapter 3), in the household economy that Mollie Orshansky made visible and that the SSA made invisible again (Chapter 1), and in the algorithmic risk assessments whose training data reproduces the gendered absences of the datasets they inherit (Chapter 10).

## What the Curve Became

The transformation Galton performed on Quetelet's normal curve can be stated precisely. Quetelet's curve answered the question: what does this population look like? Galton's curve answered a different question: where does this person rank? The first question is descriptive and horizontal — it maps variation without privileging any position within the distribution. The second question is evaluative and vertical — it converts a position in a distribution into a judgment about the person who occupies it.

This is the origin of the z-score as a ranking instrument, though Galton did not use that term. The z-score — the number of standard deviations a measurement falls from the mean — is now so embedded in quantitative practice that it is difficult to see it as anything other than a neutral tool of description. A student's test score at z = 1.5 is simply a fact about where they fall in their cohort's distribution. A patient's blood pressure at z = −0.8 is a fact about where they fall in a clinical reference range. But the z-score does not merely describe a position. It creates one. Before the distribution was constructed, the student's score was a mark on a page. After the distribution was constructed, it was a rank — a measure not of what the student knows but of where they stand relative to everyone else who took the same test. The position exists only because someone built the distribution, and the distribution exists only because someone decided to collect the data, define the population, and apply the curve.

Galton decided. The population he defined was the British public. The traits he measured were those he believed would correlate with hereditary worth. The curve he applied was Quetelet's normal distribution, repurposed from a tool of social description into a tool of human ranking. The z-score — the distance from the mean — became, in Galton's hands, a measure of how far an individual fell from mediocrity, in either direction: toward eminence or toward degeneracy. The mathematics was rigorous. The interpretation was a political choice. And the political choice was embedded so deeply in the mathematical form that the two became inseparable — which is, as MacKenzie argues, precisely the point. The co-production of the statistics and the ideology was not an accident. It was the method [@mackenzie1981_statistics_britain].

## The Mathematical Form as Authority

The final point about *Hereditary Genius* that matters for this book's argument concerns its form rather than its content. Galton's claims about hereditary ability were not new in 1869. The idea that talent ran in families was a commonplace of Victorian elite culture — it would have surprised nobody at Galton's club that judges' sons became barristers and admirals' grandsons joined the navy. What was new was the mathematical dress. Galton presented the clustering of eminence within families not as an observation — everyone could see it — but as a statistical demonstration: pedigree tables, expected rates, observed rates, deviations from chance. The data was arranged in columns. The argument was expressed in numbers. The conclusion was drawn from a quantitative comparison between the observed and the expected.

Porter's framework explains why this mattered [@porter1995trust]. The mathematical form placed Galton's claims beyond the reach of ordinary political contestation. If hereditary ability was a matter of opinion — if it was something gentlemen argued about over dinner — it could be contested by other opinions, other arguments, other experiences. But if it was a statistical fact — if the clustering of eminence within families was demonstrated by a published dataset, a quantitative method, and a mathematical model — then contesting the conclusion required contesting the method, and contesting the method required the same quantitative expertise that produced it. Galton's genius, in the precise sense relevant here, was not the discovery that talent ran in families. It was the transformation of that commonplace into a quantitative claim, insulated from democratic challenge by the authority of numbers that most of his readers could not evaluate and most of his critics could not reproduce.

The mathematical form did something else as well. It made the argument exportable. An opinion about hereditary ability is local — it belongs to the person who holds it, the class that shares it, the culture that produced it. A statistical finding is portable — it can be published in a journal, cited in a report, applied in a different country, used to justify a policy that the original author did not envision. *Hereditary Genius* was read in Britain, in America, in Germany. Its statistical framework — the normal curve applied to human populations, the ranking of individuals within a distribution, the inference from family clustering to hereditary transmission — became the template for eugenic research programmes on both sides of the Atlantic. The Eugenics Records Office at Cold Spring Harbor, established by Charles Davenport in 1910, used Galton's methods. The immigration restriction acts of the 1920s cited Galtonian-tradition research. The forced sterilisation programmes of more than thirty American states operated on the assumption that the left tail of the ability distribution was hereditary and that the state had an interest in preventing its reproduction.

None of this was inevitable. *Hereditary Genius* did not cause the American sterilisation programmes in any direct sense. But it supplied the mathematical form — the ranked distribution, the hereditary inference, the quantitative authority — that made those programmes legible as science rather than as politics. The form travelled. The politics was local. And the distance between the form and the politics — the gap between a bell curve on a page and a scalpel in a surgeon's hand — was bridged by the same mechanism that Porter identifies in every case of quantification's political use: the substitution of impersonal method for personal judgment, the replacement of a political argument with a mathematical one.
