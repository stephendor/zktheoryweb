# 6. The Ledger's Long Life: From *Biometrika* to *The Bell Curve*

The eugenic statistical tradition did not end with the Second World War and the discrediting of racial science. It went underground. The route it took — from the Galton Laboratory through psychometrics, from correlation to factor analysis, from the anthropometric card to the IQ score — is traceable with uncomfortable precision. The tools changed. The ranked distribution did not.

## Spearman's *g*

In 1904, Charles Spearman, a former army officer turned psychologist, published "'General Intelligence,' Objectively Determined and Measured" in the *American Journal of Psychology* [@spearman1904g-factor]. The paper's contribution was technical: Spearman observed that children's scores across different school subjects and mental tests were positively correlated, and he inferred the existence of a single underlying factor — which he called *g*, for general intelligence — that explained the common variance across all the tests. The method he used to extract this factor from the matrix of correlations was a precursor to what would become factor analysis: a technique for reducing many correlated measurements to a smaller number of latent dimensions.

The mathematical move is, in one sense, a compression. Many tests produce many scores. The scores are correlated. Factor analysis extracts the common pattern and represents it as a single number — the individual's loading on the general factor. The individual's position on *g* is their rank in the distribution of general intelligence. The operation is formally identical to the one Galton performed at South Kensington: many measurements, one ranking. The instruments are different — mental tests instead of dynamometers and audiometers — but the output is the same: a position in a distribution that claims to capture something essential about the person measured.

Spearman was not himself a frontline policy eugenicist. His interest was methodological, and his 1904 paper is a work of mathematical psychology rather than of political advocacy. But the apparatus he created — the correlation matrix, the extraction of a dominant first factor, the identification of that factor with a real cognitive property — was immediately available to the hereditarian research programme that the Galton Laboratory had established. If *g* was a real property of individuals, it could be inherited. If it could be inherited, it could be measured across generations. If it could be measured across generations, it could be used to make the same arguments about selective breeding, racial hierarchy, and the futility of social intervention that Galton and Pearson had made with cruder instruments. The transition from calipers to correlation matrices to factor loadings was a transition of technique, not of purpose.

Gould's critique of *g* in *The Mismeasure of Man* identifies the foundational error as reification: the treatment of a statistical abstraction — the first principal component of a correlation matrix — as though it were a real, unitary property of the brain [@gould1981_mismeasure_man]. A factor is a mathematical convenience. It summarises the common variance across a set of tests. It does not follow that the common variance has a single biological cause, or that the factor corresponds to a physical structure, or that the ranking it produces reflects anything more fundamental than the choice of tests that went into the matrix. Different tests produce different matrices. Different matrices produce different factors. The *g* that emerges from a battery of verbal, mathematical, and spatial tests is not the same *g* that would emerge from a battery of musical, social, and practical tests. The ranking is an artefact of the selection, and the selection is an artefact of the question the researcher wanted to answer. Galton wanted to rank the British population by hereditary worth. Spearman wanted to rank schoolchildren by cognitive ability. The normal curve served both purposes equally well, because the curve does not care what it is ranking. The ideology is in the question, not in the mathematics.

## Burt's Fraud

The twentieth-century figure who did the most to entrench the Galtonian ranking of intelligence in British educational policy was Cyril Burt. As the most influential educational psychologist of the mid-century, Burt used IQ testing and the hereditarian interpretation of *g* to argue for the tripartite system of secondary education — grammar schools, secondary moderns, and technical schools — on the grounds that children's cognitive abilities fell into distinct, largely hereditary categories that different kinds of schooling should serve. The 11-plus examination, which sorted children at age eleven into these categories, was a direct descendant of the anthropometric laboratory's ranking logic: measure, distribute, sort.

Burt's most influential contribution was a series of studies of identical twins raised apart. If twins who share identical genes but grow up in different environments still have highly similar IQ scores, the implication is that intelligence is primarily genetic — that the hereditary leash is tight, that environment matters little, and that social interventions aimed at raising cognitive ability are likely to fail. Burt reported correlations of around 0.77 to 0.80 between the IQ scores of separated identical twins — figures high enough to support the conclusion that intelligence was overwhelmingly heritable and that the ranked distribution of cognitive ability was, in large part, a fact about biology rather than a fact about schools, nutrition, housing, or parental income [@burt1966twins-fraud].

The data was fabricated. After Burt's death in 1971, Leon Kamin noticed that the reported correlation coefficients remained identical to three decimal places across successive publications, even as the sample sizes changed — a statistical impossibility. Further investigation revealed that Burt's documentation of the twin pairs was absent or untraceable, that his named co-authors may not have existed, and that the data had been, in Hearnshaw's blunt conclusion, "deliberately falsified." The British Psychological Society subsequently recognised Burt as guilty of scientific fraud.

The fraud matters for this chapter's argument in a specific way. The fraud was sustained for decades by the same mechanism that had sustained every previous entry in the eugenic ledger: the authority of mathematical form. Burt's correlations were published in respected journals. They were expressed in the impersonal language of statistical analysis. They were cited by other researchers, by policy-makers, by the architects of the 11-plus system. The numbers were precise — 0.771, reported to three decimal places — and their precision signalled rigour. That the precision was literally impossible, that the numbers could not have been produced by any real dataset, was noticed only when someone with the statistical expertise to evaluate the claim looked closely enough to see that the emperor's measurements were fiction.

The educational system that Burt's numbers helped to justify — the 11-plus, the tripartite sorting of children at age eleven into tracks that would largely determine their adult economic prospects — is the Anthropometric Laboratory's logic applied to schoolchildren. Measure at a single point. Place in a distribution. Sort into categories. Assign a future. The instruments are mental tests rather than dynamometers. The categories are grammar school and secondary modern rather than eminent and mediocre. The logic is identical.

## The Pioneer Fund

The institutional infrastructure that kept the Galtonian tradition alive through the postwar period was not the Galton Laboratory — which gradually distanced itself from its eugenic origins — but a private American foundation with an explicitly eugenic charter. The Pioneer Fund was incorporated in 1937 by Wickliffe Preston Draper, a textile heir whose early philanthropic activities included funding the distribution in American churches and schools of *Erbkrank*, a Nazi eugenics propaganda film [@pioneerfund1937draper].

The Fund's stated purpose was to "advance the scientific study of heredity and human differences" — language broad enough to encompass legitimate genetics research and narrow enough to sustain a decades-long programme of financing race-and-IQ research. Its major beneficiaries included Arthur Jensen, whose 1969 *Harvard Educational Review* article argued that racial differences in IQ were substantially genetic; J. Philippe Rushton, who constructed elaborate racial hierarchies of brain size, intelligence, and reproductive strategy; and Richard Lynn, who compiled international IQ datasets to argue for a global ranking of races by cognitive ability. The Southern Poverty Law Center classified the Pioneer Fund as a hate group. The Fund described itself as advancing scientific knowledge.

The relevance to this chapter is institutional rather than intellectual. The intellectual content of Pioneer-funded research added nothing to what Galton and Pearson had argued a century earlier: that human populations can be ranked by cognitive ability, that the ranking is substantially hereditary, and that policy should be designed to accommodate rather than resist the natural distribution. What the Pioneer Fund supplied was money — the funding that allowed this research programme to continue when the mainstream scientific community had moved away from it, and the institutional base from which the programme could re-enter public debate when the political conditions were favourable.

## *The Bell Curve*

The conditions became favourable in 1994, when Richard Herrnstein and Charles Murray published *The Bell Curve: Intelligence and Class Structure in American Life*. The book is the clearest modern expression of the Galtonian argument: a ranked normal distribution of cognitive ability, high heritability coefficients derived from twin and adoption studies, and the policy conclusion that social programmes — welfare, affirmative action, educational intervention — are largely futile because they attempt to correct a natural distribution that is substantially fixed by genetics.

The statistical tools are Galton's and Pearson's. The ranked distribution is the same normal curve that Galton had repurposed from Quetelet. The heritability coefficients are the same measures of hereditary transmission that Pearson had formalised as the correlation coefficient. The factor-analytic argument for *g* is Spearman's. The twin-study evidence includes data that traces, through chains of citation, to research funded by the Pioneer Fund. A substantial portion of the supportive citations in the book's most controversial chapter — the chapter on race and IQ — come from authors who received Pioneer Fund support.

*The Bell Curve* was a bestseller. It sold hundreds of thousands of copies. It was reviewed in every major American newspaper and magazine. It was debated in Congress. It shaped the welfare reform debate of the mid-1990s, providing intellectual cover for the argument that Aid to Families with Dependent Children was futile because the cognitive deficits of the poor were hereditary and irremediable. The mathematical dress — regression analyses, IQ distributions, heritability estimates, appendices of statistical methodology — placed the argument in the domain of science rather than politics, requiring technical expertise to contest.

The book's critics — and they were numerous, including Gould, whose revised 1996 edition of *The Mismeasure of Man* was in part a response — identified the same problems that had plagued the Galtonian tradition from the start. The heritability estimates were inflated. The definition of intelligence was circular. The racial categories were treated as biological rather than social. The causal inference from correlation to genetic determination was unjustified. The policy conclusions did not follow from the data even if the data were accepted at face value. But the criticism required expertise. It required engaging with regression tables and heritability formulae and factor loadings. It required the same mathematical literacy that had produced the claims. The ordinary reader, confronted with a bell curve on a page and a correlation coefficient in a footnote, had no way to evaluate the argument except by choosing which experts to trust. The mechanism of depoliticisation — the substitution of technical authority for democratic deliberation — worked exactly as it had worked for Galton's pedigree tables, for Pearson's immigration study, for Burt's twin correlations.

## The Ledger Continues

The eugenic ledger did not close with *The Bell Curve*. The argument has been updated again — this time with genomic data. Polygenic embryo scoring services, available commercially since the early 2020s, offer prospective parents the ability to screen embryos created through IVF for predicted cognitive ability, height, and disease risk, based on polygenic scores derived from genome-wide association studies. The scores are statistical predictions — weighted sums of thousands of genetic variants, each contributing a tiny fraction of the variance in a trait — and they are expressed in the same currency as every previous entry in the eugenic ledger: a position in a ranked distribution. This embryo scores in the seventy-third percentile for predicted educational attainment. That embryo scores in the forty-first. The instruments are DNA sequencers rather than calipers or mental tests. The output is a z-score.

The anthropometric laboratory's logic is intact. Measure. Rank. Select. The selection is no longer imposed by the state — it is offered as a consumer service, marketed to parents who want the best for their children. The coercion that characterised mid-century eugenics has been replaced by choice. The distributional thinking — the normal curve, the ranked percentile, the assumption that a position in a distribution tells you something meaningful about a person who does not yet exist — has not been replaced at all.

This chapter has traced the eugenic ledger from a corridor in South Kensington to a genomics laboratory in the twenty-first century. The tools have changed: calipers, pedigree tables, correlation coefficients, factor loadings, twin-study heritability estimates, genome-wide association statistics. The mathematical grammar has not. A population is measured. The measurements are arranged in a distribution. The distribution is read as a hierarchy. The hierarchy is presented as a fact about nature rather than a choice about value. And the mathematical form — the precision of the instruments, the impersonality of the numbers, the authority of the published dataset — places the hierarchy beyond the reach of those it ranks. The ledger remains open. The question is whether the grammar can be changed — whether the mathematical tools that were built to rank can be rebuilt to describe, whether the distribution can be read as a landscape of variation rather than a hierarchy of worth. That question belongs to the rest of this book.
