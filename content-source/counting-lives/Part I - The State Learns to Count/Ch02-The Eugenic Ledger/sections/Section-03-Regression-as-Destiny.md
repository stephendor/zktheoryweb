# 3. Regression as Destiny: The Mathematical Case for Selective Breeding

Galton's most consequential mathematical discovery began with sweet peas. In the late 1870s, he distributed packets of seeds of varying sizes to friends and correspondents, asking them to grow the plants and return the offspring seeds for measurement. The question was simple: do large seeds produce large offspring? The answer was yes, but with a qualification that turned out to matter more than the answer itself. Large seeds did produce offspring that were, on average, larger than the population mean. But the offspring were not as large as their parents. They had moved back toward the average. Small seeds, similarly, produced offspring that were small but less extremely so. The tendency was symmetrical and consistent: extreme values in one generation produced less extreme values in the next. Galton called this "regression toward mediocrity" — the phrase itself a window into the framework within which the discovery was made.

Galton confirmed the pattern in human data. Using records from his anthropometric work, he showed that unusually tall parents had children who were, on average, shorter than themselves, and unusually short parents had children who were taller. The regression was not toward any individual parent's height but toward the population mean — the same mediocrity that Galton had placed at the centre of his moral geometry in *Hereditary Genius*. The mathematical relationship could be expressed as a line: plot parent height on one axis, offspring height on the other, and the best-fitting line slopes toward the mean. The slope is less than one. The extremes are pulled inward. The population, left to its own devices, converges.

The mathematical finding is genuine, important, and still in use. Regression to the mean is a statistical phenomenon, not a biological one — it occurs in any paired measurement where the correlation between the two variables is less than perfect. It has nothing inherently to do with genetics. A student who scores exceptionally well on one exam will, on average, score less exceptionally on the next, whether or not the two exams measure the same ability. A city with an unusually high crime rate in one year will, on average, have a lower rate the next, whether or not any policy has changed. The regression is a property of imperfect correlation, not of biological destiny. But this is what we know now. What Galton knew — or chose to know — was different.

## The Eugenic Interpretation

Galton read regression to the mean as a warning. If the children of exceptional parents are less exceptional than their parents, then the exceptional qualities of one generation are diluted in the next. The population does not hold its gains. Without intervention, the distribution of human quality remains centred on mediocrity, the eminent tail replenished only by the occasional sport of nature, the degenerate tail continually fed by the unchecked reproduction of the unfit. The mathematical finding was correct. The interpretation — that regression constituted a case for selective breeding — was an ideological choice that the mathematics did not compel.

The choice is worth examining in detail because it is the template for a recurring move in the history this book traces: the transformation of a statistical regularity into a policy imperative. Regression to the mean describes what happens in the absence of selection. Galton concluded that the absence of selection was the problem. If superior individuals bred with each other rather than with the average population, their offspring would retain more of the parental advantage. The regression line would steepen. The right tail would thicken. The population would improve. The programme Galton proposed — and for which he coined the word "eugenics" in 1883, in *Inquiries into Human Faculty* [@galton1883inquiries] — was the systematic encouragement of reproduction among the fit and its systematic discouragement among the unfit. The mathematical justification was regression. The political programme was breeding. The gap between the two was bridged by an interpretive choice that the mathematics left entirely open.

Nothing in the regression line tells you that the population *should* be different from what it is. Nothing in the slope of the parent-offspring relationship tells you that the extremes ought to be preserved or amplified. The regression line describes a tendency. The tendency can be lamented, celebrated, or simply noted. Galton lamented it, and from that lament he built a political programme that would shape legislation, institutions, and the lives of millions of people over the following century. The lament was not in the data. It was in the man who read the data, a man whose position at the top of the Victorian class hierarchy gave him a personal interest in the conclusion that the top of the distribution was where value resided.

## Pearson's Formalisation

Karl Pearson took Galton's informal insights and built them into a formal statistical apparatus. Where Galton had drawn scatterplots and fitted lines by eye, Pearson derived the mathematics of regression and correlation from first principles, working within an assumption of multivariate normality to produce general formulae for the regression line, the standard error of estimate, and — most consequentially — the product-moment correlation coefficient *r* [@pearson1896correlation].

The correlation coefficient measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two variables. Its value ranges from −1 (perfect inverse relationship) through 0 (no linear relationship) to +1 (perfect positive relationship). The formula is elegant. The computation is straightforward. The interpretation is deceptively simple: a correlation of 0.5 between parent height and offspring height means that taller-than-average parents tend to have taller-than-average children, but that the relationship is far from deterministic. What the number does not contain is any information about *why* the relationship exists — whether it is genetic, environmental, social, or some combination of all three. The correlation coefficient measures association. It does not measure causation. This distinction, now a cliché of introductory statistics courses, was not a cliché in 1896. It was a distinction that Pearson's own work consistently blurred.

Pearson developed the correlation coefficient within the Galtonian research programme on heredity. His 1896 paper "Mathematical Contributions to the Theory of Evolution. III. Regression, Heredity, and Panmixia" is explicit about the purpose: the mathematical tools are designed to quantify the strength of hereditary transmission, to determine how tightly offspring resemble their parents, and to model the consequences of different mating patterns for the population distribution [@pearson1896correlation]. The *r* of 0.5 between parent and offspring heights was not, for Pearson, an abstract measure of association. It was a measure of hereditary determination — a number that told you how firmly the biological leash held, how much of a child's stature was fixed by parentage rather than circumstance.

The slide from association to determination is the critical move, and it happened inside the mathematics itself. Pearson's derivations assumed multivariate normality — that the joint distribution of parent and offspring measurements followed a bell-shaped surface in multiple dimensions. Under this assumption, the regression line is the best linear predictor: given a parent's measurement, the offspring's expected measurement lies on the regression line, and the correlation coefficient measures how tightly the actual measurements cluster around that line. The mathematical framework is powerful and, within its assumptions, correct. But the assumption of multivariate normality is not biologically innocent. It imposes a specific structure on the relationship — linear, symmetric, homoscedastic — that may or may not hold in any particular case. And it embeds the analysis within the Galtonian framework of normally distributed populations, ranked from left tail to right, in which the interesting question is always how strongly the ranking persists across generations.

## The Biometric Laboratory

Pearson's institutional base was the Biometric Laboratory at University College London, established with Galton's support and eventually housed in the building that bore Galton's name. The laboratory was the research engine of the British eugenic statistical tradition. Its output was prodigious: papers on the inheritance of physical traits, on the correlation between parental and offspring characteristics, on the statistical methods required to analyse hereditary data. Pearson founded *Biometrika* in 1901 as the publication vehicle for this work, and the journal became the central organ of biometric research for the next half-century.

The laboratory's research programme was inseparable from its eugenic commitments. Pearson and his collaborators — most notably Alice Lee, who co-authored several of the key heredity papers — computed correlations and regressions for father-son, father-daughter, mother-son, and mother-daughter pairs, mapping the inheritance of height, head size, eye colour, and other physical characteristics across thousands of English families [@pearson1903inheritance-physical]. The tables distinguish parental and child sex, estimate the proportion of variance "accounted for" by parental measurements, and explore the relative contributions of maternal and paternal lines. The algebra treats mothers and fathers as symmetrical sources of hereditary influence. The framing treats both as carriers of heritable quality whose reproductive choices determine the stock's trajectory.

The gender dynamics of this research are revealing. Women in the biometric datasets were measured bodies — their heights, head circumferences, and other traits were recorded as carefully as those of men. Alice Lee, who did much of the computational labour, was herself a statistical researcher of real ability. In 1901, Lee published a study that applied Pearson's own correlation methods to a dataset of male anatomists and scientists at University College London — men whose intellectual credentials were beyond dispute — and found no meaningful relationship between cranial capacity and intellectual distinction [@lee1901dataproblemevolution]. The result was a direct challenge to one of the craniometric tradition's foundational claims, delivered using the biometric laboratory's own tools. That the challenge did not redirect the laboratory's research programme — that Pearson and his colleagues continued to treat head measurements as markers of hereditary quality — is itself evidence that the eugenic questions drove the statistical apparatus, not the reverse. The tools could be turned against the ideology. The institution could not.

But the purpose of measuring women's bodies in the biometric programme was not, in general, to assess their eminence or ability — categories that Galton had defined as male. The purpose was to quantify the maternal contribution to inheritance: how much of a child's stature came from the mother, how tightly mother-daughter resemblance tracked father-son resemblance, whether maternal inheritance obeyed the same "laws" as paternal. Women were data points in the hereditary calculus. Their bodies were measured so that the efficiency of hereditary transmission could be quantified. The question the measurements served was not "what are these women capable of?" but "what do these women transmit?"

This is the pattern identified in the previous section — mathematical inclusion, social exclusion — now operating at the level of institutional research rather than individual authorship. Pearson's biometric laboratory measured women more extensively and more carefully than Galton's anthropometric laboratory ever had. The data was better, the methods were more rigorous, the sample sizes were larger. But the framework within which all of that measurement occurred still treated women primarily as vectors of hereditary transmission. The correlation between mother's height and daughter's height mattered because it quantified how much of the daughter's body was determined before she was born. The measurement was precise. The question it served was eugenic. And the precision of the measurement lent the question an authority it could not have achieved through political argument alone.

## The Tool and Its Afterlife

The correlation coefficient that Pearson developed for measuring the strength of hereditary transmission is now one of the most widely used statistical tools in the world. It appears in every introductory statistics textbook. It is computed in every social science department, every medical research unit, every market research firm, every actuarial office. The formula is the same one Pearson published in 1896. The questions it is applied to have changed beyond recognition.

A public health researcher computes the correlation between air pollution and asthma hospitalisation rates. A labour economist computes the correlation between years of education and lifetime earnings. A criminologist computes the correlation between neighbourhood poverty rates and violent crime. A welfare analyst computes the correlation between benefit sanctions and employment outcomes. In none of these cases does the researcher think of the correlation coefficient as a tool designed to measure hereditary fitness. The eugenic origin is invisible. The tool has been thoroughly depoliticised — abstracted from the question it was built to answer, generalised into a content-free measure of association, taught as a technique rather than as a history.

The question this chapter asks — and that the book returns to throughout — is whether the depoliticisation is complete. The correlation coefficient was not merely used within a eugenic research programme. It was developed as part of that programme, its mathematical form shaped by the specific questions it was designed to answer. Pearson assumed multivariate normality because that assumption was consistent with Galton's model of normally distributed populations. He focused on parent-offspring correlations because the eugenic programme required a measure of hereditary transmission. He interpreted high correlations as evidence of strong hereditary determination because the policy conclusions he wanted to draw — that selective breeding could shift the population distribution — required that the biological leash be tight.

None of this means that the correlation coefficient is a eugenic tool when used by a public health researcher in 2026. But it does mean that the tool carries a structural inheritance: it is optimised for linear relationships, it assumes a specific distributional form, and it produces a single number that collapses the complexity of a relationship into a scalar measure of "strength." These properties are features designed for a specific purpose — measuring the tightness of hereditary association — that turn out to be useful for many other purposes as well, but that also carry assumptions about the nature of the relationships they measure. The assumption of linearity, the focus on a single summary statistic, the reduction of a complex social relationship to a number between −1 and +1: these are design choices, made by Pearson, for Pearson's reasons. That they have become invisible as choices — that they now appear as the natural properties of a neutral tool — is itself part of the story this book tells. The tool's neutrality is an achievement, not a fact. And achievements can be interrogated.

MacKenzie's formulation is precise: the statistics and the ideology were co-produced [@mackenzie1981_statistics_britain, 221]. The correlation coefficient was not a neutral tool that eugenicists misused. It was a tool whose mathematical form was shaped by eugenic questions, whose development was funded by eugenic institutions, and whose early applications were designed to support eugenic policy. That it has since been applied to countless non-eugenic questions does not erase the co-production. It demonstrates something more interesting: that a tool built for one purpose can outlive that purpose, shedding its origins as it travels, until it arrives in a context so remote from its birthplace that the connection seems absurd. The correlation between benefit sanctions and employment outcomes has nothing to do with Francis Galton's sweet peas. Except that the mathematical form — the assumption of linearity, the reduction to a single coefficient, the interpretive slide from association to determination — is the same form, developed for the same reasons, carrying the same structural assumptions, now applied to the measurement of a welfare state that the tool's inventors would have considered an impediment to the eugenic improvement of the race.

MacKenzie's conclusion is worth quoting directly: "Without eugenics, statistical theory would not have developed in the way it did in Britain — and indeed might not have developed at all, at least till much later" [@mackenzie1981_statistics_britain, 221]. The claim is strong, and it carries a corollary that the rest of this book explores. If eugenics shaped the statistical tools we inherited, then the tools encode the questions eugenics asked — questions about ranking, hereditary fitness, and the identification of populations whose reproduction the state should manage. But other questions were possible. Mollie Orshansky's statistical work, which Chapters 1 and 4 examine in detail, was developed through a different motive entirely: the desire to make the material reality of poverty visible in terms specific enough that policy could not look away. What kind of statistical apparatus might have developed if that motive — the precise measurement of need rather than the ranking of worth — had driven the field from the beginning? The question is unanswerable, but it illuminates what was lost when the tools took the shape they did.
