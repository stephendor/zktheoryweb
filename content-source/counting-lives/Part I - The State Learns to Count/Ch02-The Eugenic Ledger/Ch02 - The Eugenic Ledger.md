# Ch2 – The Eugenic Ledger: Galton, Pearson, and Hierarchy

## Core Claim
The mathematical tools that govern welfare, credit, policing, and algorithmic 
classification in the twenty-first century were not invented to describe human 
populations neutrally. They were invented to rank them. Francis Galton's normal 
distribution, regression to the mean, and correlation coefficient, and Karl 
Pearson's formal statistical machinery, were designed to answer a specific 
eugenic question: how do you measure hereditary worth, identify the superior 
and inferior tails of the human distribution, and build the scientific case 
for selective breeding? The answer was a mathematical grammar — normal curve, 
z-score, regression line, correlation matrix — that is now so embedded in 
every quantitative discipline that its eugenic origins have become invisible. 
This chapter makes them visible again. Not to invalidate the tools — they 
are genuinely powerful — but to show that power and neutrality are not the 
same thing, and that the grammar we inherited encodes the questions it was 
built to answer.

## Mathematical Tools in Play
- Tool(s): Normal distribution as a moral geometry — the tails are 
  pathological by definition; z-score as a ranking instrument that 
  converts a continuous measurement into a position in a hierarchy; 
  regression to the mean as an argument about hereditary destiny; 
  correlation coefficient as a measure of the strength of inherited 
  traits; heritability coefficient as a claim about the proportion 
  of variance in a trait attributable to genetic inheritance
- Governance function: Converts the social question "who should receive 
  state support?" into the scientific question "where does this person 
  fall in the natural distribution of human worth?" — a transformation 
  that makes the withholding of support appear as the recognition of a 
  natural fact rather than a political choice. The z-score does not 
  merely describe a person's position; it assigns them a rank in a 
  moral order that the mathematics appears to have discovered rather 
  than constructed.

## Key Scenes / Narrative Beats
- [x] Opening: London, 1884 — the International Health Exhibition, 
  South Kensington; Galton charges sixpence to be measured across 
  seventeen physical and sensory tests; 9,337 visitors pass through 
  his laboratory over the exhibition's run; the data is used to 
  establish population norms and to argue that sensory acuity 
  correlates with intelligence; the laboratory is presented as 
  science, experienced as entertainment, and functions as ideology
- [x] Hereditary Genius, 1869: Galton's founding text — the statistical 
  argument that eminence runs in families at a rate incompatible with 
  chance; the methodological move from Quetelet's descriptive "average 
  man" to Galton's prescriptive hierarchy; the normal curve as an 
  argument about what humanity should look like rather than what it does
- [x] The Discovery of Regression: Galton studying sweet pea seeds and 
  human heights discovers that extreme values in one generation tend 
  toward the mean in the next; the mathematical finding is genuine and 
  important; the interpretation — that without selective breeding, 
  quality degrades — is an ideological choice dressed as a mathematical 
  necessity
- [x] Pearson's Laboratory: University College London, 1890s — Pearson 
  formalises Galton's insights into a statistical apparatus: the 
  correlation coefficient, the chi-square test, the method of moments; 
  he also founds the journal Biometrika, which becomes the vehicle for 
  applying these tools to the questions of racial fitness, immigration 
  restriction, and the hereditary basis of poverty
- [x] The Colonial Ledger: Galton's correspondence with colonial 
  administrators, his proposals for the selective settlement of 
  Africa by "superior races," Pearson's studies of Jewish immigrant 
  children in East London concluding that they are intellectually 
  inferior to British children — the same mathematical tools 
  applied to the same ranking project at imperial scale
- [x] The "Residuum": the eugenic classification of the urban poor 
  as the degenerate left tail of the British population — a category 
  that flows directly from the normal curve's pathologisation of 
  below-average performance into the welfare policy concept of the 
  "underclass"; the mathematical and the moral judgment are 
  inseparable from the start
- [x] Contemporary Echo: Charles Spearman's g-factor (1904) and 
  Cyril Burt's fraudulent twin studies as the twentieth-century 
  transmission vehicle; The Bell Curve (1994) as the Galtonian 
  argument in modern psychometric dress; the polygenic embryo 
  scoring services of the 2020s as the anthropometric laboratory 
  with better instruments

## Argument in Sections

### 1. The Threepenny Laboratory: Science as Ideology at South Kensington
Galton's 1884 anthropometric laboratory was the eugenic project's most 
brilliant public relations exercise. By charging sixpence for a measurement 
session that returned the visitor a card with their personal data, Galton 
transformed the ideological work of ranking the British population into 
a recreational activity that the subjects paid for themselves. The 
laboratory measured height, weight, arm span, strength, visual acuity, 
hearing, and reaction time — none of which Galton had established as 
measures of hereditary worth, but all of which carried the authority 
of precision instruments and numerical output. The measurements were 
used to construct population norms that could then define "deviance" 
— a statistical category that is simultaneously mathematical and moral. 
This section reads the laboratory as Scott's legibility project in 
miniature: turning the variable, particular, embodied reality of 
9,337 individual Londoners into a dataset from which a ranked 
distribution could be extracted. The sixpence was not the point; 
the ranked distribution was.

### 2. From Quetelet's Average to Galton's Hierarchy
Adolphe Quetelet's "average man" (l'homme moyen, 1835) was a descriptive 
concept — the statistical centre of a human distribution, a tool for 
understanding the typical rather than the ideal. Galton inherited the 
normal curve from Quetelet and performed a transformation that is at 
once mathematically subtle and politically enormous: he made the average 
the mediocre. In Galton's framework, the normal distribution is not 
a description of what humanity is but a measure of where each person 
falls relative to what humanity could be. The right tail is eminent; 
the left tail is degenerate; the middle is, literally, "mediocrity" 
— from the Latin for middle. The moral geometry is built into the 
vocabulary. *Hereditary Genius* (1869) applies this framework to 
the study of eminent families, arguing that intellectual greatness 
clusters in pedigrees at rates that demonstrate hereditary transmission. 
The statistical argument is weak by modern standards — the sampling 
is biased toward men with access to the institutions that confer 
eminence, the definition of eminence is circular, and the heritability 
inference conflates genetic and social transmission. But the mathematical 
form — a ranked distribution, a quantified argument, a published dataset 
— gave the ideology the authority of science before the science 
existed to contest it.

### 3. Regression as Destiny: The Mathematical Case for Selective Breeding
Galton's discovery of regression to the mean — made initially through 
studies of sweet pea seed size and subsequently confirmed in human 
height data — is a genuine mathematical insight: extreme values in 
a parent generation tend, on average, toward the population mean in 
the offspring generation. The mathematical finding is correct and 
important. The interpretation Galton placed on it was an ideological 
choice that the mathematics did not require: that without selective 
intervention, human quality would degrade toward mediocrity, and 
that the function of eugenics was to counteract regression by 
ensuring that superior individuals bred with each other rather than 
with the average population. Karl Pearson formalised this argument 
by developing the correlation coefficient — a measure of the strength 
of the relationship between parent and offspring traits — and 
demonstrating high correlations for physical and (he claimed) 
intellectual characteristics. The correlation coefficient is now 
one of the most widely used tools in all of social science; 
its origin as a device for measuring the strength of hereditary 
transmission is invisible in its current applications. This section 
makes the origin visible, and then asks what it means that the same 
tool measures both pea-height inheritance and the relationship 
between poverty and benefit sanctions.

### 4. The Colonial Ledger: Ranking at Imperial Scale
The eugenic project was never confined to the British working class. 
Galton's correspondence with colonial administrators, his public 
proposals for the selective settlement of Africa by "superior races," 
and his studies of the relative "worth" of different national and 
racial populations demonstrate that the anthropometric laboratory's 
ranking logic was always intended to operate at imperial scale. 
Karl Pearson's 1925 study of Jewish immigrant children in East 
London — concluding from head measurements and school performance 
data that they were intellectually inferior to British children and 
represented a threat to "national efficiency" — is the most 
scientifically detailed and most politically consequential 
application of the Galtonian method to the question of immigration 
restriction. The study is methodologically worthless by any 
current standard: the "British" comparison group was socioeconomically 
advantaged; the measurements were unreliable; the inference from 
school performance to hereditary intelligence was unjustified. 
But it was published in the leading statistical journal of its 
day, by the most eminent statistician in Britain, in the numerical 
language of science. This section reads the colonial ledger as 
the book's first extended case study in Porter's depoliticisation 
argument: claims that would be recognised as racist if made in 
political language were made invisible as ideology by being made 
in mathematical language.

### 5. The Residuum: When Statistics Meets the Poor Law
The eugenic concept of the "residuum" — the degenerate urban poor, 
constitutionally incapable of self-improvement, a hereditary drag 
on the national stock — is the missing link between Part I's two 
historical traditions. The word entered British political vocabulary 
in the 1880s, precisely the decade of Galton's anthropometric 
laboratory, and it located the familiar moral category of the 
"undeserving poor" inside the statistical framework of the normal 
distribution: the residuum is simply the left tail of the hereditary 
quality distribution. This is the moment the book's two threads — 
the statistical and the welfare — become explicitly entangled. 
The Poor Law's moral distinction between the deserving and 
undeserving poor had been made by magistrates and overseers 
using personal judgment; the eugenic tradition offered to replace 
that judgment with measurement. The residuum could be identified 
by its position in the distribution, its children could be 
predicted to occupy the same position, and welfare policy could 
be designed accordingly. This section traces the residuum concept 
from its eugenic origins through to its twentieth-century 
successors — the "social problem group" of the 1930s interwar 
eugenicists, Keith Joseph's "cycle of deprivation" (Ch13), 
and the contemporary "underclass" discourse that UC's 
conditionality regime implicitly invokes.

### 6. The Ledger's Long Life: From Biometrika to the Bell Curve
The eugenic statistical tradition did not end with the Second World 
War and the discrediting of racial science. It went underground 
into psychometrics, re-emerging through Charles Spearman's 
g-factor (1904), Cyril Burt's fraudulent twin studies (1950s–70s), 
and the Pioneer Fund's financing of race-and-IQ research throughout 
the postwar period. *The Bell Curve* (Herrnstein and Murray, 1994) 
is the clearest modern expression of the Galtonian argument: 
a ranked normal distribution of cognitive ability, high heritability 
coefficients, and the policy conclusion that welfare programmes 
are futile because they attempt to correct a natural distribution. 
The statistical tools are Galton's and Pearson's; the policy 
conclusions are the Poor Law's; the mathematical dress is 
contemporary psychometrics. This section draws the genealogy 
explicitly, connecting the 1884 anthropometric laboratory to 
the 1994 bestseller and forward to the polygenic embryo scoring 
services of the 2020s (Ch7) — showing that the ledger has 
never closed, only been updated with better instruments.
