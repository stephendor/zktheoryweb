# Conclusion – The Reckoning

## Core Claim
The arithmetic of power is not fate. It was built, piece by piece, decision by decision, threshold by threshold, by people who made choices — about what to measure, whose experience would generate the data, whose life would be made legible, and whose would be rendered invisible or suspicious or expendable. Those choices can be made differently. They are being made differently, right now, in communities from Detroit to Dundee, in legislation from Holyrood to The Hague, in research produced by people who refuse to let administrative convenience override the obligation to see the person behind the number. The reckoning this conclusion calls for is not a tribunal but a recognition: that mathematics has always been political, that its politics have consequences measured in human lives, and that the question of who controls the arithmetic of power is the political question of our moment.

## Argument in Sections

### 1. The Five Transitions, One Last Time
This conclusion does not retrace the five transitions in detail — seventeen chapters have done that. But it does something the chapters could not do individually: it holds all five in view simultaneously and asks what they add up to. The answer is not a conspiracy. The people who built the eugenic ledger, the RAND welfare model, the FICO score, the UC sanction algorithm, and the Palantir data integration layer did not, in most cases, think of themselves as building instruments of domination. They thought of themselves as scientists, engineers, rationalists, and reformers. That is precisely the point. The arithmetic of power does not require malice. It requires only the repeated substitution of a mathematical convenience for a human complexity, made at sufficient scale and speed that the gap between the model and the person becomes invisible to everyone except the person. The five transitions are not five conspiracies. They are five moments in which that substitution was made, institutionalised, and defended as neutral — and five moments in which people resisted it, at cost to themselves, with tools that were less powerful but more honest.

### 2. The Counter-Tradition: What Was Always There
The book's most important argument is not its critique but its recovery. The counter-tradition — Orshansky's matrices, the mutual societies' actuarial solidarity, Townsend's relational poverty measure, the Kerala People's Plan, the Isle of Eigg's governance structure, the Child Poverty (Scotland) Act's multi-measure framework — has always existed. It did not emerge in response to the arithmetic of power; in many cases it predated it, running alongside it as the road not taken rather than the road not yet built. The Rochdale Pioneers were cooperative in 1844, the same decade the Poor Law's workhouse mathematics was being perfected. Norbert Wiener wrote *The Human Use of Human Beings* in 1950, the same year RAND was applying his mathematics to welfare as a control problem. Janet Philip was building the statistical backbone of the Beveridge Report in 1942 while the same actuarial tradition was being weaponised by American insurers to redline Black neighbourhoods. The counter-tradition is not recent. It is not marginal. It is not utopian. It is documented, technically grounded, and available. What it has lacked is not ideas but power — the power to determine which mathematical culture becomes the infrastructure of governance and which remains a civic practice, a research programme, a community experiment.

### 3. What the Numbers Cannot See
Every mathematical system examined in this book has had a characteristic blindness — a category of human experience that its design excluded, a dimension of life that its feature set could not capture, a person whose existence fell outside its model. Galton's regression could not see structural disadvantage. RAND's objective function could not see the material reality of the households its parameters described. The FICO score cannot see the daughter who cares for her mother at the cost of her own credit history. The UC sanction algorithm cannot see the claimant with no broadband access who missed a digital reporting deadline. The Gangs Matrix cannot see the young Black man who is on it only because his brother is. Palantir's entity resolution graph cannot see the difference between a genuine criminal network and a community of people who share a postcode, a school, and a mosque. Each of these blindnesses was a design choice. Each of them was pointed out, at the time, by people who understood what was being excluded — and each was overridden by the institutional pressure to make the system scalable, administratively convenient, and defensible in the language of objectivity. The ethics of measurement begins with the insistence that these blindnesses are not technical limitations to be improved in the next version. They are political decisions to be contested in the present one. Orshansky spent the last thirty years of her life insisting on this, and she was right.

### 4. The Scottish Thread: A Different Country Is Possible
Scotland runs through this book not as a perfect alternative but as a proof of possibility. The Highland Clearances show what happens when an objective function defined by landowners is imposed on a people who have no standing inside the model — the most violent expression of the arithmetic of power examined anywhere in these pages. The Scottish Poor Law's community-based relief, the Red Clydeside labour movement's counter-statistics, the founding of the welfare state by a parliamentary tradition shaped by Scottish Labour — all of these are part of the same contested history. The Scottish Social Security system's dignity principle, the Child Poverty (Scotland) Act's statutory measurement framework, the land reform legislation's transparency requirements, the Isle of Eigg's community governance structure — these are not the end of that history but its present expression. Scotland has not solved the problem this book examines. It has Scottish poverty, Scottish inequality, Scottish algorithmic welfare, Scottish data surveillance. What it has, in addition and in partial contest with those things, is a set of institutions built on explicitly different principles — and that difference, however partial and imperfect, matters. It matters to the people inside those institutions. It matters to the argument of this book. And it matters personally to its author, who inherited both the poverty and the counter-tradition, and who has tried, in these pages, to be honest about the weight of both.

### 5. The Gender Thread: The Mathematics of the Invisible
Women built this book's counter-tradition as much as they were harmed by its dominant one. Mollie Orshansky, trained in home economics rather than econometrics, brought an epistemology of material specificity to a field shaped by abstraction. Janet Philip did the statistical work that made the Beveridge Report publishable while her name appeared in no headline. The Women's Budget Group produces the correct numbers about gendered austerity that the Treasury will not put in its own impact assessments. The Glasgow Disability Alliance's disabled women researchers produce knowledge about poverty that no administrative dataset can see. ATD Fourth World's co-researchers — disproportionately women, caring for children in poverty — develop the poverty indicators that official measures exclude. The care economy that feminist economists have spent fifty years trying to make visible in national accounts represents more economic activity than manufacturing and is performed overwhelmingly by women and is still, in 2026, absent from GDP. This is not coincidence and it is not oversight. The exclusion of care from measurement is the same political choice as the exclusion of Orshansky's specificity from welfare administration, the same choice as the cohabitation rule's encoding of a particular model of family, the same choice as UC's single household payment that makes women's financial dependence statistically invisible. An ethics of measurement that does not begin from the question of what women's lives require is not an ethics of measurement. It is a more polite version of the system it claims to replace.

### 6. The Choice Before Us
The arithmetic of power is, right now, accelerating. Large language models trained on data generated by and for the already-powerful are being deployed in welfare eligibility decisions, medical diagnosis, criminal risk assessment, and immigration enforcement at a speed that outpaces both regulatory response and democratic comprehension. The VC-backed companies of Ch9 are the procurement partners of the governments of Ch10–13. The EA-aligned researchers of Ch8 are the AI safety advisers of the frontier labs whose systems are being embedded in public infrastructure. The new eugenicists of Ch7 are in or adjacent to the executive branch of the most powerful state on earth. The direction of travel is clear. It is not, however, inevitable. The EU AI Act exists. The Scottish Social Security Act exists. Foxglove exists. The Data for Black Lives movement exists. The Alkire-Foster MPI exists. The Child Poverty (Scotland) Act exists. The Eigg Heritage Trust exists. The Glasgow Disability Alliance exists. The mutual aid networks of 2020 showed that millions of people, given the opportunity, will choose solidarity over sorting. The counter-tradition is not marginal. It is present, technically grounded, and fighting on every front simultaneously — in courts, in legislatures, in research institutions, in community centres, in the design specifications of public algorithms, in the questions asked of census forms, in the principles written into welfare legislation. The choice before us is not between mathematics and humanity. It is between a mathematics that counts only what is convenient for those who build it and a mathematics that counts what is real for those who live inside it. That choice is being made right now. This book has tried to make clear what is at stake in making it.

### 7. The Last Number
Mollie Orshansky's original poverty line was not a number. It was an argument that a number could be honest — that it was possible to build a mathematical measure of human need that preserved the specificity of actual lives rather than abstracting them into administrative convenience, that was transparent enough to be contested, that was humble enough to acknowledge what it could not see, and that was grounded enough in material reality to mean something to the people it described. That argument was not wrong. It was defeated — repeatedly, institutionally, deliberately — by a different argument: that what matters is what is measurable, that what is measurable is what is administratively convenient, and that administrative convenience is the closest approximation to justice that large systems can achieve. This book has argued, across seventeen chapters, that the defeat was political rather than technical, that the argument Orshansky made is still available, and that the institutions needed to make it prevail are being built — slowly, partially, at enormous personal cost to the people building them, in the face of the arithmetic of power's full institutional weight. The last number in this book is not a statistic. It is the number of people, unnamed in these pages, who went to a food bank this week because a system that could not see them decided they did not qualify for what they needed to survive. That number is not abstract. It is specific. It is contestable. It is, in Orshansky's tradition, the beginning of the argument, not its end.
