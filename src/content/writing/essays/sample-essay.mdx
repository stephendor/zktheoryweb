To measure poverty is to draw a line. Below the line: insufficiency, deprivation, a claim on public action. Above it: adequacy, however precarious. The act of drawing that line is seldom treated as the political act it plainly is.

<Sidenote number={1}>
  Sen's *Development as Freedom* (1999) remains the canonical critique of income-only conceptions of welfare. The capability approach reframes poverty as the deprivation of substantive freedoms rather than mere resource shortfall.
</Sidenote>

The history of poverty measurement in Britain is, in part, a history of strategic ambiguity. Rowntree's "primary poverty" standard of 1901 — the cost of calories sufficient to maintain "merely physical efficiency" — was deliberately austere.<sup aria-describedby="sn-1">1</sup> It was designed to deflect the accusation that what he was calling poverty was really improvidence. A family that spent any penny on tobacco or the music hall was, by definition, not primarily poor.

## The Equivalence Scale Problem

How we convert household income into individual welfare requires an equivalence scale: a set of weights that specifies how much more (or less) a household of a given size and composition needs to achieve the same standard of living as a reference household. The mathematics beneath this conversion determines, in very concrete terms, how many children are counted as poor.

Consider the following: if we move from the OECD-modified scale to the McClements scale — a change that sounds technical enough to be bureaucratic — we shift the measured child poverty rate in the UK by several percentage points. This is not measurement error. It is a consequence of an embedded normative judgement about economies of scale in household consumption.

$$
y_{\text{eq}} = \frac{y}{(A + \alpha_c \cdot C)^\theta}
$$

Where $y$ is household income, $A$ the number of adults, $C$ the number of children, $\alpha_c$ the child cost parameter, and $\theta$ the economies-of-scale parameter. Each of these quantities is contested.

> Poverty lines are always normative artefacts. The question is not whether they embody values but whose values they embody and whether those values have been made transparent.

## Topological Intuitions

Recent work in topological data analysis offers an intriguing complement to conventional poverty measurement. Rather than asking "who is below the line?", a TDA approach asks: what is the *shape* of the deprivation distribution? Are the poor a connected mass, or isolated clusters? Is multi-dimensional deprivation concentrated in a few high-dimensional pockets, or distributed across a Betti-number-rich landscape?

These are not merely poetic metaphors. Persistent homology can identify structural features of the deprivation distribution that standard summary statistics — the headcount ratio $H$, the poverty gap $PG$, the FGT family $P_\alpha$ — simply cannot capture.

Whether such topological features map onto actionable policy levers remains an open question. But asking the question is itself a reframing: it insists that poverty is not a threshold but a topology.
