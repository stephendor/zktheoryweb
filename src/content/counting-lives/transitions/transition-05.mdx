## The Defining Shift

The contemporary era does not merely continue the technocratic turn; it radicalises it. The algorithmic state deploys poverty measurement at a granularity and speed that no earlier technology permitted — individual risk scores, predictive welfare eligibility algorithms, automated benefit sanctions, machine-learning models for child protection surveillance — and does so in a bureaucratic and legal context that renders the underlying measurement choices almost entirely opaque to the people they affect. The defining shift is not in the goals of measurement, which remain recognisably continuous with the neoliberal era, but in the technical capacity to implement those goals at scale and the corresponding collapse of the political visibility of measurement choices.

## Key Political and Technical Forces

Three forces distinguish this era. First, the digitalisation of welfare administration — the migration of benefit systems to online platforms, the accumulation of administrative microdata, the integration of data across government agencies — created the infrastructure for algorithmic governance. Second, the commercial machine-learning revolution, driven by the same data and computing infrastructure developed for advertising and logistics, provided the technical toolkit that welfare bureaucracies adopted. Third, the political project of austerity, intensified after 2008, created an administrative incentive structure in which automated systems were presented as neutral efficiency tools while their primary function was to reduce eligibility and enforce compliance.

Virginia Eubanks's *Automating Inequality* (2018) provides the essential empirical anatomy of this era: the algorithmic tools that police the American poor are not aberrations but logical extensions of the measurement and surveillance functions that poverty policy has always performed.

## Chapters in This Transition

This transition runs across four chapters. **Chapter 10** examines the diffusion of risk scoring from actuarial science into welfare administration, tracing the genealogy of eligibility algorithms. **Chapter 11** analyses predictive policing and child welfare algorithms as case studies in how machine learning operationalises structural poverty as individual risk. **Chapter 12** turns to automated financial surveillance — credit scoring, benefit fraud detection, real-time income assessment — and its distributional effects. **Chapter 13** examines the explainability crisis: what it means that neither the administrators nor the governed can read the measurement systems that determine access to the social minimum.
