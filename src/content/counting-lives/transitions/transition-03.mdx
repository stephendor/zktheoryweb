## The Defining Shift

The thirty years following the Second World War produced a second and distinct transformation in poverty measurement: the technocratic turn. Where the Victorian era had asked *who are the poor?* and the welfare state era had asked *what does the state owe them?*, the cybernetics and systems era asked *how do we optimise the system?* Social policy became an engineering problem. Poverty became a variable in a larger model of economic management. The figures who drove this transformation were not social reformers but operations researchers, economists attached to government planning bureaux, and the new think-tanks — above all, RAND Corporation in the United States — that provided the technical architecture of post-war governance.

## Key Political and Technical Forces

Two forces were decisive. First, the quantitative revolution in economics — the diffusion of econometrics, input-output analysis, and later computable general equilibrium models — gave governments tools for modelling the effect of transfers and benefits on aggregate consumption and labour supply. Poverty measurement was increasingly tied to macroeconomic modelling. Second, the Cold War ideological context pressured Western governments to demonstrate that capitalism could eliminate poverty without resort to socialist redistribution. The result was a mode of measurement that could show progress — perpetually declining headcount ratios — while systematically setting thresholds so low that structural poverty was guaranteed to remain invisible.

Mollie Orshansky's development of the US poverty threshold inside the Social Security Administration in the early 1960s is the paradigmatic instance: a technically careful measure deployed in a political context that selected the lowest defensible line and then froze it.

## Chapters in This Transition

**Chapter 5** examines the post-war diffusion of cybernetics and systems thinking into social governance, tracing how feedback-loop logic reshaped the conceptual vocabulary of welfare administration. **Chapter 6** turns to the specific case of the Orshansky threshold — its actuarial basis, its institutionalisation in the War on Poverty, and the political choices embedded in a supposedly technical measurement decision. Together they show how the language of optimisation obscured the ideological character of measurement choices that would define American poverty policy for the next half-century.
