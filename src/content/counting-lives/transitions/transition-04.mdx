## The Defining Shift

The neoliberal era brought the most fundamental conceptual revision to poverty measurement since Rowntree. Where previous transitions had argued about how to measure poverty, the market consensus challenged the premise that meaningful measurement was possible — or desirable — in the first place. The political project of the 1980s and 1990s, on both sides of the Atlantic, required a vocabulary in which structural poverty disappeared as a category of political concern, replaced by individual labour market failure, welfare dependency, and cultural deficit. Measurement did not disappear, but it was reoriented: from distributional analysis to behavioural economics, from headcount ratios to activation incentives.

## Key Political and Technical Forces

The intellectual infrastructure of this shift drew on several interlocking arguments. Human capital theory, originating in the Chicago School, reframed poverty as underinvestment in productive capacity — a problem of individual choice rather than structural constraint. The concept of the poverty trap provided a pseudo-technical justification for benefit reductions by modelling the disincentive effects of means-tested transfers. And the growing political influence of think-tanks funded by corporate donors ensured that these arguments circulated as settled technocratic wisdom rather than as contested political claims. The consequence was a generation of poverty measurement practice that selected metrics — employment rates, benefit receipt levels, individualised risk scores — that made the welfare state's residual function visible while making its distributional failures invisible.

## Chapters in This Transition

This transition is examined across three chapters. **Chapter 7** traces the intellectual history of human capital theory and its displacement of structural accounts of poverty in mainstream economics. **Chapter 8** analyses the political economy of welfare reform in Britain and the United States during the 1980s and 1990s, showing how measurement choices were instrumentalised to justify retrenchment. **Chapter 9** examines the third-way synthesis of the late 1990s — the attempt to reconcile social justice rhetoric with market logic — and the measurement regime it produced: one in which poverty was permanently on the verge of being solved.
