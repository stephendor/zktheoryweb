Karl Pearson was the founding figure of modern mathematical statistics, the creator of the chi-squared test, the product-moment correlation coefficient, and the method of moments, and the founder of *Biometrika* (1901) — the first journal of applied statistics. He also directed the Galton Laboratory at University College London (later renamed the Biometric Laboratory) and was an ardent, doctrinaire eugenicist from early career to late life, a combination that shaped both the techniques he created and the institutional apparatus through which they were disseminated.

Pearson's intellectual biography is one of the most striking instances of the entanglement of political commitment and scientific method in the history of social science. His early socialism gave way to a science of "national efficiency" in which the statistical measurement of human heredity was to provide the basis for a programme of selective breeding. His commitment to quantitative rigour was absolute and was directed, above all, at ensuring that the claims of eugenics were not merely ideological but mathematical. The result was a body of statistical technique that outlasted the eugenic programme itself and entered the mainstream of social and natural science.

In Chapter 2, Pearson is examined as the figure who completed the mathematisation of Galton's programme and created the institutional structures — the laboratory, the journal, the training programme — through which statistical methods were exported from eugenics to social science more broadly. The chapter traces how the laundering of eugenic methods as neutral techniques depended on separating mathematical form from political content, and how that separation was never complete.
