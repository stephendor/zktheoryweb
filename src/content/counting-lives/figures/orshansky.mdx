Mollie Orshansky was an economist in the Division of Research and Statistics of the Social Security Administration, a Jewish woman from New York whose family had been poor, and the unwitting architect of the official poverty line that still governs US social policy. In two papers published in the Social Security Bulletin in 1963 and 1965 ("Children of the Poor" and "Counting the Poor"), she developed a set of poverty thresholds based on the minimum food budget from the USDA's Economy Food Plan, multiplied by a factor of three derived from the finding that low-income families spent approximately one-third of their income on food.

Orshansky presented her thresholds as provisional estimates, explicitly noting their limitations: they were based on an emergency food budget, not a nutritionally adequate one; the food-expenditure multiplier was derived from 1955 data that was already a decade old when she wrote; and they reflected Rowntree's subsistence logic rather than any conception of what it meant to participate adequately in mid-century American society. She calculated multiple thresholds at different levels and recommended using the higher ones. Political officials in 1965 chose the lower.

Once institutionalised as the official poverty line, Orshansky's thresholds were frozen and adjusted only for price inflation, never for changes in living standards — a decision that guaranteed the measured poverty rate would fall steadily as real incomes rose, regardless of whether the actual conditions of the poor had improved relative to the society around them. Orshansky spent her later career criticising this institutionalisation. She appears in Chapter 6 as the paradigmatic case of the technocratic turn — careful technical work deployed in a political context that selected the option that minimised the visible scale of the poverty problem — and returns in Chapter 16 in the book's discussion of what an adequacy-based poverty measure would look like.
