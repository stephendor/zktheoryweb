Virginia Eubanks is a political scientist, writer, and activist whose work examines the intersection of technology, poverty, and social justice. She is best known for *Automating Inequality: How High-Tech Tools Profile, Police, and Punish the Poor* (2018), a rigorous and accessible investigation of algorithmic governance systems deployed against low-income Americans: the Allegheny Family Screening Tool for child welfare risk assessment, the Indiana automated Medicaid and welfare eligibility system, and the coordinated entry system for homeless services in Los Angeles.

*Automating Inequality* argues that these systems do not represent a technological transformation of poverty administration but a continuation — by more efficient and less legally visible means — of the surveillance, gatekeeping, and punishment functions that poverty policy has always performed. The difference is that algorithmic systems can operate at greater scale, with greater speed, and with far less accountability than the bureaucrats they replaced. The people most affected — overwhelmingly poor women of colour — have no meaningful right of appeal and no accessible explanation for why the algorithm scored them as it did.

Eubanks's work is central to two chapters. In Chapter 10, she provides the essential empirical anatomy of how risk scoring functions in welfare administration, showing how actuarial methods developed for insurance contexts were repurposed for social services. In Chapter 15, she appears in the counter-mathematics block as a practitioner-theorist whose community technology projects model an alternative: the design of measurement systems in collaboration with the communities they affect, rather than on behalf of the institutions that govern them. Her concept of "the digital poorhouse" — the algorithmic extension of the nineteenth-century workhouse's coercive and surveillance functions — directly bridges this book's historical argument and its contemporary analysis.
