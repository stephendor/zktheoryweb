Francis Galton was Charles Darwin's cousin, a product of the Victorian scientific gentry, and one of the most consequential and troubling figures in the history of measurement. A polymath of extraordinary range — he made contributions to meteorology, cartography, anthropology, psychology, and statistics — he is best known as the founder of eugenics and the originator of the statistical concepts of regression and correlation.

Galton's eugenic programme was inseparable from his statistical work. His anthropometric laboratory at South Kensington (1884–1885) collected systematic measurements of thousands of visitors — height, weight, strength, visual acuity — with the explicit purpose of studying the heritability of human traits and designing a science of breeding for "improvement." From this work he derived the concept of regression-to-the-mean (originally called "regression to mediocrity") and the idea of the correlation coefficient, both of which became foundational tools of twentieth-century social science after being stripped of their explicitly eugenic context.

Galton appears in Chapter 2, where the book argues that regression and correlation cannot be understood as neutral techniques that Galton happened to develop for problematic purposes: the techniques were designed *for* and *by* the eugenic programme. He is a key figure in both the Scottish thread (as the mathematician who extended Quetelet's social physics into a dynamic science of inheritance) and the gender thread (as the collector of data in which women appear primarily as reproductive inputs, reducing their economic lives to fertility measurements).
