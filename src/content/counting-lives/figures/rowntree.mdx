Benjamin Seebohm Rowntree was a chocolate manufacturer, a Liberal social reformer, and the founder of modern poverty survey methodology. His three surveys of York — in 1901, 1936, and 1950 — created the empirical template within which poverty measurement in Britain developed for the next century: a systematic household-by-household survey producing an estimate of the percentage of the population living below a defined threshold, with the threshold itself determined by calculating the minimum income required to maintain "merely physical efficiency."

The 1901 survey, *Poverty: A Study of Town Life*, is Rowntree's foundational work. Its central methodological innovation was the distinction between "primary poverty" — households whose income was insufficient to maintain physical efficiency even if spent entirely on necessities — and "secondary poverty" — households with sufficient income that was being "wasted" on non-essential expenditure. The primary poverty threshold was calculated from dietary science: a minimum caloric requirement, translated into the cheapest available foods, and multiplied by a factor for non-food necessities. This budget-standard approach to poverty measurement — the idea that the poverty line could be derived from the minimum cost of a scientifically specified bundle of goods — became the dominant paradigm of poverty measurement for most of the twentieth century, and its direct descendant is the Orshansky threshold still used as the official US poverty line.

Rowntree appears centrally in Chapter 1, where the book analyses both the genuine reformist ambition behind his surveys and the ideological choices — about whose dietary needs counted, what constituted waste, and where the threshold should be set — that were embedded in his apparently technical measurement decisions.
