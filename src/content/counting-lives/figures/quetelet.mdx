Adolphe Quetelet was born in Ghent in 1796 and trained initially as a mathematician before becoming director of the Royal Observatory of Brussels, a post he held for nearly half a century. He is the pivotal figure in the emergence of social statistics as a discipline: the thinker who first systematically applied the mathematics of error theory — above all, the normal distribution — to the description of human populations and social conditions.

His central innovation was the concept of the *homme moyen*, the average man. In *Sur l'homme et le développement de ses facultés* (1835), Quetelet argued that just as the scatter of astronomical measurements around a true value could be described by a normal curve, so the physical and moral variation of a human population could be described by the same form, with the mean representing not just a statistical artefact but a natural ideal. Poverty, on this view, was a statistical deviation rather than a product of social structure — the poor were outliers from the natural norm, not victims of an unjust order.

Quetelet appears centrally in Chapter 1, where his social physics is examined as the founding ideological move of modern poverty measurement: the naturalisation of inequality through the prestige of mathematical form. His influence runs through the Scottish thread, as he was the key conduit through which the European statistical tradition — itself partly shaped by Scottish Enlightenment social philosophy — entered British and eventually global poverty analysis.
