William Beveridge was a Liberal economist and social administrator whose 1942 report *Social Insurance and Allied Services* — universally known as the Beveridge Report — provided the intellectual blueprint for the post-war British welfare state. Trained at Oxford and shaped by the Fabian tradition, he spent the inter-war years as director of the London School of Economics before being tasked by the wartime coalition government with chairing an interdepartmental committee on social insurance whose modest administrative brief he transformed into a comprehensive vision for the elimination of the "Five Giants": Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, and Idleness.

The Beveridge Report proposed a system of flat-rate contributory insurance covering all citizens from cradle to grave, providing a universal social minimum below which no citizen would be allowed to fall. Its political impact was enormous: published in 1942, it became a bestseller and a major part of the wartime political consensus that swept the Labour Party to power in 1945. The welfare state that resulted was not identical to Beveridge's blueprint — the Treasury exerted considerable downward pressure on benefit levels and the universalism was qualified in practice — but it was the closest any British government came to implementing a coherent anti-poverty measurement framework.

Beveridge appears in Chapter 3, where the book examines both the aspiration of the Report — a genuinely universal social minimum — and its embedded assumptions, particularly the male breadwinner model that structured the treatment of married women in the insurance framework (examined in the gender thread) and the actuarial logic that set benefit levels at subsistence rather than adequacy.
