## A Different Mathematics

The history this book has traced is a history of reductions. The poor become a threshold. The household becomes a caloric minimum. The welfare claimant becomes a risk score. The Global South becomes a purchasing-power-parity line. Each reduction erases something real — a texture of experience, a layer of causation, a form of agency — in the service of a number that is more tractable, more manipulable, and more useful to the powers that commission the counting. The question this chapter asks is: does it have to be this way? Are there mathematical frameworks that could describe poverty without reducing it in this way? This chapter argues that topological data analysis offers the beginning of an answer.

This chapter's core argument is that the shape of poverty — its multi-dimensional topology — can be described mathematically without reduction to a scalar, and that this description is prerequisite to a solidarity-based measurement practice.

### In This Chapter

- How persistent homology's scale-independent description of topological features in high-dimensional data spaces provides a mathematical framework for representing co-deprivation that does not require prior commitment to a fixed threshold or a single dimension of measurement
- How the Mapper algorithm's construction of a simplicial complex from a data-to-filter-function provides a visual and analytical representation of the poverty landscape — its connected components, transitional zones, and isolated pockets — without the loss of qualitative complexity that dimensionality reduction entails
- How the concept of a Betti number as a topological invariant provides a mathematically rigorous way to count the independent dimensions of deprivation in a dataset, offering a richer poverty count than the scalar threshold while remaining interpretable
- How these tools differ, in their epistemological assumptions and their political affordances, from the statistical methods that have dominated poverty measurement — what they can and cannot do, and why their limitations matter as much as their capabilities

### Connection Forward
