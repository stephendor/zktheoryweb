## The Database State

In December 2021, the UK Department for Work and Pensions quietly renewed its data-analytics contract with Palantir Technologies. The contract — worth £480,000 for that period — gave Palantir access to data from Universal Credit, Personal Independence Payment, and other benefits systems, to be integrated in its Foundry platform for 'fraud and error detection'. Palantir's relationship with the DWP was one of several dozen welfare-analytics contracts the company held globally; it had similar arrangements with welfare agencies in Australia, New Zealand, and several US states. The contracts were, in each case, large in scope, vaguely specified in public documentation, and effectively immune to external audit. When civil society organisations requested details under freedom of information legislation, they were met with commercial confidentiality claims. When academic researchers attempted to assess the systems' effects on benefit recipients, they found no mechanism to do so. The poverty surveillance infrastructure being constructed was, by design, not describable.

This chapter argues that the Palantir welfare model represents not a technical upgrade to poverty administration but its delegation to actors whose accountability structures are designed to resist democratic oversight.

### In This Chapter

- How Palantir's Gotham platform, developed for intelligence and counter-terrorism use, was adapted for welfare fraud detection with minimal modification to its underlying data-integration and anomaly-detection logic — importing military surveillance assumptions into civilian poverty governance
- How the company's contract structures, intellectual property protections, and public-interest exemption claims combine to create a practical accountability void around welfare algorithms affecting millions of claimants in multiple countries simultaneously
- How Palantir's presence in welfare administration connects the libertarian political philosophy that produced PayPal, Palantir, and the broader Thiel network to the algorithmic poverty governance described in Chapter 10 — closing a circle between Silicon Valley's political philosophy and its commercial applications
- How the 'hostile environment' administrative culture in UK immigration and benefits policy created the political conditions that made Palantir's welfare contracts possible — and what this suggests about the relationship between political will and technical capacity in the algorithmisation of poverty
