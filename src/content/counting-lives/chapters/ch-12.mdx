## The Number That Governs

Bill Fair and Earl Isaac launched their first credit scoring system in 1956. By the early twenty-first century, the FICO score they had pioneered governed access to mortgages, car loans, and credit cards for hundreds of millions of people in the United States alone. In the United Kingdom, Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion maintained equivalent infrastructure for a similarly large fraction of the adult population. These were not neutral measures of financial behaviour. They were algorithms trained on historical data that encoded decades of racially discriminatory lending in their baseline parameters, translated impoverished circumstances into number form, and then used those numbers to enforce further exclusion — a self-reinforcing loop in which poverty scores determined access to the capital that could, in principle, end poverty. The credit score was the most successful poverty-measurement instrument in history, and its success consisted precisely in its success at preventing anyone from noticing that it was a poverty measurement instrument at all.

This chapter argues that the credit score constitutes the foundational infrastructure of the algorithmic poverty governance described in this section, and that understanding it as poverty measurement is essential to understanding what the algorithmic present has done.

### In This Chapter

- How credit scoring emerged from mid-century American consumer finance and was shaped, from its origins, by the racial and spatial geography of federal mortgage policy — through the Home Owners' Loan Corporation neighbourhood grades to the redlined maps embedded in bureau baseline data
- How FICO's extension from consumer credit into employment screening, rental eligibility, and insurance pricing proceeded without systematic democratic deliberation, creating a de facto social scoring system by accretion of commercial decisions rather than public policy
- How the proposed expansion of credit scoring into 'alternative data' — social media, utilities, rental payments — represents an intensification of algorithmic poverty governance into previously unmeasured domains, reproducing discrimination at greater resolution rather than eliminating it
- How European credit scoring frameworks differ from the US model and what those differences reveal about the political choices embedded in credit data governance — showing that the current architecture is not technically inevitable but the product of specific legal, commercial, and political decisions
