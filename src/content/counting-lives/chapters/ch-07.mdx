## The Currency of Disruption

In 1998, Peter Thiel and Max Levchin founded Confinity with a vision of creating a digital currency immune to governmental debasement. By the time PayPal went public in 2002, that vision had been partially realised — and partially transformed into something more politically significant: a demonstration that financial infrastructure was hackable, that legacy payment systems could be disrupted, and that the resulting products could reach populations that traditional banks had declined to serve. The anti-poverty implications that Silicon Valley drew from this experience were predictable in retrospect. Poverty was a problem of financial exclusion. Exclusion was a problem of inadequate infrastructure. Build better financial rails, and poverty would diminish along them. The state, with its cumbersome welfare systems and measurement bureaucracies, was not the solution; it was the obstacle.

This chapter argues that this framing was not merely analytically inadequate but structurally conservative — converting a political question (who controls the infrastructure, and in whose interest?) into a technical one (how do we extend the infrastructure?).

### In This Chapter

- How PayPal's founding ideology — libertarian, anti-state, convinced of market efficiency and suspicious of political solutions — shaped the philanthropic imagination of the Silicon Valley generation that became billionaires in its wake
- How the Gates Foundation's financial-inclusion agenda built on and extended this framework, constructing a global measurement infrastructure centred on banking access rates and mobile-money penetration while marginalising distributional measures
- How the startup methodology's emphasis on measurable outputs and rapid iteration was applied to poverty programmes, producing an evaluation culture incompatible with the long-horizon institutional change that structural poverty reduction historically requires
- How the rollout of mobile money platforms in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia created new forms of financial precarity alongside improved access — a dynamic that the financial-inclusion measurement paradigm was structurally unable to capture
