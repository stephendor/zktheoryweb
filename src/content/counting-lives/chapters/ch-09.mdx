## The Portfolio of the Poor

In 2001, Jacqueline Novogratz founded Acumen with the proposition that poverty could be addressed through 'patient capital' — investment returns measured over a decade rather than a quarter, calibrated against social outcomes as well as financial ones. The proposition was influential; within a decade, impact investing had become a recognised asset class, the World Bank under Jim Yong Kim had restructured its development financing around blended capital models, and 'social return on investment' had become a standard vocabulary in philanthropic circles. The measurement infrastructure assembled to serve this paradigm — SROI calculations, impact metrics, ESG scores, development impact bond contracts — represents the most recent large-scale attempt to convert poverty reduction into a number that capital markets can trust and trade.

This chapter argues that the attempt fails, systematically and predictably, because the measurement framework it imports from venture capital is calibrated for the wrong objective function entirely.

### In This Chapter

- How the impact investment movement translated venture capital's stage-gating logic into a development-finance methodology, and what this meant for the time horizons, outcome metrics, and accountability structures of poverty programmes managed to investor expectations
- How social-return-on-investment (SROI) calculations, despite their apparent sophistication, reproduce the core limitation of cost-effectiveness analysis — measuring only outcomes that can be monetised within the investment horizon and by the party holding the measurement instrument
- How the World Bank's development impact bond programme illustrates the central paradox of financialised impact measurement: the drive to quantify social value creates powerful incentives to optimise for measurable proxies rather than the underlying condition being addressed
- How the ESG scoring industry — a multi-billion-dollar measurement apparatus — has systematically failed to distinguish genuine redistribution from reputational management, producing a market for the performance of social responsibility rather than for its substance
