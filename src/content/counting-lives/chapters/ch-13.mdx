## The Respectability of Numbers

In 1990, the United Nations Development Programme published its first Human Development Report, introducing the Human Development Index as a counter to the World Bank's GDP-centric development measurement. Amartya Sen and Mahbub ul Haq had designed it to capture what development was for — the expansion of human capability and freedom rather than the accumulation of national income. The gesture was transformative in intent. Within a decade, it had been institutionalised. The HDI appeared in annual league tables, drove donor-country diplomacy, and shaped the conditions attached to development finance. What got lost in the institutionalisation was precisely what had made it argue for transformation: the political claim that people had a right to lead the lives they had reason to value, and that current distributions of power prevented them from doing so. The number survived. The argument was quarantined.

This chapter argues that the respectable calculus of international poverty measurement is not a description of the world but a set of political decisions made to look like measurements.

### In This Chapter

- How the World Bank's international poverty line was constructed from the Orshansky logic applied to cross-national income data, and how the choice of threshold level has been determined not by any principled standard of adequacy but by the political tolerance of donor-country governments for visibility of global inequity
- How the Alkire-Foster MPI introduced genuine methodological innovation in multidimensional measurement while embedding specific political choices about dimensions, weights, and cutoffs that reflect donor priorities rather than poor-community priorities — choices that academic respectability has largely placed beyond scrutiny
- How the SDG indicator framework's 231-indicator architecture disciplines global development policy by making measurability a prerequisite for political visibility — goals that cannot be captured in existing survey instruments do not enter the framework, regardless of their importance to poor populations
- How professional poverty-measurement discourse — conferences, journals, working papers, technical notes — performs a politics of scientific neutrality that insulates its practitioners from accountability to the people their indicators affect
