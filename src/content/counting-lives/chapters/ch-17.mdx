## The Ethics of Counting

Every act of measurement is a moral act. To count the poor is to decide what counts as poverty, whose experience counts as evidence, and what political consequences the count should carry. The history this book has traced is full of people who made these choices while claiming they were making no choices at all — who presented their thresholds, their surveys, their algorithms, and their indices as discoveries of social fact rather than constructions of political reality. This chapter proposes that the alternative is not to abandon measurement — we cannot govern without counting — but to govern measurement ethically: with transparency about its assumptions, accountability to its subjects, and humility about its limits.

This chapter's core argument is that ethical poverty measurement is a practice, not a methodology — it requires political conditions that no technical refinement can substitute for.

### In This Chapter

- How the philosophy of science's accounts of classification and quantification — Hacking's dynamic nominalism, Porter's trust in numbers, Desrosières's politics of large numbers — provide a theoretical framework for understanding poverty measurement not as a description of a pre-existing reality but as a social practice that participates in constructing the reality it claims to describe
- How the principle of transparency in measurement — making explicit the choices embedded in thresholds, weights, and dimensions — can be institutionalised in poverty statistics agencies, and how examples from the Office for National Statistics and the US Census Bureau illustrate both the possibility and the limits of transparency within existing institutional structures
- How the concept of a 'right to explanation' in algorithmic decision-making, developed in GDPR and consumer protection frameworks, could be extended to cover all poverty measurements that carry policy consequences — giving the measured a legal basis for challenging the measurements that govern them
- How the political conditions for ethical measurement — community data sovereignty, counter-expertise funding, meaningful consultation mechanisms, legal accountability — are not technical conditions but political ones that require redistribution of the same power that poverty measurement has historically been used to protect
