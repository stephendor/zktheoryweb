## The Hungry City

In the 1840s, Adolphe Quetelet proposed that the same normal distribution describing the scatter of cannonball impacts around a target also described the distribution of human bodies — and by extension, human social conditions. The poor were not victims of an unjust order; they were statistical deviations from the social mean. By the time Seebohm Rowntree conducted his pioneering survey of York in 1901, this statistical imagination had acquired a new, harder edge: a poverty line expressed in shillings per week, calculated from minimum dietary requirements, and designed to answer a question that was at once scientific and deeply political — what was the least a working family could survive on? The answer Rowntree gave was not a neutral description of nutritional need but an ideologically loaded threshold, shaped by assumptions about industrial labour, masculine bodies, and the limits of reformist ambition. The poverty line, when it arrived, arrived as a number. What it concealed was a politics.

This chapter's core argument is that poverty was first converted into a number not by neutral science but by a political act that statistics made invisible.

### In This Chapter

- How the Statistical Account of Scotland (1791–1799) established the template for systematic social survey across an entire polity, linking geographic, demographic, and economic data in a single administrative project
- How Quetelet's concept of the *homme moyen* transformed poverty from a moral condition into a statistical deviation, naturalising inequality through the geometry of the bell curve and making the poor legible as a calculable population
- How Rowntree designed his poverty threshold — the minimum dietary standard and its conversion into a weekly income floor — and what choices, embedded in that design, his methodology made invisible
- How the resulting poverty line served simultaneously as a scientific instrument and as a political limit below which Liberal reformers refused to let themselves see conditions fall
