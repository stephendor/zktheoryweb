## The Social Machine

Norbert Wiener published *Cybernetics* in 1948 and *The Human Use of Human Beings* in 1950. Between them, these texts proposed a new science of communication and control that applied equally to servo-mechanisms, brains, and social institutions. Poverty, in this framework, was not an injustice to be rectified by redistribution but a malfunction to be corrected by adjustment — a feedback error in the social system that better information, better measurement, and better management could eliminate. By the mid-1950s, this language had migrated from MIT's research laboratories into the War Department, the RAND Corporation, and — within a decade — into the Office of Economic Opportunity and the planning divisions of welfare agencies across the developed world. Robert McNamara's application of RAND's planning-programming-budgeting methodology to the Department of Defense, and subsequently its import into domestic social policy, marked the moment when the social sciences became engineering.

This chapter's core argument is that the technocratic turn in poverty measurement was not merely a change of method but a change of politics — one that neutralised poverty as a claim of justice by converting it into a systems-engineering problem.

### In This Chapter

- How Wiener's cybernetic framework — feedback, entropy, information, control — was applied to social policy by operations researchers who had cut their teeth on wartime logistics and whose methods carried the assumptions of that origin
- How McNamara's Planning, Programming, and Budgeting System became the template for cost-effectiveness analysis in social programmes, transplanting a military logic of measurable output into the architecture of welfare administration
- How the first large-scale social surveys — the Current Population Survey, the Survey of Consumer Finances — built the data infrastructure that modern poverty measurement depends on, and how that infrastructure was shaped by federal Cold War priorities
- How the administrative machinery of welfare — case files, eligibility databases, client tracking systems — became the first iteration of the surveillance infrastructure that later algorithmic systems would inherit, expand, and automate
