## The Mathematics of Fitness

When Francis Galton published *Hereditary Genius* in 1869, he was not inventing a new science so much as giving statistical form to a set of prejudices that Victorian society already held. Poverty was heritable; intelligence was heritable; moral character was heritable. What Galton added was measurement — a way of attaching numbers to these convictions and so converting ideology into science. His student and intellectual successor Karl Pearson, working in the Biometric Laboratory at University College London from the 1890s onward, refined these methods into a technical apparatus of extraordinary sophistication and political toxicity. The chi-square test, the correlation coefficient, the standard deviation: these are Pearson's tools, forged in eugenics, donated without apology to the social sciences, and still in daily use. The history of poverty measurement inherited a tainted toolkit — and that inheritance has never been fully reckoned with.

This chapter's core argument is that the statistical methods we use to measure poverty carry the fingerprints of the political programme that built them.

### In This Chapter

- How Galton's concept of regression to the mean encoded a naturalisation of social hierarchy into the mathematical fabric of statistics, making the persistence of inequality appear as a law of nature
- How Pearson's Biometric Laboratory established the institutional model for large-scale social data collection — creating practices of survey design, data cleaning, and statistical inference that poverty researchers reproduced without inheriting the laboratory's later self-criticism
- How women's bodies functioned in the eugenic data machinery as sources of reproductive data, and how this structural reduction of women to their fertility shaped the gendered assumptions embedded in later poverty surveys
- How Scottish Enlightenment philosophy — with its emphasis on the improvement of human faculties through education and environment — provided an intellectual context that both shaped and was distorted by Galton's hereditarian claims

### Connection Forward

Chapter 3 traces the disruption of the eugenic consensus by the catastrophe of the First World War and the political pressures that produced the Beveridge welfare settlement — but shows how the arithmetic of the normal working life carried forward assumptions that eugenics had made legible.
