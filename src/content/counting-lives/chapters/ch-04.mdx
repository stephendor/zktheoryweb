## The Ledger on the Mantelpiece

In Lambeth, over the years 1909 to 1913, Maud Pember Reeves and a team of Fabian Women's Group investigators spent months recording the week-by-week household accounts of working-class families living on roughly a pound a week. The result, *Round About a Pound a Week*, is one of the most revealing documents in the history of poverty research. It traces, in granular detail, what it actually meant to manage a household on an income at or below Rowntree's poverty line — the groceries purchased, the pennies allocated to rent, coal, clothing, and bread, the impossible calculations performed by women who were, in effect, managing continuous financial crises without the vocabulary of crisis. It is also a profound implicit critique of Rowntree's method: the nutritional minimums he had carefully calculated were simply unachievable, in practice, by women managing actual kitchens with actual families and demands on their time that his survey had not thought to count.

This chapter's core argument is that women's domestic accounting practices constituted a counter-archive of poverty measurement — one whose epistemological claims the official tradition has systematically refused to absorb.

### In This Chapter

- How the settlement house tradition generated a mode of poverty investigation grounded in proximity, sustained relationship, and participant observation — a methodology that foregrounded process and context rather than snapshot income thresholds
- How women investigators' use of domestic account books as primary data constituted a methodological departure from the male-dominated survey tradition — one that privileged the granular and the relational over the aggregate and the measurable
- How working-class women's own domestic accounting — the penny-by-penny management of a household at the edge of subsistence — represents a form of measurement knowledge produced outside the academy and systematically undervalued by it
- How the Beveridge settlement and its household notion of 'the family unit' absorbed and neutralised the feminist critique, incorporating women into the welfare arithmetic as dependants rather than as bearers of independent economic claims
