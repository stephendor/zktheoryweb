## The Cheapest Adequate Diet

In 1963, Mollie Orshansky, a statistician at the Social Security Administration, published a paper in the *Social Security Bulletin* proposing poverty thresholds derived from the USDA's Economy Food Plan — the cheapest diet the Department of Agriculture believed could sustain life, multiplied by three on the assumption that food accounted for roughly one third of a working family's budget. Orshansky did not design this measure to become the official poverty line of the United States. She designed it to produce a better count of poor children. But in 1965, in the heat of the War on Poverty, Lyndon Johnson's Council of Economic Advisers adopted it — choosing her lower-bound alternative over the higher thresholds she had also produced — and it has remained the basis of the official American poverty measure ever since. The line's durability is not a mark of its accuracy. It is a mark of its political convenience.

This chapter's core argument is that the Orshansky line's six-decade longevity is a political achievement, not a technical one.

### In This Chapter

- How RAND's culture of systems analysis and cost-effectiveness thinking shaped the analytical infrastructure of the War on Poverty, producing an evaluation paradigm that privileged measurable programme efficiency over distributional outcomes
- How Orshansky's own ambivalence about the measure she had devised — she understood its limitations and said so, repeatedly — was systematically set aside as the line hardened into official policy and the threshold became fixed
- How the political choice between Orshansky's lower and higher thresholds was determined not by technical superiority but by the size of the programme liability each figure implied — a decision that has constrained poverty measurement in the United States for sixty years
- How the racially marked character of American poverty was rendered invisible by a measure that was technically neutral with respect to race but structurally blind to the mechanisms producing racial wealth gaps
