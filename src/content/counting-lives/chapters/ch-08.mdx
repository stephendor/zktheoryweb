## The Spreadsheet of Suffering

Peter Singer's 1972 essay *Famine, Affluence, and Morality* proposed a simple and devastating argument: if you can prevent something very bad from happening without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, you are obliged to do so. By the 2010s, this argument had been operationalised by William MacAskill, Toby Ord, and the Effective Altruism movement into a quantitative research programme — one that asked not merely whether you should give but where you should give, at what cost per disability-adjusted life year averted, and how you could know. The attempt to answer these questions rigorously, with randomised control trials and systematic cost-effectiveness reviews published by GiveWell and the Open Philanthropy Project, produced a measurement apparatus of genuine sophistication. It also produced a politics — not despite its claimed neutrality but precisely because of it.

This chapter argues that EA's poverty measurement framework is a form of depoliticised politics: a systematic encoding of conservative assumptions about the bounds of the possible in the grammar of scientific neutrality.

### In This Chapter

- How Singer's utilitarian argument and its translation by MacAskill into institutional form created a new genre of poverty measurement centred on the individual act of charitable donation rather than the structural conditions producing the poverty that donation partially and temporarily addresses
- How J-PAL's adoption of the RCT as the gold standard of poverty-intervention evaluation created a methodological hierarchy that systematically disfavoured the structural, the political, and the long-term — interventions that are difficult to randomise do not enter the evidence base
- How the DALY encodes specific and contestable assumptions about the relative value of different kinds of human experience, and how these assumptions shape which interventions appear cost-effective and which do not, in ways that reflect the preferences of those who designed the metric
- How the movement's longtermist wing represents the endpoint of a historical trajectory in which poverty measurement abstracts away from actually existing poor people — a trajectory that this book has been tracing since Quetelet's average man
