## The Automated Caseworker

In 2009, Indiana contracted with IBM to automate its public assistance eligibility system. Over the following decades, Virginia Eubanks documented what happened to the people on the receiving end of the resulting decisions: families losing food stamps because of data-entry errors they had no mechanism to appeal; homeless youth screened out of coordinated entry systems by algorithms they could not see or challenge; families flagged for child welfare investigation by prediction models trained on correlates of poverty rather than evidence of abuse. Eubanks called this the 'digital poorhouse' — an allusion to the institutions of the previous century whose logic, she argued, the new algorithmic systems had not replaced but replicated in code. This chapter opens Part III and the fifth transition by taking her argument as its starting point: the algorithmisation of welfare is not a technical modernisation of poverty administration but a political continuity with it.

This chapter's core argument is that the transition to algorithmic welfare governance represents the automation of existing exclusions, not their transcendence.

### In This Chapter

- How the redlining practices of the Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) and the Federal Housing Administration established a spatial data infrastructure for racial exclusion that twentieth-century credit scoring and twenty-first-century algorithmic welfare systems inherited without interruption
- How predictive analytics deployed in child welfare, housing allocation, and benefits administration use postcode, neighbourhood, and census-tract data as proxies for race, reproducing racial disparities in service delivery while maintaining nominal colour-blindness
- How the shift from entitlement-based to risk-based welfare administration — from 'do you meet the threshold?' to 'what is your risk score?' — transformed the epistemological foundations of poverty governance and foreclosed new categories of legal challenge
- How the opacity mechanisms protecting proprietary welfare algorithms — trade secrecy, confidentiality agreements, the complexity shield — constitute a specific threat to the rights of welfare claimants, and what alternative accountability architectures advocates have proposed
