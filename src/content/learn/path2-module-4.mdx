## Same Income, Different Lives

Consider two households, each reporting annual income of £24,000. The first is a lone adult renting a one-bedroom flat in Leeds. The second is a couple with two school-age children renting a three-bedroom house in the same city. Both households appear identically in any analysis that uses cash income without adjustment. Both occupy the same position on the income distribution. Both are equally far from the 60%-of-median poverty line.

Neither observation is useful for understanding either household's material circumstances. The couple's £24,000 must cover rent for a larger property, food for four, clothing for growing children, school transport, and childcare. The lone adult's £24,000 covers none of these additional costs. To compare their living standards, income must be converted to a common denominator that reflects how purchasing power is distributed across different-sized households. This conversion is equivalisation, and the formula used to perform it embeds a complete — if largely unstated — theory of domestic economics.

## The Equivalisation Formula

The basic structure of an equivalisation scale is simple. Households are assigned an **equivalence factor** $S$ based on their composition, and the household's actual income $y$ is divided by this factor to produce equivalised income $y_e$:

$$
y_e = \frac{y}{S(n_a, n_c)}
$$

where $n_a$ is the number of adults and $n_c$ is the number of children. Different scales assign different values to the function $S$.

The **original OECD scale**, introduced in the 1980s and used extensively in cross-national comparison, assigns:

$$
S = 1.0 + 0.7(n_a - 1) + 0.5 \cdot n_c
$$

A couple with no children has $S = 1.7$; a couple with two children has $S = 2.7$. This means the couple with children needs 2.7 times the income of a lone adult to achieve the same living standard.

The **Modified OECD scale** — adopted by Eurostat and by the UK's Households Below Average Income series in 2005 — revises downward the weights for both additional adults and children:

$$
S = 1.0 + 0.5(n_a - 1) + 0.3 \cdot n_c
$$

Under this scale the same couple with two children has $S = 2.1$, not 2.7. The same £24,000 household income produces a higher equivalised figure, placing the family further from the poverty threshold. The change in scale moved hundreds of thousands of children out of official poverty in the UK without altering a single household's actual income — purely by revising the assumed weight of a child from 0.5 to 0.3.

## The McClements Scale and Political History

Before the Modified OECD scale, the UK used the **McClements scale**, developed by D.J. McClements for the DHSS in 1977 from UK Family Expenditure Survey data. McClements assigned weights separately for children by age, on the grounds that older children consume more than younger ones:

| Household member | McClements weight |
|---|---|
| First adult | 1.00 |
| Spouse | 0.57 |
| Child aged 0–1 | 0.09 |
| Child aged 5–7 | 0.21 |
| Child aged 13–15 | 0.36 |

The granularity appeared precise and empirically grounded. In practice, the weights reflected 1970s patterns of household expenditure that aged rapidly: they were based on what households *did* spend, not what they *needed* to spend, and they were derived from a period before the expansion of formal childcare, before the internet, and before the sharp rise in children's clothing and activity costs.

The McClements scale's replacement with the Modified OECD scale in 2005 was a decision made by statisticians in consultation with the DWP, not a parliamentary vote. Yet it reclassified millions of households. The shift illustrates the central point of this module: equivalisation scales are not technical corrections to the data. They are policy decisions that determine who is counted as poor.

## Economies of Scale and Hidden Assumptions

The sub-linear structure of all equivalisation scales — where the equivalence factor grows less than proportionally with household size — reflects the assumption of **economies of scale** in household consumption. Larger households share fixed costs: accommodation, heating, appliances, and shared food preparation are assumed to cost less per person in a larger group than in a smaller one.

This assumption is empirically contested. Families with young children face significant fixed costs — nappies, pushchairs, car seats, childcare — that do not exhibit economies of scale. Families in poverty have less capacity to realise economies of scale because they lack the capital to buy in bulk or invest in energy-efficient equipment. The sub-linear assumption systematically flatters the equivalised incomes of larger households, most of whom are families, at the expense of measuring their true material circumstances.

## Using the Interactive: Equivalisation Comparator

The **Equivalisation Comparator** below applies all three scales to the same stylised UK income distribution of 200 households — spanning single adults to couples with two children — and displays the resulting poverty rates side by side. No household's income changes; only the formula changes.

The **summary cards** show the poverty rate under each scale at a glance. At the default 60%-of-median threshold, the Original OECD scale — which assigns a higher weight to children (0.5 vs 0.3) — returns a higher poverty rate than the Modified OECD scale. This is the arithmetic of the 2005 reform: lowering the child weight raised equivalised income for families, moving them above the threshold.

The **poverty rate bar chart** makes the three-way comparison explicit. The bars are sized to the same scale, so the visual gap represents the policy-relevant divergence.

The **income distribution chart** shows the kernel density of equivalised income under each scale. Notice that the distributions overlap substantially — the scales do not fundamentally rearrange the income order — but the poverty threshold (dashed vertical line) falls at a different point on each distribution because the median equivalised income is scale-dependent.

The **threshold slider** adjusts the poverty line from 40% to 75% of median. At lower thresholds (absolute hardship), the three scales converge: households at the very bottom are poor under any formula. At higher thresholds, the divergence widens because the scales differ most in how they treat middle-income families with children.

The key observation: move the slider and watch all three rates change in unison. None of the formulas is measuring a fixed, observer-independent quantity. Each is measuring poverty as defined by its assumptions about what different households need.
