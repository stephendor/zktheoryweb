## The Shape of Injustice

The welfare state is not a neutral distribution mechanism. It is a structured system that embeds decisions — about who qualifies for what benefit, at what income threshold, subject to which conditionality — into algorithms, databases, and assessment protocols. When these structures produce systematically worse outcomes for particular populations — by protected characteristic, by geography, by employment history — that disparity has a geometrical form. Certain groups of people are located in regions of welfare space that are topologically separated from the pathways to support; their trajectories form closed loops within zones of persistent deprivation; the decision boundaries of algorithmic assessment tools enclose them in ways that are structurally resistant to individual improvement. This is the argument of **Counting Lives**, Chapters 14–16, translated into the language of TDA: discrimination and deprivation are partly topological phenomena, and understanding their structure requires tools that can describe topology.

This module develops the operational TDA techniques for structural bias detection and algorithmic fairness auditing. It connects the technical methods of Modules 3–9 to the political and ethical concerns of the research programme, and it names both the power and the limits of TDA as a fairness tool.

## Mapper as a Structural Bias Detector

The Mapper graph (Module 6) is the most directly interpretable TDA tool for detecting structural separation of protected groups. The procedure:

1. Construct a Mapper graph from welfare-trajectory data using a filter function that does not include the protected characteristic (e.g., benefit-receipt history, income trajectory — not ethnicity or disability status).
2. Colour each node of the graph by the proportion of protected-group members it contains.
3. Examine whether protected-group nodes cluster at the periphery of the graph (sparse nodes with few edges) or are distributed throughout the interior (well-connected nodes).

Peripheral clustering in the Mapper graph encodes a precise topological claim: protected-group members are located in welfare-state positions that are structurally isolated from the pathways available to the majority population. Structural isolation is distinguishable from simple clustering (having different mean welfare outcomes) because it captures connectivity: an isolated node is not merely lower on some welfare scale, it has fewer routes to adjacent welfare states, fewer cross-group connections, and fewer recovery pathways.

Run a Mapper parameter sensitivity analysis (Module 6, Mapper Parameter Lab) to confirm that the peripheral clustering is robust: it should persist across a range of resolution (15–35) and gain (20–45%) settings. A peripheral cluster that appears only at one specific parameter combination is not a robust finding.

## Persistent Homology of Decision Boundaries

Algorithmic welfare systems — Universal Credit assessments, predictive child-welfare scoring, housing benefit calculations — produce decision boundaries: the locus in input space where the algorithm transitions from one decision (grant, deny, refer) to another. The topology of these boundaries carries fairness information that summary statistics cannot capture.

For a trained scoring model $f: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ (where $f(x) > 0.5$ triggers a benefit denial), the decision boundary is the level set $f^{-1}(0.5)$. To analyse its topology:

1. Sample data points $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ with $|f(x_i) - 0.5| < \epsilon$ for small $\epsilon$ (the near-boundary population).
2. Restrict to the sub-population defined by a protected group (e.g., households with a disability indicator).
3. Compute the VR persistence diagram of this restricted near-boundary sample.
4. Compute the same diagram for the majority population.
5. Compare diagrams via Wasserstein distance (Module 9). A large Wasserstein distance indicates structurally different boundary geometries for the two groups.

A persistent H₁ feature in the protected-group boundary diagram — but not in the majority diagram — indicates a loop in the decision boundary near the protected group: a topological trap where the boundary encloses a region from which households cannot escape through incremental improvements in any single welfare variable. This is the topological signature of a "ceiling" effect in algorithmic scoring: not a gradient but a structural enclosure.

## Connecting to Counting Lives: Chapters 14–16

**Chapter 14** of *Counting Lives* documents the structural geography of poverty in post-devolution Scotland, arguing that deprivation is not randomly distributed but forms topological patterns — islands of concentrated poverty separated from recovery pathways by structural barriers. The persistent H₀ features in welfare data from Scottish local authorities provide the formal evidence: isolated components in the Mapper graph of welfare trajectories correspond directly to the historically persistent deprivation areas identified in Chapter 14.

**Chapter 15** analyses how algorithmic benefit assessment — specifically the Universal Credit migration from legacy benefits in the UK — restructured the topology of welfare access. Pre-migration, the benefit system had smoother topological structure: continuous pathways between income levels and benefit states. Post-migration, the Mapper graph of claimant trajectories shows a bifurcated structure: a well-connected core population transitioning smoothly through the system and a peripheral population locked into a cyclical pattern of conditionality compliance, sanctioning, and crisis payments. The persistent H₁ feature in the post-migration data — the poverty loop — is the mathematical object at the centre of the chapter's argument.

**Chapter 16** addresses the ethics and politics of topological knowledge: knowing the shape of injustice does not automatically produce the will to change it. The chapter draws on the CARE principles developed by Kukutai and colleagues for Indigenous data governance — Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics — and asks whether TDA-based poverty analysis can satisfy these principles or whether it risks producing another layer of expert-produced knowledge that communities cannot govern or act on.

## When Topology Is Not Enough

TDA provides a language and a set of tools for describing structural injustice with precision. It does not provide the political, legal, or institutional mechanisms to intervene. A Mapper graph showing structural separation of protected groups from benefit pathways is a powerful exhibit in a policy argument; it is not, by itself, a policy instrument.

The limits of TDA as a fairness tool are:

**Description without prescription.** Topological analysis identifies what structure exists but does not specify what change would dissolve it. A persistent loop in benefit-denial trajectories can be identified and measured; which policy lever would remove it — sanctioning reform, assessment methodology change, conditionality removal — is a political question.

**Scale without causality.** TDA characterises the global geometry of outcomes but does not identify the causal mechanisms producing it. Two systems with superficially different rules can produce topologically identical separation; two systems with identical rules can produce topologically different separations depending on implementation context.

**Analysis without participation.** The most sophisticated topological fairness audit is still an analysis *of* communities rather than *by* them. The CARE principles — particularly Authority to Control and Collective Benefit — require governance structures that TDA does not provide and that researchers using TDA must build through separate, non-mathematical means.
