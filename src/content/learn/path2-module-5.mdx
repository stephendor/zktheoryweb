## The Geometry of the Taper

In 2023, a warehouse operative in Swindon earning £1,100 per month received a Universal Credit award calculated after applying a 55 per cent taper to her earnings above the work allowance. For every additional pound she earned, she kept 45 pence and lost 55 pence in benefit. Her effective marginal tax rate, incorporating National Insurance and income tax, was above 70 per cent across a wide range of her potential earnings. She was working the maximum hours available to her and found that an additional shift generated almost no increase in her net weekly income.

The taper rate of 55 per cent — reduced from 63 per cent in 2021 — was the product of a constrained optimisation exercise. The Treasury sought to minimise the cost of the Universal Credit caseload subject to the constraint that work always paid more than non-employment. That optimisation problem had a solution. What it did not have was a mechanism for weighting the operative's effective marginal tax rate against the fiscal savings it generated.

## Welfare as an Optimisation Problem

Modern welfare economics models the relationship between poverty policy and labour supply as a constrained optimisation problem. The state chooses a benefit schedule $B(y)$ — the amount of benefit paid as a function of earned income $y$ — to maximise a social welfare function subject to a budget constraint and incentive compatibility conditions.

The standard formulation is:

$$
\max_{B(\cdot)} \int_0^{\infty} v\bigl(c(y), l(y)\bigr) \, f(y) \, dy \quad \text{subject to} \quad \int_0^{\infty} B(y) \, f(y) \, dy \leq E
$$

where $v(c, l)$ is the utility function over consumption $c$ and leisure $l$, $f(y)$ is the distribution of earned income across the population, and $E$ is the total expenditure envelope. The taper rate $\tau$ — the rate at which $B$ is withdrawn as $y$ increases — is the key design parameter: a higher $\tau$ reduces fiscal cost but increases the effective marginal tax rate faced by claimants in the phaseout range.

The intellectual lineage of this approach runs from Mirrlees's optimal income tax theory in 1971 through the Diamond-Saez synthesis of the 2000s to the Treasury modelling that underpins each benefit reform. It is a sophisticated framework, and within its own terms it is mathematically coherent. Its limitations lie not in the algebra but in the objective function: the social welfare function $v$ is chosen by the modeller, and its precise specification determines whose interests are maximised.

In practice, Treasury optimisation models for Universal Credit have been calibrated principally against fiscal cost and labour supply participation, with claimant welfare measured indirectly through consumption and participation proxies. The direct welfare cost of operating under a high taper rate — stress, time poverty, inability to plan — does not appear in the objective function because it cannot readily be monetised and entered into the integral.

## Conditionality as Constraint Manipulation

Benefit conditionality — sanctions, work-search requirements, mandatory appointments — is the operational complement to the optimisation model. If the taper rate represents the incentive structure of the benefit schedule, conditionality represents an additional constraint: the threat of sanction shifts the effective value of non-participation downward, tightening the binding constraint on labour supply.

The logic is explicitly drawn from principal-agent theory. The state (principal) cannot perfectly observe the claimant's (agent's) effort. Conditionality is a monitoring and enforcement mechanism designed to reduce shirking under information asymmetry. The DWP's sanctions regime has been explicitly modelled on this framework in academic reviews commissioned by the department.

The model's fragility appears when the asymmetric information assumption is wrong. If non-participation is driven by disability, caring responsibilities, labour market conditions, or mental illness rather than observable effort-shirking, then conditionality punishes structural constraint as though it were choice. The optimisation calculus continues to generate formal solutions, but those solutions systematically misidentify where the welfare-maximising intervention lies.

## Using the Interactive: Benefit Taper Calculator

The **Benefit Taper Calculator** below displays the Universal Credit budget constraint — net income as a function of gross earnings. Two controls shape the curve.

**Household type** switches the work allowance between £673/month (no housing element) and £404/month (with housing element). Watch the curve shift left: a lower work allowance means the taper zone starts earlier, and a claimant at any given earnings level keeps less. This is the first political choice embedded in the formula: what counts as a "typical" household.

**Show pre-2021 (63% taper)** overlays the budget constraint that applied before the October 2021 reform. The gap between the two lines is the value of the rate cut — at any earnings level in the taper zone, claimants today keep 8p more per pound than they did under the old rate. Hover along the curve to read the exact net income and effective marginal rate at any point.

What the interactive does not yet show is a freely adjustable taper rate and a fiscal cost readout — features that would make the "optimal rate is a political choice" argument fully concrete. Those enhancements are planned for Phase 6.
