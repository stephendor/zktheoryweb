## The Data That Came Back as Harm

In the early 1990s, the Havasupai Tribe of the Grand Canyon provided blood samples to researchers at Arizona State University for a study on the genetic causes of type 2 diabetes, which had reached epidemic levels in their community. The samples were given in the context of a specific, consented research purpose: to help explain and address a health crisis the community was experiencing. What happened next was not what the community had agreed to. Over the following decade, the same samples were used in studies on schizophrenia, migration patterns, and population genetics — research that the Havasupai had never been asked about and had no reason to sanction. Some of the findings were used to argue that the tribe's ancestors had migrated from Asia, contradicting creation stories central to Havasupai cultural identity and their legal claims to ancestral land.

The tribe eventually sued the university, winning a settlement and the return of physical samples in 2010. But the research had been published, the data had circulated, and the harm — reputational, cultural, and legal — could not be fully recalled. The case is not unusual. It illustrates a structural problem with standard research ethics frameworks when applied to Indigenous communities: individual informed consent is insufficient protection when the data describes collective cultural, genetic, and historical heritage.

## The Sovereignty Argument

The scholars Tahu Kukutai and John Taylor, in their foundational collection *Indigenous Data Sovereignty: Toward an Agenda*, argue that Indigenous peoples have the right to govern the collection, ownership, and application of data about their communities, their territories, and their ways of life. This claim is not a demand for opacity. It is an assertion of sovereignty — the same principle that gives nation-states the right to control what foreign governments can retrieve from their territory or their citizens.

Indigenous data sovereignty movements emphasise two points that are often missed in mainstream research ethics discussions. First, the unit of governance should be the collective, not the individual; individual consent frameworks cannot protect communal rights. Second, data is not separable from the knowledge systems and social relationships from which it is derived. Health data about an Indigenous community reflects traditional ecological knowledge, community social structures, and historical trauma caused by colonial policies. Treating it as raw material available for any researcher's use severs that connection.

The New Zealand-based Te Mana Raraunga network, established in 2015, has been one of the most influential organisations developing practical frameworks for Maori data sovereignty. Similar movements have emerged in Australia, Canada, and across Pacific Island nations, often coordinating internationally to develop shared principles and advocacy strategies at bodies such as the United Nations Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues.

## The CARE Principles

In response to the limitations of the dominant FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) framework for open data, researchers working with Indigenous communities developed the CARE principles as an explicit counterpoint. FAIR was designed to maximise the technical usability of data for the research community. CARE asks a prior question: usable by whom, for whose benefit?

The four CARE principles are: **Collective Benefit** — data governance should enable Indigenous peoples and their communities to derive benefit from data about them, not merely make data available to outside researchers; **Authority to Control** — Indigenous nations have the right to govern data about their people, lands, and resources, including the right to say no; **Responsibility** — those who work with Indigenous data have a responsibility to build community capacity and nurture positive relationships; **Ethics** — the rights and wellbeing of Indigenous peoples should be the primary ethical standard, not abstract research norms that do not account for historical power imbalances.

CARE does not replace FAIR; it reframes the question. Open science principles can still apply within a CARE framework, but openness cannot be the default — it must be a Collective benefit determination made with, rather than about, the community.

## What an Interactive on Data Governance Would Show

A future interactive tool for this module would allow you to simulate a data governance design process for a hypothetical community research project — working through the CARE principles one by one and observing how different governance decisions change who benefits from the research and what risks the community carries. The tension between individual consent frameworks and collective governance rights would be made navigable through concrete scenario choices. The interactive does not yet exist, but the exercise to run in your own context is to review the data governance section of any community research proposal you have encountered and ask: at which point does the community have a binding veto over the research design?
