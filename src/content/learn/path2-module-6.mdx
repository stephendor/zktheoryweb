## A Number Arrives Before the Person

In 2021, the Welsh Government commissioned a predictive analytics tool to identify children at risk of adverse outcomes in early childhood. The system assigned each newborn a score derived from administrative data held by health, social services, and the benefits system. Before a social worker had met a family, before a health visitor had completed an assessment, a number had been generated. That number would shape how services were allocated, how intensively families were visited, and — in some configurations — whether further assessment was triggered automatically.

The Welsh system was subsequently paused after concerns about data governance. But the logic it embodied is now pervasive in welfare administration across the United Kingdom. Local authorities use algorithmic scores to prioritise housing waiting lists. DWP uses behavioural predictors to identify claimants at risk of fraud. Predictive policing tools score individuals' arrest likelihood before any offence has occurred. A score precedes, and increasingly determines, the human encounter.

## Logistic Regression and the Poverty Score

The most common technical foundation for welfare prediction models is **logistic regression**: a generalised linear model that estimates the probability of a binary outcome (benefit fraud, child maltreatment, long-term unemployment) as a function of a set of input features. Given a feature vector $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_p)$, the model estimates:

$$
\hat{p} = \sigma(\boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \mathbf{x} + \beta_0) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \mathbf{x} + \beta_0)}}
$$

where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the **sigmoid function**, $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is the vector of learned coefficients, and $\beta_0$ is the intercept. Each coefficient $\beta_j$ represents the change in log-odds of the outcome per unit increase in $x_j$, holding all other features constant.

The log-odds formulation makes the model's underlying structure explicit:

$$
\log\!\frac{\hat{p}}{1 - \hat{p}} = \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \mathbf{x} + \beta_0
$$

This is a linear model of the log-odds, which means that the contribution of each feature to the final score is additive and proportional. A claimant's predicted poverty risk is, literally, a weighted sum of their administrative characteristics — and the weights $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ are learned from historical data in which the outcomes were shaped by the same institutional choices that are now being modelled as natural facts.

## The Training Data Problem

Logistic regression, and all supervised learning methods, learn from data in which both the features $\mathbf{x}$ and the outcomes $y$ have been observed. In welfare applications, the outcome variable is typically an administrative classification: previously identified fraud, previous social care intervention, previous long-term unemployment. These classifications are not direct observations of underlying facts — they are the products of prior decisions by investigators, case workers, and adjudicators who were themselves subject to bias, resource constraints, and institutional priorities.

A model trained on previous fraud classifications learns the pattern of who was previously investigated and found fraudulent. It does not learn the pattern of who committed fraud. If investigators disproportionately audited claimants in certain postcodes, the model will score claimants in those postcodes more highly — not because they are more likely to be fraudulent, but because they are more likely to have been investigated. The model encodes the investigatory bias as though it were a property of the claimants.

This feedback loop — sometimes called **label bias** or **measurement bias** — is particularly acute in welfare contexts because the populations most intensively surveilled by welfare administration are precisely the marginalised groups whose circumstances are most poorly represented in the training data.

## The Decision Threshold as Policy

Converting a predicted probability $\hat{p}$ to an actionable classification requires a decision threshold $\tau$: the claimant is flagged if $\hat{p} \geq \tau$. The choice of $\tau$ determines the false positive rate (proportion of low-risk claimants wrongly flagged) and the false negative rate (proportion of high-risk claimants wrongly cleared). These two error rates trade off against each other as $\tau$ varies.

In welfare fraud detection, a low $\tau$ minimises missed cases but flags large numbers of legitimate claimants for intrusive investigation. A high $\tau$ reduces intrusive false positives but misses more genuine cases. The welfare consequences of each error type are asymmetric: false positives impose investigation costs on innocent claimants; false negatives represent financial loss to the DWP. Routinely, $\tau$ is set to minimise the latter at the expense of the former — a policy choice embedded in a technical parameter, largely invisible to public scrutiny.

## Using the Interactive: Decision Threshold Explorer

The **Decision Threshold Explorer** below presents a simplified logistic regression model trained on a synthetic welfare dataset of 300 claimants. Each claimant has been assigned a predicted probability score (0–1) by the model and a true label (genuinely high-risk or low-risk). Two demographic groups are included: Group A (majority, 200 claimants, 20% true base rate) and Group B (minority, 100 claimants, 40% true base rate with noisier model calibration due to label bias).

The **metrics summary** shows four key statistics. The True Positive Rate (TPR) — the proportion of genuinely high-risk claimants correctly flagged — corresponds to the proportion of vulnerable claimants who receive attention. The False Positive Rate (FPR) — the proportion of genuinely low-risk claimants wrongly flagged — represents the burden of intrusive investigation imposed on innocent claimants. As you lower τ, TPR rises but FPR rises with it. As you raise τ, TPR falls and fewer high-risk cases are caught.

The **ROC curve** plots every possible (FPR, TPR) pair across all thresholds. The orange dot marks the current τ. A perfect model would press the curve into the top-left corner; a random model produces the diagonal reference line. The Area Under the Curve (AUC) summarises overall model discrimination, independent of threshold choice.

The **Group A vs Group B comparison** is the politically consequential panel. Because the model is less well-calibrated for Group B — it assigns some genuinely low-risk Group B claimants elevated scores due to historical investigatory patterns — Group B's FPR is systematically higher than Group A's at the same threshold τ. Lowering τ to improve TPR for Group B simultaneously increases FPR — imposing disproportionate false-positive burdens on the minority group. This is the mechanism that makes threshold selection a policy act with distributional consequences that a single aggregate accuracy figure cannot reveal.

The threshold of τ = 0.50 shown by default is arbitrary. The DWP systems described in Chapters 10–12 do not publish their threshold values; this interactive makes visible what that omission conceals.
