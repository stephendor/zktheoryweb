## Are Yesterday's Poor Different From Today's?

Standard poverty modelling asks: given that a household is poor today, what is the probability it will be poor next year? The answer is estimated from data as a transition probability, and the whole analysis rests on the Markov assumption: what matters is where you are now, not how long you have been there or what path brought you. But experienced poverty researchers are deeply suspicious of this assumption. A household that has been in poverty for five years has different social capital, different physical and mental health, different relationships with institutions, than one newly fallen into poverty this year — even if their observed income and consumption figures are identical today. The Markov assumption, by collapsing all this history into current-state labels, potentially misrepresents the underlying social dynamics. The Markov memory ladder is a topological method for testing whether this suspicion is warranted, without assuming any specific functional form for the memory effect.

## The Markov Assumption and Its Limitations

A **first-order Markov chain** on a state space $S$ is a stochastic process $\{X_t\}_{t \geq 0}$ satisfying:

$$
P(X_{t+1} = s \mid X_t, X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_0) = P(X_{t+1} = s \mid X_t) \quad \forall s \in S, t \geq 0.
$$

The distribution of tomorrow's state depends only on today's. For poverty modelling with categorical states (poor/near-poor/non-poor), this reduces to a $3 \times 3$ transition matrix estimated from observed year-to-year transitions. A higher-order Markov model extends the conditioning window: $(X_{t+1} \mid X_t, X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_{t-m+1})$ for memory order $m$.

Testing whether $m > 1$ matters is typically done with likelihood-ratio tests comparing transition matrices of different orders. These tests are parametric: they assume the extra history enters transition probabilities in a specific linear or log-linear form. If the true memory structure is non-linear — if, for example, the effect of prolonged poverty interacts with household composition in a complex way — parametric tests may fail to detect it.

## The Ladder Construction

The **Markov memory ladder** addresses this by using topology instead of parametric assumptions. The construction proceeds in three stages:

**Stage 1 — Define order-$m$ embeddings.** For each household $i$ observed at times $t, t+1, \ldots, t+m-1$, form the concatenated vector:

$$
\mathbf{x}_i^{(m)} = (x_{i,t}, x_{i,t+1}, \ldots, x_{i,t+m-1}) \in \mathbb{R}^{mk},
$$

where $k$ is the number of variables measured per period. This embeds the household's $m$-period history as a single point in $mk$-dimensional space. The collection of all such vectors forms an order-$m$ **history point cloud**.

**Stage 2 — Compute persistence diagrams.** Apply the Vietoris–Rips filtration to each history point cloud $\mathcal{X}^{(m)}$ for $m = 1, 2, \ldots, M$, obtaining a sequence of persistence diagrams $\text{Dgm}^{(m)}$.

**Stage 3 — Climb the ladder.** Compute the Wasserstein distance between consecutive diagrams:

$$
d_m = W_2\!\left(\text{Dgm}^{(m)},\, \text{Dgm}^{(m+1)}\right), \quad m = 1, 2, \ldots, M-1.
$$

The sequence $d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_{M-1}$ is the **memory ladder**. If the sequence decays to zero — each step adds less and less new topological structure — the process is consistent with Markov dynamics: history beyond the current state adds no structural information. If the sequence remains elevated or exhibits a non-monotone pattern — new topological features continue to appear as the history window expands — the data carries genuine memory that violates the Markov assumption.

## Reading the Ladder

Three archetypal ladder patterns have substantively different interpretations:

A **steeply decaying ladder** ($d_1 \gg d_2 \approx d_3 \approx \cdots \approx 0$) indicates that one period of history beyond the current state captures all structural memory. First-order Markov dynamics held, or at most a second-order model is sufficient.

A **slowly decaying ladder** ($d_1 > d_2 > \cdots > 0$, all non-negligible) indicates long-range memory: households accumulating disadvantage across many periods remain topologically distinct from those with shorter poverty spells, at all history lengths tested. Standard Markov models are fundamentally inappropriate for this population.

A **non-monotone ladder** ($d_m$ rises at some orders and falls at others) indicates complex temporal structure — perhaps seasonal cycles in poverty incidence, institutional intervention points that generate new topological features at specific lag distances, or heterogeneous subpopulations with different memory structures that become visible at different temporal scales.

## From the Ladder to Policy

The practical import of a non-trivial ladder is methodological and substantive. Methodologically, it invalidates transition-matrix based poverty trap definitions that implicitly assume Markov dynamics. Substantively, it provides evidence that poverty has a *path-dependent* structure: where you end up depends not just on where you are but on how long you have been there and what route you took. This finding has direct implications for policy design: interventions targeted at households currently at the poverty threshold will be less effective than the Markov model predicts if prolonged poverty has permanently altered a household's structural position in the data manifold.

## How This Module Connects

The Markov memory ladder is first introduced theoretically in **Chapter 10** of *Counting Lives*, which traces the intellectual history of poverty-trap models from Lewis (1954) to Dasgupta–Ray (1986) to contemporary panel-data approaches, and argues that all of them implicitly assume a topology of poverty dynamics that the data may not support. **Chapter 11** presents early empirical results using the ladder on UK panel data, identifying memory depths of $m = 3$ to $m = 5$ in different subpopulations. The full formal development appears in **Paper 1** of the research portfolio, which treats the **markov-memory-ladder** method as the primary technical contribution.
