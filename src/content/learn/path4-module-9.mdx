One outlier point that can only be matched to a diagonal projection at distance 0.9 contributes 0.9 to the bottleneck distance regardless of how well all other points are matched. ## Beyond the Diagram: The Vectorisation Problem

A persistence diagram is a mathematically precise object — a multi-set of points in the extended plane — but it is not a vector. This creates a fundamental obstacle for statistical analysis. Standard statistical tools (regression, clustering, hypothesis testing with parametric tests, neural networks, random forests) operate on fixed-dimensional vector spaces. A collection of $N$ persistence diagrams, each a multi-set of varying cardinality, cannot be passed to a scikit-learn pipeline without transformation. The problem is not merely computational; it reflects a genuine mathematical structure mismatch.

Three approaches have been developed to close this gap. First, **diagram distances** (bottleneck and Wasserstein) allow non-parametric comparison of diagrams directly, supporting the permutation tests of Module 5 and the distance-based clustering of this module. Second, **persistence landscapes** convert each diagram to a function over the real line, which can then be discretised to a fixed-length vector. Third, **persistence images** project the diagram onto a fixed 2D grid via a Gaussian kernel, producing a compact array directly usable in image-processing pipelines. Each approach involves a stability trade-off: how much information from the original diagram is preserved, and how sensitive is the vectorisation to small perturbations in the point cloud?

## Diagram Distances

**Bottleneck distance** between diagrams $X$ and $Y$ is the $L^\infty$ norm over optimal matchings:

$$d_B(X, Y) = \inf_\gamma \sup_{x \in \text{Dgm}(X)} \| x - \gamma(x) \|_\infty$$

where the infimum is over all bijections $\gamma$ between $\text{Dgm}(X) \cup \Delta$ and $\text{Dgm}(Y) \cup \Delta$ (extending both diagrams with the diagonal $\Delta$ to handle cardinality mismatches). The bottleneck distance is determined by the single worst-matched point pair — the most displaceable feature in the optimal assignment. It is the most conservative metric and is directly bounded by the stability theorem.

**Wasserstein-$p$ distance** generalises this to the $L^p$ norm:

$$W_p(X, Y) = \left( \inf_\gamma \sum_{x \in \text{Dgm}(X)} \| x - \gamma(x) \|^p \right)^{1/p}$$

With $p = 1$, the Wasserstein-1 distance sums absolute displacements and is robust to individual outliers — a few drastically mismatched noise features inflate it less than they inflate bottleneck. With $p = 2$, Wasserstein-2 emphasises large displacements quadratically. For statistical testing (Module 5), Wasserstein-1 is preferred; for applications where the strongest features should dominate the comparison, bottleneck or Wasserstein-$\infty$ is appropriate.

## Persistence Landscapes

A **persistence landscape** converts a persistence diagram into a sequence of functions $\lambda_k: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by:

$$\lambda_k(t) = \sup\left\{ m \geq 0 : \exists (b,d) \in \text{Dgm},\; b+m \leq t \leq d-m \right\}$$

where the supremum is over the $k$-th largest value. Each birth–death pair $(b, d)$ contributes a tent function centred at $(b+d)/2$ with height $(d-b)/2$. The first landscape function $\lambda_1$ is the envelope of the tallest tent at each parameter position; $\lambda_2$ is the second-tallest; and so on.

Persistence landscapes have two critical properties. **Stability:** a perturbation of $\epsilon$ in bottleneck distance between diagrams produces a perturbation of at most $\epsilon$ in the $L^\infty$ norm of the landscape functions. **Linearisation:** landscapes live in a Banach space, making the Fréchet mean (pointwise average) well-defined and easily computable. This means you can legally average persistence diagrams by averaging their landscape functions — a cornerstone of statistical inference.

## Persistence Images

**Persistence images** provide an alternative fixed-dimensional vectorisation. The idea: convert each diagram to a weighted sum of Gaussian kernels placed at each birth–death point, then evaluate on a fixed grid. The weight function $f(b, d) = (d - b)$ (persistence) ensures that long-lived features contribute more mass to the image than noise features.

Persistence images tend to be more compact than landscape vectors (a $20 \times 20$ pixel image is 400 dimensions; a landscape with 5 functions at 200 steps is 1,000 dimensions) and perform better empirically in supervised learning tasks. However, they involve a smoothing bandwidth parameter (Gaussian kernel width) that must be tuned, and the discrete grid resolution introduces a mild information loss. For welfare data with a small number of prominent features, persistence images are a practical default for building feature matrices for regression.

## Practical Comparison: Which to Use

The choice between bottleneck distance, Wasserstein distance, landscapes, and images depends on the downstream task:

| Task | Recommended approach |
|---|---|
| Two-sample hypothesis test | Wasserstein-1 (Module 5 protocol) |
| Clustering many diagrams | Persistence landscape + k-means or hierarchical clustering |
| Supervised classification | Persistence image as feature vector |
| Detecting change in strongest feature | Bottleneck distance |
| Averaging diagrams (Fréchet mean) | Landscape average (pointwise) |

For the welfare-research programme in this path, landscape vectorisation is the primary tool for comparing TDA summaries across geographic areas or demographic groups, and Wasserstein-1 distance is the test statistic for the permutation tests confirming that topological differences are statistically significant.
