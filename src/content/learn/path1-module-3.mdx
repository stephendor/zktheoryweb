## Points Are Not Enough

A crowd of people tells you very little about the society they form. You need to know who knows whom, which groups share resources, which communities overlap, and which are isolated. Points — raw measurements of isolated individuals — carry the same limitation. A point cloud of 5,000 households, each plotted in six-dimensional space, is a set of isolated locations. Without any structure connecting them, you cannot ask whether the households cluster into recognisable groups, whether there are closed loops of mutual dependency, or whether some region of the distribution is topologically void — a gap in the landscape of lived experience. Simplicial complexes are the mathematical machinery that wires points together into higher-dimensional structure, making those questions answerable.

## Simplices: The Building Blocks

A **$k$-simplex** is the simplest possible $k$-dimensional geometric object spanned by $k+1$ points in general position (no three on a line, no four on a plane, and so on). Concretely:

- A **0-simplex** is a single point.
- A **1-simplex** is an edge connecting two points.
- A **2-simplex** is a filled triangle spanned by three points.
- A **3-simplex** is a solid tetrahedron spanned by four points.

Each lower-dimensional face of a $k$-simplex is itself a simplex: a triangle has three edges and three vertices; a tetrahedron has four triangular faces, six edges, and four vertices. These faces are called the **faces** of the simplex.

## Simplicial Complexes

A **simplicial complex** $K$ is a collection of simplices satisfying two rules:

$$
\sigma \in K \text{ and } \tau \subseteq \sigma \implies \tau \in K, \quad \text{and} \quad \sigma, \tau \in K \implies \sigma \cap \tau \in \{ \emptyset \} \cup K.
$$

In plain language: every face of every simplex in $K$ must also belong to $K$, and any two simplices either do not intersect at all or they intersect in a simplex that is itself already in $K$. The first rule ensures completeness — you cannot have a triangle without its edges and vertices. The second ensures consistency — two triangles sharing an edge share exactly that edge, not an overlapping region.

A simplicial complex built on a finite point cloud is a combinatorial object: you can list all its simplices in a table, encode it as a sparse matrix, and manipulate it on a computer without approximation. This is what makes it the right data structure for computational topology.

## Constructing Complexes from Data: Vietoris–Rips

The most widely used construction in TDA is the **Vietoris–Rips complex** $\text{VR}(X, r)$ at scale $r$. Given a finite metric space $(X, d)$:

A subset $\sigma \subseteq X$ of $k+1$ points is a $k$-simplex in $\text{VR}(X, r)$ if and only if every pair of points in $\sigma$ is within distance $r$:

$$
\sigma \in \text{VR}(X, r) \iff \max_{x, y \in \sigma} d(x, y) \leq r.
$$

The Vietoris–Rips condition is purely pairwise: to check whether a set of points forms a simplex, you only need to consult the pairwise distance matrix. There is no need to compute ball intersections or check geometric conditions in high-dimensional space. This makes $\text{VR}(X, r)$ efficiently computable for datasets with hundreds of variables.

As $r$ increases from zero to infinity, the Vietoris–Rips complex grows: at $r = 0$, only points (0-simplices); at small $r$, edges between nearby points; at moderate $r$, triangles and tetrahedra filling in; eventually, at large $r$, a fully connected simplex on all points. This growing sequence of complexes — one for each value of $r$ — is called a **filtration** and is the subject of Module 5.

## Why Triangles Matter: Detecting Loops

The topological significance of triangles is subtle. Consider three points $A$, $B$, $C$ forming a triangle. If the complex contains all three edges ($AB$, $BC$, $CA$) but *not* the filled triangle $ABC$, then the three edges enclose an empty loop — a one-dimensional hole. If the filled triangle is included, the hole is "filled in" and disappears. This is the mechanism by which topology distinguishes a circle (a loop, no interior) from a disk (a loop with interior filled in).

In poverty data, loops at the population level can represent closed circuits of disadvantage: a subset of households that are all pairwise near each other in deprivation-variable space, forming a cluster — but with a topological void at the centre. That void might indicate a bimodal distribution within the cluster, or a structural gap in a social programme's coverage. The algebraic tool that detects and counts such loops is **homology**, covered in Module 4.

## The Čech Complex: A Theoretical Companion

The **Čech complex** $\text{Čech}(X, r)$ is an alternative construction with stronger theoretical guarantees. A set $\sigma$ of points is a simplex in $\text{Čech}(X, r)$ if and only if the balls $B(x, r)$ share a common intersection:

$$
\sigma \in \text{Čech}(X, r) \iff \bigcap_{x \in \sigma} B(x, r) \neq \emptyset.
$$

By the Nerve Lemma, the Čech complex is homotopy equivalent to the union of balls $\bigcup_{x \in X} B(x, r)$, so it captures the topology of the thickened point cloud exactly. However, checking ball intersections in high dimensions is computationally expensive. The Vietoris–Rips complex is a 2-approximation: $\text{VR}(X, r) \subseteq \text{Čech}(X, r\sqrt{2}) \subseteq \text{VR}(X, r\sqrt{2})$. For most applications this approximation is tight enough, and Vietoris–Rips is the standard choice in practice.

## Using the Interactive: Filtration Playground

The **Filtration Playground** below is precisely the tool this module describes. Load a preset point cloud — or place your own points on the canvas — then advance the radius slider to watch the Vietoris–Rips complex build in real time.

At radius zero you have isolated points: the 0-simplices only, β₀ equal to the number of points. As the radius grows, edges appear between pairs of points that fall within distance $r$ of each other. Watch the **Connected components** counter in the right panel: every time two clusters merge through a new edge, β₀ decreases by one. When a triple of mutually close points all acquire their pairwise edges simultaneously, the filled triangle (2-simplex) appears — the hole closes and β₁ stays at zero. But if the three edges appear before the triangle is filled in, a loop forms and β₁ rises by one.

Three things to verify from this module: (1) the **closure property** — every face of every simplex is present (you will never see a triangle without all three of its edges); (2) the **pairwise condition** — a triangle appears exactly when all three pairwise distances are within $r$, not when any geometric centroid condition is met; (3) the **loop-filling mechanism** — a loop registered in β₁ disappears when the enclosing triangle is added, not merely when the perimeter edges are all present.

The step-by-step playback mode (the arrows beside the slider) is useful for watching individual homological events: a birth or death of a connected component or loop corresponds to a single edge or triangle being added. Module 5 formalises precisely which births and deaths persist across the full filtration.

## How This Module Connects

The construction of simplicial complexes from point clouds is the technical core of both **Paper 1** and **Paper 2** in the research portfolio. Paper 1 applies the Vietoris–Rips filtration to longitudinal poverty panel data, arguing that the topology of the resulting complex encodes information about poverty trap dynamics not visible in transition matrices. Paper 2 develops a variant construction adapted to survey data with missing values, where the standard Vietoris–Rips condition must be relaxed. Both papers depend on the **persistent-homology** method: the algebraic machinery that measures which topological features of a simplicial complex survive as the scale parameter $r$ increases.
