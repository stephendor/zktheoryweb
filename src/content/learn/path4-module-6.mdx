To test whether the bridge is real: (1) vary the resolution parameter ±20% and check whether the bridge persists or dissolves into separate chains — a real bridge will be robust across a range of resolutions; (2) examine which data points constitute the bridge node and check whether they form a coherent sociological group; (3) compare the bridge structure to null graphs generated by running Mapper on randomly permuted filter values — if the bridge appears in fewer than 5% of null permutations, it is statistically significant.'
  - question: 'Why does choosing DBSCAN over single-linkage clustering in the Mapper pipeline affect the graph topology, and which choice is more appropriate for welfare data with outliers?'
    answer: 'Single-linkage clustering connects clusters by their closest pair of points, making it highly sensitive to "chaining": a sequence of nearby points can link two distant groups into one cluster even if only a narrow tendril of points connects them. In welfare data, this means that even a few atypical households can bridge two welfare populations that are genuinely separated, creating spurious connections in the Mapper graph. DBSCAN, by contrast, defines clusters as contiguous high-density regions and treats low-density points as noise (label -1), assigning them to no cluster rather than forcing them into the nearest group. For welfare data — which typically contains genuine outliers (households with anomalous rent payments, administrative recording errors, households in exceptional crises) — DBSCAN produces a Mapper graph that is more faithful to the dense-population structure and less distorted by outlier chains. The trade-off is that DBSCAN requires tuning two parameters (eps and min_samples), adding to the analysis burden documented in Module 6 and the Mapper Parameter Lab.'
  - question: 'The cover condition for Mapper requires that every point in the image of the filter function is covered by at least one interval in the cover U. Why does violating this condition produce misleading graphs rather than merely incomplete ones?'
    answer: 'If a point in f(X) fails to be covered by any interval in U, the preimage f⁻¹(uncovered point) is a set of data points that belongs to no cluster in the Mapper construction. Those points are silently dropped from the graph entirely — they contribute no nodes and no edges. In welfare data, this is not random: points in uncovered regions of the filter-function range share similar filter values, meaning they form a coherent sub-population (e.g., households with median filter values, or households at a specific income threshold). Silently excluding a coherent sub-population produces a graph that misrepresents the global structure: a loop may appear broken (two chains rather than a cycle) because the nodes that would complete the cycle were dropped, or two genuinely connected populations may appear as two isolated components because the bridging group was excluded. The cover condition is not a technicality; it is a data-completeness guarantee that must be verified before interpreting any Mapper graph.'

## From Persistence to Navigation

Persistent homology tells you *that* topological features exist in your data — that there is a loop, that there are two clusters, that a void separates groups in variable space. It does not easily tell you *which data points* constitute those features, or what the local neighbourhood looks like around each structural element. For researchers who want to navigate the data rather than merely audit its global topology — to identify which households belong to which cluster, what makes the clusters distinctive, and where the transition zones between clusters lie — the **Mapper algorithm** is the operative tool. Mapper converts a high-dimensional point cloud into a graph that is simultaneously topologically faithful and directly readable. Nodes represent clusters of similar data points; edges represent non-trivial overlap between adjacent clusters. The graph is small even when the underlying data is large, making it a natural substrate for exploratory and explanatory analysis of welfare trajectories.

## Mapper Theory: A Practitioner Summary

Mapper's theoretical foundation is the nerve construction. Given a cover $\mathcal{U} = \{U_i\}$ of a topological space $X$ — a collection of overlapping open sets satisfying:

$$\forall x \in f(X), \; \exists U_i \in \mathcal{U} : x \in U_i$$

the **nerve** of the cover is the simplicial complex whose simplices correspond to non-empty intersections: a $k$-simplex exists for every $(k+1)$-tuple $\{U_{i_0}, \ldots, U_{i_k}\}$ with $U_{i_0} \cap \cdots \cap U_{i_k} \neq \emptyset$. The Nerve Theorem ensures that the nerve is homotopy equivalent to the union $\bigcup_i U_i$ when the cover elements are contractible.

The Mapper algorithm operationalises this for data by:

1. **Choosing a filter function** $f: X \to \mathbb{R}$ that maps each data point to a scalar.
2. **Covering the image** $f(X) \subset \mathbb{R}$ with overlapping intervals $\{I_j\}$ parameterised by resolution $r$ (number of intervals) and gain $g$ (percent overlap between adjacent intervals).
3. **Pulling back**: for each $I_j$, extracting the preimage $f^{-1}(I_j)$ — the set of data points whose filter value falls in $I_j$.
4. **Clustering** each preimage with a clustering algorithm, producing one cluster per connected component.
5. **Building the graph**: one node per cluster; one edge between clusters from adjacent intervals when they share at least one data point.

The resulting graph is a Mapper graph: a 1-complex (nodes and edges) that approximates the topological skeleton of the data at the resolution determined by the cover parameters.

## Choosing a Filter Function

The filter function is the most consequential design choice in Mapper. Different filter functions highlight different aspects of the data's structure.

**PCA first component** uses the direction of maximum variance in the data as the filter. It is the natural default when you want the Mapper graph to respect the principal axis of variation — for welfare data, this typically aligns with a poverty-severity gradient. The resulting graph encodes trajectories along the most variable dimension.

**Density filter** maps each point to the local density estimate (e.g., the Gaussian kernel density evaluated at that point). High-density regions generate densely connected graph nodes; low-density regions generate sparse nodes or isolated sub-graphs. For welfare data, using density as a filter reveals boundary populations — households at the fringes of the distribution — as sparse nodes typically at one end of the graph, with high-density central-tendency households forming the dense interior.

**Eccentricity filter** maps each point to the mean distance from that point to all other points. Points with high eccentricity (far from the data's centroid in average distance) become filter extremes. Eccentricity tends to reveal outlying or anomalous observations as the endpoints of the graph, making it useful for detecting systematically marginalised populations: households that are simultaneously or persistently distant from all other household types in welfare space will appear at the graph's periphery regardless of which PCA component is most active.

## Cover Parameter Selection

The two cover parameters govern the resolution–noise trade-off in the Mapper graph.

**Resolution** (number of intervals) determines how finely the filter range is divided. Too low a resolution collapses the data into a few large nodes with many inter-node edges, obscuring fine-grained structure; too high a resolution produces many small nodes with fragile edges (present or absent slightly depending on which data points land in each interval), amplifying noise. For a dataset of $n = 5{,}000$ points, a resolution of 20–40 intervals is typically appropriate for an initial exploration.

**Gain** (percent overlap between adjacent intervals) governs how much cross-interval sharing occurs. Higher gain means adjacent preimage sets share more data points, producing more edges and a more connected graph. Lower gain produces fewer edges and more disconnected components. A gain of 25–30% is a conventional default; values above 40% risk creating spurious connections between distant parts of the filter range; values below 15% risk fragmentation of genuinely connected populations.

## The Mapper Parameter Lab Interactive

The **Mapper Parameter Lab** paired with this module provides a real-time parameter tuner for Mapper applied to a synthetic welfare dataset. Drag the resolution slider and observe how the graph topology changes: at low resolution the graph collapses to a thin chain; at high resolution it fragments into isolated components. Adjust the gain slider and watch edges appear and disappear between adjacent nodes. The gain–resolution interaction is the core parameter-tuning challenge: you are searching for a stable structural region in the (resolution, gain) parameter space where the graph topology is qualitatively consistent across a range of settings. A loop visible across resolution 20–35 with gain 25–40% is a robust Mapper finding; a loop that appears only at one precise parameter combination is not.

The lab also lets you switch the filter function between PCA first component, density, and eccentricity, and observe how the graph reorders itself around each filter. This experiment directly demonstrates the filter-choice consequence: the same point cloud can appear as a loop, a flare, or two disconnected chains depending purely on the filter function used, with no change to the underlying data.
