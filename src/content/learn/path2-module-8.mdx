## The Survey That Talked Back

In 2016, residents of a housing estate in East Manchester were invited to participate in a new kind of poverty survey. Rather than being asked to confirm or deny a predetermined list of deprivations designed by researchers, they were given blank data sheets and asked to describe, in their own terms, what a decent life in their neighbourhood required. Their answers — broadband internet, a safe walking route to the school, a GP appointment that could be had within a week — were aggregated and analysed by the community itself. The resulting dataset was owned by the residents' association, not the research institution. Before any external body could access it, they had to negotiate terms.

The project was small. Its methodological claims were contested. But it represented a distinct break with the tradition of poverty measurement traced in the preceding seven modules: for the first time in the measurement encounter, the people being counted held the pen.

## The Epistemology of Measurement Power

Every poverty measurement system embeds a theory of knowledge: a set of assumptions about who knows what poverty is, whose definition should prevail, and who has the legitimate authority to act on the resulting data. The tradition from Rowntree through Universal Credit has been consistently expert-driven. Researchers, statisticians, and policymakers define the dimensions of poverty, specify the thresholds, collect the data, and publish analyses that determine how resources are allocated — with the measured population as passive subjects of an administrative process.

Data justice scholarship — developed by Linnet Taylor, the Data Justice Lab at Cardiff, and community organisations in the Global South — argues that this structure is not a neutral technical choice. It is a power relation. Who controls the measurement controls the narrative of need, and the narrative of need determines the political conditions of possibility for redistribution.

The formalisation of this critique has produced several distinct proposals. The **CARE principles** for Indigenous data governance specify that data collection should produce Collective Benefit for the community it represents; that communities have the Authority to Control their own data; that collectors bear Responsibility for the relationship; and that data use must follow Ethics defined by the community. The CARE principles do not reject statistical methodology — they propose that the governance structure around it should be democratised.

The mathematical implications are significant. A poverty measure derived from participatory data is not merely a different dataset: it is a different measurement function. If we write a conventional poverty measure as $M: \mathcal{X} \to [0,1]$ where $\mathcal{X}$ is the space of household characteristics and $M$ maps them to a poverty score, participatory statistics changes the constitution of $\mathcal{X}$ itself — which dimensions are included, how they are scaled, and what the range of $M$ is held to represent. These are choices that the participatory framework places in the hands of the measured population.

## Topological Tools for Counter-Measurement

Persistent homology offers a particular promise for participatory statistics. Its primary output — the persistence diagram $\text{dgm}(X)$ of a point cloud $X$ — is **coordinate-free**: it depends only on the pairwise distances between points, not on any externally chosen coordinate system or weighting scheme. This means that if a participatory dataset assigns dimensions of deprivation according to community priorities rather than administrative categories, the topological analysis remains valid without requiring the researcher to specify how those dimensions should be aggregated.

Formally, given a filtered simplicial complex $K_0 \subset K_1 \subset \cdots \subset K_n$ built from the participatory point cloud, the $k$-dimensional persistence module tracks the lifetimes of topological features:

$$
H_k(K_0) \to H_k(K_1) \to \cdots \to H_k(K_n)
$$

The resulting barcode, summarising which features are born at filtration step $\epsilon_i$ and die at $\epsilon_j$, encodes the multi-scale structure of deprivation in a form that is stable under small perturbations of the data and does not privilege any particular threshold or aggregation rule. The poverty boundaries revealed by persistent homology are intrinsic to the community's own experience of deprivation — not imposed by an external equivalisation formula or poverty line.

## Goodhart's Law and the Politics of Metrics

Any poverty measure that becomes a target will eventually cease to describe the thing it was designed to measure. This is Goodhart's Law, and it has been observed empirically in every major poverty reduction programme the UK has attempted. Child poverty fell by the official measure between 2003 and 2010 in part because benefit increases moved households just above the 60%-of-median threshold; households far below the threshold showed little improvement, but improvement in their position contributed minimally to progress on the headline metric.

Counter-mathematics does not solve Goodhart's problem by finding a better metric. It proposes that the instability of any single metric under political pressure is an argument for distributing metric authority — for having multiple, community-controlled poverty measures that no single government can optimise its way through. The resulting landscape of measures is harder to navigate and resists simple international comparison. But it is far more robust against the dynamic by which any fixed measure eventually becomes a target and any target eventually becomes an obstacle to the reality it was once designed to illuminate.

## Using the Interactive: Barcode Comparator

The **Barcode Comparator** presents the same 30 synthetic households described in two ways: once using official DWP deprivation index dimensions (income, employment, health, education, housing) and once using the community-defined dimensions from the hypothetical East Manchester participatory project (safety, green space access, neighbourhood trust, public transport, childcare access).

The data have been deliberately designed to contain three groups. Ten households are officially deprived (low income and employment scores) but community-resilient: they live in neighbourhoods with high trust, good safety scores, and accessible green space. Ten households are not officially deprived but community-isolated: their income and employment scores are acceptable, but they lack access to green space, their neighbourhood trust is very low, and childcare is unavailable. The final ten households are consistently deprived: both systems record them as poorly off.

Because the two coordinate systems arrange these 30 points differently — official deprivation clusters the first and last groups together (both have low income) while separating the middle group; community dimensions cluster the first group separately (both have high trust and safety) and place the middle group near the consistently deprived — the Vietoris-Rips filtration on the two projected point clouds detects different topological structure.

The **H₀ barcode** shows how connected components merge as the filtration radius ε grows. Under the official coordinate system, the three groups behave differently: the first and third groups (both officially deprived) merge early at small ε, while the second group (not officially deprived) joins later. Under the community coordinate system, the groupings are rearranged: the first group (community-resilient) and the second group (community-isolated) are separated by the same ε values, but they form different early clusters.

The **H₁ barcode** shows loops: configurations of households that are mutually near-connected but do not form a simple cluster. The number and persistence of H₁ intervals differs between the two systems, reflecting the different geometric structure of deprivation under each measurement framework.

The **bottleneck distance** at the top quantifies how different the two persistence diagrams are. A value of 0 would mean the topological features are identical: the two measurement systems would produce the same structural picture of deprivation. A positive value means that the official and community systems record structurally different patterns in the same population. This is not a statistical artefact — it is the mathematical formalisation of the claim in Chapter 16 that different measurement frameworks can produce genuinely different rather than merely differently-labelled pictures of poverty.

The stability theorem of Cohen-Steiner et al. (2007) guarantees that if two measurement functions differ by at most δ in the L∞ norm, their persistence diagrams differ by at most δ in the bottleneck distance. The bottleneck distances observed here exceed what could be explained by measurement error alone — they reflect a structural difference in the dimensions of welfare that the two frameworks choose to encode.

## How This Module Connects

**Chapter 15** of *Counting Lives* introduces the data justice framework and its intellectual lineage, situating participatory statistics within the broader critique of administrative measurement developed throughout the book. **Chapter 16** examines concrete UK experiments with community data governance — residents' associations, data trusts, and participatory budgeting processes — and assesses both their achievements and the structural obstacles they have encountered. **Chapter 17** closes the book by proposing a synthesis: that the mathematical tools of TDA and the political tools of data justice are not merely compatible but mutually necessary — topology provides the formal stability guarantees that make participatory data analytically tractable, while data justice provides the democratic legitimacy that gives the resulting analysis political force.
