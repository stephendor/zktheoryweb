## The Body the Engineers Did Not Design For

In the standard automotive crash test, the dummy in the driver's seat weighs seventy-seven kilograms, stands one hundred and seventy-seven centimetres tall, and has the proportions of a fiftieth-percentile male body. For decades, this was the only body that car safety systems were systematically designed and tested for. Female crash-test dummies that modelled average female body characteristics — smaller stature, different bone density distribution, different seatbelt positioning relative to anatomy — were introduced in some test protocols only from the 1990s onwards, and even then were often relegated to passenger rather than driver positions. The consequence, documented by Caroline Criado Perez in *Invisible Women*, is that women are significantly more likely to sustain serious injury in a car crash of the same severity as one that leaves a male occupant uninjured. Not because female bodies are inherently more fragile, but because the safety systems were engineered for a body that was treated as the default human being. The "default human" was male.

This is not an isolated design failure. It is a systemic pattern affecting a strikingly diverse range of domains — from the dimensions of personal protective equipment to the smartphone screen sizes that are too large for an average female hand to operate comfortably one-handed. Each design default reflects a data collection gap: a survey that did not measure female bodies, a clinical trial that enrolled only male participants, an engineering standard that was set when the relevant workforce was entirely male. Data gaps are not random. They cluster around the lives and bodies of groups that were not included in the production of knowledge or the design of systems.

## Power in the Data Pipeline

Catherine D'Ignazio and Lauren Klein, in *Data Feminism*, map these patterns onto a broader argument about power in the data pipeline. They identify seven principles, but the most foundational is this: the forces that shape what data is collected, by whom, about whom, and for what purpose are the same forces that shape every other aspect of social life. Data is not collected in a laboratory of neutral observation. It is collected by institutions that have budgets, incentive structures, reporting obligations, and historical legacies. Those institutions were built in social contexts that elevated certain kinds of knowledge and certain kinds of knowers above others.

Consider the measurement of the gender pay gap. In the United Kingdom, the headline figure — women earn approximately eighty pence for every pound earned by men — is routinely contested on the basis that it does not control for occupation, hours worked, or career stage. This is technically correct: when you control for these factors, a portion of the gap narrows. But the feminist data argument is precisely that those factors are themselves products of gendered social structures. Women are concentrated in lower-paying occupations in part because those occupations absorbed women when higher-paying professions were legally closed to them, and in part because occupations that became feminised experienced pay compression as a consequence of that feminisation. Women work fewer paid hours in part because they perform the vast majority of unpaid care work. Controlling for these variables does not neutralise the gender pay gap; it hides the structural causes that produced it.

## The Invisibility of Care

The most consequential feminist data gap in macroeconomics is the treatment of unpaid care and domestic work — childcare, elder care, cooking, cleaning, and the emotional labour of maintaining relationships and households — as economically invisible. GDP, the dominant measure of national economic performance, counts only goods and services exchanged in markets for money. If a parent pays a nursery to look after their child, that transaction appears in GDP. If a grandparent provides the same care unpaid, it does not. The decision to measure only market activity was baked into national accounting conventions in the 1940s, at a moment when the formal economy was predominantly male and domestic work predominantly female. The consequence is that an enormous quantity of productive economic activity systematically disappears from the metrics used to allocate public resources and evaluate policy performance.

Feminist economists have repeatedly calculated the scale of this invisibility. Estimates of the monetary value of unpaid care and domestic work in high-income countries consistently range from twenty to fifty percent of official GDP — in most estimates, a value larger than any single sector of the formal economy. The invisibility is not a technical necessity of national accounting. It is a political choice that has profound consequences: when care work is invisible in the measurement systems used to justify funding decisions, investment in care infrastructure — childcare centres, paid parental leave, support for informal carers — appears as a cost rather than as a contribution to economic productivity.

## What a Gender Gap Calculator Would Reveal

A future interactive tool for this module would allow you to explore how differently the gender pay gap looks depending on the data source, the variables controlled for, and the analytical choices made. You could observe the gap narrowing or widening depending on whether occupational segregation is treated as a control variable or as part of the phenomenon to be explained. The interactive does not yet exist, but the methodological question it embodies — what counts as a legitimate control and what counts as hiding a cause — is central to any politically charged quantitative analysis.
