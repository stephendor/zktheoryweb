## What Is a Topological Space?

Topology is often described as "rubber-sheet geometry" — the study of properties that survive continuous stretching, bending, and twisting, but not tearing or gluing. A circle and a square are topologically the same; a circle and a figure-eight are not.

More formally, a **topological space** is a set $X$ together with a collection of open sets $\tau$ satisfying three axioms: the empty set and $X$ itself are in $\tau$, arbitrary unions of open sets are in $\tau$, and finite intersections of open sets are in $\tau$.<sup aria-describedby="sn-1">1</sup>

<Sidenote number={1}>The three axioms encode our intuition about "nearness" without requiring a notion of distance. This is precisely what makes topology more general than geometry.</Sidenote>

## Continuous Maps and Homeomorphisms

A function $f: X \to Y$ between topological spaces is **continuous** if the preimage of every open set in $Y$ is open in $X$. When $f$ is a bijection and both $f$ and $f^{-1}$ are continuous, we call it a **homeomorphism** — a topological equivalence.

The classic example: a coffee mug is homeomorphic to a donut (torus), since both have exactly one hole. Neither is homeomorphic to a sphere, which has none.

## Why This Matters for Data

When we represent a population dataset as a point cloud in $\mathbb{R}^n$, each variable becomes a dimension and each household a point. The *shape* of this cloud — how points cluster, whether they form loops, where the voids lie — encodes structural inequality that summary statistics cannot capture.

Topological Data Analysis asks: what is the persistent shape of poverty data across all scales of resolution? The answer, as we will see in later modules, reveals features invisible to conventional econometric methods.

## From Spaces to Simplicial Complexes

In practice, we cannot work with continuous spaces on a computer. Instead, we build **simplicial complexes**: combinatorial approximations made of vertices (points), edges (pairs), triangles (triples), and higher-dimensional simplices. The Vietoris–Rips and Čech complexes are two standard constructions we will explore in detail in the next module.
