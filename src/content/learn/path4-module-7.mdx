## The Problem with Snapshots

Standard Vietoris–Rips persistence is a snapshot technique. It computes the topology of a point cloud at one moment in time, across all scales simultaneously, but at a fixed population of data points. For welfare research based on cross-sectional surveys, this is appropriate: the survey captures a population at one point in time, and the persistence diagram characterises the topology of that cross-sectional cloud. But the most compelling poverty-research questions are longitudinal: do households cycle through welfare states? Does the topological structure of poverty change in response to a policy reform? Are the same households responsible for the cyclical dynamics in wave 3 and in wave 8, or has the cycle's composition shifted entirely?

These questions require tracking topological features through time. Standard persistence cannot do this: computing separate VR diagrams for each survey wave produces a sequence of diagrams with no formal connection between them. You can measure the bottleneck distance between consecutive diagrams (as Module 9 addresses), but this tells you only how much the topology changed, not whether a specific feature — a particular poverty cycle — survived, died, and was replaced, or continuously evolved. **Zigzag persistence** resolves this by extending persistent homology to filtrations where the maps between consecutive complexes need not be inclusions.

## Zigzag Filtrations: Theory

A standard filtration is a nested sequence $K_0 \subseteq K_1 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq K_n$ where every map is an inclusion. A **zigzag filtration** replaces this with a sequence where maps alternate in direction:

$$M: \mathbb{F}^{n_1} \xleftarrow{f_1} \mathbb{F}^{n_2} \xrightarrow{f_2} \mathbb{F}^{n_3} \xleftarrow{f_3} \cdots$$

Each arrow can point forward (inclusion: the complex grows) or backward (restriction: the complex shrinks). The algebraic object associated with a zigzag filtration is a **zigzag module** — a sequence of vector spaces and linear maps as above. The fundamental theorem of persistent homology generalises to this setting: every zigzag module over a field $\mathbb{F}$ decomposes uniquely into interval modules, each corresponding to a bar $[i, j]$ meaning a feature was "alive" across the indices from $i$ to $j$ in the sequence.

For time-indexed data with $T$ survey waves, the natural zigzag construction inserts **union complexes** between consecutive waves. Between wave $t$ and wave $t+1$, form the union complex $K_t \cup K_{t+1}$ — the simplicial complex that includes all simplices from both waves simultaneously. The zigzag sequence then reads:

$$K_1 \hookrightarrow K_1 \cup K_2 \hookleftarrow K_2 \hookrightarrow K_2 \cup K_3 \hookleftarrow K_3 \hookrightarrow \cdots$$

The forward arrows ($\hookrightarrow$) are inclusions of each wave complex into the union; the backward arrows ($\hookleftarrow$) are inclusions of the next wave complex into the union. This doubling construction is the standard approach for longitudinal data and is implemented in the GUDHI library under `SimplexTree.compute_zigzag_persistence`.

## Applications to Poverty Trajectories

The motivating application for zigzag persistence in this research programme is tracking poverty-cycle dynamics across the waves of a longitudinal household panel (e.g., the UK Household Longitudinal Study, UKHLS). Households enter the panel, are observed for some waves, and then leave — either by attrition, emigration, or panel redesign. The population at each wave is therefore not nested inside the population at the next wave: new households enter, and others exit. Standard persistent homology treats each wave independently. Zigzag persistence treats the sequence of waves as a single coherent object and asks: which H₁ features (poverty cycles) persist across multiple waves?

A zigzag H₁ bar spanning waves 3 through 7 means: a one-dimensional cycle appeared in the welfare-trajectory data during wave 3 (when the corresponding union complex first contained a non-trivial H₁ class), remained visible through all intermediate unions, and was filled in during the wave-7 transition. This is a formal statement about a persistent structural feature of the time-varying data — not the statement that the same households were cycling throughout, but that cyclical dynamics were detectable and robust across that five-wave window.

Computational cost scales with the total number of simplices across all waves and their unions. For a 10-wave panel with 500 landmarks per wave, the simplex count at each union complex is approximately $2 \times \binom{500}{2} \approx 250{,}000$ edges, making each union step tractable. Without landmark subsampling, the computation is infeasible for typical social-science dataset sizes.

## Comparing Zigzag Bars Across Studies

The bar-code produced by zigzag persistence is indexed by position in the filtration sequence, not by a continuous scale parameter. This introduces an alignment challenge when comparing results across two studies with different wave counts or different panel compositions. The appropriate comparison metric is still a variant of bottleneck or Wasserstein distance, but now computed over the space of zigzag bar-codes rather than standard persistence diagrams. This is an active area of TDA theory development — stable metrics for zigzag persistence are established in dimension zero but remain an open question in full generality for H₁. For applied work, the pragmatic approach is to report the bar spans qualitatively (which index ranges show persistent features) and to complement with standard VR persistence on a pooled cross-wave snapshot for comparison.
