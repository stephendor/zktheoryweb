## When Do Households Return to Where They Started?

Imagine tracking a cohort of 2,000 households across twelve years of annual surveys, recording income, employment status, housing security, and health. You arrange them as a point cloud in four-dimensional variable space and apply the Vietoris–Rips construction from Module 3, increasing the scale parameter $r$ from zero upward. At each value of $r$ you compute the Betti numbers from Module 4. You notice something: at scales between $r = 0.3$ and $r = 1.1$, the first Betti number $\beta_1 = 1$. A loop exists. At $r = 1.2$, a triangle fills in and the loop disappears. That loop was born at $r = 0.3$ and died at $r = 1.2$: its persistence is $1.2 - 0.3 = 0.9$. It persists across a wide range of scales — wider than most other features in the data. Is this a signal of genuine cyclical structure in poverty trajectories, or noise? The persistence diagram gives you the evidence to decide.

## Filtrations: Shape Across Scales

A **filtration** of a simplicial complex $K$ is a nested sequence of sub-complexes:

$$
\emptyset = K_0 \subseteq K_1 \subseteq K_2 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq K_n = K.
$$

For the Vietoris–Rips construction, the filtration is indexed by the scale parameter $r$: as $r$ increases from zero to the diameter of the point cloud, simplices are added one by one whenever their pairwise distances all become $\leq r$. Each step $K_i \hookrightarrow K_{i+1}$ is an inclusion of simplicial complexes — every simplex in $K_i$ remains in $K_{i+1}$, and some new simplices are added.

The filtration encodes the *multi-scale* geometry of the data. There is no single "correct" scale at which to analyse a point cloud; different scales reveal different structural features. The persistence diagram captures all of them simultaneously.

## Birth, Death, and Persistence

As we move through the filtration, homology classes in each dimension $k$ are created and destroyed. A **birth** occurs at scale $r_b$ when a $k$-cycle appears that was not the boundary of any higher-dimensional chain in $K_{r_b}$ — a genuinely new hole opens. A **death** occurs at scale $r_d > r_b$ when that cycle becomes a boundary in $K_{r_d}$ — the hole is filled in by new simplices. The **persistence** of the feature is:

$$
\text{pers} = r_d - r_b.
$$

Features with $r_d = \infty$ are called **essential**: they never die and persist to the end of the filtration. In a connected point cloud filtered by Vietoris–Rips, exactly one 0-dimensional class (the entire connected component) is essential; all others die as the filtration merges components together.

## The Persistence Diagram

The **persistence diagram** $\text{Dgm}_k(K)$ is the multi-set of points $\{(r_b, r_d)\}$ in the extended plane $\mathbb{R}^2 \cup \{(r, \infty)\}$, one point for each $k$-dimensional topological feature created and destroyed in the filtration. The horizontal axis records birth time; the vertical axis records death time. Because death always occurs after birth, all points lie strictly above the diagonal $r_d = r_b$.

The distance of a point from the diagonal is half its persistence: $\frac{1}{2}(r_d - r_b)$. Points close to the diagonal have low persistence and are typically attributed to noise; points far from the diagonal have high persistence and represent robust topological structure. The persistence diagram thus provides a principled, scale-free decomposition of the data's topology into signal and noise.

## Stability: Why Diagrams Are Trustworthy

A key theoretical guarantee is the **stability theorem**: small perturbations to the point cloud (resampling noise, measurement error) produce small perturbations to the persistence diagram, as measured by the **bottleneck distance** $d_b$. For two filtrations derived from point clouds $X$ and $X'$:

$$
d_b\!\left(\text{Dgm}(X),\, \text{Dgm}(X')\right) \leq d_H(X, X'),
$$

where $d_H$ is the Hausdorff distance between the two point clouds. This Lipschitz stability means that the diagram is a statistically consistent estimator of the underlying population topology: with enough data, the empirical persistence diagram converges to the persistence diagram of the population distribution.

## Using the Interactive: Persistence Diagram Builder

The **Persistence Diagram Builder** paired with this module lets you place points on a canvas, and watch the Vietoris–Rips filtration unfold in real time. As you drag the scale slider rightward, the interactive adds edges and fills triangles on the canvas, and simultaneously plots each topological feature as it is born and as it dies — you can see a point appear in the diagram and then move upward (its death value increasing) with each new simplex. Experiment with configurations that produce clear loops (a ring of points will generate one long-lived $H_1$ class) and those that produce no loops (a filled disk generates only a cluster in $H_0$). Notice that the diagonal accumulates many short-lived points regardless of the configuration — these are the noise features, born and immediately killed by nearby triangles. The one or two points far from the diagonal are the features worth interpreting.

## How This Module Connects

The persistence diagram is the primary output of all empirical analyses in **Paper 1** and **Paper 2** of the research portfolio. Paper 1 constructs persistence diagrams from panel poverty data and interprets the $H_1$ signal as evidence for poverty trap dynamics; the long-lived loop in the diagram is the paper's central exhibit. Paper 2 develops a persistence-diagram methodology for survey data with partial non-response, confronting the stability theorem's dependence on Hausdorff distance when observations are missing. Both papers rely on the **persistent-homology** pipeline — the algorithmic implementation of filtration, birth–death tracking, and diagram construction using matrix reduction algorithms over finite fields.
