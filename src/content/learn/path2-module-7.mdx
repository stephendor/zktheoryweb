## The Explanation That Cannot Be Given

In 2019, a 47-year-old man in Birmingham received a letter from a local housing authority informing him that his application for social housing had been deprioritised by an automated system. Under Article 22 of the UK GDPR, he had the right to request a meaningful explanation of the automated decision. He exercised that right. The authority's response described the general categories of data used by the system — credit history, tenancy record, employment status — and stated that the model had assessed his application as lower priority than others in the queue.

It did not explain the specific basis for his deprioritisation. It could not: the neural network at the core of the system had been trained on historical allocation decisions, and while its aggregate accuracy on held-out test data was documented, the specific learned representations driving any individual prediction were not accessible to the authority's administrators, let alone to the applicant. The right to explanation encountered the limits of the model's interpretability.

## How Neural Networks Learn

A **feedforward neural network** learns a function $f: \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}$ by composing multiple layers of parameterised transformations. With $L$ hidden layers, the computation is:

$$
\mathbf{h}^{(1)} = \phi\!\left(W^{(1)} \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}^{(1)}\right), \quad \mathbf{h}^{(\ell)} = \phi\!\left(W^{(\ell)} \mathbf{h}^{(\ell-1)} + \mathbf{b}^{(\ell)}\right), \quad \hat{y} = W^{(L+1)} \mathbf{h}^{(L)} + b^{(L+1)}
$$

where $\phi$ is a nonlinear activation function (commonly ReLU: $\phi(z) = \max(0, z)$), and $W^{(\ell)}, \mathbf{b}^{(\ell)}$ are the learned weight matrices and bias vectors at layer $\ell$. The network is trained by minimising a loss function $\mathcal{L}(\hat{y}, y)$ using gradient descent, iteratively adjusting all weights to reduce prediction error on the training data.

The interpretability problem arises from the composition of nonlinear functions. In logistic regression, the mapping from inputs to output is linear in the log-odds, and each feature's contribution can be stated as a single number with clear units. In a deep network, a feature's influence is distributed nonlinearly across all layers — its effect depends on the values of all other features, changes across different input regions, and cannot be summarised as a single coefficient. There is no equivalent of the $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ vector.

## Post-Hoc Explanation Methods and Their Limits

The most widely deployed explanation technique in welfare systems is **SHAP** (SHapley Additive exPlanations), which estimates each feature's marginal contribution to a specific prediction by averaging the contribution of that feature across all possible orderings of the feature set. For a prediction $\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})$, the SHAP value for feature $j$ is:

$$
\phi_j = \sum_{S \subseteq \mathcal{F} \setminus \{j\}} \frac{|S|!\,(|\mathcal{F}|-|S|-1)!}{|\mathcal{F}|!} \bigl[\hat{f}(S \cup \{j\}) - \hat{f}(S)\bigr]
$$

where $\mathcal{F}$ is the full feature set and the sum is over all subsets $S$ not containing $j$. The SHAP values are guaranteed to sum to the prediction: $\sum_j \phi_j = \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) - E[\hat{f}]$. This property is appealing for regulatory compliance — each feature "accounts for" a measurable portion of the score.

The practical limitations are severe. SHAP values are **local approximations**: they explain why the model gave this individual a particular score, but they do not describe any global logic that applies across cases. Two individuals with similar SHAP explanations may have arrived at the same score through entirely different internal representations. The values are also computationally expensive for large feature sets, leading practitioners to approximate them — and the approximations are known to be unstable under small perturbations of the input. A claimant whose score sits near the decision threshold may receive a different SHAP explanation after a trivial change in their reported circumstances, even if their score does not change.

## Adversarial Auditing and Its Structural Limits

Without access to model weights, training data, or deployment code, external auditors must probe algorithmic systems from the outside — constructing test queries, submitting structured applications, or analysing population-level outcome data. Several civil society organisations in the UK have developed adversarial audit methodologies for this purpose.

The structural problem is asymmetric access. The auditor must hypothesise which demographic dimensions to probe, construct test inputs that isolate the variable of interest, and demonstrate a systematic pattern rather than individual cases. System operators can contest audit methodologies, claim that test conditions do not reflect production behaviour, and invoke commercial confidentiality over deployment details. The legal burden of proof in discrimination claims under the Equality Act 2010 requires the claimant to establish a prima facie case before the burden shifts to the defendant — a standard difficult to meet from the outside of a black-box system.

## Using the Interactive: SHAP Instability Demonstrator

The **SHAP Instability Demonstrator** presents a synthetic near-threshold welfare claimant and a small fixed-weight neural network. Exact SHAP values are computed via subset enumeration (2^4 = 16 subsets for 4 features: age, income score, benefit duration, household size). Because this is the exact Shapley computation — not an approximation — the instability demonstrated here is intrinsic to the local structure of the model, not an artefact of the sampling algorithm.

The **score gauge** shows the predicted risk score and whether the claimant is flagged (≥ τ = 0.50) or cleared. The grey tick indicates the baseline score; as you adjust feature values, the fill colour shifts between green (cleared) and red (flagged).

The **SHAP bar chart** shows each feature's current attribution (orange/blue bars) alongside the baseline attribution (faded grey). Positive SHAP values push the score above the baseline; negative values push it below. The sum of all SHAP values equals score − baseline.

The **instability panel** tracks two quantities: the change in predicted score (Δ Score) and the maximum change across all four feature attributions (Δ SHAP). If the ratio Δ SHAP / Δ Score exceeds 2×, the panel highlights it in red. This is the mathematically precise version of the claim in the module text: the explanation can shift more than the prediction does.

To reproduce the core instability result: nudge the *income score* slider slightly upward from its baseline position (say, from 0.42 to 0.50). The score changes modestly. But a different feature — *benefit duration* or *household size* — may jump to become the dominant positive or negative contributor as the local gradient rotates. Slide back and forward across this region to watch the attributions oscillate while the score stays close to 0.50.

This is not a fault of the specific network used here. It is a structural property of SHAP in the neighbourhood of highly curved decision boundaries, which are common in neural networks near the decision threshold. A claimant in this region — "borderline" in practical terms — is precisely the person for whom the explanation matters most and for whom it is most unstable.
