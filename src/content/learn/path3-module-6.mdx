## The Score That Preceded the Knock on the Door

In Allegheny County, Pennsylvania, before a child welfare caseworker visits a family reported for a concern, they may consult a number: a risk score produced by the Allegheny Family Screening Tool (AFST), an algorithm that draws on over a hundred variables from county administrative databases to estimate the probability that a child will be removed from their family within two years of an initial referral. The variables include whether household members have criminal records, whether they have received public mental health services, whether they are enrolled in public benefits programmes. The algorithm was designed with scientific rigour and transparent methodology; its creators published their validation studies and data sources.

Virginia Eubanks, who studied the AFST in depth for her book *Automating Inequality*, asks a question the validation studies cannot answer: what does it mean that using mental health services and receiving public benefits are predictors in a child abuse risk model? Families seeking mental health support are not more likely to harm their children. Families receiving food stamps are not more dangerous to their children than families with equivalent income who do not claim benefits. These variables predict involvement with supervised services — which is to say, they predict poverty and prior contact with public agencies. The algorithm is, in effect, a poverty scoring system with child welfare as its legitimating frame. And because caseworkers using the tool work under caseload pressure, a high score shapes attention in ways that are difficult to consciously override.

## How Algorithms Encode Inequality

The core problem Eubanks identifies is not that algorithms are wrong in a correctible sense. It is that algorithms trained on historical data reproduce historical patterns of surveillance and exclusion. Social scientists call this *feedback loop bias* or *proxy discrimination*: a model that uses characteristics statistically correlated with race, income, or disability as predictors will produce racially, economically, or ableistically disparate outcomes even without any explicitly discriminatory variable in the input data. This is not a failure of the algorithm; it is the algorithm working precisely as designed, learning from a world that was already unequal.

The opacity of automated systems compounds the problem. A human caseworker who makes a discriminatory judgement can, in principle, be required to explain their reasoning and held accountable for it. An algorithm produces a number. The technical complexity of the underlying model — the feature engineering, the model architecture, the threshold-setting — is opaque not only to affected families but often to the front-line workers who use the tool. This combination of opacity and apparent scientific objectivity is what Eubanks calls the "digital poorhouse": systems that automate and thereby legitimise the exclusion and surveillance of poor communities in ways that are systematically harder to contest than the equivalent human decisions would be.

## Contestability and the Right to Explanation

The legal and advocacy response to automated decision-making has coalesced around two concepts: *transparency* and *contestability*. The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation, in Article 22, establishes a right not to be subject to solely automated decisions that produce significant legal or similar effects — a right that in theory should cover algorithmic benefit sanctions, welfare eligibility decisions, and risk scoring systems used in social work. In practice, agencies frequently evade the spirit of this requirement by inserting a nominal human reviewer into the process without providing that reviewer with the information, the time, or the institutional support needed to exercise genuine independent judgement on the algorithm's outputs.

Community audit approaches — where advocacy organisations or academic researchers attempt to reverse-engineer algorithmic systems by systematically submitting test cases and analysing outputs — have produced some of the most significant findings in this area. They are also expensive, slow, and dependent on the cooperation of or successful litigation against agencies that have little institutional incentive to be audited.

## What the Scoring Threshold Explorer Shows

The interactive tool embedded in this module — the Scoring Threshold Explorer — allows you to observe directly why threshold-setting is not a technical but a political act. The tool presents a simulated population with two sub-groups (representing, for instance, families in areas of high and low deprivation) that have been assigned risk scores by a model. You control the threshold *tau* above which a case is flagged for intervention. As you move *tau*, you can observe the differential rate of false positives and false negatives across the two sub-groups. At almost every threshold value, one sub-group carries a higher rate of one type of error than the other.

This is not a bug in the model. It is a mathematical near-impossibility: it can be shown that when two groups have different base rates for the outcome being predicted, no model can simultaneously achieve equal false positive rates, equal false negative rates, and equal overall accuracy across both groups. Every threshold is a choice about which kind of error to impose more heavily on which group. Algorithmic systems that present themselves as objective are, at the threshold-setting stage, making an ethical and political determination about which community bears the cost of the model's uncertainty.

## Counter-Data Strategies

Community organisations facing algorithmic systems that cannot easily be challenged through legal or regulatory channels have developed counter-data strategies: the collection of alternative evidence bases that document the real-world impacts of algorithmic decisions and provide the empirical foundation for advocacy and litigation. Participatory data collection, community-controlled databases, and systematic documentation of refusals and sanctions have all been used to demonstrate patterns of discriminatory impact that individual cases, handled in isolation, would never surface.

These strategies connect the Data Justice path back to its starting point. The question of who counts (Module 1) — whose experience is captured in data and whose is excluded — turns out to be inseparable from the question of who is harmed by algorithmic systems that act on data at scale. The communities most harmed are consistently those whose data is collected through surveillance rather than service, and who have the least capacity to contest the decisions that data subsequently produces.
