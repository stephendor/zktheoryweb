## The Neighbourhood That Did Not Exist

In the years before the 2010 United States Census, demographers at the Census Bureau already knew which communities they would undercount. Remote tribal lands, densely packed urban blocks with high rates of informal housing, and neighbourhoods where residents had generations of reason to distrust government representatives at the door — all of these would be systematically under-represented in the final tally. For the Oglala Sioux Tribe on the Pine Ridge Reservation in South Dakota, the consequences were not abstract. Federal formula funding for housing, healthcare, and education flows in proportion to the counted population. Every person missed in the census is a recurring annual loss: not just a name absent from a spreadsheet, but a school meal not funded, a clinic visit not planned for, a road not maintained. The question "who counts?" is, at root, a question about who receives the resources and representation that the state distributes through its numbers.

## What Gets Counted and Why

The history of official statistics is a history of decisions about what matters. Every census, survey, and administrative register encodes choices — choices that are political in the deepest sense. Who is defined as a household member? What races are named? Which kinds of work are classified as employment? Is a person sleeping rough counted once, or not at all?

These decisions are rarely made maliciously. More often they reflect the assumptions and interests of the people designing the instruments. Early twentieth-century British surveys of poverty excluded the very poorest because investigators could not reliably reach them, producing estimates of deprivation that were conveniently low. The US Census classified enslaved Black Americans as three-fifths of a person not from ignorance but as a deliberate political compromise that amplified the voting power of slaveholding states. Even today, definitions of unemployment that exclude workers who have given up searching produce headline figures that understate the true scale of labour market distress.

The category "homeless person" illustrates the stakes with particular clarity. Depending on whether a government counts only rough sleepers, or whether it includes people in temporary accommodation, sofa-surfing with friends, or staying in refuges for survivors of domestic violence, the measured homeless population may range across an order of magnitude. Funders, planners, and politicians all respond to the number that appears in official records. Communities whose housing crises are rendered invisible by narrow definitions see fewer resources, fewer dedicated services, and less political urgency directed their way.

## Who Is Harmed by Not Being Counted

There is a useful distinction between two kinds of counting failure. The first is *technical undercounting* — when people who should be included in a survey are simply missed, because they move frequently, lack a fixed address, or do not speak the survey language. The second is *structural invisibility* — when the categories and questions of a survey are designed in ways that never could have captured a group's experience, because the people who designed it did not imagine that group as a subject worth counting.

Both failures produce real harm, but structural invisibility is harder to diagnose and resist. If a national wellbeing survey asks about relationship status in binary terms, non-binary and gender-nonconforming people are not just undercounted — they are pre-emptively excluded from the conceptual framework. If an economic productivity measure treats only paid labour as productive work, then the unpaid care labour that sustains every economy becomes literally unthinkable as an economic contribution. These absences are not gaps in data that better sampling would fill; they are gaps in the imagination of the people who decide what questions are worth asking.

## What a Missing Data Explorer Would Show

This module is the first in Path 3, and a future interactive tool will make these dynamics visible at scale. A missing data explorer would allow you to select a population group, apply different census definitions and collection methods, and observe how the measured size and welfare profile of that group changes as a result. You would see, for instance, how changing the definition of "employed" by one word shifts the headline unemployment rate by multiple percentage points — with real consequences for political pressure and policy responses. The interactive does not yet exist, but the exercise to run mentally is this: for any statistic you use in your work, ask what decisions were made before a single respondent was interviewed. Who was assumed to be in scope? What categories were treated as natural? That prior architecture shapes everything that follows.
