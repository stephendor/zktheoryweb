## How Similar Are These Two Households?

You are studying economic deprivation across 800 local authorities in England using six variables: unemployment rate, median household income, proportion of households in fuel poverty, child benefit claimant rate, average years of education among adults, and access to green space. You want to identify which authorities face structurally similar patterns of disadvantage — not just similar on one axis, but similar across the whole profile. To do that, you need to define formally what it means for two authorities to be *near* each other in this six-dimensional space. The choice is not obvious, and it is not arbitrary. The metric you choose will determine which clusters the analysis finds, which loops it detects, and ultimately which policy conclusions you draw.

## What a Metric Space Is

A **metric space** is a set $X$ equipped with a **distance function** $d: X \times X \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying four axioms. For all points $x$, $y$, $z$ in $X$:

$$
d(x,y) \geq 0, \quad d(x,y) = 0 \iff x = y, \quad d(x,y) = d(y,x), \quad d(x,z) \leq d(x,y) + d(y,z).
$$

The four lines encode, respectively: distances are non-negative; two points are at zero distance only if they are the same point; distance is symmetric; and no detour is shorter than a direct path (the triangle inequality). These four axioms are the minimal conditions under which "nearness" has a consistent meaning.

Notice that the axioms do not fix a single formula for $d$. Many different distance functions satisfy them. The **Euclidean distance** $d_2(x,y) = \sqrt{\sum_i (x_i - y_i)^2}$ is the most familiar: it treats all coordinates symmetrically and corresponds to the straight-line distance in ordinary space. The **Manhattan distance** $d_1(x,y) = \sum_i |x_i - y_i|$ sums absolute differences along each axis — more robust to outliers in individual variables, because a single extreme coordinate cannot dominate the sum through squaring. The **Chebyshev distance** $d_\infty(x,y) = \max_i |x_i - y_i|$ cares only about the single worst-performing axis. Each of these is a valid metric; each will produce a different point cloud geometry from the same data.

## Point Clouds

A **point cloud** is simply a finite collection of points in some metric space $(\mathbb{R}^n, d)$. Each point represents one observation — one household, one local authority, one time-period — and its coordinates are the values of the measured variables. The cloud as a whole is a finite metric space: a set with a well-defined pairwise distance between every pair of points.

The jump from a continuous probability distribution to a finite point cloud involves real information loss. A distribution over $\mathbb{R}^n$ specifies the probability of every possible combination of variable values; a point cloud records only the $N$ instances that were actually observed. But for TDA, this is sufficient: the *shape* of the cloud — how it clusters, loops, and voids at different scales — carries the structural information we care about, even from a finite sample.

## Scale and Normalisation

One practical complication in constructing a point cloud from social data is that variables are measured on radically different scales. Income in thousands of pounds, years of education in single digits, and proportions between zero and one will not combine sensibly into a Euclidean distance without standardisation. When income varies between £15,000 and £85,000 and education between 7 and 16 years, the squared income difference will numerically swamp the education difference even if both matter equally for deprivation. Standard approaches include **z-score normalisation** (subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation, so each variable has mean zero and variance one) and **min-max normalisation** (rescaling each variable to the interval $[0, 1]$). The right choice depends on domain knowledge: if income truly matters more than green-space access, you might *want* it to dominate the metric.

## Neighbourhoods and $\varepsilon$-Balls

Once a metric is fixed, we can define the **$\varepsilon$-ball** around a point $x$:

$$
B(x, \varepsilon) = \{ y \in X : d(x, y) < \varepsilon \}.
$$

This is the set of all points within distance $\varepsilon$ of $x$. The $\varepsilon$-ball is the primitive notion of neighbourhood: as $\varepsilon$ varies from zero to infinity, the ball expands from containing only $x$ itself to containing the entire point cloud. Systematically expanding $\varepsilon$ and recording what happens — which points enter a neighbourhood, which pairs of points become connected — is the foundation of the Vietoris–Rips filtration introduced in Module 3.

## From Individual Points to Global Structure

The power of metric-space thinking becomes visible only when we look at the global arrangement of many points simultaneously. A single point tells you nothing; ten points with a clear gap between two clusters tells you something about the population's structure; hundreds of points that form a loop in two-dimensional projection tells you that there are two distinct pathways through the deprivation landscape and that they are cyclically connected. None of these conclusions are accessible from summary statistics — they require reasoning about the collective geometry of the point cloud.

## Using the Interactive: Point Cloud & Distance Explorer

The **Point Cloud & Distance Explorer** below makes the ε-ball definition concrete and visceral. Three preset clouds are available: **Scattered** (12 roughly uniform points), **Two clusters** (two tight groups separated by a gap), and **Ring** (16 points on a circle, the canonical topology-revealing shape).

**Click any point** to select it as the centre of the ε-ball. The ball is drawn on the chart and highlighted points — those within distance ε of the centre — are shown in orange. Points outside the ball are shown in blue. The status bar reports the count and indices of neighbours.

**ε slider** controls the radius. At small ε you see only the immediate neighbourhood; drag it rightward and the ball expands, capturing more points. With the **Two clusters** preset, try finding the value of ε at which the first inter-cluster connection forms — this is precisely the scale at which the filtration begins to merge the two components.

**Metric toggle** switches between Euclidean (L₂, circle ball) and Manhattan (L₁, diamond ball). The same ε, the same point, but the set of neighbours changes because the shape of "closeness" has changed. At ε = 0.35, a point diagonally situated at distance (0.2, 0.2) from the centre is inside the Euclidean ball (d₂ ≈ 0.28) but outside the Manhattan ball (d₁ = 0.40). This is not a numerical accident — it is the visual form of the metric-choice problem in social data.

**Distance matrix** (collapsed by default) shows every pairwise distance. Expand it to read the exact distance between any two points. When a point is selected, its row and column are highlighted. Cell colour intensity encodes relative magnitude: darker orange = larger distance.

## How This Module Connects

The formal treatment of distance and metric spaces draws directly on the mathematical framework introduced in **Chapter 2** of *Counting Lives*, which analyses how nineteenth-century poverty surveyors — Booth, Rowntree, Bowley — operationalised the notion of a household's "distance from sufficiency". The historical choices made about what variables to measure and how to aggregate them into a single deprivation score are, in retrospect, choices about metric structure: what it means for two households to be similarly deprived. The modern metric-space formalisation makes those choices explicit and auditable in a way that the original surveyors could not.
