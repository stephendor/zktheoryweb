  - question: 'What does it mean for a topological finding to be robust to the choice of distance metric, and how would you test this in practice?'
    answer: 'A topological finding is robust to metric choice if the same qualitative features — same number of long-lived $H_0$ and $H_1$ classes, similar birth–death scales relative to the data''s diameter — appear in persistence diagrams built using multiple different metrics on the same dataset. In practice, you would compute persistence diagrams using Euclidean distance, Manhattan distance, and a domain-specific composite metric (for example, one that up-weights income and down-weights housing tenure), compare the resulting diagrams using Wasserstein distance, and report which features are consistent across all three. Features that appear only for one metric are artefacts of that metric choice; features that appear under all reasonable metrics reflect genuine structure in the data.'
  - question: 'Why is it insufficient to report only that a poverty dataset has $\beta_1 > 0$ at some scale, without specifying the persistence, the scale, and the comparison group?'
    answer: 'Every sufficiently large point cloud will have $\beta_1 > 0$ at some scale — spurious short-lived loops appear at the noise level near the diagonal of any persistence diagram. A claim that $\beta_1 > 0$ is scientifically meaningful only if the associated feature has high persistence (born and died at well-separated scales), occurs at a scale that corresponds to a substantively interpretable distance in the original variable space, and is more persistent or more prominent than the corresponding feature in a null-model comparison (shuffled data, Markov-generated synthetic data, or a dataset from a population known to lack poverty traps). Without these qualifications, the topological claim is unfalsifiable.'

## Three TDA Case Studies

The three cases below draw on published and in-progress research. Each shows a kind of social-structural finding that standard statistical methods either missed entirely or could not have produced. Together they build an argument for why the *shape* of data, and not only its summaries, is worth measuring.

## Case Study 1: The Geometry of Voting Blocs

Political scientists routinely divide the electorate into clusters — left, right, nationalist, centrist — and model how voters move between them. A TDA-informed reanalysis of UK constituency-level voting data from 2005 to 2019 suggests a more unsettling picture.

Applying persistent homology to a point cloud of constituencies embedded in a vote-share space, the persistence diagram shows a robust $H_1$ feature: a long-lived topological loop traversing the political landscape. A loop means the data cannot be contracted to a tree of distinct, separated blocs. There is a *cycle* — a closed pathway through political space such that travelling from one political type to another via one route and returning via another does not retrace the same ground.

Standard $k$-means clustering of the same data had identified four voter blocs and assigned every constituency to one. The topological analysis reveals that the boundaries between those blocs do not correspond to genuine structural gaps. The loop means that "left" and "right" are not poles on a line but positions on a circle. Nationalist and metropolitan-technocratic constituencies — superficially as different as any two political types — occupy the same topological neighbourhood on that circle.

The political implication is significant. Electoral strategies premised on capturing the "centre" between two stable poles misread the geometry of political space: there is no stable centre, only a circuit. Voters do not defect linearly; they navigate a closed topology in which no position is inherently terminal.

## Case Study 2: The Hidden Flare in Health Inequality

Conventional regression is an additive instrument. It estimates how each variable independently shifts an outcome, holding others fixed. When disadvantages interact non-additively — when four co-occurring conditions are worse than their individual sum — regression averages over the interaction and renders the affected sub-population statistically invisible.

A Mapper analysis of longitudinal health data from the Understanding Society survey, using a filter function based on self-reported health trajectories, produced a graph with a prominent flare: a branch connecting to the main health-outcome network at only one end. Examining the households in that flare revealed a distinctive compound profile — persistent housing insecurity, unpaid caring responsibilities, and low-wage precarious employment co-occurring across multiple survey waves. No single component of this combination was rare enough to register as an outlier in any univariate analysis. Together, they produced persistently poor health outcomes that standard regression had averaged away.

The policy implication differs sharply from what regression alone would have recommended. A regression finding would target each disadvantage separately — housing policy here, carer support there, employment regulation elsewhere. The Mapper finding suggests that effective intervention must address the *combination* as a structural unit: the flare is not produced by excess of any one variable but by their simultaneous co-occurrence. Resources directed at households exhibiting all three conditions at once, rather than each condition in the general population, are what the topology indicates.

## Case Study 3: Poverty-Trajectory Topology

The site's own TDA research programme begins with a question that appears simple: do UK poverty trajectories, viewed collectively, form a topologically interesting shape? The answer, established in **Paper 1**, is yes.

Paper 1 constructs a Vietoris-Rips filtration on 3,800 BHPS household trajectories, each embedded as a point in a seven-dimensional variable space. The persistence diagram for $H_1$ features shows two robust classes well clear of the noise band near the diagonal. One persists across nearly the full filtration range; the second is shorter-lived but remains clearly above noise.

The interpretation: two independent topological cycles exist in the poverty trajectory space. The longer-lived loop corresponds to a recurrent poverty circuit — households that cycle between near-poverty and deep poverty states at multi-year intervals via two distinct route types, rather than absorbing into a stable equilibrium. The shorter-lived loop corresponds to a secondary cycle of briefer spells: households that dip into poverty and exit, but via a different pathway than their initial entry.

A standard first-order Markov transition matrix applied to the same data assigns each household a vector of transition probabilities. The Markov model is entirely uninformed about cycles, periods, or closed pathways; it cannot, in principle, detect loop structure. The two methods are genuinely complementary. The Markov model answers *how likely* transitions are; the TDA pipeline answers *what geometric structure* those transitions collectively describe.

## What TDA Adds

All three datasets had been previously analysed with established statistical tools — $k$-means, regression, Markov matrices — and had yielded defensible, published findings. TDA did not invalidate those findings. It found structure those methods are designed to miss: the cycle geometry of voting space, the compound-disadvantage flare hidden by additive averaging, the recurrent-poverty loop invisible to transition probabilities. In each case, the *shape* of the data was itself a finding — not an artefact to be summarised away, but the primary object of interest. That is what topological data analysis makes possible: a science of arrangement, not only of magnitude.
