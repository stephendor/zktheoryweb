## Introduction

Intergenerational mobility has been central to British sociology since the Oxford Mobility Study. A generation of research has established that parental socioeconomic position predicts children's educational attainment, class position, and income. Most of this literature treats mobility as a status comparison: where did your parent end up, and where did you end up?

This paper asks a different question: does the *structure* of a career — its topological shape over time — transmit from parent to offspring? If career topology is heritable, this suggests that the mechanisms of intergenerational disadvantage extend beyond status transmission to encompass the transmission of career-path instability patterns themselves.

## Background

### Intergenerational Mobility Research

Classic British mobility studies (Goldthorpe 1980; Marshall, Swift, and Roberts 1997) focus on class positions at career peak. Life-course sociology has enriched this with attention to trajectories, but trajectory comparison has been limited to sequence distance methods that do not produce topologically meaningful representations.

### Topological Similarity Measures

To measure topological similarity between parent and offspring trajectories, this paper uses the Wasserstein distance between persistence diagrams — a metric that is interpretable, satisfies stability theorems, and is robust to small perturbations in the data.

## Methods

Matched parent–offspring pairs are constructed from the BHPS using household identifier linkages. Parents' trajectories are observed from labour market entry through age 50; offspring trajectories are observed equivalently. Both are standardised to 30-year sequences. Persistent homology is computed for each individual, and the Wasserstein distance between parent and offspring persistence diagrams is compared to a bootstrap permutation null.

Mediation analysis uses the `mediation` R package with bootstrapped standard errors.

## Data

BHPS household panel data (1991–2009), using household identifier linkage to construct matched parent–offspring pairs. Final matched sample: approximately 1,200 parent–offspring dyads with complete 30-year trajectories for both members.

## Results

### Intergenerational Topology Transmission

The mean Wasserstein distance between matched parent–offspring pairs is significantly smaller than the bootstrap permutation null (z = 4.2, p < 0.001), indicating that parent and offspring career topologies are more similar than chance. The effect holds across all four parent–offspring gender combinations, but is largest for father–son pairs.

### Mediation by Education

Education mediates 28% of the topological transmission effect. The remaining 72% represents direct transmission, consistent with mechanisms including occupational sector inheritance, geographic immobility, and network-mediated access to stable employment.

## Discussion

The heritability of career topology extends the concept of intergenerational transmission from status to structure. The finding that cyclical employment instability (H₁ topology) is more heritable than overall employment level (H₀) is theoretically significant: it suggests that the specific pattern of career precariousness — the rhythm of in-and-out of work — is transmitted between generations, not only the average level of disadvantage.

## Conclusion

Employment trajectory topology transmits intergenerationally in UK panel data, with effect sizes comparable to earnings elasticity estimates. The heritability of instability topology specifically motivates the network-based analyses in Paper 8.
