# Topological Data Analysis Research Programme

## What This Programme Is

This research programme applies **topological data analysis** (TDA) — a branch of mathematics concerned with the shape and structure of data — to two connected problems in social science: the structure of employment trajectories over the working life, and the measurement of poverty.

The programme comprises **ten papers** arranged in three stages. Each stage builds on the last, moving from foundational methods (Stage 1: Papers 1–3) through applied structural analysis (Stage 2: Papers 4–6) to advanced machine learning and policy applications (Stage 3: Papers 7–10).

## Why Topology?

Standard statistical methods model employment as a sequence of independent transitions — from employed to unemployed, from unemployed to inactive. These methods implicitly assume that what happens next depends only on what is happening now. The **Markov Memory Ladder** (Paper 1) tests this assumption directly and finds it wanting: UK employment histories carry persistent structural memory that Markov models miss.

Topology provides the language for describing that memory. Rather than asking "what is the probability of transition $A \to B$?", topology asks "what is the *shape* of the space of all trajectories?" Loops, clusters, flares, and voids in that shape correspond to meaningful social phenomena: precarious cycling between employment states, stable career types, early labour market exit, and structural poverty traps.

## Three Stages of the Programme

### Stage 0–1: Foundations and Exploration (Papers 1–3)

**Paper 1 — The Markov Memory Ladder** establishes the foundational result: UK employment trajectories exhibit topological structure significantly in excess of a Markov-1 null model. It introduces the MML framework and provides the benchmark against which all subsequent analyses are calibrated.

**Paper 2 — Mapper for Interior Trajectory Structure** applies the Mapper algorithm to reveal clusters, flares, and loops within the employment trajectory space. Five robust topological clusters are identified, including a distinctive flare sub-population of early labour-market exits.

**Paper 3 — Zigzag Persistence for Business Cycle Topology** extends the analysis through time, showing how the topology of employment trajectory space changes during recessions. Topological complexity leads official unemployment statistics by approximately two quarters.

### Stage 2: Structural Applications (Papers 4–6)

**Paper 4 — Multi-Parameter PH for Poverty Trap Detection** introduces two-parameter persistent homology to distinguish genuine poverty traps from extended transient poverty, identifying a topological signature of structural trapping concentrated in specific regional and demographic sub-populations.

**Paper 5 — Cross-National Welfare State Topology** applies the TDA framework comparatively across 16 European countries, showing that welfare state regime type is associated with systematic differences in employment trajectory topology.

**Paper 6 — Intergenerational Topological Inheritance** demonstrates that the structural shape of employment trajectories — not only outcomes — transmits across generations, with cyclical instability topology showing the highest heritability.

### Stage 3: Advanced Methods and Policy (Papers 7–10)

**Paper 7 — Geometric Trajectory Forecasting** converts the topological characterisation into a prediction framework, demonstrating that geometric trajectory features substantially outperform Markov-1 baselines in 12-month employment prediction.

**Paper 8 — GNNs on Household Social Graphs** embeds individual topological features in household social graphs, using Graph Neural Networks to reveal social contagion of employment instability at the household level.

**Paper 9 — Combinatorial Complex Neural Networks** extends the network analysis to higher-order neighbourhood structures, achieving state-of-the-art prediction by explicitly modelling group-level employment dynamics.

**Paper 10 — Topological Fairness Analysis** closes the programme by turning TDA on the foundational measurement question: are standard poverty thresholds geometrically equitable across demographic groups? Findings motivate a plural measurement framework informed by topological fairness criteria.

## Methods and Data

The programme draws on a consistent set of TDA methods — persistent homology, Mapper, zigzag persistence, multi-parameter persistence, and topological neural networks — applied to UK panel survey data (BHPS and Understanding Society) and cross-national microdata (EU-SILC).

For technical details, see the [Methods](/tda/methods) reference pages. For data access and construction protocols, see the [Data Sources](/tda/data-sources) documentation.

## Code and Replication
